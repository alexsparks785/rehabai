# Winged Scapula Exercises: Strengthen and Stabilize Your Shoulder Blade

A winged scapula occurs when the shoulder blade protrudes from the back like a wing rather than lying flat against the ribcage. This can cause shoulder pain, weakness, and difficulty with overhead movements. Most cases respond well to targeted strengthening exercises.

## Understanding Winged Scapula

**What's happening:**
- Shoulder blade doesn't stay flat against ribs
- Inner border or bottom corner sticks out
- Usually visible when pushing against wall or doing push-ups
- May be subtle or quite pronounced

**Common causes:**
- **Serratus anterior weakness** (most common) — long thoracic nerve injury or disuse
- **Trapezius weakness** — spinal accessory nerve injury
- **Rhomboid weakness** — less common
- Poor posture and muscle imbalances
- Trauma or surgery complications
- Viral illness (can affect nerves)

**Symptoms:**
- Visible shoulder blade protrusion
- Shoulder pain or aching
- Difficulty reaching overhead
- Weakness with pushing movements
- Fatigue with arm activities
- Clicking or snapping sensation

## Assessment

### Wall Push-Up Test
1. Stand facing wall, hands on wall
2. Do a push-up motion
3. Look at shoulder blades in mirror or have someone watch
4. **Winging:** Shoulder blade pops out prominently

### Overhead Reach Test
1. Raise arms overhead
2. Note if one shoulder blade wings
3. Compare sides

## Serratus Anterior Strengthening

The serratus anterior is the primary muscle that holds the scapula against the ribs. This is usually the focus of treatment.

### Serratus Punches (Wall)
Start here if very weak:
1. Stand facing wall
2. Place palm on wall, arm straight
3. Push into wall, protracting shoulder blade
4. Feel shoulder blade wrap around ribs
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. 3 sets of 12

### Serratus Punches (Supine)
1. Lie on back
2. Hold light weight or no weight
3. Arm straight toward ceiling
4. Push toward ceiling (shoulder blade lifts off floor)
5. Lower shoulder blade back down
6. 3 sets of 12

### Serratus Punches (Standing with Band)
1. Band anchored behind you
2. Arm straight in front
3. Punch forward, protracting shoulder blade
4. Control return
5. 3 sets of 12 each arm

### Push-Up Plus
Key exercise for serratus:
1. Push-up position (on knees if needed)
2. Do a push-up
3. At the top, push further—round upper back
4. Protract shoulder blades fully
5. Return to normal push-up top position
6. 3 sets of 10

### Quadruped Protraction
1. On hands and knees
2. Without bending elbows, push floor away
3. Round upper back, spread shoulder blades
4. Let shoulder blades come together
5. 3 sets of 12

### Wall Slides with Protraction
1. Back against wall
2. Arms in W position
3. Slide up to Y position
4. At top, push arms forward slightly (protract)
5. Slide back down
6. 2 sets of 12

## Trapezius Strengthening

If trapezius weakness is involved (less common):

### Shoulder Shrugs
1. Hold weights at sides
2. Shrug shoulders toward ears
3. Hold 3 seconds
4. Lower with control
5. 3 sets of 15

### Prone Y Raises
1. Lie face down
2. Arms at 45° (Y position)
3. Thumbs up
4. Lift arms toward ceiling
5. Squeeze between shoulder blades
6. 3 sets of 12

### Prone T Raises
1. Lie face down
2. Arms out to sides (T position)
3. Lift toward ceiling
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 3 sets of 12

### Face Pulls
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. Rotate so thumbs point back
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 3 sets of 15

## Rhomboid Strengthening

### Rows
1. Band or cable at chest height
2. Pull toward body
3. Squeeze shoulder blades together
4. Control return
5. 3 sets of 12

### Prone Rows
1. Lie face down on bench
2. Arms hanging
3. Row weights up
4. Squeeze at top
5. 3 sets of 12

### Band Pull-Aparts
1. Hold band in front, arms extended
2. Pull band apart, squeezing back
3. Control return
4. 3 sets of 15

## Scapular Stability Exercises

### Scapular Clock
1. Stand facing wall, forearm on wall
2. Make circles with shoulder blade
3. Like moving around a clock face
4. 10 circles each direction

### Dynamic Hug
1. Arms out to sides
2. Bring arms forward like hugging
3. Round upper back, protract fully
4. Open arms back out
5. 15 repetitions

### Serratus Wall Slide
1. Forearm on wall, elbow at shoulder height
2. Slide arm up the wall
3. Keep pressing into wall throughout
4. Slide back down
5. 3 sets of 10 each arm

### Bear Position Hold
1. On hands and knees
2. Lift knees 1-2 inches off floor
3. Keep back flat
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. Builds scapular stability

## Stretching

Tight muscles can contribute to winging:

### Pec Stretch
1. Doorway stretch
2. Forearm on doorframe
3. Step through
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Lat Stretch
1. Hold doorframe or pole
2. Push hips back
3. Feel stretch under arm
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Upper Trap Stretch
1. Drop ear to shoulder
2. Gentle hand pressure
3. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Posture Corrections

### Sitting
1. Sit tall
2. Shoulders back but relaxed
3. Don't let shoulder blades wing forward
4. Computer screen at eye level

### Standing
1. Stack ears over shoulders
2. Gentle shoulder blade squeeze
3. Core engaged

### During Activities
1. Avoid prolonged forward reaching
2. Keep elbows closer to body when possible
3. Strengthen for demands of your activities

## Progression

### Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Foundation
- Wall serratus punches
- Supine serratus punches
- Quadruped protraction
- Basic rows

### Phase 2 (Weeks 3-4): Build
- Push-up plus (on knees)
- Band serratus work
- Y-T exercises
- Increased repetitions

### Phase 3 (Weeks 5-8): Strengthen
- Push-up plus (full)
- Heavier resistance
- Wall slides with protraction
- Bear holds

### Phase 4 (Weeks 8+): Maintain
- Regular serratus work
- Include in overall routine
- Progress as able

## Daily Routine

### Morning (10 minutes)
1. Quadruped protraction (2 min)
2. Serratus punches (3 min)
3. Wall slides (3 min)
4. Posture check (2 min)

### Evening (15 minutes)
1. Push-up plus (4 min)
2. Y-T raises (4 min)
3. Rows (4 min)
4. Stretching (3 min)

## When to Seek Help

See a doctor or physical therapist if:
- Winging appeared suddenly after injury
- Significant weakness
- Pain is severe
- No improvement after 6-8 weeks
- Winging getting worse
- Other neurological symptoms

**They may:**
- Test nerve function
- Order imaging
- Provide specific nerve rehab
- Rule out other conditions

## Recovery Timeline

**Muscle weakness (no nerve damage):** 4-8 weeks
**Mild nerve involvement:** 3-6 months
**Significant nerve injury:** 6-24 months (some cases resolve spontaneously)

## What to Expect

**Exercise CAN:**
- Strengthen serratus anterior
- Improve scapular position
- Reduce pain
- Improve function
- Often fully resolve winging

**Important notes:**
- If nerve damage is cause, recovery depends on nerve healing
- Consistency is crucial
- May take months for full improvement
- Most cases improve significantly with proper exercise

Winged scapula caused by muscle weakness responds very well to targeted strengthening. Focus on serratus anterior exercises, be patient, and stay consistent for best results.
