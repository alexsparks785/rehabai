# Rotator Cuff Repair Exercises: Post-Surgery Recovery Guide

Rotator cuff repair surgery reattaches torn tendons to the bone, but surgery is just the beginning. The tendon needs months to heal and the shoulder needs careful rehabilitation to regain strength and function. Rush too fast and you risk re-tear; progress too slowly and you risk stiffness.

## Understanding Your Recovery

### What Was Done

During rotator cuff repair:
- Torn tendon(s) reattached to bone with sutures and anchors
- May include: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis, or teres minor
- Often includes subacromial decompression

### Healing Timeline

**Tendon-to-bone healing:**
- Weeks 0-6: Initial attachment, very fragile
- Weeks 6-12: Strengthening bond, still healing
- Weeks 12-24: Maturing repair
- 6-12 months: Full healing

**Key insight:** The repair is weakest when you feel best. As pain decreases, the tendon is still healing.

### Factors Affecting Recovery

- Tear size (small vs. massive)
- Number of tendons repaired
- Tissue quality
- Patient age
- Compliance with restrictions

## Phase 1: Protection Phase (Weeks 0-6)

### Sling Use
- Wear sling as directed (typically 4-6 weeks)
- Remove only for exercises and hygiene
- Sleep in sling or with pillow support

### Goals:
- Protect healing repair
- Control pain and swelling
- Prevent shoulder stiffness
- Maintain elbow/wrist/hand mobility

### 1. Pendulum Exercises

Start within first few days.

**How to do it:**
1. Remove sling
2. Lean forward, support with good arm
3. Let affected arm hang completely relaxed
4. Gently sway body to create small arm circles
5. 2-3 minutes, 3-4 times daily

**Key:** Arm is PASSIVE—body creates motion, not shoulder muscles.

### 2. Elbow, Wrist, and Hand Exercises

Prevent stiffness in non-operated areas.

**How to do it:**
- Elbow: Bend and straighten fully
- Wrist: Circles, flexion/extension
- Hand: Make fist, spread fingers, ball squeezes
- Throughout the day

### 3. Scapular Retractions (Pinches)

**How to do it:**
1. Sitting or standing
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together gently
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Relax
5. 10-15 repetitions
6. Don't shrug shoulders

### 4. Passive External Rotation (Supine)

**When:** Usually 2-4 weeks post-op, per surgeon protocol.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, elbow at side bent 90°
2. Use wand or other arm to rotate hand outward
3. Go ONLY to limit prescribed by surgeon
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

**Critical:** No active motion. Other arm does all the work.

### 5. Passive Forward Flexion (Supine)

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back
2. Hold wand with both hands
3. Use good arm to lift affected arm forward/overhead
4. Go to prescribed limit
5. Hold 5 seconds at top
6. 10-15 repetitions

### What to AVOID Phase 1:
- Any active shoulder motion
- Lifting anything (even coffee cup)
- Reaching behind back
- Pushing or pulling
- Sleeping on operated side
- Driving (usually)

## Phase 2: Early Motion (Weeks 6-12)

### Sling Use
- Weaned off sling (typically weeks 4-6)
- May use for comfort or protection in crowds

### Goals:
- Achieve full passive range of motion
- Begin active-assisted motion
- Start gentle rotator cuff activation

### 6. Active-Assisted Forward Flexion

**How to do it:**
1. Standing or sitting
2. Hold wand with both hands
3. Use good arm to help lift both arms forward
4. Progress range as tolerated
5. 15-20 repetitions
6. 3-4 times daily

### 7. Active-Assisted External Rotation

**How to do it:**
1. Hold wand, elbows at sides
2. Use good arm to push affected arm into external rotation
3. Progress range gradually
4. 15-20 repetitions

### 8. Wall Walking (Flexion)

**How to do it:**
1. Face wall, arm's length away
2. Walk fingers up wall as high as able
3. Slowly walk down
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Progress range daily

### 9. Table Slides

**How to do it:**
1. Sit at table, affected forearm on table
2. Slide arm forward, allowing shoulder to flex
3. Slide back
4. Use towel under arm for smooth glide
5. 15-20 repetitions

### 10. Supine Active Flexion (Gravity-Eliminated)

First active motion.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arm at side
2. Lift arm toward ceiling (not overhead yet)
3. Use other hand to assist if needed
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Progress toward overhead

### 11. Isometric Rotator Cuff

Muscle activation without movement.

**External rotation:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Press back of wrist into wall/doorframe
3. Hold 5 seconds at 25-50% effort
4. 10 repetitions

**Internal rotation:**
1. Same position
2. Press palm into wall/doorframe
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

## Phase 3: Active Motion (Weeks 12-16)

### Goals:
- Full active range of motion
- Progressive strengthening
- Improve scapular control

### 12. Active Forward Flexion (Standing)

**How to do it:**
1. Standing, arm at side
2. Lift arm forward and overhead
3. Control descent
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. No weight yet

### 13. Side-Lying External Rotation

Beginning rotator cuff strengthening.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Affected elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Rotate hand toward ceiling
4. Lower slowly
5. 15-20 repetitions
6. NO weight initially
7. Add 1-2 lbs when ready

### 14. Prone Row

Scapular and posterior shoulder strengthening.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down, arm hanging
2. Pull elbow toward ceiling
3. Squeeze shoulder blade
4. Lower slowly
5. 15 repetitions
6. Start without weight, progress to 2-5 lbs

### 15. Prone T Raises

Lower trapezius and posterior deltoid.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down, arms out to sides
2. Lift arms toward ceiling, thumbs up
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. Hold 3 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions
6. No weight initially

### 16. Prone Y Raises

Lower trapezius focus.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down, arms in Y position
2. Lift arms, thumbs up
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. Hold 3 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

### 17. Scaption (Empty Can Modified)

Supraspinatus strengthening.

**How to do it:**
1. Standing, arm at side
2. Lift arm at 30-45° from front (scaption plane)
3. Thumb up (not down like empty can)
4. Stop at shoulder height
5. Lower slowly
6. 15 repetitions
7. Progress with light weight

## Phase 4: Strengthening (Weeks 16-24)

### Goals:
- Progressive resistance training
- Full functional motion
- Prepare for return to activity

### 18. External Rotation with Band

**How to do it:**
1. Band anchored at elbow height
2. Elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Rotate outward against resistance
4. Control return
5. 3 sets x 15 repetitions
6. Progress resistance

### 19. Internal Rotation with Band

**How to do it:**
1. Same setup, pull inward
2. Elbow stays at side
3. Control the motion
4. 3 sets x 15 repetitions

### 20. Rows (Cable or Band)

**How to do it:**
1. Pull toward body
2. Squeeze shoulder blades
3. Control return
4. 3 sets x 12-15 repetitions

### 21. Lat Pulldown (Light)

**How to do it:**
1. Medium grip, controlled motion
2. Pull to upper chest
3. Control return
4. 3 sets x 12-15 repetitions
5. Start at 30-40% normal weight

### 22. Wall Push-Ups

Progressive closed-chain exercise.

**How to do it:**
1. Hands on wall at shoulder height
2. Lower chest to wall
3. Push back
4. 3 sets x 15 repetitions
5. Progress: incline, then floor

### 23. Shoulder Press (When Cleared)

Usually 4-5+ months post-op.

**How to do it:**
1. Start very light
2. Press overhead
3. Control descent
4. 3 sets x 12-15 repetitions
5. Progress weight slowly

## Phase 5: Return to Activity (Months 6+)

### Goals:
- Sport-specific preparation
- Full strength restoration
- Return to unrestricted activity

### Typical Timeline:
- Light lifting: 4-6 months
- Swimming: 5-6 months
- Golf: 4-6 months
- Tennis: 6-9 months
- Throwing: 9-12 months
- Contact sports: 9-12 months

### Criteria for Return:
- Full pain-free range of motion
- Strength >85% of other side
- Negative clinical tests
- Sport-specific movements pain-free
- Surgeon clearance

## Precautions Throughout Recovery

### Weeks 0-6:
- No active shoulder motion
- No lifting (even light objects with operated arm)
- No driving
- No reaching behind back

### Weeks 6-12:
- No lifting >1-2 lbs
- No sudden movements
- No reaching behind back under load
- No pushing or pulling

### Weeks 12-24:
- Progress weights gradually
- Avoid heavy overhead lifting
- Listen to pain—back off if sharp pain

## Warning Signs

**Contact your surgeon if:**
- Sudden increase in pain
- Loss of motion that was achieved
- Popping or giving way sensation
- Signs of infection (fever, redness, drainage)
- Inability to progress as expected

## Factors That May Extend Recovery

- Larger tear (massive repair)
- Multiple tendons repaired
- Poor tissue quality
- Revision surgery
- Non-compliance with restrictions
- Complications (stiffness, re-tear)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Protect the repair** — First 6-12 weeks are critical
2. **Passive before active** — Let other arm or therapist move your shoulder initially
3. **Tendon healing takes time** — 4-6 months for solid healing
4. **Pain isn't your guide** — You'll feel good before you're healed
5. **Strengthen progressively** — Build back slowly
6. **Follow your surgeon's protocol** — Every repair is different

Rotator cuff repair rehabilitation requires patience. The goal is a strong, functional shoulder for life—not a fast return that risks re-tear. Work closely with your surgeon and physical therapist, follow the phases, and trust the process.
