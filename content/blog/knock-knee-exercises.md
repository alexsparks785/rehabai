# Knock Knee Exercises: Strengthen and Align Your Legs

Knock knees (genu valgum)—when the knees angle inward and touch while the ankles remain apart—can cause knee pain, hip issues, and problems up and down the kinetic chain. While severe structural cases may need medical intervention, many people with mild to moderate knock knees can improve alignment and reduce symptoms through targeted exercises.

## Understanding Knock Knees

**What's happening:**
- Knees angle toward midline
- Ankles remain separated when knees touch
- Often accompanied by foot pronation (flat feet)
- May be structural (bone) or functional (muscle imbalance)

**Common causes:**
- Weak hip abductors (glute medius)
- Tight adductors (inner thigh)
- Weak external hip rotators
- Flat feet or overpronation
- Obesity
- Some developmental conditions
- Normal in children (usually resolves by age 7)

**Associated problems:**
- Knee pain (especially patellofemoral)
- IT band syndrome
- Hip pain
- Ankle problems
- Increased ACL injury risk
- Early knee arthritis

## Assessment

### Standing Test
1. Stand with feet together
2. Note if knees touch before ankles
3. Measure gap between ankles when knees touch
4. Check if one side worse than other

### Single Leg Squat Test
1. Stand on one leg
2. Slowly squat down
3. Watch knee in mirror
4. **Knock knee pattern:** Knee dives inward

### Walking/Running Assessment
1. Watch knee position during gait
2. Note if knees collapse inward at any point
3. Video from front is helpful

## Phase 1: Release Tight Muscles

### Adductor (Inner Thigh) Foam Rolling
1. Lie face down
2. One leg out to side, bent at 90°
3. Roller under inner thigh
4. Roll from knee toward groin
5. 2-3 minutes each leg

### IT Band Foam Rolling
1. Side-lying on roller
2. Roller under outer thigh
3. Roll from hip to just above knee
4. 2-3 minutes each side

### Hip Flexor Release
1. Face down, ball under front of hip
2. Find tender spots
3. Hold 30-60 seconds
4. Breathe deeply

## Phase 2: Stretch Tight Muscles

### Adductor Stretch (Seated)
1. Sit tall, soles of feet together
2. Knees out to sides
3. Lean forward from hips
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Adductor Stretch (Side Lunge)
1. Wide stance
2. Shift weight to one side
3. Bend that knee
4. Keep other leg straight
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Frog Stretch
1. On hands and knees
2. Spread knees wide apart
3. Feet turned out
4. Sit back toward heels
5. Hold 30-60 seconds

### 90/90 Stretch
1. Sit with front leg bent 90° in front
2. Back leg bent 90° to side
3. Sit tall
4. Lean forward over front leg
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Phase 3: Strengthen Weak Muscles

### Hip Abductor Exercises

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent 45°
2. Keep feet together
3. Lift top knee toward ceiling
4. Don't let hips roll back
5. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Side-lying, bottom knee bent
2. Top leg straight, slightly behind
3. Lift top leg toward ceiling
4. Don't rotate hip forward
5. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Banded Lateral Walks**
1. Band around ankles or above knees
2. Quarter squat position
3. Step sideways
4. Keep tension on band
5. 20 steps each direction

**Monster Walks**
1. Band around ankles
2. Quarter squat
3. Walk forward at 45° angles
4. Maintain band tension
5. 20 steps

### Hip External Rotation Exercises

**Clamshells (already listed)**

**Seated External Rotation**
1. Sit on chair, feet flat
2. Band around outside of knees
3. Push knees apart against band
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 3 sets of 15

**Fire Hydrants**
1. On hands and knees
2. Lift knee out to side
3. Keep 90° bend at knee
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

### Glute Strengthening

**Glute Bridges**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Band above knees (push out against it)
3. Lift hips
4. Squeeze glutes at top
5. 3 sets of 15

**Single Leg Glute Bridge**
1. Same as above
2. Extend one leg
3. Bridge on one leg
4. Keep hips level
5. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Hip Thrusts**
1. Upper back on bench
2. Band above knees
3. Feet flat on floor
4. Drive hips up
5. Keep knees pushed out
6. 3 sets of 12

## Phase 4: Movement Retraining

### Wall Squat with Ball
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Small ball between knees
3. Squat down
4. Push knees into ball
5. Practice correct knee tracking
6. 3 sets of 12

### Bodyweight Squat with Band
1. Band above knees
2. Stand shoulder-width apart
3. Squat down
4. Push knees OUT against band
5. Watch knees track over toes
6. 3 sets of 12

### Single Leg Squat to Box
1. Stand on one leg in front of box/chair
2. Slowly lower to sit
3. Focus on keeping knee over foot
4. Stand up
5. Watch in mirror—no knee cave
6. 3 sets of 8 each leg

### Step Downs with Control
1. Stand on step (6-8 inches)
2. Slowly lower opposite foot to floor
3. Keep stance knee tracking over foot
4. Tap floor and return
5. 3 sets of 10 each leg

### Walking with Awareness
1. Walk slowly
2. Think about pushing knees slightly outward
3. Don't let knees collapse inward
4. Practice 5 minutes daily

## Foot Considerations

Flat feet contribute to knock knees. Address both:

### Arch Strengthening
1. Short foot exercise (foot doming)
2. Towel scrunches
3. Marble pickups
4. See flat feet exercises

### Footwear
- Shoes with arch support
- Consider orthotics if significant pronation
- Avoid completely flat shoes

## Daily Routine

### Morning (10 minutes)
1. Clamshells (2 min each side)
2. Monster walks (3 min)
3. Glute bridges with band (3 min)

### During Day
1. Awareness during walking
2. Don't stand with knees locked inward
3. Squeeze glutes periodically

### Evening (15 minutes)
1. Foam rolling (5 min)
2. Adductor and hip stretches (4 min)
3. Full strengthening routine (6 min)

## Exercise Modifications

### During Squats
- Use band above knees
- Think "knees out"
- Don't go deep if form breaks down
- Start with box squats

### During Lunges
- Watch front knee in mirror
- Keep it tracking over foot
- Use lighter weights until form is solid

### During Running
- Shorten stride initially
- Focus on knee alignment
- May need gait retraining with professional

## Progress Timeline

**Week 1-2:** Learn exercises, build awareness
**Week 3-4:** Notice improved muscle activation
**Month 2:** Better control during squats
**Month 3-6:** Visible alignment improvement
**Ongoing:** Maintenance required

## When to Seek Help

See a doctor or physical therapist if you have:
- Significant pain with activity
- Very severe knock knees (large ankle gap)
- One leg much worse than other
- No improvement after 2-3 months
- History of knee surgery or injury

**They may recommend:**
- Custom orthotics
- Bracing
- More intensive PT
- Surgical evaluation (rare, severe cases)

## What's Realistic

**Exercise CAN:**
- Strengthen muscles that control alignment
- Improve functional knee tracking
- Reduce symptoms and pain
- Prevent worsening
- Improve movement patterns

**Exercise CANNOT:**
- Change bone structure
- Fix severe skeletal deformity
- Work overnight

Most functional knock knees respond well to consistent strengthening of the hip abductors and external rotators. The key is building strength AND movement awareness so the knees track properly during daily activities and exercise.
