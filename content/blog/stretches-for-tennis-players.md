Tennis demands explosive power, quick direction changes, and repetitive overhead motions. This combination creates specific flexibility needs—and specific injury risks. Tight shoulders limit your serve, restricted hips slow your movement, and stiff forearms lead to tennis elbow.

These stretches target the key areas tennis players need: shoulders, hips, core rotation, and forearms. Use them before matches to prepare and after to recover.

## Why Tennis Players Need to Stretch

**The serve is demanding.** Overhead motions require excellent shoulder and thoracic mobility.

**Quick direction changes stress the hips.** Lateral movement and lunging need flexible, stable hips.

**Rotation powers your strokes.** Tight thoracic spine and hips limit rotational power.

**Repetitive stress causes injury.** Tennis elbow, shoulder problems, and back pain are common without proper flexibility.

## The Best Stretches for Tennis Players

### 1. Shoulder External Rotation Stretch

**What it targets:** Rotator cuff, shoulder mobility for serving

**How to do it:**
1. Hold a tennis racket or towel behind your back
2. Right hand reaches over your right shoulder, left hand behind your back from below
3. Gently pull down with your left hand to stretch your right shoulder into external rotation
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Switch arms

### 2. Cross-Body Shoulder Stretch

**What it targets:** Posterior shoulder, important after serving

**How to do it:**
1. Bring your right arm across your body at shoulder height
2. Use your left hand to pull your right arm closer to your chest
3. Keep your right shoulder down
4. Feel the stretch in the back of your shoulder
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Switch sides

### 3. Doorway Chest Stretch

**What it targets:** Pectorals, front shoulders—opens chest for full serve motion

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in a doorway
2. Place forearms on door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step one foot forward
4. Lean through the doorway
5. Feel the stretch in your chest
6. Hold 30-45 seconds

**Variation:** Move elbows higher and lower to target different chest fibers.

### 4. Thoracic Rotation (Seated or Standing)

**What it targets:** Mid-back rotation—essential for groundstrokes and serve

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in a chair or stand with feet shoulder-width apart
2. Cross arms over chest or hold racket across shoulders
3. Keep hips facing forward
4. Rotate your torso to the right as far as comfortable
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Rotate to the left
7. Repeat 3 times each direction

### 5. Open Books (Lying Thoracic Rotation)

**What it targets:** Deep thoracic mobility, chest opening

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your right side with knees bent 90 degrees
2. Extend both arms in front of you, palms together
3. Keeping knees stacked, rotate your left arm up and over toward the floor behind you
4. Let your head follow your hand
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Switch sides

### 6. Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling)

**What it targets:** Hip flexors—tight from crouching and lunging

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on your right knee, left foot flat in front
2. Keep torso upright
3. Push hips forward gently
4. Squeeze right glute for deeper stretch
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Switch sides

### 7. 90-90 Hip Stretch

**What it targets:** Hip rotation in both directions—crucial for court movement

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with front leg bent 90 degrees in front (shin parallel to body)
2. Back leg bent 90 degrees to the side
3. Sit tall, lean slightly forward over front leg
4. Hold 30-45 seconds
5. Switch positions (rotate body to reverse legs)

### 8. Figure-4 Stretch

**What it targets:** Glutes, piriformis, hip external rotators

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent
2. Cross right ankle over left knee
3. Reach through and clasp behind left thigh
4. Pull thigh toward chest
5. Feel stretch in right hip/glute
6. Hold 30-45 seconds
7. Switch sides

### 9. Standing Quad Stretch

**What it targets:** Quadriceps—heavily used in tennis movement

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on your left leg (hold racket or fence for balance)
2. Bend right knee, grab right foot behind you
3. Pull heel toward glute
4. Keep knees together, stand tall
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Switch legs

### 10. Standing Calf Stretch

**What it targets:** Calves—crucial for quick starts and stops

**How to do it:**
1. Stand facing fence or wall
2. Step right foot back, keep it straight
3. Press heel into ground
4. Lean forward, bending front knee
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Bend back knee slightly to target soleus
7. Hold 20 seconds
8. Switch legs

### 11. Wrist Flexor Stretch

**What it targets:** Forearm flexors—prevents golfer's elbow

**How to do it:**
1. Extend your right arm, palm up
2. Use left hand to gently pull fingers toward floor
3. Keep arm straight
4. Feel stretch in inner forearm
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Switch arms

### 12. Wrist Extensor Stretch

**What it targets:** Forearm extensors—prevents tennis elbow

**How to do it:**
1. Extend your right arm, palm down
2. Use left hand to gently pull hand toward floor
3. Keep arm straight
4. Feel stretch in outer forearm
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Switch arms

### 13. Lateral Lunge Stretch

**What it targets:** Inner thighs, hips—for lateral movement

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet wide apart
2. Shift weight to your right, bending right knee
3. Keep left leg straight, foot flat
4. Feel stretch in left inner thigh
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Switch sides

### 14. Standing Side Bend

**What it targets:** Obliques, lats—for reaching and serving

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart
2. Reach right arm overhead
3. Lean to the left
4. Feel stretch along right side
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Switch sides

## Pre-Match Warm-Up Routine (10 minutes)

Dynamic stretches before play:

1. **Arm circles:** 10 forward, 10 backward
2. **Trunk rotations (standing):** 10 each direction
3. **Leg swings front-to-back:** 10 each leg
4. **Leg swings side-to-side:** 10 each leg
5. **Lateral lunges (dynamic):** 5 each side
6. **Walking lunges with rotation:** 5 each side
7. **High knees:** 20 reps
8. **Practice swings:** Shadow groundstrokes and serves at increasing intensity

## Post-Match Recovery Routine (15 minutes)

Static stretches after play:

1. **Wrist flexor stretch:** Both arms, 30 seconds each
2. **Wrist extensor stretch:** Both arms, 30 seconds each
3. **Cross-body shoulder stretch:** Both sides, 30 seconds each
4. **Doorway chest stretch:** 45 seconds
5. **Open books:** Both sides, 45 seconds each
6. **Hip flexor stretch:** Both sides, 45 seconds each
7. **Figure-4 stretch:** Both sides, 45 seconds each
8. **Standing quad stretch:** Both legs, 30 seconds each
9. **Calf stretches (both variations):** Both legs, 30 seconds each

## Stretches for Common Tennis Injuries

### Tennis Elbow Prevention:
- Wrist extensor stretch (critical)
- Wrist flexor stretch
- Forearm massage/rolling

### Shoulder Pain Prevention:
- Cross-body stretch
- Doorway chest stretch
- External rotation stretches
- Thoracic rotation

### Lower Back Pain Prevention:
- Hip flexor stretch
- Open books
- Cat-cow
- Figure-4 stretch

### Knee Pain Prevention:
- Quad stretch
- Hamstring stretch
- Calf stretch
- IT band stretch

## Off-Court Flexibility Routine (20 minutes)

Do 2-3 times per week for optimal flexibility:

1. **Cat-cow:** 15 cycles
2. **Open books:** 60 seconds each side
3. **Thread the needle:** 45 seconds each side
4. **Doorway chest stretch:** 90 seconds (vary arm positions)
5. **Cross-body shoulder stretch:** 45 seconds each side
6. **Hip flexor stretch:** 60 seconds each side
7. **90-90 hip stretch:** 45 seconds each position
8. **Figure-4 stretch:** 60 seconds each side
9. **Hamstring stretch (supine):** 45 seconds each leg
10. **Quad stretch (lying):** 45 seconds each leg
11. **Calf stretches:** 30 seconds each variation, each leg
12. **Wrist stretches:** 30 seconds each direction, each arm

## The Bottom Line

Tennis performance depends on mobility through your:

1. **Shoulders**—for serving and overhead shots
2. **Thoracic spine**—for rotation power
3. **Hips**—for movement and stroke production
4. **Forearms**—to prevent tennis elbow

Make stretching as important as your court time. A consistent flexibility routine will improve your game and keep you playing pain-free for years.
