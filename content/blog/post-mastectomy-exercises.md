# Post-Mastectomy Exercises: Restore Arm and Shoulder Function

After mastectomy or breast cancer surgery, your arm and shoulder need careful rehabilitation. Surgery affects muscles, lymph nodes, and nerves, leading to tightness, weakness, and risk of lymphedema. The right exercises—started at the right time—restore function, reduce complications, and help you return to normal activities.

## Understanding Post-Mastectomy Recovery

Breast surgery affects more than breast tissue:

**Surgical impacts:**
- Chest muscles cut or moved (especially with reconstruction)
- Lymph nodes removed (axillary dissection)
- Skin tightness from incisions
- Nerve damage (numbness, tingling common)
- Drain tubes initially limiting movement

**Potential complications:**
- Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis)
- Cording (axillary web syndrome)
- Lymphedema (arm swelling)
- Postural changes
- Chronic pain

**Exercise goals:**
- Restore full shoulder range of motion
- Prevent frozen shoulder
- Reduce lymphedema risk
- Rebuild strength
- Improve posture

## When to Start Exercises

**Important:** Follow your surgeon's specific instructions. General timeline:

**Days 1-7 (Drains in):**
- Deep breathing exercises
- Walking
- Gentle hand and wrist movements
- Limited shoulder movement (usually below 90 degrees)

**Week 1-2 (After drains removed):**
- Begin gentle range-of-motion exercises
- Progress as surgeon allows

**Weeks 2-6:**
- Progressive stretching
- Full range-of-motion work
- Begin gentle strengthening

**Weeks 6+:**
- Continue stretching
- Progressive strengthening
- Return to activities

## Phase 1: Early Exercises (Days 1-14)

### Deep Breathing

Prevents pulmonary complications and gently moves chest:

1. Sit or recline comfortably
2. Place hand on belly
3. Breathe deeply into belly
4. Exhale slowly
5. 10 breaths, 3-4 times daily

### Hand and Wrist Exercises

**Finger exercises:**
1. Make a fist, then spread fingers wide
2. Touch each finger to thumb
3. 10 repetitions, several times daily

**Wrist circles:**
1. Rotate wrists in circles
2. 10 each direction
3. Several times daily

### Arm Elevation (While Drains In)

1. Lie on back
2. Support affected arm with other hand
3. Gently lift arm toward ceiling (not overhead)
4. Stay below 90 degrees unless cleared
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. 5-10 repetitions, 3 times daily

### Ball Squeeze

1. Squeeze soft ball or rolled sock
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. Release
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Promotes circulation, reduces swelling

## Phase 2: Range of Motion (Weeks 2-6)

Once drains are out and surgeon clears, progress to:

### Wall Climbing

1. Face wall, arm's length away
2. Place hand on wall at waist level
3. Walk fingers up wall as high as comfortable
4. Hold at top 5-10 seconds
5. Walk fingers back down
6. 10 repetitions, 2-3 times daily
7. Mark progress height with tape

### Side Wall Climbing

1. Stand sideways to wall, affected side toward wall
2. Walk fingers up wall
3. Hold at top 5-10 seconds
4. Walk back down
5. 10 repetitions, 2-3 times daily

### Wand/Stick Exercises

Use a broomstick, cane, or yardstick:

**Overhead lift:**
1. Hold wand with both hands, shoulder-width
2. Lie on back
3. Use good arm to help lift wand overhead
4. Go as far as comfortable
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. Lower slowly
7. 10 repetitions

**Side stretch:**
1. Hold wand behind back horizontally
2. Use good arm to gently push wand toward affected side
3. Feel stretch under arm
4. Hold 10-20 seconds
5. 5-10 repetitions

### Shoulder Circles

1. Lean forward, supporting yourself with good arm on table
2. Let affected arm hang
3. Swing arm in small circles
4. 10 circles each direction
5. Let gravity assist the stretch

### Shoulder Blade Squeeze

1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Release
5. 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 3: Advanced Stretching (Weeks 4-8)

### Corner Stretch

1. Stand facing corner
2. Place forearms on each wall
3. Lean in gently
4. Feel stretch across chest
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. 3-5 repetitions

### Doorway Stretch

1. Stand in doorway
2. Place affected arm on door frame
3. Gently turn body away
4. Feel stretch across chest
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Vary arm height for different stretch angles

### Cross-Body Stretch

1. Use good arm to pull affected arm across chest
2. Feel stretch in back of shoulder
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. 2-3 repetitions

### Hands Behind Back Stretch

1. Hold towel behind back—good hand up, affected hand down
2. Good hand gently pulls towel up
3. Feel stretch in affected shoulder
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. Progress to holding hands directly

## Phase 4: Strengthening (6+ Weeks)

Start with light resistance (1-2 lbs or resistance band):

### Bicep Curls

1. Hold light weight
2. Curl weight toward shoulder
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. 2 sets

### Shoulder Flexion

1. Hold light weight
2. Raise arm forward to shoulder level
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. 2 sets

### Shoulder Abduction

1. Hold light weight
2. Raise arm out to side to shoulder level
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. 2 sets

### Rows

1. Lean forward, support with good arm
2. Hold weight in affected arm
3. Pull elbow back, squeezing shoulder blade
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 repetitions
6. 2 sets

### Wall Push-Ups

1. Face wall, hands on wall at shoulder height
2. Bend elbows, lean toward wall
3. Push back
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Progress to incline, then floor push-ups

## Lymphedema Prevention

If lymph nodes were removed, take precautions:

### During Exercise

- Wear compression garment if recommended
- Progress gradually—don't suddenly increase intensity
- Monitor arm for swelling after exercise
- Stop if arm feels heavy or achy

### Daily Precautions

- Avoid blood pressure, injections, blood draws in affected arm
- Wear gloves for gardening
- Use sunscreen (avoid burns)
- Avoid tight jewelry or clothing on arm
- Moisturize skin to prevent cracks

### Self-Massage (Lymphatic Drainage)

1. Start at neck—light strokes toward heart
2. Move to armpit area—light circular movements
3. Work up the arm from fingers toward armpit
4. Very light pressure—like petting a cat
5. Do daily, especially if noticing any swelling

## Cording (Axillary Web Syndrome)

Tight cords under the skin from armpit down arm are common:

**Symptoms:**
- Visible tight bands under skin
- Pain with stretching
- Limited arm movement

**Treatment:**
- Continue stretching (gently)
- Massage along the cords
- Physical therapy if severe
- Usually resolves in weeks to months

## Sample Daily Routine

### Morning (15 minutes)

1. Deep breathing: 10 breaths
2. Wall climbing: 10 reps
3. Side wall climbing: 10 reps
4. Wand overhead lift: 10 reps
5. Shoulder circles: 10 each direction
6. Corner stretch: 3 × 20 seconds

### Evening (15 minutes)

1. All stretches from morning
2. Shoulder blade squeezes: 15 reps
3. Strengthening exercises (when cleared): 2 sets each
4. Lymphatic self-massage: 5 minutes

### Throughout Day

- Check posture—avoid rounding forward
- Move arm regularly—don't hold still for long periods
- Walk for general circulation

## When to Contact Your Doctor

Call if you notice:

- Increasing redness, warmth, or swelling at incision
- Fever
- New drainage or opening of incision
- Significant increase in arm swelling
- Severe pain not controlled by medication
- Signs of infection

## Long-Term Considerations

**Continue exercises:**
- Stretching remains important long-term
- Maintain strength and flexibility
- Monitor for lymphedema indefinitely

**Return to activities:**
- Discuss specific activities with your surgeon
- Return to exercise gradually
- Avoid heavy lifting on affected side initially
- Swimming is excellent when cleared

**Emotional recovery:**
- Physical activity improves mood
- Support groups can help
- Be patient with your body

## The Bottom Line

Post-mastectomy rehabilitation is essential for full recovery:

1. **Start early** - As soon as surgeon allows
2. **Be consistent** - Daily exercises work best
3. **Progress gradually** - Don't rush
4. **Protect against lymphedema** - Take precautions seriously
5. **Be patient** - Full recovery takes months

Your body has been through a lot. With consistent, gentle exercise, you can restore your arm and shoulder function, reduce complications, and return to the activities you love. Take it one day at a time, and celebrate each small victory along the way.
