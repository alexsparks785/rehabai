# Groin Strain Exercises: Recovery and Prevention

A groin strain can be one of the most frustrating injuries—it lingers, it recurs, and it affects almost every movement involving your legs. But with the right rehabilitation approach, you can fully recover and significantly reduce your risk of re-injury.

## Understanding Groin Strains

### The Adductor Muscles

Your groin muscles (adductors) run along the inner thigh, connecting your pelvis to your thigh bone. They pull your leg toward the midline of your body (adduction) and help stabilize your pelvis during movement.

The main adductor muscles:
- Adductor longus (most commonly injured)
- Adductor brevis
- Adductor magnus
- Gracilis
- Pectineus

### Strain Grades

**Grade 1 (Mild):**
- Muscle fibers stretched or microtorn
- Mild pain with activity
- Minimal strength loss
- Recovery: 1-2 weeks

**Grade 2 (Moderate):**
- Partial muscle tear
- Moderate pain, some bruising
- Noticeable weakness
- Recovery: 3-6 weeks

**Grade 3 (Severe):**
- Complete or near-complete tear
- Severe pain, significant bruising
- Marked weakness or inability to contract
- Recovery: 6-12+ weeks

### Why Groin Strains Recur

- Returning to activity too soon
- Inadequate strengthening (especially eccentric)
- Poor hip mobility
- Weak core and hip stabilizers
- Muscle imbalances

## Phase 1: Acute Phase (Days 1-7)

### Goals
- Reduce pain and swelling
- Maintain range of motion
- Begin gentle activation

### PRICE Protocol

- **Protect:** Avoid activities that cause pain
- **Rest:** Relative rest, not complete immobilization
- **Ice:** 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours
- **Compression:** Supportive shorts or wrap
- **Elevation:** When resting

### Gentle Range of Motion

**Supine Groin Stretch (Very Gentle):**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Let knees fall apart (frog position)
3. Only go to the point of mild sensation, NOT pain
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. 3-5 times daily

**Hip Circles:**
1. Lie on back
2. Small circles with one leg (lifted slightly)
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Keep movements pain-free

### Isometric Activation

**Adductor Squeeze (Isometric):**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Place a pillow or ball between your knees
3. Squeeze gently at 25-50% effort
4. No movement should occur
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. 10-15 repetitions, 3 times daily

**Pain should not increase during or after this exercise.**

## Phase 2: Subacute Phase (Weeks 1-3)

### Goals
- Restore range of motion
- Begin strengthening
- Maintain cardiovascular fitness

### Stretching (Progressive)

**Standing Adductor Stretch:**
1. Stand in wide stance
2. Shift weight to one side, bending that knee
3. Keep other leg straight
4. Feel stretch along inner thigh of straight leg
5. Hold 30 seconds each side
6. Repeat 3 times

**Butterfly Stretch:**
1. Sit with soles of feet together
2. Hold ankles, not feet
3. Gently press knees toward floor using elbows
4. Keep back straight
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Repeat 3 times

**Side-Lying Adductor Stretch:**
1. Lie on your side (injured side up)
2. Bend bottom leg for stability
3. Hold top ankle and pull heel toward buttock
4. Then let knee drop toward floor
5. Hold 30 seconds

### Strengthening (Gradual)

**Side-Lying Adduction:**
1. Lie on injured side
2. Bend top leg and place foot in front for stability
3. Lift bottom (injured) leg toward ceiling
4. Lower with control
5. 3 sets of 10-15

**Squeeze Ball/Pillow (Progressive):**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Ball between knees
3. Squeeze at 50-75% effort
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 3 sets of 15

**Bridges:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze ball between knees while bridging
3. Hold at top 3-5 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

### Cardiovascular Maintenance

- Swimming (avoid breaststroke kick)
- Upper body cycling (arms only)
- Pool walking/jogging

## Phase 3: Strengthening Phase (Weeks 3-6)

### Progressive Adductor Strengthening

**Standing Adduction with Band:**
1. Attach band to fixed point at ankle height
2. Stand sideways to anchor
3. Pull leg across body against resistance
4. Control the return
5. 3 sets of 12-15 each leg

**Copenhagen Adduction (Modified):**
1. Side-lying, top leg on a bench or chair
2. Lift bottom (injured) leg up to meet the bench
3. Lower with control
4. Start with bent knee version
5. 3 sets of 8-10

**Copenhagen Adduction (Full):**
1. Side plank position, top leg on bench
2. Lift bottom leg up to meet bench while holding plank
3. Lower with control
4. 3 sets of 6-10 each side

**This is one of the most effective exercises for groin strength and injury prevention.**

**Sumo Squats:**
1. Wide stance, toes out
2. Squat down, knees tracking over toes
3. Feel adductors work
4. 3 sets of 12-15

### Hip Strengthening

**Clamshells:**
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Lift top knee, keeping feet together
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Lateral Band Walks:**
1. Band around ankles or above knees
2. Quarter squat position
3. Step sideways, maintaining tension
4. 2 sets of 15 steps each direction

**Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift:**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Hinge at hip, reaching opposite leg back
3. 3 sets of 10 each leg

### Core Strengthening

**Dead Bug:**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees at 90 degrees
2. Lower opposite arm and leg
3. Keep lower back pressed down
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Side Plank:**
1. Start modified (on knees)
2. Progress to full side plank
3. Hold 20-30 seconds each side

**Pallof Press:**
1. Stand sideways to cable/band
2. Press away from body
3. Resist rotation
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

## Phase 4: Return to Sport (Weeks 6+)

### Criteria for Progression

- Full pain-free range of motion
- Strength 80%+ of uninjured side
- No pain during strengthening exercises
- Adductor squeeze test pain-free

### Running Progression

**Week 1:** Walk 5 min, jog 1 min × 5
**Week 2:** Walk 2 min, jog 3 min × 4
**Week 3:** Continuous jog 15-20 minutes
**Week 4:** Add intervals (jog, stride, jog)

### Agility Progression

**Stage 1:** Forward/backward jogging
**Stage 2:** Lateral shuffles (slow)
**Stage 3:** Carioca
**Stage 4:** Cutting at 45 degrees, then 90 degrees
**Stage 5:** Sport-specific movements at 50%, 75%, 100%

### Sport-Specific Training

- Practice movements that stress the groin (kicking, skating, quick direction changes)
- Start at low intensity, progress gradually
- Include eccentric adductor work in warm-up

## Prevention Program

### Ongoing Exercises (2-3x per week)

**Copenhagen Adduction:**
- The single best exercise for groin injury prevention
- 3 sets of 8-10 each side

**Sumo Squats:**
- 3 sets of 15

**Single-Leg RDL:**
- 3 sets of 10 each leg

**Lateral Lunges:**
- 3 sets of 10 each side

### Pre-Activity Warm-Up

1. Light jog: 5 minutes
2. Dynamic adductor stretches (leg swings side to side)
3. Lateral lunges: 10 each side
4. Adductor activation (squeeze): 10 reps
5. Progressive sprints: 50%, 75%, 90%

### Flexibility Maintenance

- Daily adductor stretching
- Hip flexor stretching (tight hip flexors stress adductors)
- Don't overstretch—moderate flexibility is sufficient

## Sample Weekly Program

### Acute Phase (Week 1)

**Daily:**
- Ice: 15-20 minutes, 3-4 times
- Gentle stretching: As tolerated
- Isometric squeeze: 15 reps, 3 times

### Subacute Phase (Weeks 2-3)

**Daily:**
- Stretching routine: 5 minutes
- Side-lying adduction: 3 × 15
- Bridges with squeeze: 3 × 15

**Cardio:** Pool exercise, upper body work

### Strengthening Phase (Weeks 4-6)

**3-4 times per week:**
- Band adduction: 3 × 12 each leg
- Copenhagen (modified): 3 × 8 each side
- Sumo squats: 3 × 12
- Core exercises: 3 × 10 each

### Return to Sport (Week 6+)

- Running progression
- Agility training
- Sport-specific drills
- Maintenance strengthening: 2-3x per week

## Key Takeaways

1. **Don't rush back**—groin strains recur when rehabilitation is incomplete
2. **Copenhagen exercises** are gold standard for prevention
3. **Eccentric strengthening** builds resilient muscle
4. **Address hip and core** strength—they protect the groin
5. **Progressive return to sport**—cutting and kicking last
6. **Continue prevention exercises** even after full return
7. **Warm up properly**—dynamic stretching and activation before activity

Groin strains can be stubborn, but with patient, progressive rehabilitation, full recovery is expected. Take the time to build strength and stability, and you'll not only recover but reduce your risk of future injury.
