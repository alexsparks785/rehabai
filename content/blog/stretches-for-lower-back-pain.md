Lower back pain affects almost everyone at some point. While there are many causes, tight muscles in and around the lower back are often part of the problem—and stretching can be part of the solution.

These stretches target the key muscle groups that contribute to lower back pain: hip flexors, hamstrings, glutes, and the back muscles themselves. Done consistently, they can reduce stiffness, improve mobility, and decrease pain.

## Before You Start

**When stretching helps:** Muscle tightness, stiffness after sitting, mild chronic pain, and recovery from minor strains.

**When to see a doctor first:** Severe pain, pain radiating down your leg, numbness or weakness, pain after injury, or pain that worsens despite rest.

**How to stretch safely:**
- Warm up first (even a few minutes of walking)
- Move into stretches slowly
- Hold each stretch 30-60 seconds
- Breathe normally—don't hold your breath
- Feel a gentle pull, not sharp pain
- Stretch both sides equally

## The 12 Best Stretches for Lower Back Pain

### 1. Knee-to-Chest Stretch

**What it stretches:** Lower back, glutes

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. Bring one knee toward your chest
3. Clasp your hands behind your thigh or over your shin
4. Gently pull your knee closer until you feel a stretch in your lower back
5. Hold 30-60 seconds
6. Switch legs and repeat
7. Option: Pull both knees to chest simultaneously

**Why it works:** Gently flexes the lower spine and stretches the muscles along your lower back and glutes.

### 2. Child's Pose

**What it stretches:** Lower back, lats, hips

**How to do it:**
1. Start on hands and knees
2. Sit your hips back toward your heels
3. Reach your arms forward on the floor
4. Let your forehead rest on the ground
5. Relax and breathe into your lower back
6. Hold 30-60 seconds or longer

**Variations:**
- Knees together for more lower back stretch
- Knees wide for more hip opening
- Arms by your sides for a more relaxed version

**Why it works:** Gently stretches the entire posterior chain while allowing the lower back to relax and decompress.

### 3. Cat-Cow Stretch

**What it stretches:** Entire spine, lower back muscles

**How to do it:**
1. Start on hands and knees, wrists under shoulders, knees under hips
2. **Cow:** Inhale, drop your belly, lift your chest and tailbone, look slightly up
3. **Cat:** Exhale, round your spine toward ceiling, tuck chin and tailbone
4. Move slowly between positions, following your breath
5. Repeat 10-15 cycles

**Why it works:** Mobilizes the entire spine through flexion and extension, loosening stiff segments and warming up back muscles.

### 4. Supine Twist

**What it stretches:** Lower back, obliques, glutes

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. Extend arms out to sides in a "T" position
3. Let both knees fall to one side toward the floor
4. Keep both shoulders on the ground
5. Turn your head to look toward the opposite side
6. Hold 30-60 seconds
7. Return to center and repeat on other side

**Why it works:** Rotational stretching releases tension in the lower back and can help with stiffness and minor misalignments.

### 5. Figure-4 (Piriformis) Stretch

**What it stretches:** Piriformis, deep hip rotators, glutes

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. Cross your right ankle over your left knee
3. Reach through and clasp your hands behind your left thigh
4. Pull your left thigh toward your chest
5. Feel the stretch in your right hip and glute
6. Hold 30-60 seconds
7. Switch sides

**Why it works:** Tight piriformis and hip rotators are a common contributor to lower back pain. This stretch directly targets them.

### 6. Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling Lunge)

**What it stretches:** Hip flexors (iliopsoas), quads

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on one knee with the other foot flat in front (half-kneeling position)
2. Keep your torso upright
3. Gently push your hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of your back hip
4. Squeeze the glute of your back leg for a deeper stretch
5. Hold 30-60 seconds
6. Switch sides

**Tip:** Place a towel or pad under your knee for comfort.

**Why it works:** Tight hip flexors pull on the pelvis and create stress on the lower back. This is crucial for anyone who sits a lot.

### 7. Hamstring Stretch (Supine)

**What it stretches:** Hamstrings

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back
2. Lift one leg toward the ceiling, keeping it straight
3. Hold behind your thigh or calf (or use a strap/towel)
4. Gently pull your leg toward you until you feel a stretch in the back of your thigh
5. Keep your other leg flat on the floor
6. Hold 30-60 seconds
7. Switch legs

**Why it works:** Tight hamstrings tilt the pelvis backward and flatten the natural lumbar curve, contributing to lower back pain.

### 8. Seated Spinal Twist

**What it stretches:** Lower back, obliques, glutes, outer hip

**How to do it:**
1. Sit on the floor with legs extended
2. Bend your right knee and cross your right foot over your left leg
3. Place your right hand on the floor behind you
4. Place your left elbow on the outside of your right knee
5. Twist your torso to the right, looking over your right shoulder
6. Hold 30-60 seconds
7. Switch sides

**Why it works:** Seated twists mobilize the spine and stretch the muscles that rotate the trunk.

### 9. Pelvic Tilt

**What it stretches/activates:** Lower back, core activation

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. Flatten your lower back against the floor by tilting your pelvis
3. Hold this position for 5-10 seconds
4. Release and allow your back to arch naturally
5. Repeat 10-15 times

**Why it works:** Pelvic tilts gently mobilize the lower spine and help you learn to control pelvic position, which affects lower back stress.

### 10. Prayer Stretch (Extended Child's Pose)

**What it stretches:** Lower back, lats, shoulders

**How to do it:**
1. Start on hands and knees
2. Sit back toward your heels
3. Walk your hands forward as far as comfortable
4. Let your chest sink toward the floor
5. Reach actively through your fingertips
6. Hold 30-60 seconds or longer

**Variation:** Walk hands to the left, then right, to stretch each side of the back.

**Why it works:** Combines spinal flexion with lat stretching for a comprehensive lower back release.

### 11. Standing Back Extension

**What it stretches:** Opens the front of the spine (extension)

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart
2. Place your hands on your lower back, fingers pointing down
3. Gently lean backward, supporting your lower back with your hands
4. Look slightly upward
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. Return to standing
7. Repeat 3-5 times

**Why it works:** If you've been sitting or flexing forward, extension can provide relief. Especially good after prolonged sitting.

### 12. Doorway or Wall Hip Flexor Stretch

**What it stretches:** Hip flexors, quads

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in a doorway or near a wall
2. Step one foot forward, keeping your back foot in place
3. Bend your front knee while keeping your back leg straight
4. Push your hips forward until you feel a stretch in your back hip
5. Hold 30-60 seconds
6. Switch sides

**Why it works:** A standing alternative to the kneeling hip flexor stretch, accessible anywhere.

## Creating Your Lower Back Stretching Routine

### Morning Routine (5-10 minutes)
Wake up your spine and prepare for the day:
1. Knee-to-chest stretch (both sides)
2. Cat-cow (10 cycles)
3. Child's pose
4. Hip flexor stretch (both sides)

### After Sitting (5 minutes)
Counter the effects of prolonged sitting:
1. Standing back extension (5 reps)
2. Hip flexor stretch (both sides)
3. Hamstring stretch (both sides)

### Evening Routine (10-15 minutes)
Decompress after your day:
1. Supine twist (both sides)
2. Figure-4 stretch (both sides)
3. Hamstring stretch (both sides)
4. Cat-cow (10 cycles)
5. Child's pose (hold 1-2 minutes)

## Tips for Better Results

**Be consistent.** Daily stretching is more effective than occasional intense sessions.

**Breathe into the stretch.** Deep breathing helps muscles relax and allows deeper stretching.

**Don't bounce.** Hold stretches steadily; bouncing can cause muscle strain.

**Stretch warm muscles.** After a shower, after walking, or after exercise is ideal.

**Stay hydrated.** Hydration affects muscle flexibility.

**Address both sides.** Even if one side feels tighter, stretch both equally.

## When Stretching Isn't Enough

If stretching doesn't improve your pain after 2-4 weeks of consistent practice, consider:

- **Strengthening exercises:** Weak core and glutes often contribute to back pain
- **Posture correction:** How you sit, stand, and sleep matters
- **Activity modification:** Identify movements that aggravate your pain
- **Professional help:** Physical therapist, chiropractor, or doctor evaluation

## The Bottom Line

These stretches target the muscles that most commonly contribute to lower back pain. Done consistently—daily or multiple times per day—they can significantly reduce stiffness and discomfort.

Start with a few stretches that feel good and build from there. Make stretching a habit, not a reaction to pain, and your lower back will thank you.
