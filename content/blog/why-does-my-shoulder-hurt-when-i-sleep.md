# Why Does My Shoulder Hurt When I Sleep? Causes and Solutions

Few things are more frustrating than being exhausted but unable to sleep because of shoulder pain. You toss, you turn, you rearrange pillows—nothing helps. Night shoulder pain has specific causes, and understanding them is the key to finally getting rest.

## Why Shoulders Hurt More at Night

Before diving into causes, it helps to understand why night makes shoulder pain worse:

**1. Stillness increases stiffness**
During the day, movement keeps joints lubricated and blood flowing. At night, lack of motion allows inflammation to settle.

**2. Position creates compression**
Lying on your side directly compresses shoulder structures. Even lying on your back can strain an irritated shoulder.

**3. No distractions**
During busy days, you may not notice pain. In quiet darkness, there's nothing to distract you from discomfort.

**4. Inflammatory cycles**
Cortisol (your natural anti-inflammatory) drops at night, potentially increasing pain perception.

## Common Causes of Night Shoulder Pain

### 1. Rotator Cuff Problems

The most common culprit. The rotator cuff tendons can become:
- **Tendinitis:** Inflamed from overuse
- **Tendinosis:** Degenerative changes over time
- **Partial or full tears:** From injury or wear

**Why it's worse at night:**
- Lying down reduces blood flow to tendons
- Side-lying compresses the supraspinatus
- Arm position can pinch irritated structures

**Telltale signs:**
- Pain on the outside of shoulder/upper arm
- Weakness lifting arm overhead
- Pain reaching behind your back
- Gradual onset (unless traumatic tear)

### 2. Shoulder Impingement

The rotator cuff tendons get pinched between bones when you raise your arm. At night:
- Certain positions create impingement
- Inflammation from daytime activities peaks
- Bursa swelling adds to the squeeze

### 3. Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis)

The shoulder capsule becomes inflamed and tight, severely limiting motion.

**Night pain pattern:**
- Often the worst pain of any shoulder condition at night
- Pain with any movement in bed
- Profound stiffness in morning
- May wake you multiple times

### 4. Shoulder Arthritis

Osteoarthritis or inflammatory arthritis affecting the shoulder joint.

**Characteristics:**
- Deep, aching pain
- Morning stiffness lasting 30+ minutes
- Grinding or crunching sensation
- Gradual loss of motion

### 5. Bursitis

The bursa (fluid-filled sac) becomes inflamed, often alongside rotator cuff issues.

**Signs:**
- Point tenderness at shoulder tip
- Pain lying directly on the shoulder
- Swelling may be visible
- Often responds quickly to rest

### 6. Referred Pain

Sometimes the shoulder isn't the source:
- **Neck problems:** Cervical disc issues can cause shoulder pain
- **Heart conditions:** Left shoulder/arm pain with exertion (seek immediate care)
- **Gallbladder:** Right shoulder pain, especially after fatty meals
- **Diaphragm irritation:** Shoulder tip pain

## Red Flags: Seek Immediate Care

- Chest pain or pressure with shoulder pain
- Shortness of breath
- Shoulder pain with jaw or arm numbness
- Fever with shoulder pain and swelling
- Sudden severe weakness after injury
- Visible deformity

## Sleep Position Solutions

### If You're a Side Sleeper

**On the painful shoulder (avoid if possible):**
- Places direct pressure on irritated structures
- Compresses blood flow to rotator cuff
- Often the worst position

**On the non-painful side:**
- Hug a pillow to support the painful arm
- Keep painful shoulder slightly forward
- Pillow between knees helps spinal alignment

### If You're a Back Sleeper

**Modifications:**
- Small pillow or rolled towel under the painful arm
- Keep arm slightly away from body
- Avoid letting arm fall overhead
- Support elbow so shoulder isn't stressed

### If You're a Stomach Sleeper

This position is tough on shoulders:
- Arms typically go overhead or under pillow
- Creates sustained rotator cuff compression
- Try transitioning to side or back sleeping

## Immediate Relief Strategies

### Pillow Positioning

**The "armrest" technique:**
Place a pillow beside you and rest your forearm on it, keeping the shoulder in neutral position.

**Body pillow support:**
Hug a body pillow to keep the painful shoulder from rolling forward.

### Temperature Therapy

- Ice before bed: 15-20 minutes to reduce inflammation
- Some prefer heat for muscle relaxation
- Experiment to see what helps you

### Gentle Movement Before Bed

5 minutes of pendulum exercises and gentle stretching can reduce overnight stiffness:
1. Pendulum circles (let arm hang, make small circles)
2. Gentle cross-body stretch
3. Easy shoulder rolls

### Medication Timing

If using over-the-counter anti-inflammatories:
- Take 30-60 minutes before bed
- Consult doctor about appropriate use
- Don't rely on this long-term without addressing the cause

## Daytime Strategies That Improve Nights

What you do during the day affects your nights:

### Posture Matters

- Avoid prolonged reaching or overhead work
- Take breaks from computer work
- Don't carry heavy bags on the painful shoulder

### Smart Exercise

**Do:**
- Rotator cuff strengthening (external rotation, rows)
- Scapular stabilization exercises
- Gentle range of motion work

**Avoid (temporarily):**
- Overhead pressing
- Bench press with heavy weight
- Throwing or reaching behind

### Activity Modification

- Limit aggravating activities during flare-ups
- Use your other arm for reaching
- Sleep on your good side

## When to See a Doctor

Seek professional evaluation if:
- Pain persists more than 2-3 weeks
- You can't sleep despite position changes
- Weakness is developing
- Range of motion is decreasing
- Pain is getting progressively worse
- You had a specific injury

## Treatment Options

**Physical therapy:** Often the most effective long-term solution. Addresses strength, mobility, and movement patterns.

**Corticosteroid injection:** Can provide relief for bursitis or significant inflammation while you work on underlying issues.

**Imaging:** X-ray or MRI may be needed to assess rotator cuff integrity or arthritis.

**Surgery:** Reserved for cases that don't respond to conservative treatment, particularly significant rotator cuff tears.

## Recovery Timeline

- **Bursitis:** Often improves in 2-4 weeks with proper care
- **Tendinitis:** 4-8 weeks with consistent treatment
- **Frozen shoulder:** 12-24 months (but treatment speeds improvement)
- **Rotator cuff tears:** Variable; some heal conservatively, others need surgery

## The Bottom Line

Night shoulder pain is disruptive but usually treatable. Start with sleep position modifications and pillow support. Address daytime factors like posture and overuse. If conservative measures don't help within a few weeks, get it checked out—you deserve restful sleep.
