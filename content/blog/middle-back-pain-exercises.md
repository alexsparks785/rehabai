# Middle Back Pain Exercises: Relieve Thoracic Spine Discomfort

That ache between your shoulder blades. The stiffness that makes twisting uncomfortable. The tension that builds through hours of sitting. Middle back pain affects the thoracic spine—the section from the base of your neck to where your ribs end—and it's become increasingly common in our desk-bound world.

Unlike the lower back, which moves freely, your thoracic spine is designed for stability—your ribs attach here, protecting vital organs. But this stability can become rigidity, and rigidity creates pain.

Let's restore mobility and relieve that middle back.

## Understanding Middle Back Pain

Your thoracic spine consists of 12 vertebrae (T1-T12), each connected to a rib. This region should allow rotation, flexion, extension, and side-bending—but modern life often locks it up.

**Common causes:**
- Poor posture (rounded shoulders, forward head)
- Prolonged sitting
- Weak upper back muscles
- Tight chest muscles
- Rib joint dysfunction
- Muscle strain from overuse
- Stress and tension

**Symptoms may include:**
- Dull ache between shoulder blades
- Sharp pain with certain movements
- Stiffness when twisting or bending
- Pain that wraps around the ribs
- Muscle tightness and trigger points

**When to seek medical attention:**
- Pain after trauma
- Pain with breathing difficulties
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fever
- Numbness or weakness in legs
- Changes in bladder or bowel function

## Mobility Exercises

Restore movement to a stiff thoracic spine.

### Thoracic Extension on Foam Roller
The most effective mobilization for a stiff middle back.

1. Lie on your back with a foam roller across your mid-back
2. Support your head with your hands (fingers interlaced behind head)
3. Let your upper back extend over the roller
4. Don't arch your lower back—keep core engaged
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Move roller to different segments of your thoracic spine
7. Spend 2-3 minutes total

### Cat-Cow
1. On hands and knees, wrists under shoulders, knees under hips
2. Inhale: Drop belly, lift head and tailbone (cow)
3. Exhale: Round back, tuck chin and pelvis (cat)
4. Move slowly, emphasizing the mid-back portion
5. 15-20 cycles

### Thread the Needle
Rotational mobility for the thoracic spine.

1. Start on hands and knees
2. Reach your right arm under your body to the left
3. Lower your right shoulder toward the ground
4. Feel the rotation in your middle back
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Return and repeat on the other side
7. Do 5-8 reps each side

### Open Book
1. Lie on your side, knees bent and stacked
2. Arms extended in front of you, palms together
3. Open your top arm like a book, rotating your upper back
4. Follow your hand with your eyes
5. Keep knees together—rotation comes from mid-back
6. Hold 3-5 breaths at the end
7. Do 8-10 reps each side

### Quadruped Thoracic Rotation
1. On hands and knees
2. Place one hand behind your head
3. Rotate that elbow down toward your opposite wrist
4. Then rotate up toward the ceiling
5. Move through full available range
6. Do 10-12 reps each side

### Seated Thoracic Rotation
1. Sit on a chair, feet flat, or straddle the chair backward
2. Cross arms over chest
3. Rotate upper body to one side
4. Keep hips facing forward
5. Hold 3-5 seconds
6. Return and rotate other direction
7. Do 10 reps each side

## Stretches for Middle Back Relief

### Child's Pose with Lat Emphasis
1. Kneel and sit back toward your heels
2. Extend arms forward on the ground
3. Walk hands to the right for left side stretch
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Walk hands to the left for right side stretch
6. Hold 30 seconds
7. Return to center, hold 30 seconds

### Doorway Chest Stretch
Tight chest muscles pull the middle back into poor positions.

1. Stand in a doorway
2. Place forearms on door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step forward through doorway
4. Feel stretch across chest and front shoulders
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Repeat with elbows higher, then lower

### Upper Trap Stretch
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Tilt right ear toward right shoulder
3. Reach left arm down toward floor
4. For more stretch, gently pull head with right hand
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Levator Scapulae Stretch
1. Turn head 45 degrees to right
2. Look down toward right armpit
3. Use right hand to gently increase stretch
4. Feel it at top of shoulder blade and side of neck
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Pec Minor Stretch
1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearm on frame, elbow above shoulder
3. Step through and rotate away slightly
4. Feel stretch deep in chest, near armpit
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Strengthening Exercises

Weak muscles can't support good posture.

### Prone Y-T-W Raises
Strengthens the muscles that hold your shoulder blades in place.

1. Lie face down on a bench, bed, or floor
2. **Y:** Raise arms at 45 degrees forward, thumbs up. Lower. 10-12 reps.
3. **T:** Raise arms directly out to sides. Lower. 10-12 reps.
4. **W:** Pull elbows back, squeezing shoulder blades. 10-12 reps.

### Band Pull-Aparts
1. Hold resistance band in front of you at shoulder height
2. Arms extended, light tension on band
3. Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades together
4. Return slowly
5. Do 15-20 reps

### Rows
Any rowing variation strengthens the mid-back.

**Band Row:**
1. Anchor band at chest height
2. Pull toward your lower ribs
3. Squeeze shoulder blades together
4. Return slowly
5. 12-15 reps

**Dumbbell Row:**
1. Support on bench with one hand and knee
2. Row dumbbell to hip with other arm
3. 10-12 reps each side

### Scapular Squeezes
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together and down
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Release
5. Do 15-20 reps

### Face Pulls
1. Anchor band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. Externally rotate at end (show palms behind you)
4. Squeeze upper back
5. 15-20 reps

### Wall Angels
Teaches proper shoulder blade movement.

1. Stand with back against wall
2. Press lower back, upper back, and head against wall
3. Raise arms to "goal post" position (elbows at 90 degrees)
4. Try to keep everything against the wall
5. Slide arms up and down
6. Do 15 reps

## Self-Massage Techniques

### Tennis Ball Release
1. Place two tennis balls in a sock
2. Lie on your back with balls on either side of your spine
3. Position at a tight/tender area
4. Relax into the balls for 30-60 seconds
5. Move to adjacent areas
6. Avoid pressing directly on the spine

### Foam Roller Massage
1. Lie with foam roller along your spine (vertical)
2. Let shoulders and arms fall open
3. Breathe deeply
4. Hold 2-3 minutes

### Trigger Point Release
1. Find tender spots in your mid-back muscles
2. Apply sustained pressure with a ball or your hand
3. Hold until tenderness decreases (30-90 seconds)
4. Breathe and try to relax the muscle

## Sample Routines

### Quick Morning Routine (5 minutes)
- Cat-cow: 10 cycles
- Thread the needle: 5 each side
- Thoracic rotation (seated): 8 each side
- Arm circles: 15 each direction

### Desk Break Routine (3 minutes)
- Seated rotation: 8 each side
- Shoulder blade squeezes: 10 reps
- Chest doorway stretch: 20 seconds each side
- Upper trap stretch: 20 seconds each side

### Complete Middle Back Session (15-20 minutes)
**Mobility (5 minutes):**
- Thoracic extension on foam roller: 2-3 minutes
- Cat-cow: 15 cycles
- Thread the needle: 5 each side
- Open book: 8 each side

**Stretching (5 minutes):**
- Child's pose with lat emphasis: 1 minute total
- Doorway chest stretch: 30 seconds each position
- Upper trap stretch: 30 seconds each side
- Levator scapulae stretch: 30 seconds each side

**Strengthening (5-7 minutes):**
- Prone Y-T-W: 2 × 10 each
- Band pull-aparts: 2 × 15
- Band rows: 2 × 12
- Wall angels: 12 reps

**Self-massage (3 minutes):**
- Tennis ball or foam roller work

## Posture Throughout the Day

Exercises help, but daily habits matter more.

### At Your Desk
- Screen at eye level
- Keyboard at elbow height
- Feet flat on floor
- Sit tall, shoulders back
- Take breaks every 30-45 minutes

### Standing
- Weight evenly distributed
- Shoulders back and down
- Head over shoulders (not jutting forward)

### Phone Use
- Bring phone up to eye level
- Avoid looking down for extended periods

### Sleeping
- Side sleepers: pillow between knees
- Back sleepers: pillow under knees
- Avoid stomach sleeping

## The Bigger Picture

Middle back pain is often a symptom of how you use your body day after day. Exercises address the symptoms, but lasting relief requires:

- Posture awareness throughout the day
- Regular movement breaks
- Workstation ergonomics
- Stress management (tension lives in the upper back)
- Consistent mobility work

The thoracic spine should move. When it doesn't, pain follows. Restore that movement, strengthen the supporting muscles, and maintain awareness of your daily habits.

Your middle back will thank you with fewer aches and more freedom of movement.
