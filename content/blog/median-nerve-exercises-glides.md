# Median Nerve Exercises: Glides for Carpal Tunnel and Arm Numbness

The median nerve is the primary nerve of the hand, controlling most of your grip and fine motor function. When it gets compressed—most commonly at the wrist in carpal tunnel syndrome—you experience numbness, tingling, and weakness that can significantly impact daily life.

Nerve glides are a cornerstone of conservative treatment, helping restore normal nerve mobility and reduce symptoms without surgery.

## Understanding the Median Nerve

### Nerve Pathway

The median nerve travels:
1. **Neck:** Forms from C5-T1 nerve roots
2. **Armpit:** Through brachial plexus
3. **Upper arm:** Runs along inner arm with brachial artery
4. **Elbow:** Between heads of pronator teres muscle
5. **Forearm:** Between finger flexor muscles
6. **Wrist:** Through carpal tunnel
7. **Hand:** Controls thumb, index, middle, and half of ring finger

### Common Compression Sites

- **Carpal tunnel** (wrist): Most common—80% of cases
- **Pronator teres** (elbow/forearm): "Pronator syndrome"
- **Ligament of Struthers** (upper arm): Rare
- **Anterior interosseous** (forearm): Motor only, no numbness

### Symptoms of Median Nerve Issues

**Sensory:**
- Numbness in thumb, index, middle fingers
- Tingling, especially at night
- "Pins and needles" sensation
- Burning pain in hand/wrist

**Motor:**
- Weak grip
- Difficulty with pinch strength
- Dropping objects
- Trouble with buttons, writing
- Thumb weakness (severe cases)

## Median Nerve Glides

These exercises mobilize the nerve through its pathway. Two main approaches: sliders (nerve moves back and forth) and tensioners (sustained stretch).

### 1. Basic Median Nerve Slider

The foundation exercise—gentle and effective.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand or sit with arm at side
2. Extend arm to side, palm up, elbow straight
3. Extend wrist back (fingers toward ceiling)
4. While extending wrist: tilt head toward that arm
5. Then flex wrist (fingers toward floor): tilt head away
6. Alternate smoothly 15-20 times
7. Perform 3-4 times daily

**Key concept:** When wrist extends (tensions nerve below), head tilts toward arm (slackens from above). This creates a sliding motion.

### 2. Median Nerve Glide Sequence (6-Position)

Classic progression through increasing tension.

**Position 1:** Fist at shoulder, wrist neutral
**Position 2:** Extend wrist back
**Position 3:** Extend fingers and thumb
**Position 4:** Turn forearm palm-up (supination)
**Position 5:** Straighten elbow
**Position 6:** Tilt head away (maximum tension)

**How to do it:**
- Move through positions 1-6 slowly
- Hold each 3-5 seconds
- Return through positions
- 5-10 full sequences
- Stop at any position that increases symptoms significantly

### 3. Tabletop Median Nerve Glide

Easy to perform at a desk.

**How to do it:**
1. Place palm flat on table, fingers pointing backward
2. Keep elbow straight
3. Gently lean body away from hand
4. Feel stretch through forearm and wrist
5. Hold 5 seconds, release
6. 10-15 repetitions

### 4. Prayer Stretch with Nerve Glide Component

Combines stretch with neural mobilization.

**How to do it:**
1. Press palms together in prayer position
2. Keep fingers pointing up
3. Lower hands toward waist while keeping palms together
4. When you feel stretch, separate and extend fingers wide
5. Return to prayer, repeat
6. 10-15 repetitions

### 5. Advanced Median Nerve Tensioner

Use only after basic glides are comfortable.

**How to do it:**
1. Arm out to side, palm up
2. Extend wrist fully (fingers toward ceiling)
3. Extend all fingers wide
4. Straighten elbow completely
5. Tilt head away from arm
6. Depress shoulder (push down)
7. Hold 5-10 seconds
8. 5-8 repetitions

**Caution:** This creates maximum tension. Stop if symptoms increase.

### 6. Median Nerve Glide with Shoulder Movement

Full upper extremity mobilization.

**How to do it:**
1. Arm at side, palm forward
2. Raise arm sideways while extending wrist and fingers
3. At shoulder height, tilt head away
4. Lower arm while flexing wrist and tilting head toward
5. Smooth, continuous motion
6. 10-15 repetitions

## Stretches for Tight Muscles

Muscle tightness contributes to nerve compression. These stretches create space.

### 7. Wrist Flexor Stretch

Addresses carpal tunnel compression.

**How to do it:**
1. Extend arm in front, palm up
2. Use other hand to pull fingers down toward floor
3. Keep elbow straight
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 2-3 times, multiple times daily

### 8. Pronator Stretch

For pronator syndrome.

**How to do it:**
1. Extend arm, palm up
2. Rotate forearm outward (supinate fully)
3. Use other hand to increase rotation
4. Hold 30 seconds each arm

### 9. Wrist Extensor Stretch

Balances forearm flexibility.

**How to do it:**
1. Extend arm, palm down
2. Pull fingers toward body with other hand
3. Keep elbow straight
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 2-3 times each arm

### 10. Pec Minor Stretch

Opens thoracic outlet region.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearm against frame, elbow at 90°
3. Step through with same-side leg
4. Feel stretch in front of shoulder/chest
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Strengthening Exercises

After acute symptoms improve, strengthen the muscles the median nerve controls.

### 11. Thumb Opposition

Key median nerve function.

**How to do it:**
1. Touch thumb tip to each fingertip
2. Make firm contact
3. Can add resistance with rubber band
4. 10 complete cycles
5. 2-3 sets

### 12. Pinch Grip Strengthening

Targets thenar muscles (thumb base).

**How to do it:**
1. Hold soft ball or putty
2. Squeeze between thumb and fingers
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. Focus on thumb strength

### 13. Wrist Curls

Forearm strength without aggravating symptoms.

**How to do it:**
1. Rest forearm on table, wrist over edge
2. Hold light weight (1-2 lbs)
3. Palm up: curl wrist up and down
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. Progress weight gradually

### 14. Finger Flexion with Resistance

Strengthens flexor muscles.

**How to do it:**
1. Place rubber band around all fingers
2. Make fist against resistance
3. Or squeeze therapy putty
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. 2-3 sets

## Carpal Tunnel-Specific Protocol

If carpal tunnel syndrome is your diagnosis:

### During Flare-ups:
1. Nerve glides 4-6 times daily (basic slider only)
2. Wrist stretches every 2 hours
3. Night splint to keep wrist neutral
4. Ice 10-15 minutes as needed
5. Avoid aggravating activities

### Maintenance Phase:
1. Nerve glides 2-3 times daily
2. Strengthening 3x weekly
3. Stretches after computer work
4. Ergonomic modifications
5. Wrist neutral during sleep

## Daily Exercise Program

### Morning (5 minutes):
1. Wrist circles: 10 each direction
2. Basic median nerve slider: 15 reps
3. Wrist flexor stretch: 30 seconds each
4. Thumb opposition: 10 cycles

### At Work (every 2 hours, 2 minutes):
1. Prayer stretch with glide: 10 reps
2. Tabletop nerve glide: 10 reps
3. Shake hands gently

### Evening (7 minutes):
1. 6-Position nerve glide sequence: 5-10 reps
2. All stretches: 30 seconds each
3. Strengthening exercises
4. Final gentle nerve sliders

## Lifestyle Modifications

### Sleep Position
- **Wear a wrist splint** — Keeps wrist neutral, reduces night symptoms
- **Avoid sleeping on hands** — Compresses nerves
- **Pillow under arm** — Keeps wrist straight

### Computer Ergonomics
- Keyboard at elbow height
- Wrist neutral (not bent up or down)
- Take breaks every 30-60 minutes
- Consider ergonomic keyboard/mouse

### Work Modifications
- Reduce repetitive gripping
- Alternate hands when possible
- Use power tools instead of manual
- Pad hard surfaces that press on palms

### Activities to Modify
- Cycling (padded gloves, relaxed grip)
- Weightlifting (wrist wraps, neutral grip)
- Knitting/crafts (frequent breaks)
- Gaming (ergonomic setup, breaks)

## Progression Guidelines

**Week 1-2:**
- Basic nerve sliders only
- Gentle stretches
- Wrist splint at night
- Avoid aggravating activities

**Week 3-4:**
- Progress to 6-position sequence
- Begin isometric strengthening
- Continue night splinting

**Week 5-8:**
- Add tensioner exercises
- Isotonic strengthening
- Gradual return to activities

**Week 8+:**
- Full program
- Maintenance routine
- Activity-specific training

## Warning Signs

**Stop exercises and see a doctor if:**
- Symptoms worsen with exercises
- Constant numbness (not just intermittent)
- Weakness increasing
- Muscle wasting at thumb base
- Symptoms affecting work/sleep significantly

**Seek immediate care if:**
- Sudden severe symptoms after trauma
- Complete loss of sensation
- Inability to move thumb

## When to Consider Other Treatment

If exercises don't help after 6-8 weeks:
- **Physical therapy:** Hands-on nerve mobilization, modalities
- **Corticosteroid injection:** Reduces inflammation
- **Splinting:** Custom splints for day and night
- **Surgery:** Carpal tunnel release (if conservative fails)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Night symptoms are classic** — Splinting at night often helps most
2. **Sliders before tensioners** — Progress gradually
3. **Posture matters** — Check neck and shoulder position too
4. **Consistency is key** — Multiple short sessions beat one long one
5. **Don't ignore worsening symptoms** — Early treatment is more effective

Carpal tunnel and other median nerve issues respond well to conservative treatment in most cases. With consistent nerve glides, appropriate stretching, and ergonomic modifications, many people avoid surgery entirely. Be patient—nerves heal slowly, but they do heal.
