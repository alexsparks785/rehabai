# Shoulder Impingement Exercises: Relieve Pain and Restore Function

Shoulder impingement is one of the most common causes of shoulder pain, especially in people who lift weights, swim, or do overhead work. That pinching sensation when you raise your arm can make daily activities miserable and training impossible.

The good news: most cases respond well to targeted exercises. Here's how to relieve the pain and restore healthy shoulder function.

## Understanding Shoulder Impingement

Shoulder impingement occurs when the tendons of the rotator cuff get pinched between the bones of the shoulder—typically the humeral head (arm bone) and the acromion (part of the shoulder blade).

**What gets impinged:**
- Supraspinatus tendon (most common)
- Biceps tendon
- Subacromial bursa

**Common causes:**
- Poor posture (rounded shoulders)
- Weak rotator cuff muscles
- Tight chest and front shoulder muscles
- Repetitive overhead movements
- Bone spurs or structural abnormalities
- Training errors (too much, too fast)

## Symptoms of Shoulder Impingement

- Pain when raising arm overhead
- Pain reaching behind your back
- Weakness with overhead movements
- Pain lying on the affected shoulder
- Aching at rest, sharp pain with movement
- Pain that worsens with activity

## Before Starting: Important Notes

**See a doctor if:**
- Pain is severe or worsening
- Significant weakness in the arm
- Pain follows a specific injury
- Night pain that disrupts sleep significantly
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks of conservative treatment

**Exercise principles:**
- Avoid movements that cause sharp pain
- Some discomfort is okay; sharp pinching is not
- Progress gradually
- Consistency matters more than intensity

## Phase 1: Reduce Pain and Inflammation

Before strengthening, calm things down.

### 1. Pendulum Swings

Gentle movement that reduces stiffness without loading the joint.

**How to do it:**
1. Lean forward, supporting yourself with one arm
2. Let affected arm hang relaxed
3. Gently swing arm in small circles
4. 10 circles each direction
5. Then swing forward/back, side to side
6. Do 2-3 times daily

### 2. Passive Range of Motion

Movement without muscle effort.

**How to do it:**
1. Use your good arm to move the affected arm
2. Lift it forward, out to the side, across the body
3. Move within pain-free range only
4. Hold positions briefly
5. Several times daily

### 3. Ice

Reduce inflammation after activity.

**How:** 15-20 minutes after exercise or when painful.

## Phase 2: Restore Mobility

Address the tightness contributing to impingement.

### 4. Doorway Chest Stretch

Tight pecs pull shoulders forward, worsening impingement.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearm on door frame, elbow at 90 degrees
3. Step through with same-side foot
4. Rotate body away from arm
5. Feel stretch in chest and front of shoulder
6. Hold 30 seconds each side
7. Try different arm heights

### 5. Cross-Body Shoulder Stretch

Stretches posterior shoulder capsule.

**How to do it:**
1. Bring affected arm across body at shoulder height
2. Use other hand to gently pull upper arm toward chest
3. Keep shoulder down (don't let it shrug)
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 2-3 times

### 6. Sleeper Stretch

Targets internal rotation restriction.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on affected side
2. Affected arm in front, elbow bent 90 degrees
3. Use other hand to gently push forearm toward floor
4. Rotate shoulder inward
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Be gentle—this can be intense

### 7. Thoracic Extension

Stiff upper back limits shoulder movement.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on foam roller placed across upper back
2. Support head with hands
3. Let upper back extend over roller
4. Move to different segments
5. Spend 1-2 minutes

## Phase 3: Strengthen the Rotator Cuff

Weak rotator cuff muscles can't center the humeral head properly.

### 8. Side-Lying External Rotation

Fundamental rotator cuff strengthening.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Hold light weight (1-3 lbs) in affected hand
3. Elbow bent 90 degrees, pinned to side
4. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
5. Lower slowly
6. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

**Key:** Start very light. This should not cause pain.

### 9. Prone Y Raises

Strengthens lower traps and external rotators.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face-down on bench or floor
2. Arms hanging or extended
3. Raise arms to form a Y shape, thumbs up
4. Squeeze shoulder blades together
5. Hold 2-3 seconds at top
6. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### 10. Prone T Raises

Targets middle traps and rhomboids.

**How to do it:**
1. Same position as Y raises
2. Raise arms straight out to sides (T shape)
3. Thumbs up
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### 11. Face Pulls

Excellent for external rotators and rear delts.

**How to do it:**
1. Cable or band at face height
2. Pull toward face, separating hands
3. Finish with hands beside ears, elbows high
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

### 12. Band Pull-Aparts

Simple but effective.

**How to do it:**
1. Hold band at shoulder height, arms extended
2. Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades
3. Hands finish at shoulder level
4. Control the return
5. 15-25 reps, 2-3 sets

## Phase 4: Scapular Stability

Proper shoulder blade movement prevents impingement.

### 13. Wall Slides

Teaches proper scapular mechanics.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Arms in "goalpost" position against wall
3. Slide arms up overhead, keeping contact with wall
4. Slide back down
5. 10-15 reps

**Challenge:** Keeping lower back, upper back, and arms all touching the wall.

### 14. Scapular Push-Ups

Strengthens serratus anterior.

**How to do it:**
1. Push-up position (or on knees)
2. Without bending elbows, let chest sink between shoulder blades
3. Push through hands to spread shoulder blades apart
4. Only shoulder blade movement—no elbow bend
5. 15-20 reps

### 15. Low Rows with Squeeze

Builds retraction strength.

**How to do it:**
1. Cable or band row
2. At the end of each row, squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold the squeeze 2-3 seconds
4. 12-15 reps, 3 sets

## Sample Exercise Program

### Daily Routine (10 minutes)

**Mobility:**
1. Pendulum swings: 1 minute
2. Doorway chest stretch: 30 seconds each side
3. Cross-body stretch: 30 seconds each side

**Strengthening:**
4. Side-lying external rotation: 2 x 15
5. Band pull-aparts: 2 x 20
6. Prone Y raises: 2 x 12

### Comprehensive Routine (20 minutes, 3x/week)

**Mobility:**
1. Thoracic extension: 2 minutes
2. Doorway stretch (multiple angles): 2 minutes total
3. Cross-body stretch: 30 seconds each side
4. Sleeper stretch: 30 seconds each side

**Strengthening:**
5. Side-lying external rotation: 3 x 15
6. Prone Y raises: 3 x 12
7. Prone T raises: 3 x 12
8. Face pulls: 3 x 15
9. Scapular push-ups: 2 x 15
10. Wall slides: 2 x 12

## Exercise Modifications to Avoid Pain

### Overhead Pressing

**Problem:** Often causes impingement pain.

**Modifications:**
- Reduce range of motion (don't go all the way up)
- Use neutral grip (palms facing each other)
- Try landmine press instead
- Reduce weight significantly

### Lateral Raises

**Problem:** Common impingement trigger.

**Modifications:**
- Keep thumbs up or slightly up
- Don't raise above shoulder height
- Reduce weight
- Try cable lateral raises

### Bench Press

**Problem:** Can aggravate impingement.

**Modifications:**
- Use narrower grip
- Keep elbows at 45-degree angle (not flared)
- Don't lower bar too deep
- Try floor press to limit range

### Pull-Ups/Lat Pulldowns

**Modifications:**
- Use neutral grip
- Don't go behind the neck
- Reduce range if needed
- Focus on scapular control

## Timeline for Recovery

**Weeks 1-2:** Focus on pain reduction and gentle mobility. Avoid aggravating movements.

**Weeks 3-4:** Begin light strengthening. Pain should be decreasing.

**Weeks 5-8:** Progress strengthening exercises. Gradually reintroduce normal activities.

**Weeks 8-12:** Return to full activity with modifications as needed.

**Long-term:** Continue maintenance exercises 2-3x weekly to prevent recurrence.

**Important:** Everyone progresses differently. Listen to your body.

## Preventing Recurrence

Once recovered, prevent future impingement:

**Training habits:**
- Warm up shoulders before pressing
- Balance pushing with pulling exercises
- Include rotator cuff work weekly
- Progress gradually—avoid sudden volume increases

**Posture:**
- Address rounded shoulders
- Take breaks from desk work
- Strengthen upper back

**Technique:**
- Proper form on all pressing movements
- Don't flare elbows excessively
- Full range of motion when healthy

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consider physical therapy or medical evaluation if:
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks
- Pain is severe or worsening
- Significant weakness
- Unable to do daily activities
- Previous shoulder surgeries or injuries

## The Bottom Line

Shoulder impingement responds well to targeted exercise—but it takes patience. The combination of mobility work, rotator cuff strengthening, and scapular stability addresses the root causes of impingement.

Avoid movements that cause sharp pain while you rehabilitate. Modify exercises rather than abandoning training entirely. With consistent effort, most people return to full, pain-free function.

Fix the weakness, restore the mobility, and build shoulders that work properly.
