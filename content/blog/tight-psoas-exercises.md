# Tight Psoas Exercises: Release the Hidden Source of Back and Hip Pain

The psoas (pronounced SO-az) is a deep hip flexor that connects your spine to your legs. When it's tight, it can cause lower back pain, hip pain, and postural problems. This "hidden" muscle is often overlooked but can be the key to solving chronic pain.

## Understanding the Psoas

**What it is:**
- Deep hip flexor muscle
- Runs from lower spine (T12-L5) to inner thigh
- Only muscle connecting spine to legs
- Works with iliacus (together called iliopsoas)

**What it does:**
- Flexes hip (lifts thigh)
- Stabilizes spine
- Influences posture
- Affects walking gait

**Why it gets tight:**
- Prolonged sitting (shortened position)
- Stress and anxiety (protective tension)
- Overuse in running/cycling
- Compensation for weak core or glutes
- Sleeping in fetal position
- Poor posture

**Problems from tight psoas:**
- Lower back pain
- Hip pain (front of hip)
- Difficulty standing straight
- Anterior pelvic tilt
- Pain when rising from sitting
- Pinching in hip crease
- Affected walking stride

## Signs of Tight Psoas

### Thomas Test
1. Sit at edge of table/bed
2. Lie back, pull one knee to chest
3. Let other leg hang
4. **Tight psoas:** Thigh rises above horizontal
5. Test both sides

### Standing Signs
- Excessive lower back arch
- Belly pushed forward
- Difficulty standing tall
- Pain after standing from sitting

## Self-Release Techniques

### Psoas Release with Ball
**Proceed carefully—this is intense:**
1. Lie face down
2. Place soft ball (tennis or softer) 2 inches below and to side of navel
3. Let body weight sink slowly
4. Breathe deeply
5. Start with 30-60 seconds
6. Can increase time as tolerated
7. Should feel deep pressure, not sharp pain

### Constructive Rest Position
**Passive release:**
1. Lie on back
2. Knees bent, feet flat
3. Feet wider than hips
4. Let knees fall together
5. Arms relaxed at sides
6. Stay 10-20 minutes
7. Gravity releases psoas

### Supported Supine Release
1. Lie on back
2. Prop calves on chair/ottoman
3. Creates 90° at hips and knees
4. Relax completely
5. 10-15 minutes
6. Gentle release

## Stretching Exercises

### Half-Kneeling Psoas Stretch
**The key stretch:**
1. Kneel on one knee (affected side down)
2. Front foot flat
3. **Tuck pelvis under** (posterior pelvic tilt)
4. Squeeze back glute
5. Keep torso upright
6. Feel deep stretch in front of back hip
7. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

**Critical:** Without tucking pelvis, you just arch your back instead of stretching psoas.

### Couch Stretch
1. Face away from couch/wall
2. Place back knee on floor, shin against couch
3. Front foot flat, far enough forward
4. Stay upright
5. Tuck pelvis
6. Hold 1-2 minutes each side

### Low Lunge with Lift
1. Low lunge position, back knee down
2. Tuck pelvis
3. Raise arms overhead
4. Lean slightly back
5. Feel deep hip flexor stretch
6. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Standing Psoas Stretch
1. Stand in split stance
2. Tuck pelvis under
3. Squeeze back glute
4. Shift weight forward
5. Keep torso tall
6. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Supine Psoas Stretch
1. Lie on edge of bed
2. Pull one knee to chest
3. Let other leg hang off edge
4. Let gravity stretch psoas
5. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

## Mobility Exercises

### Hip Circles (Supine)
1. Lie on back
2. Pull one knee toward chest
3. Make circles with knee
4. 10 each direction
5. Repeat other leg

### Leg Swings (Sagittal)
1. Stand, hold support
2. Swing leg forward and back
3. Keep torso stable
4. 15-20 swings each leg
5. Gradually increase range

### 90/90 Hip Transitions
1. Sit with both legs bent 90°
2. Front leg in front, back leg to side
3. Rotate to switch leg positions
4. Move smoothly
5. 10 transitions

### Active Leg Lowering
1. Lie on back
2. Raise one leg to 90°
3. Slowly lower to floor
4. Keep lower back flat
5. 10 each leg
6. Teaches psoas control

## Strengthening (Balance the Psoas)

Tight psoas often comes with weak glutes and core:

### Glute Bridges
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 3 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

### Dead Bug
1. Lie on back
2. Arms to ceiling, knees bent 90°
3. Lower opposite arm and leg
4. Keep back flat (psoas must lengthen)
5. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Bird Dog
1. On hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep spine neutral
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Hip Thrusts
1. Upper back on bench
2. Feet flat on floor
3. Drive hips up
4. Squeeze glutes at top
5. 3 sets of 12

### Plank
1. Forearm plank
2. Body straight
3. Tuck pelvis slightly
4. 3 sets of 30-60 seconds

## Lifestyle Modifications

### Sitting
- Don't sit more than 30-60 minutes
- Use standing desk periodically
- Sit with hips above knees
- Avoid deep couch sitting

### Sleeping
- Avoid fetal position
- Back sleeping with pillow under knees
- Side sleeping with pillow between knees

### Posture
- Stand tall
- Don't let pelvis tip forward
- Engage core and glutes

### Stress Reduction
The psoas responds to stress:
- Deep breathing
- Constructive rest position
- Yoga/meditation
- Regular relaxation

## Daily Routine

### Morning (10 minutes)
1. Constructive rest position (5 min)
2. Half-kneeling stretch (2 min each side)
3. Glute bridges (1 min)

### Work Breaks (every hour)
- Stand and walk
- Quick standing stretch
- Hip circles

### Evening (15 minutes)
1. Psoas release with ball (5 min)
2. Full stretching routine (5 min)
3. Strengthening exercises (5 min)

## Progress Timeline

**Week 1-2:** Begin release work, temporary relief
**Week 3-4:** Noticeable improvement in flexibility
**Month 2:** Significant changes, less pain
**Ongoing:** Maintenance required

## When to Seek Help

See a professional if:
- Pain is severe
- Numbness or tingling in legs
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks
- History of hip or back issues
- Symptoms worsening

## What to Expect

**Exercise CAN:**
- Release psoas tension
- Reduce back and hip pain
- Improve posture
- Better hip mobility
- Less pain standing from sitting

**Keys to success:**
- Consistent stretching (daily)
- Address sitting habits
- Strengthen opposing muscles
- Include relaxation (stress affects psoas)
- Be patient—deep muscles take time

The psoas is often the missing piece of the puzzle for people with chronic back or hip pain. If you sit a lot and nothing else has helped, focusing on this hidden muscle might be the answer.
