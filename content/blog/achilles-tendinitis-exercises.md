# Achilles Tendinitis Exercises: Heal Your Tendon and Return to Activity

Achilles tendinitis is one of the most common overuse injuries, especially in runners and athletes. The good news: research strongly supports exercise as the primary treatment. Here's how to heal your Achilles and get back to doing what you love.

## Understanding Achilles Tendinitis

### What's Happening

The Achilles tendon connects your calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus) to your heel bone. Tendinitis occurs when the tendon becomes irritated and inflamed, typically from:

- Sudden increase in activity
- Repetitive stress without adequate recovery
- Tight calf muscles
- Poor footwear
- Weak calf/ankle complex
- Training errors

### Types of Achilles Problems

**Insertional Tendinitis**
- Pain at the heel bone attachment
- Often involves bone spurs
- Aggravated by pressure from shoes

**Midportion Tendinitis**
- Pain 2-6 cm above the heel
- Most common type
- Often has a tender nodule

**Tendinosis**
- Chronic degeneration without inflammation
- Tendon may be thickened
- Requires longer rehabilitation

### Signs and Symptoms

- Pain and stiffness in the morning
- Pain at start of activity that may warm up
- Tenderness when squeezing the tendon
- Swelling or thickening
- Pain after prolonged sitting
- Worse with stairs, hills, or jumping

## The Foundation: Load Management

Before exercises, understand this principle: tendons need load to heal, but too much load prevents healing.

### The Sweet Spot

- **Too little load:** Tendon weakens
- **Too much load:** Continued irritation
- **Just right:** Stimulates healing and strengthening

### Pain Monitoring

Use the 24-hour rule:
- Some discomfort during exercise is okay (up to 4/10)
- Pain should settle within 24 hours
- If pain is worse the next morning, you did too much
- If no pain at all, you can progress

## Phase 1: Pain Reduction (Week 1-2)

When pain is acute, focus on calming things down.

### Isometric Calf Raises

Isometrics reduce pain without aggravating the tendon.

1. Stand on both feet
2. Rise onto toes slightly (not full height)
3. Hold for 30-45 seconds
4. Rest 1 minute
5. 4-5 repetitions, 2-3x daily

**Progression:** Single-leg holds when tolerated

### Gentle Calf Stretching

Only if it doesn't increase pain.

**Wall Stretch (Gastrocnemius):**
1. Hands on wall, one foot back
2. Keep back knee straight
3. Lean forward until gentle stretch
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 3 repetitions each leg

**Bent Knee Stretch (Soleus):**
1. Same position
2. Bend back knee slightly
3. Feel stretch lower in calf
4. Hold 30 seconds

### Relative Rest

- Reduce aggravating activities (don't stop completely)
- Maintain fitness with non-impact activities (swimming, cycling)
- Use heel lifts in shoes if helpful
- Avoid barefoot walking initially

## Phase 2: Progressive Loading (Week 2-6)

The most important phase. Eccentric exercises have the strongest evidence.

### Eccentric Heel Drops (The Alfredson Protocol)

The gold standard exercise for Achilles tendinitis.

**Straight Knee (Gastrocnemius):**
1. Stand on edge of step on balls of feet
2. Rise up on both feet
3. Shift weight to affected leg
4. Slowly lower heel below step level (3 seconds)
5. Use other leg to rise back up
6. 15 reps, 3 sets, twice daily

**Bent Knee (Soleus):**
1. Same setup, but keep knee slightly bent
2. This targets the soleus muscle
3. 15 reps, 3 sets, twice daily

**Key Points:**
- The lowering (eccentric) phase is what matters
- Use both legs to go up, one leg to go down
- Expect some discomfort (up to 4/10 is acceptable)
- Do these every day, even with mild pain

### Progression of Eccentric Exercises

**Week 2-3:** Body weight only, both versions
**Week 3-4:** Add light weight (backpack with books)
**Week 4-6:** Continue adding weight as tolerated

### Seated Calf Raises

For soleus strengthening.

1. Sit with knees bent, weight on thighs
2. Raise heels off ground
3. Lower slowly (3 seconds)
4. 15-20 reps, 3 sets

### Single-Leg Balance

Builds calf and ankle stability.

1. Stand on one foot
2. Maintain balance for 30-60 seconds
3. Progress to: eyes closed, unstable surface, while moving arms

## Phase 3: Strength Building (Week 4-8)

Once pain is manageable, build real strength.

### Heavy Slow Resistance

An alternative to pure eccentrics with similar results.

**Bilateral Calf Raises:**
1. Stand on edge of step
2. Rise up slowly (3 seconds)
3. Lower slowly (3 seconds)
4. Start with body weight, progress to added weight
5. 8-12 reps, 3-4 sets, every other day

**Single-Leg Calf Raises:**
1. Same as above, one leg
2. Use wall for balance initially
3. 8-12 reps, 3-4 sets each leg

### Progression Guidelines

- Add weight when you can complete all sets with good form
- Progress by 5-10% per week
- Goal: Single-leg calf raise with 25-30% body weight added

### Ankle Strengthening

**Resistance Band Exercises:**

*Plantarflexion:*
1. Band around forefoot, ends in hands
2. Point toes against resistance
3. 15-20 reps

*Dorsiflexion:*
1. Band anchored in front
2. Pull toes toward shin
3. 15-20 reps

*Inversion/Eversion:*
1. Band anchored to side
2. Turn foot in/out against resistance
3. 15-20 reps each direction

## Phase 4: Return to Activity (Week 6-12+)

Gradually reintroduce sport-specific activities.

### Plyometric Progression

Only when single-leg calf raises are pain-free with added weight.

**Level 1:**
- Double-leg hopping in place
- Jump rope (both feet)
- Box step-downs

**Level 2:**
- Single-leg hopping in place
- Lateral hops
- Forward/backward hops

**Level 3:**
- Running-specific drills
- Skipping
- Bounding

**Level 4:**
- Sport-specific movements
- Cutting, pivoting
- Full return to activity

### Running Return Protocol

If you're a runner:

**Week 1:** Walk/run intervals (1 min run, 2 min walk × 10)
**Week 2:** 2 min run, 1 min walk × 8
**Week 3:** 4 min run, 1 min walk × 6
**Week 4:** 8 min run, 2 min walk × 3
**Week 5:** 15 min continuous, then walk
**Week 6+:** Gradual increase (10% per week rule)

Always:
- Run on flat surfaces initially
- Avoid hills until pain-free for 2+ weeks
- Continue strength exercises 2-3x/week

## Insertional Tendinitis Modifications

If pain is at the heel attachment:

### Avoid Full Dorsiflexion

- Don't lower heel below step level
- Do exercises on flat ground initially
- Avoid stretches that compress the insertion

### Modified Heel Drops

1. Stand on flat ground
2. Rise up on toes
3. Lower slowly to flat (not below)
4. 15 reps, 3 sets

### Heel Lift

- Use a heel lift in all shoes
- Reduces stretch on insertion
- Can use 6-12mm lift

### Progress Carefully

Insertional tendinitis often takes longer to resolve. Be patient.

## Sample Weekly Program

### Daily
- Eccentric heel drops: 3×15 each version (straight and bent knee)
- Gentle calf stretching: 2×30 seconds each leg

### Monday/Wednesday/Friday
**Strengthening:**
- Heavy slow resistance calf raises: 4×10
- Single-leg balance: 3×45 seconds each
- Ankle band exercises: 2×15 each direction

### Tuesday/Thursday
**Active Recovery:**
- Low-impact cardio (cycling, swimming): 20-30 min
- Calf stretching: 3×30 seconds
- Foam rolling: 2-3 minutes

### Weekend
- One strength session
- One longer low-impact cardio session
- Continue daily eccentric protocol

## What Else Helps

### Footwear

- Supportive shoes with cushioned heels
- Avoid flat shoes and flip-flops
- Consider heel lifts
- Replace worn running shoes

### Load Management

- Don't increase training volume more than 10% per week
- Avoid sudden changes (new shoes, new surfaces, speedwork)
- Include recovery days

### Other Treatments

These may help alongside exercises:
- Ice after activity (15-20 minutes)
- NSAIDs short-term for acute pain
- Night splints (keep tendon stretched)
- Massage or instrument-assisted soft tissue work
- Shockwave therapy (for chronic cases)

## When to Seek Help

See a doctor or physical therapist if:

- No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise
- Sudden severe pain (possible tendon rupture)
- Unable to walk normally
- Significant swelling or bruising
- Pain that worsens despite rest

## Timeline Expectations

**Mild Cases:** 4-6 weeks
**Moderate Cases:** 6-12 weeks
**Chronic/Severe Cases:** 3-6 months

The key is consistency. Daily exercises, proper load management, and patience lead to recovery. Most people return to full activity, but rushing the process increases re-injury risk.

## Prevention Once Healed

- Continue calf strengthening 2x/week
- Gradual training progressions only
- Address any underlying issues (tight calves, weak hips)
- Good footwear always
- Listen to early warning signs

## The Bottom Line

Achilles tendinitis responds best to:
1. **Relative rest** initially (not complete rest)
2. **Eccentric exercises** (the Alfredson protocol)
3. **Progressive loading** (heavy slow resistance)
4. **Gradual return to activity** (structured protocol)
5. **Patience** (tendons heal slowly)

The evidence is clear: exercise works better than rest. Start with isometrics if acute, progress to eccentrics, build strength, then return to activity. Your Achilles will thank you.
