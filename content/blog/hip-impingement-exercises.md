# Hip Impingement Exercises: Manage FAI and Reduce Pain

Hip impingement (femoroacetabular impingement or FAI) occurs when the bones of the hip joint don't fit together perfectly, causing friction during movement. The right exercises can reduce pain, improve mobility, and strengthen the muscles that support your hip—often helping you avoid surgery.

## Understanding Hip Impingement

**What's happening:**
- Abnormal bone shape causes contact between femur and pelvis
- Friction damages cartilage and labrum
- Pain typically with hip flexion and internal rotation
- Common in young, active adults

**Types:**
- **Cam:** Extra bone on femoral head (ball)
- **Pincer:** Extra bone on acetabulum (socket)
- **Mixed:** Both types present

**Common symptoms:**
- Groin pain (most common)
- Pain with sitting, squatting, or pivoting
- Stiffness after sitting
- Catching or clicking
- Pain with hip flexion
- Limited range of motion

## Important Considerations

**See a doctor/PT if:**
- Symptoms not improving with exercise
- Significant mechanical symptoms (locking, catching)
- Pain limiting daily activities
- Need for proper diagnosis

**General principles:**
- Avoid positions that cause impingement
- Strengthen hip muscles to improve mechanics
- Improve mobility in tolerable ranges
- Don't push through pinching pain

## Positions to Avoid or Modify

**Limit or avoid:**
- Deep squats (past 90°)
- Bringing knee to chest
- Sitting with knees higher than hips
- Pigeon pose and deep hip stretches
- Excessive internal rotation

**Modifications:**
- Squat to box/chair (limit depth)
- Wider stance squats (toes out)
- Sit with hips higher than knees
- Avoid stretches that cause pinching

## Hip Strengthening Exercises

### Glute Activation

**Glute Bridges**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Feet hip-width, not too close to buttocks
3. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
4. Don't overextend at top
5. 3 sets of 15

**Single Leg Glute Bridge**
1. Same position
2. Extend one leg
3. Bridge on single leg
4. 3 sets of 10 each

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent 45° (not 90°)
2. Feet together
3. Lift top knee
4. Keep pelvis stable
5. 3 sets of 15 each side

### Hip Abductors

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Lie on side
2. Keep top leg straight, slightly behind body
3. Lift toward ceiling
4. Don't rotate hip forward
5. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Standing Hip Abduction**
1. Stand, hold support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. Keep toes pointing forward
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Banded Lateral Walks**
1. Band around ankles or above knees
2. Quarter squat position
3. Step sideways
4. 20 steps each direction

### Hip External Rotators

**Seated External Rotation**
1. Sit in chair
2. Band around outside of knees
3. Push knees apart
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 3 sets of 12

**Standing External Rotation**
1. Band around ankles
2. Slight knee bend
3. Rotate foot outward against band
4. 3 sets of 12 each

### Core Stability

**Dead Bug (Modified)**
1. Lie on back
2. Knees bent 90° (not pulled to chest)
3. Lower opposite arm and leg
4. Keep pelvis stable
5. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Plank**
1. Forearm plank
2. Body in straight line
3. Don't let hips sag or pike
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

**Bird Dog**
1. On hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep spine neutral
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

## Hip Mobility Exercises

Focus on mobility that doesn't cause pinching:

### Hip Flexor Stretch (Modified)
1. Half-kneeling position
2. Lean forward gently
3. Don't let pelvis tip forward excessively
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Quadriceps Stretch
1. Side-lying or standing
2. Grab ankle behind
3. Keep knees together
4. Hold 30 seconds each

### Hamstring Stretch (Supine)
1. Lie on back
2. Raise one leg, keep straight
3. Hold behind thigh (not calf)
4. Don't pull knee toward chest
5. Hold 30 seconds each

### Adductor Stretch
1. Wide stance
2. Shift weight to one side
3. Keep other leg straight
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Hip Circles (Controlled)
1. On hands and knees
2. Small circles with knee
3. Stay in pain-free range
4. 10 each direction, each hip

### Supine Hip Rotation
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Feet wider than hips
3. Let knees drop to one side
4. Only go to comfortable range
5. Hold 20 seconds each side

## Exercises to Modify or Avoid

**Deep Squats:**
- Limit to 90° or less
- Use wider stance
- Toes turned out
- Box squat to control depth

**Lunges:**
- Shorter stance
- Don't let front knee go past toes excessively
- Avoid deep lunges

**Hip Flexor Stretches:**
- Avoid bringing knee to chest
- Modify traditional stretches

**Cycling:**
- Raise seat height
- Avoid deep hip flexion at top of pedal stroke

**Rowing:**
- May need to limit hip flexion
- Modify technique

## Low-Impact Cardio Options

- Walking
- Swimming (avoid breaststroke kick if painful)
- Elliptical (if comfortable)
- Cycling (with seat modifications)
- Aqua jogging

## Daily Routine

### Morning (10 minutes)
1. Glute bridges (2 min)
2. Clamshells (3 min)
3. Modified hip flexor stretch (2 min)
4. Core work (3 min)

### Throughout Day
- Sit with hips above knees
- Take breaks from prolonged sitting
- Avoid crossed legs

### Evening (15 minutes)
1. Full strengthening routine (10 min)
2. Mobility work (5 min)

## Progress Tracking

Monitor:
- Pain level during activities
- Range of motion changes
- Strength improvements
- Ability to do daily activities

## Sample Weekly Schedule

**Monday, Wednesday, Friday:**
- Full hip strengthening routine
- Mobility exercises

**Tuesday, Thursday:**
- Low-impact cardio
- Light stretching

**Weekend:**
- Active rest
- Walking
- Activities as tolerated

## When to Seek Help

See a healthcare provider if:
- No improvement after 6-8 weeks
- Pain increasing
- Mechanical symptoms worsening
- Unable to do daily activities
- Night pain

**Treatment options:**
- Physical therapy (specialized hip PT)
- Anti-inflammatory medications
- Injections (diagnostic or therapeutic)
- Arthroscopic surgery (if conservative care fails)

## Post-Surgery Considerations

If surgery is needed:
- Exercises are crucial for recovery
- PT protocol typically 4-6 months
- Similar strengthening with progressions
- Avoid impingement positions during healing

## What to Expect

**With conservative care:**
- Many people manage symptoms successfully
- Strengthening helps significantly
- May need ongoing exercise
- Activity modification long-term

**Realistic expectations:**
- Bone shape doesn't change with exercise
- Muscle strength improves mechanics
- Pain reduction is achievable
- Full return to activity possible for many

**Timeline:**
- 4-6 weeks: Initial improvement
- 3-6 months: Significant progress
- Ongoing: Maintenance exercises

Hip impingement doesn't have to end your active lifestyle. With targeted strengthening, activity modification, and patience, most people significantly improve their symptoms and function.
