# How to Design Your Own Workout Program: A Step-by-Step Guide

Following someone else's workout program is fine. But learning to **design your own** gives you the power to adapt training to your specific goals, schedule, equipment, and preferences.

This guide teaches you the fundamentals of exercise programming—the same principles used by personal trainers and coaches—so you can build effective workouts for yourself.

---

## The Five Questions Every Program Must Answer

Before writing a single exercise, answer these:

### 1. What's the Goal?

**Be specific:**
- ❌ "Get in shape"
- ✅ "Build muscle and lose 10 pounds over 12 weeks"
- ✅ "Run a 5K in under 25 minutes"
- ✅ "Squat 1.5x bodyweight"

**Common goals:**
- Build muscle (hypertrophy)
- Get stronger (strength)
- Lose fat (body composition)
- Improve endurance (cardio)
- Get more athletic (power, speed)
- General health and fitness

Your goal determines everything else—exercise selection, sets, reps, rest, and frequency.

### 2. How Many Days Per Week?

**Be realistic about your schedule:**

| Days Available | Recommendation |
|----------------|----------------|
| 2 days | Full body each session |
| 3 days | Full body OR upper/lower/full |
| 4 days | Upper/lower split |
| 5 days | Upper/lower + full OR push/pull/legs + upper/lower |
| 6 days | Push/pull/legs × 2 |

**Rule:** More frequency isn't always better. Consistency beats intensity.

### 3. What Equipment Do You Have?

Program around what's available:
- **Full gym:** All options open
- **Home gym:** Dumbbells, barbell, rack
- **Minimal:** Bands, bodyweight, pull-up bar
- **Nothing:** Bodyweight only

Don't design a program requiring equipment you don't have access to.

### 4. How Much Time Per Session?

**Be honest:**
- 20-30 min: Focus on compound movements, circuits
- 45-60 min: Standard workout, adequate rest
- 60-90 min: Full workout with accessories, longer rest

Shorter sessions work if you train more frequently.

### 5. What's Your Training History?

**Beginner (0-1 year):**
- Full body workouts
- Focus on learning movement patterns
- Faster progress, less volume needed

**Intermediate (1-3 years):**
- Can handle more volume and splits
- Need more variation
- Progress slows

**Advanced (3+ years):**
- Specialized programming
- Higher volume/intensity
- Slower progress, periodization important

## Step 1: Choose Your Split

### Full Body (2-3 days/week)

**Best for:** Beginners, limited time, general fitness

**Structure:** Train all major muscle groups each session

**Sample week:**
- Monday: Full Body A
- Wednesday: Full Body B
- Friday: Full Body A

**Pros:** High frequency per muscle, fewer sessions needed
**Cons:** Sessions can be long, less focus on specific areas

### Upper/Lower (4 days/week)

**Best for:** Intermediate lifters, muscle building

**Structure:**
- Day 1: Upper body
- Day 2: Lower body
- Day 3: Rest
- Day 4: Upper body
- Day 5: Lower body
- Days 6-7: Rest

**Pros:** Good balance of frequency and volume
**Cons:** Requires 4 days commitment

### Push/Pull/Legs (3-6 days/week)

**Best for:** Intermediate to advanced, muscle building

**Structure:**
- Push: Chest, shoulders, triceps
- Pull: Back, biceps, rear delts
- Legs: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves

**3-day version:** Each once per week
**6-day version:** Each twice per week

**Pros:** High volume per session, good recovery
**Cons:** Lower frequency per muscle (3-day), high time commitment (6-day)

### Muscle Group Split (4-5 days/week)

**Best for:** Advanced bodybuilding focus

**Example:**
- Day 1: Chest/Triceps
- Day 2: Back/Biceps
- Day 3: Shoulders
- Day 4: Legs
- Day 5: Arms (optional)

**Pros:** Maximum volume per muscle
**Cons:** Lower frequency, not ideal for strength or beginners

## Step 2: Select Exercises

### The Movement Pattern Approach

Instead of thinking "what muscles," think "what movements":

**Essential patterns to include:**

| Pattern | Examples |
|---------|----------|
| Squat | Back squat, goblet squat, leg press |
| Hinge | Deadlift, RDL, hip thrust |
| Horizontal Push | Bench press, push-up, dumbbell press |
| Horizontal Pull | Barbell row, cable row, dumbbell row |
| Vertical Push | Overhead press, landmine press |
| Vertical Pull | Pull-up, lat pulldown, chin-up |
| Carry/Core | Farmer's walk, plank, dead bug |
| Single Leg | Lunge, Bulgarian split squat, step-up |

**A complete program hits all patterns weekly.**

### Compound vs. Isolation

**Compound exercises:** Multiple joints, multiple muscles
- Squat, deadlift, bench press, row, pull-up, overhead press
- **Do these first** when fresh

**Isolation exercises:** Single joint, one muscle
- Curls, lateral raises, leg curls, flyes
- **Do these after** compounds

**General rule:** 70-80% compounds, 20-30% isolation

### How Many Exercises Per Workout?

| Workout Length | Exercises |
|----------------|-----------|
| 30 min | 4-5 |
| 45 min | 5-7 |
| 60 min | 6-8 |
| 75+ min | 8-10 |

**Quality over quantity.** Better to do fewer exercises well than rush through many.

## Step 3: Determine Sets and Reps

### Rep Ranges by Goal

| Goal | Rep Range | Sets | Rest |
|------|-----------|------|------|
| Strength | 1-5 | 4-6 | 3-5 min |
| Hypertrophy | 6-12 | 3-4 | 1-2 min |
| Endurance | 12-20+ | 2-3 | 30-60 sec |

**Note:** There's overlap. You build muscle in all ranges; these are optimal zones.

### Weekly Volume Guidelines

**Volume = sets × reps × weight**

For muscle growth, research suggests **10-20 sets per muscle group per week**.

**Beginner:** Start at lower end (10-12 sets/muscle/week)
**Intermediate:** Middle range (12-16 sets/muscle/week)
**Advanced:** Higher end (16-20+ sets/muscle/week)

**Example - Intermediate chest volume:**
- Bench Press: 4 sets
- Incline DB Press: 3 sets
- Cable Fly: 3 sets
- **Total: 10 sets/week for chest**

### The "Hard Sets" Concept

Not all sets count equally. **Hard sets**—those within 1-3 reps of failure—drive adaptation.

- Warm-up sets: Don't count
- Easy sets (5+ reps from failure): Minimal stimulus
- Hard sets (1-3 RIR): Drive progress

**Aim for:** Most working sets at 1-3 RIR (reps in reserve)

## Step 4: Order Your Exercises

### Priority Principle

Put the most important exercises first when you're freshest:

1. **Power/explosive movements** (if included)
2. **Main compound lifts** (squat, deadlift, bench, etc.)
3. **Secondary compounds**
4. **Isolation exercises**
5. **Core/abs** (usually last)

### Sample Exercise Order

**Upper Body Day:**
1. Bench Press (main compound)
2. Barbell Row (main compound)
3. Overhead Press (secondary compound)
4. Pull-ups (secondary compound)
5. Lateral Raises (isolation)
6. Tricep Pushdowns (isolation)
7. Bicep Curls (isolation)

**Lower Body Day:**
1. Back Squat (main compound)
2. Romanian Deadlift (main compound)
3. Leg Press (secondary compound)
4. Walking Lunges (secondary compound)
5. Leg Curls (isolation)
6. Calf Raises (isolation)

## Step 5: Plan Progression

### Why Progression Matters

Your body adapts to stress. To keep improving, you must **progressively overload**—gradually increase demands over time.

### Methods of Progression

**1. Add Weight**
- Most straightforward
- Add 2.5-5 lbs when you hit top of rep range
- Example: Hit 3×10 at 100 lbs → Next session try 105 lbs

**2. Add Reps**
- Stay at same weight, do more reps
- Example: 3×8 → 3×9 → 3×10 → add weight

**3. Add Sets**
- Increase volume over time
- Example: Week 1-2: 3 sets → Week 3-4: 4 sets

**4. Decrease Rest**
- Same work in less time = more demanding
- Better for endurance/conditioning goals

**5. Improve Technique**
- Better form = more effective stimulus
- Often overlooked as progression

### Simple Progression Model

**For each exercise, track:**
- Weight used
- Reps achieved each set
- RPE/difficulty

**Progression rule:**
- Hit target reps on all sets → Increase weight next session
- Miss target reps → Stay at weight until you hit it

**Example:**
- Goal: 3×10
- Session 1: 100 lbs for 10, 9, 8 reps (didn't hit 3×10)
- Session 2: 100 lbs for 10, 10, 9 reps (still working)
- Session 3: 100 lbs for 10, 10, 10 reps (success!)
- Session 4: 105 lbs for 8, 7, 7 reps (restart process)

## Step 6: Build In Recovery

### Deload Weeks

Every 4-6 weeks, reduce training stress:
- Cut volume by 40-50%
- Keep some intensity
- Allow accumulated fatigue to dissipate

**Deload signs you need one now:**
- Strength declining
- Persistent fatigue
- Poor sleep
- Nagging aches
- Decreased motivation

### Rest Days

**Minimum:** 1-2 full rest days per week
**Active recovery:** Walking, light swimming, yoga

**Don't train the same muscle group on consecutive days** (unless advanced and intentionally doing so).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Too Much Volume Too Soon

**Problem:** Starting with advanced volume leads to burnout/injury
**Fix:** Start conservative, add volume gradually

### Mistake 2: No Progression Plan

**Problem:** Doing the same workout for months, wondering why you're not improving
**Fix:** Track workouts, have a clear progression strategy

### Mistake 3: All Isolation, No Compounds

**Problem:** Bicep curls and lateral raises won't build a foundation
**Fix:** Build program around compound movements first

### Mistake 4: Neglecting Patterns

**Problem:** All push, no pull (hello, rounded shoulders)
**Fix:** Balance pushing and pulling, include all movement patterns

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Recovery

**Problem:** Training 7 days a week with no deloads
**Fix:** Plan rest days and deload weeks

### Mistake 6: Program Hopping

**Problem:** Switching programs every 2 weeks
**Fix:** Stick with a program for 8-12 weeks minimum before changing

## Key Takeaways

1. **Answer the five questions** before programming (goal, days, equipment, time, experience)
2. **Choose a split** that matches your schedule and goal
3. **Build around movement patterns**, not just muscles
4. **Compounds first**, isolation after
5. **Set appropriate volume** (10-20 sets/muscle/week for growth)
6. **Have a progression plan** and track your workouts
7. **Include recovery** (rest days, deload weeks)
8. **Be consistent** - the best program is one you'll actually follow

You now have the tools to design your own training. Start simple, track everything, adjust based on results. Welcome to programming.
