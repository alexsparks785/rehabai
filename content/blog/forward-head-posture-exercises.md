# Forward Head Posture Exercises: Fix Text Neck and Stand Taller

Look around any coffee shop, office, or subway car—almost everyone is hunched over their phone, head jutting forward. This "text neck" or forward head posture is epidemic, causing neck pain, headaches, and a host of other problems. But it's fixable with the right exercises.

## Understanding Forward Head Posture

For every inch your head moves forward from its neutral position, it adds approximately 10 pounds of stress to your neck muscles. The average head weighs 10-12 pounds—but at 2-3 inches forward (common with phone use), your neck muscles are supporting 30-40 pounds.

### Signs You Have Forward Head Posture

- Head sits in front of your shoulders (not stacked above)
- Chin pokes forward
- Rounded upper back (often accompanies FHP)
- Neck pain and stiffness
- Headaches (especially at base of skull)
- Shoulder pain
- Jaw pain (TMJ issues)

### The Muscle Imbalance

**Tight/overactive muscles:**
- Suboccipitals (base of skull)
- Upper trapezius
- Levator scapulae
- SCM (sternocleidomastoid)
- Pectoralis major and minor

**Weak/lengthened muscles:**
- Deep neck flexors
- Lower trapezius
- Rhomboids
- Serratus anterior

Fixing forward head posture requires stretching the tight muscles and strengthening the weak ones.

## Phase 1: Release Tight Muscles

Start by releasing the muscles that pull your head forward.

### Suboccipital Release

These tiny muscles at the base of your skull are almost always tight:

1. Lie on your back
2. Place two tennis balls in a sock, tied to keep them together
3. Position the balls at the base of your skull (one on each side)
4. Let your head rest on the balls
5. Breathe deeply and relax for 2-3 minutes
6. Gently nod your head "yes" and "no" while on the balls

### Upper Trapezius Stretch

1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Tilt your ear toward your shoulder (don't lift shoulder)
3. For more stretch, gently pull head with hand
4. Hold 30-45 seconds
5. Repeat 3 times each side

### Levator Scapulae Stretch

1. Sit with good posture
2. Turn your head 45 degrees to one side
3. Look down toward your armpit
4. Gently pull head forward and down with your hand
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Repeat 3 times each side

### SCM Stretch

1. Sit or stand tall
2. Tilt your head back slightly and turn to one side
3. Feel the stretch along the front/side of your neck
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. Repeat 3 times each side

### Pec Stretch (Doorway)

Tight pecs pull shoulders forward, contributing to FHP:
1. Stand in a doorway
2. Place forearms on the door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step one foot through the doorway
4. Lean forward until you feel a stretch across your chest
5. Hold 30-45 seconds
6. Repeat with elbows higher and lower to target different fibers

## Phase 2: Strengthen Weak Muscles

Now strengthen the muscles that pull your head back and support good posture.

### Chin Tucks

The single most important exercise for forward head posture:

1. Sit or stand with your back against a wall
2. Pull your chin straight back (not down)
3. Imagine making a "double chin"
4. Your head should move backward, not tilt
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. Repeat 10-15 times, multiple times daily

**Key:** The movement is horizontal, not up or down. Think of sliding your head back on a shelf.

### Chin Tucks with Resistance

Progress from basic chin tucks:
1. Place your fingers on your chin
2. Push your chin back against your fingers
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

### Deep Neck Flexor Activation

Lying chin tuck:
1. Lie on your back without a pillow
2. Gently tuck your chin (like nodding "yes")
3. Hold 10 seconds while breathing normally
4. Repeat 10-15 times

**Progression:**
1. Same position
2. Lift head slightly off the floor while keeping chin tucked
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 3 sets of 10

### Wall Angels

Strengthens lower traps and rhomboids:
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Press head, upper back, and hips into wall
3. Arms at 90 degrees (goalpost position)
4. Press arms into wall
5. Slide arms up and down while maintaining contact
6. 2 sets of 10

**Challenge:** If your head doesn't touch the wall, that shows how forward it is. Work toward full contact.

### Prone Y-T-W Raises

Excellent for lower trap and scapular stabilizers:

**Y's:**
1. Lie face down on a bed or bench, head off the edge
2. Arms hanging down, thumbs up
3. Raise arms at 45 degrees (forming a Y)
4. Squeeze shoulder blades together
5. Lower slowly
6. 2 sets of 10

**T's:**
1. Same position
2. Raise arms directly to the side (forming a T)
3. 2 sets of 10

**W's:**
1. Same position
2. Elbows bent, rotate arms up
3. 2 sets of 10

### Face Pulls (with Band)

1. Attach a band at eye level
2. Grip with both hands
3. Pull toward your face, separating hands
4. Elbows high, squeeze shoulder blades together
5. 3 sets of 15

### Scapular Squeezes

1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together and down
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Repeat 15-20 times

## Phase 3: Postural Integration

Build awareness and habits for lasting change.

### Wall Posture Check

Use this as a daily reset:
1. Stand with heels, butt, upper back, and head against wall
2. There should be a small gap at your lower back and neck
3. Hold this position for 30-60 seconds
4. Step away and try to maintain the posture

### Doorway Posture Reminder

Every time you walk through a doorway:
1. Chin tuck
2. Shoulders back
3. Walk through tall

### Brugger's Relief Position

Perfect for desk workers:
1. Sit at the edge of your chair
2. Feet slightly apart, toes out
3. Arch your lower back slightly
4. Rotate arms outward, palms forward
5. Separate fingers and point thumbs back
6. Tuck chin
7. Hold 10 seconds, repeat frequently throughout the day

### Thread the Needle

Improves thoracic rotation (often limited with FHP):
1. Start on hands and knees
2. Thread one arm under your body
3. Let your shoulder lower toward the floor
4. Rotate as far as comfortable
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Repeat 3 times each side

## Daily Program

### Morning Routine (10 minutes)

1. Suboccipital release: 2 minutes
2. Upper trap stretch: 30 seconds each side
3. Levator scapulae stretch: 30 seconds each side
4. Doorway pec stretch: 45 seconds
5. Wall chin tucks: 15 repetitions
6. Wall angels: 10 repetitions
7. Scapular squeezes: 15 repetitions

### Throughout the Day

**Every hour:**
- Chin tucks: 5 repetitions
- Brugger's position: 10 seconds
- Check and correct posture

**Phone/computer use:**
- Raise phone to eye level
- Position monitor at eye level
- Use a document holder

### Evening Routine (10 minutes)

1. All stretches from morning: 30 seconds each
2. Prone Y-T-W: 10 reps each
3. Face pulls: 15 reps (if you have a band)
4. Deep neck flexor holds: 10 × 10 seconds
5. Thread the needle: 3 each side

## Ergonomic Adjustments

### Computer Setup

- **Monitor:** Top of screen at eye level
- **Distance:** Arm's length away
- **Keyboard:** Elbows at 90 degrees
- **Chair:** Supports lower back, feet flat

### Phone Use

- Raise phone to eye level (don't drop head)
- Use voice-to-text when possible
- Take breaks from screen time
- Use a phone stand or holder

### Sleeping

- **Back sleepers:** Thin pillow or cervical pillow
- **Side sleepers:** Pillow that keeps head neutral (not tilted)
- **Avoid:** Stomach sleeping, thick pillows

## Expected Timeline

With consistent effort:

- **Week 1-2:** Increased awareness of posture
- **Week 3-4:** Easier to maintain correct position
- **Week 6-8:** Noticeable improvement in resting posture
- **Week 12+:** Significant correction in most people
- **Ongoing:** Maintenance required (posture reverts without continued attention)

## When to Seek Help

See a professional if:

- Neck pain is severe or worsening
- Numbness or tingling in arms
- Headaches are frequent or severe
- Dizziness with neck movement
- No improvement after 8 weeks of exercises

A physical therapist can provide manual therapy and identify specific issues contributing to your posture.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Chin tucks are king**—do them constantly throughout the day
2. **Stretch what's tight** (suboccipitals, traps, pecs)
3. **Strengthen what's weak** (deep neck flexors, lower traps)
4. **Fix your workspace**—ergonomics matter enormously
5. **Raise your phone**—don't drop your head
6. **Be patient**—posture takes months to change, not days
7. **Make it a habit**—doorway reminders, hourly checks

Forward head posture develops over years of poor habits. Correcting it takes consistent effort, but the payoff—less pain, fewer headaches, better appearance, and improved breathing—is worth it. Start with chin tucks today and build from there.
