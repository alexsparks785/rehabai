# High Arch Exercises: Support and Strengthen Cavus Feet

High arches (pes cavus) present opposite challenges to flat feet. While the high arch looks athletic, it can cause problems—poor shock absorption, ankle instability, and pain along the outside of the foot. These exercises help improve mobility, strengthen supporting muscles, and reduce discomfort.

## Understanding High Arches

**What's happening:**
- Arch is higher than normal
- Weight concentrates on heel and ball of foot
- Less ground contact during standing/walking
- Foot tends to roll outward (supination)

**Common issues:**
- Plantar fasciitis (arch and heel pain)
- Metatarsalgia (ball of foot pain)
- Ankle sprains (instability)
- Claw toes or hammertoes
- Achilles tendon problems
- Calluses on heel and ball of foot

**Causes:**
- Often inherited
- Neurological conditions (Charcot-Marie-Tooth, etc.)
- Sometimes develops after injury

## Goals of Exercise

- Improve foot flexibility
- Strengthen intrinsic foot muscles
- Enhance ankle stability
- Improve shock absorption
- Reduce risk of ankle sprains

## Stretching and Mobility

### Plantar Fascia Stretch
Essential for high arches—the plantar fascia is often tight.
1. Sit and cross affected foot over knee
2. Pull toes back toward shin
3. Feel stretch along arch
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 3 times
6. Do before getting out of bed

### Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius)
1. Face wall, hands on wall
2. Step affected foot back
3. Keep knee straight, heel down
4. Lean forward until stretch
5. Hold 30 seconds each leg

### Calf Stretch (Soleus)
1. Same position as above
2. Bend back knee while keeping heel down
3. Feel stretch lower in calf
4. Hold 30 seconds each leg

### Achilles Stretch
1. Stand on step, heels hanging off
2. Lower one heel below step level
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat other side

### Toe Extension Stretch
1. Kneel sitting back on heels
2. Tuck toes under
3. Sit back gently on feet
4. Feel stretch along bottom of toes
5. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Golf Ball Roll
1. Place golf ball under arch
2. Roll with moderate pressure
3. Spend extra time on tight spots
4. 2-3 minutes each foot

## Strengthening Exercises

### Toe Yoga
1. Lift big toe while pressing other toes down
2. Then press big toe down, lift others
3. Alternate 10 times each
4. Takes practice—harder than it sounds

### Towel Scrunches
1. Place towel flat under foot
2. Scrunch toward you using only toes
3. 2-3 full towel lengths
4. Focus on controlled movement

### Short Foot Exercise
1. Sit with feet flat
2. Without curling toes, try to shorten foot
3. Draw ball of foot toward heel
4. Arch will rise slightly
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. 3 sets of 10

### Marble Pickups
1. Place marbles on floor
2. Pick up one at a time with toes
3. Transfer to cup
4. 20 marbles each foot
5. Builds toe dexterity and strength

### Heel Raises
1. Stand on flat ground
2. Rise onto balls of feet
3. Hold 2 seconds at top
4. Lower slowly
5. 3 sets of 15

### Single Leg Balance
1. Stand on one foot
2. Hold 30 seconds
3. Progress to eyes closed
4. Then unstable surface

## Ankle Stability Exercises

High arches increase ankle sprain risk—these exercises are essential.

### Ankle Circles
1. Sit or lie with foot elevated
2. Draw large circles with foot
3. 10 each direction
4. Move through full range

### Ankle Alphabet
1. Foot elevated
2. "Write" alphabet with big toe
3. Move only from ankle
4. Gets full range of motion

### Band Exercises (4 Directions)

**Dorsiflexion:**
1. Loop band around forefoot
2. Anchor to stable object in front
3. Pull foot toward shin
4. 15 reps

**Plantarflexion:**
1. Band anchored behind you
2. Point foot away against resistance
3. 15 reps

**Inversion:**
1. Band anchored to outside
2. Turn sole of foot inward
3. 15 reps

**Eversion:**
1. Band anchored to inside
2. Turn sole of foot outward
3. 15 reps—important for supinators

### Single Leg Hops
Progress to these once basic balance is solid:
1. Small hops on one foot
2. Side to side
3. Forward and back
4. Stick the landing
5. Start with 5-10, build up

## Lower Leg Strengthening

### Eccentric Calf Lowering
1. Rise on both feet
2. Shift weight to one leg
3. Lower slowly (4 seconds)
4. Rise on both, repeat
5. 3 sets of 12 each leg

### Anterior Tibialis Strengthening
Strengthens muscle that lifts foot.
1. Sit with legs extended
2. Loop band around foot
3. Anchor band in front
4. Pull foot toward shin against resistance
5. 3 sets of 15

### Peroneal Strengthening
Critical for ankle stability in high arches.
1. Sit with legs extended
2. Loop band around outside of forefoot
3. Turn foot outward against resistance
4. 3 sets of 15 each foot

## Hip and Glute Work

Weak hips can affect foot mechanics.

### Clamshells
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Keep heels together
3. Lift top knee
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

### Monster Walks
1. Band around ankles
2. Quarter squat position
3. Walk sideways
4. Keep tension on band
5. 20 steps each direction

### Single Leg RDL
1. Stand on one leg
2. Hinge forward at hips
3. Reach toward ground
4. Keep standing knee slightly bent
5. 3 sets of 10 each leg

## Daily Routine

### Morning (5 minutes)
1. Plantar fascia stretch in bed (1 min)
2. Calf stretches (2 min)
3. Ankle circles (1 min)
4. Golf ball roll (1 min)

### Evening (10 minutes)
1. All stretches (3 min)
2. Towel scrunches and toe exercises (3 min)
3. Ankle band work (3 min)
4. Balance practice (1 min)

### Weekly
- Full lower leg strengthening routine (2-3x)
- Hip and glute work (2x)
- Progress balance exercises

## Footwear Considerations

**Choose shoes with:**
- Cushioning (compensates for poor shock absorption)
- Neutral or cushioned running shoes (not stability)
- Room in toe box (avoid compressing toes)
- Good heel counter

**Consider:**
- Custom orthotics designed for high arches
- Heel cups or cushioned insoles
- Avoiding completely flat shoes

**Avoid:**
- Hard, thin soles
- Stability or motion control shoes
- Shoes that force more supination

## Activity Modifications

**For running:**
- Ensure proper cushioned shoes
- Don't increase mileage too quickly
- Include ankle strengthening in routine
- Consider softer running surfaces

**For sports:**
- Ankle strengthening is essential
- Consider taping or bracing for sports with cutting
- Warm up ankles thoroughly

## When to Seek Help

See a podiatrist or orthopedist if you have:
- Progressive arch increase
- Significant pain limiting activities
- Frequent ankle sprains
- Numbness or weakness
- Family history of neurological conditions
- Clawed or hammertoes worsening

**They may recommend:**
- Custom orthotics
- Bracing for severe cases
- Physical therapy
- Evaluation for underlying conditions
- Rarely, surgical consultation

## What to Expect

**Short-term (2-4 weeks):**
- Reduced tightness with stretching
- Improved ankle mobility

**Medium-term (1-3 months):**
- Better ankle stability
- Reduced pain with activities
- Improved foot strength

**Long-term:**
- Fewer ankle sprains
- Better shock absorption
- Ability to handle more activity

**Realistic expectations:**
- Exercises won't lower arch height
- Can significantly improve function and reduce symptoms
- Consistent effort required

High arches don't have to mean pain or instability. With appropriate stretching, strengthening, and footwear choices, you can move confidently and comfortably.
