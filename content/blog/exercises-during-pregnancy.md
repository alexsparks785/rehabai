# Exercises During Pregnancy: Safe Workouts by Trimester

Exercise during pregnancy isn't just safe—it's beneficial for both you and your baby. But what's safe changes as your body changes.

Here's your trimester-by-trimester guide to staying active.

## Benefits of Prenatal Exercise

### For You

- Reduced back pain
- Less constipation and bloating
- Improved mood and energy
- Better sleep
- Reduced risk of gestational diabetes
- Easier labor and faster recovery
- Lower risk of excessive weight gain
- Reduced risk of C-section

### For Baby

- Healthier birth weight
- Potentially easier transition to life outside womb
- May improve baby's heart health
- Lower stress hormones

## Before You Start

### Get Medical Clearance

Talk to your healthcare provider before exercising, especially if you have:
- Heart or lung disease
- Cervical insufficiency
- Multiple pregnancy (twins+) with risk factors
- Placenta previa after 26 weeks
- Preterm labor risk
- Preeclampsia
- Severe anemia

### General Guidelines

**Do:**
- Stay hydrated (drink before, during, after)
- Wear supportive shoes and sports bra
- Avoid overheating (skip hot yoga, hot tubs)
- Eat enough to fuel activity
- Listen to your body

**Don't:**
- Exercise to exhaustion
- Hold your breath during exertion
- Exercise lying flat on back after first trimester
- Do activities with fall or abdominal trauma risk
- Ignore warning signs

## First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

### What's Happening

- Fatigue and nausea are common
- Few physical limitations yet
- Body is adjusting hormonally
- Most activities still safe

### Safe Exercises

**Walking**
- Excellent low-impact option
- As much as feels comfortable
- Can continue throughout pregnancy

**Swimming**
- Supports body weight
- No overheating risk
- Great for nausea

**Strength Training**
- Can continue normal routine (with modifications)
- Avoid very heavy lifting
- Focus on form over weight

**Yoga (Prenatal or Modified)**
- Avoid hot yoga
- Skip deep twists and inversions
- Great for flexibility and stress

**Cycling (Stationary)**
- No fall risk
- Good cardio option

### First Trimester Tips

- If nausea is bad, even a short walk helps
- Morning may be better if you have evening fatigue
- Stay hydrated—dehydration worsens nausea
- Don't stress about performance—maintenance is fine

## Second Trimester (Weeks 13-26)

### What's Happening

- Energy often returns ("honeymoon trimester")
- Belly starts to show
- Balance begins to shift
- Relaxin hormone loosens joints

### Modifications Needed

**Avoid lying flat on back**
- After ~16-20 weeks, uterus can compress major blood vessels
- Modify exercises to incline or side-lying

**Watch your balance**
- Center of gravity shifts
- Use support when needed
- Avoid unstable surfaces

**Don't overstretch**
- Relaxin makes joints looser
- Easier to overdo stretches
- Stay within comfortable range

### Safe Exercises

**Walking** - Continue as tolerated

**Swimming** - Ideal as belly grows (water supports weight)

**Stationary cycling** - Still safe

**Prenatal yoga** - Modified for growing belly

**Strength training** - With modifications:
- Incline bench instead of flat
- Supported exercises
- Moderate weights
- Avoid exercises on belly

**Low-impact aerobics** - Prenatal classes ideal

### Second Trimester Sample Routine

**3x weekly strength:**
- Goblet squats: 3×12
- Incline push-ups: 3×10
- Seated rows: 3×12
- Glute bridges: 3×15
- Side-lying leg raises: 3×12 each
- Bird dogs: 3×10 each side

**Daily:**
- Walking: 20-30 minutes
- Stretching: 10 minutes

## Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40)

### What's Happening

- Baby is large, belly is big
- Fatigue may return
- Shortness of breath common
- Pelvic pressure increases
- Braxton Hicks contractions

### Further Modifications

**Lower intensity**
- Body is working hard just existing
- Reduce intensity as needed
- Walking may replace running

**Shorter sessions**
- 20-30 minutes may be plenty
- Multiple short sessions okay

**More support**
- Use chairs, walls for balance
- Seated exercises may feel better

**Pelvic floor focus**
- Kegels become more important
- Prepare for labor and recovery

### Safe Exercises

**Walking** - Remain gold standard

**Swimming/Water aerobics** - Feels amazing when heavy

**Prenatal yoga** - Very modified (props, shorter holds)

**Pelvic floor exercises** - Kegels, deep breathing

**Upper body strength** - Seated exercises

**Gentle stretching** - Hip openers, back relief

### Third Trimester Sample Routine

**2-3x weekly:**
- Seated shoulder press: 2×12
- Seated rows: 2×12
- Wall push-ups: 2×12
- Seated leg extensions: 2×12
- Standing calf raises (with support): 2×15
- Pelvic tilts: 2×15

**Daily:**
- Walking: 15-20 minutes (or as tolerated)
- Pelvic floor exercises
- Gentle stretching: 10 minutes

## Exercises to Avoid During Pregnancy

### Throughout Pregnancy

❌ **Contact sports** (soccer, basketball, hockey)
❌ **High fall risk** (skiing, horseback riding, gymnastics)
❌ **Scuba diving** (pressure changes dangerous for baby)
❌ **Hot yoga/hot Pilates** (overheating risk)
❌ **High altitude** (above 6,000 ft if not acclimated)
❌ **Lying flat on back** (after first trimester)
❌ **Heavy weightlifting** (breath-holding, pressure)
❌ **Jumping and jarring movements** (later pregnancy)

### Exercise-Specific Modifications

| Exercise | Modification |
|----------|--------------|
| Crunches/sit-ups | Avoid—do bird dogs, pelvic tilts instead |
| Flat bench press | Incline bench |
| Lying leg raises | Side-lying or standing |
| Deep squats | Stop at comfortable depth |
| Jumping | Step instead of jump |
| Running | Walk if uncomfortable |
| Planks | Incline or hands-and-knees |

## Warning Signs: Stop and Call Your Provider

Stop exercising and contact your healthcare provider if you experience:

- Vaginal bleeding
- Regular painful contractions
- Amniotic fluid leakage
- Chest pain
- Dizziness or feeling faint
- Headache
- Calf pain or swelling
- Decreased fetal movement
- Shortness of breath before exertion

## Pelvic Floor Exercises (Kegels)

### Why They Matter

- Support growing uterus
- Prepare for labor
- Aid postpartum recovery
- Prevent incontinence

### How to Do Them

1. Identify the muscles (stop urine mid-stream—those muscles)
2. Squeeze and lift those muscles
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Release fully
5. Repeat 10-15 times
6. Do 3 sets daily

### Tips

- Don't hold breath
- Don't squeeze abs, thighs, or glutes
- Can do anywhere (no one knows)
- Consistency matters more than duration

## Sample Weekly Schedule

### Active Pregnancy (All Trimesters—Adjust Intensity)

**Monday:** Strength training (30 min) + walking (15 min)
**Tuesday:** Swimming or water aerobics (30 min)
**Wednesday:** Prenatal yoga (30-45 min)
**Thursday:** Walking (30 min)
**Friday:** Strength training (30 min) + stretching
**Saturday:** Longer walk or hike (as tolerated)
**Sunday:** Rest or gentle stretching

### Less Active/Third Trimester

**Daily:** Walking (15-20 min) + pelvic floor exercises + stretching (10 min)
**2-3x weekly:** Gentle strength (20 min) OR prenatal yoga/water exercise

## Returning to Exercise Postpartum

### Timeline

- **Vaginal delivery:** Can start gentle walking and pelvic floor work within days if feeling okay
- **C-section:** Wait 6-8 weeks for most exercise, earlier walking okay
- **Clearance:** Get provider approval before returning to full exercise (usually 6-week checkup)

### Gradual Return

- Start with walking and pelvic floor
- Add strength gradually
- Watch for pelvic floor issues (leaking, pressure)
- Core recovery takes time (diastasis recti may need rehab)
- Be patient—your body did something incredible

## The Bottom Line

Exercise during pregnancy is safe and beneficial for most women. The key principles:

- **Get medical clearance**
- **Listen to your body** (modify as needed)
- **Stay hydrated and cool**
- **Avoid high-risk activities**
- **Something is better than nothing**

Your body knows what it's doing. Support it with gentle movement, and it will support you through pregnancy, labor, and recovery.
