# Diastasis Recti Exercises: Heal Your Abdominal Separation

That stubborn belly pooch that won't go away no matter how many crunches you do? If you've been pregnant, it might be diastasis recti—a separation of your abdominal muscles. The good news: the right exercises can help close the gap and restore core function. But the wrong exercises can make it worse.

## Understanding Diastasis Recti

### What Is It?

Diastasis recti is a separation of the rectus abdominis muscles (your "six-pack" muscles) along the linea alba—the connective tissue running down the center of your abdomen. During pregnancy, this tissue stretches to accommodate the growing baby. For many women, it doesn't fully recover on its own.

### Signs You May Have Diastasis Recti

- A visible bulge or "dome" down the center of your belly when you sit up
- A gap you can feel between your ab muscles
- Lower back pain
- Pelvic floor dysfunction
- Poor posture
- Bloating or digestive issues
- Difficulty with core exercises

### Self-Assessment

1. Lie on your back with knees bent
2. Place fingers horizontally across your belly button
3. Lift your head and shoulders slightly (like starting a crunch)
4. Feel for a gap between the muscles
5. Note the width (how many fingers fit) and depth (how far fingers sink)

**Width:** 2+ finger-widths typically indicates diastasis
**Depth:** How much tension you feel matters as much as width

## Exercises to AVOID

These exercises can worsen diastasis:

- **Traditional crunches and sit-ups**
- **Planks** (until you can control the bulging)
- **Double leg lifts**
- **Exercises that cause belly "doming" or "coning"**
- **Heavy lifting** without proper core engagement
- **Twisting exercises** under load

**The test:** If your belly bulges or domes during an exercise, that exercise is too advanced for you right now.

## Phase 1: Reconnection (Weeks 1-4)

Before strengthening, you need to reconnect with your deep core muscles.

### Diaphragmatic Breathing

Foundation of diastasis recovery:
1. Lie on your back, knees bent
2. Place one hand on chest, one on belly
3. Breathe in through nose—belly rises, ribs expand sideways
4. Breathe out through mouth—belly falls naturally
5. Notice your pelvic floor: inhale = relaxes, exhale = gently lifts
6. Practice 5 minutes, 3 times daily

### Connection Breath

1. Same position as above
2. Inhale, letting belly expand
3. Exhale slowly, gently drawing belly button toward spine
4. Simultaneously do a gentle Kegel (pelvic floor lift)
5. This is your "core activation"—exhale, draw in, lift up
6. Hold 5-10 seconds
7. Inhale and release completely
8. 10 repetitions, 3 times daily

### Heel Slides with Connection Breath

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Exhale and activate core (connection breath)
3. Slowly slide one heel away from body
4. Only go as far as you can maintain core activation
5. Slide back
6. Release and switch legs
7. 10 each leg

### Toe Taps

1. Lie on back, knees bent 90 degrees, feet off floor
2. Exhale and activate core
3. Lower one toe to tap the floor
4. Return to start
5. Switch legs
6. 10 each leg

**Watch for doming—if your belly bulges, reduce the range of motion.**

### Bent Knee Fall-Outs

1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Exhale and activate core
3. Let one knee fall out to the side
4. Keep pelvis stable—don't let it rock
5. Bring knee back
6. 10 each side

## Phase 2: Gentle Strengthening (Weeks 4-8)

Progress when you can do Phase 1 exercises without doming.

### Dead Bug (Modified)

1. Lie on back, arms toward ceiling
2. Knees bent 90 degrees, feet off floor
3. Exhale, activate core
4. Lower ONE arm overhead while maintaining position
5. Return and switch arms
6. 10 each arm

**Progression:** Add opposite leg lowering (arm + leg)

### Bird Dog (On Hands and Knees)

1. Start on hands and knees
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Extend one arm forward
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. Return and switch
6. 10 each arm

**Progression:** Add opposite leg extension

### Glute Bridge with Core Activation

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Squeeze glutes and lift hips
4. Keep core engaged throughout
5. Lower slowly
6. 15 repetitions

### Side-Lying Leg Lifts

1. Lie on your side
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Lift top leg
4. Lower with control
5. 15 each side

### Wall Push-Ups

1. Stand facing wall, hands at shoulder height
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Bend elbows, lean toward wall
4. Push back
5. Maintain core engagement throughout
6. 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-12)

Progress when Phase 2 exercises are easy and dome-free.

### Full Dead Bug

1. Lie on back, arms up, knees at 90 degrees
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Lower opposite arm and leg toward floor
4. Keep lower back pressed into ground
5. Return and switch
6. 10 each side

### Bird Dog with Holds

1. Hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 10 each side

### Modified Plank (On Knees)

Only attempt when you can maintain flat belly:
1. Plank position on knees
2. Maintain core activation
3. No doming or sagging
4. Hold 10-30 seconds
5. Build duration gradually

### Side Plank (Modified)

1. Side-lying, prop on elbow and knees
2. Lift hips off ground
3. Hold 15-30 seconds
4. 3 each side

### Squat with Core Activation

1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
2. Exhale, activate core
3. Squat down
4. Maintain activation throughout
5. Exhale on the way up
6. 15 repetitions

### Pallof Press (Anti-Rotation)

1. Stand sideways to a resistance band anchored at chest height
2. Hold band at chest with both hands
3. Exhale, activate core
4. Press band straight out
5. Resist the rotation
6. Hold 5 seconds
7. 10 each side

## Phase 4: Functional Core (Weeks 12+)

### Full Plank (When Ready)

1. Only attempt when modified plank is easy
2. Watch for any doming
3. Start with 10-15 seconds
4. Build gradually

### Single-Leg Glute Bridge

1. Lie on back, one leg extended
2. Bridge up on single leg
3. Maintain core activation
4. 10 each leg

### Farmer's Carry

1. Hold weights at sides
2. Walk with good posture
3. Core engaged
4. 30-60 seconds

### Lunges with Core Focus

1. Step into lunge
2. Exhale and engage core throughout
3. Push back to standing
4. 10 each leg

## Daily Program

### Morning (10 minutes)

1. Diaphragmatic breathing: 10 breaths
2. Connection breath: 10 reps
3. Heel slides: 10 each leg
4. Toe taps: 10 each leg
5. Dead bug (appropriate level): 10 each side
6. Glute bridge: 15 reps

### Evening (10 minutes)

1. Connection breath: 10 reps
2. Bird dog: 10 each side
3. Side-lying leg lifts: 15 each side
4. Modified plank: 3 × 20 seconds (when ready)
5. Pallof press: 10 each side (when ready)

### Throughout the Day

- Practice core activation during daily activities
- Exhale and engage before lifting anything (including your baby!)
- Check posture regularly
- Avoid breath-holding during exertion

## Tips for Faster Healing

### Posture Matters

- Stand tall, shoulders back
- Avoid rib flaring (ribs should be down, not jutting out)
- Stack ribs over pelvis

### Movement Patterns

- Exhale on exertion (lifting, pushing, pulling)
- Roll to side to get out of bed (not sitting straight up)
- Engage core before lifting

### Support Options

- Abdominal binder or support garment (early postpartum)
- Can help during activity
- Not a substitute for exercise

### Nutrition and Hydration

- Support tissue healing with protein
- Stay hydrated for connective tissue health
- Consider collagen supplementation (discuss with provider)

## When to Seek Help

See a pelvic floor physical therapist if:

- Gap doesn't improve after 8-12 weeks of exercises
- You're unsure if you're doing exercises correctly
- You have pelvic floor dysfunction (leaking, prolapse symptoms)
- You have back pain that persists
- You want a personalized assessment and program

A specialist can:
- Assess your specific gap and tension
- Use hands-on techniques
- Check for pelvic floor involvement
- Progress you safely

## What to Expect

### Timeline

- **Weeks 1-4:** Learning to connect with deep core
- **Weeks 4-8:** Gap may begin to reduce; function improves
- **Weeks 8-12:** Noticeable improvement in gap and strength
- **Months 3-6:** Significant healing for most women
- **Months 6-12:** Continued improvement; some gaps may not fully close but function is restored

### Success Metrics

Don't just focus on gap width. Success includes:
- Reduced or no doming during activity
- Better core function
- Less back pain
- Improved posture
- Ability to do activities without symptoms

**A small gap with good tension is better than a closed gap with poor function.**

## Key Takeaways

1. **Avoid crunches**—they can worsen the separation
2. **Breathe first**—diaphragmatic breathing is foundation
3. **Exhale and engage** before any exertion
4. **No doming**—if your belly bulges, the exercise is too hard
5. **Progress gradually**—rushing causes setbacks
6. **Function over gap width**—tension matters as much as size
7. **Be patient**—healing takes months, not weeks

Diastasis recti is common and treatable. With consistent, appropriate exercise, most women see significant improvement. Focus on reconnecting with your deep core, progress gradually, and celebrate the functional improvements along the way.
