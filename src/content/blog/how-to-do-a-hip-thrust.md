The hip thrust is the king of glute exercises. While squats and deadlifts work the glutes, nothing isolates and loads them quite like the hip thrust. If building a stronger, more powerful backside is your goal, this exercise belongs in your program.

Here's how to do it right.

## Why Hip Thrust?

### Superior Glute Activation

Research shows hip thrusts activate the glutes more than squats or deadlifts. The position keeps tension on the glutes throughout the movement, especially at the top where they're fully contracted.

### Direct Loading

Unlike squats (where quads do significant work) or deadlifts (where back and hamstrings contribute heavily), hip thrusts allow you to load the glutes directly and heavily.

### Builds Strength and Size

Hip thrusts build both strength and hypertrophy in the glutes—improving athletic performance, aesthetics, and posterior chain function.

### Safer for Some

For those with back issues or limited squat/deadlift mobility, hip thrusts provide a way to train the lower body with less spinal loading.

## Hip Thrust Setup

### Equipment Needed

- A sturdy bench (standard flat bench works)
- Barbell and plates (or dumbbells for lighter weights)
- Barbell pad (highly recommended for comfort)

### Bench Position

The bench should hit at approximately your lower shoulder blade/upper back area when you're in position. Too high and you'll slide down; too low and you'll hyperextend your back.

### Body Position

1. Sit on the floor with your back against the bench
2. Roll barbell over your legs to your hip crease
3. Place a pad around the bar for comfort
4. Feet flat on floor, about shoulder-width apart
5. Feet positioned so knees will be at roughly 90° at the top

## Executing the Hip Thrust

### Starting Position

- Upper back against bench, roughly at shoulder blade level
- Barbell across hip crease, hands stabilizing the bar
- Feet flat on floor, shoulder-width apart
- Chin slightly tucked

### The Movement

1. **Drive through heels** to lift hips toward ceiling
2. **Squeeze glutes hard** at the top
3. At the top: body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees
4. Knees should be at approximately 90°
5. **Hold briefly** at the top, squeezing glutes
6. **Lower with control**, not letting hips touch ground
7. Repeat without fully resting at bottom

### Top Position Checkpoints

- Hips fully extended (no sagging)
- Glutes squeezed maximally
- Shins approximately vertical
- No hyperextension of lower back
- Chin stays tucked (don't throw head back)
- Ribs down, core braced

### Breathing

- Inhale at the bottom
- Exhale during the thrust or at the top
- Maintain core tension throughout

## Common Hip Thrust Mistakes

### Hyperextending the Lower Back

**The problem:** Arching excessively at the top instead of achieving hip extension through the glutes.

**The fix:** Focus on tucking your pelvis under (posterior pelvic tilt) at the top. Think about squeezing glutes to lift, not arching your back.

### Pushing Through Toes

**The problem:** Weight shifts to toes, reducing glute activation and increasing quad work.

**The fix:** Drive through heels. You should be able to lift your toes off the ground at the top.

### Feet Too Close or Too Far

**The problem:** Wrong foot position changes the muscle emphasis and can reduce glute activation.

**The fix:** At the top of the movement, shins should be roughly vertical (90° knee angle). Adjust foot position to achieve this.

### Letting Knees Cave

**The problem:** Knees collapse inward during the movement.

**The fix:** Actively push knees outward in line with toes. Use a band around knees as a cue if needed.

### Not Squeezing at the Top

**The problem:** Rushing through the movement, missing the peak contraction.

**The fix:** Pause at the top and squeeze glutes as hard as possible for 1-2 seconds.

### Bar Position Too High or Low

**The problem:** Bar on belly button (too high) or thighs (too low).

**The fix:** Position bar at hip crease—the fold where your torso meets your thighs.

### Bench Too High or Too Low

**The problem:** Wrong bench height changes the movement mechanics.

**The fix:** Bench should contact your upper back/lower shoulder blade area. You may need to adjust bench or use a shorter/taller platform.

## Hip Thrust Variations

### Bodyweight Hip Thrust

Start here if you're new to the movement.

Same setup but no weight. Master the squeeze and body position before adding load. Build to 3 sets of 20 before adding weight.

### Dumbbell Hip Thrust

Easier to get into position than barbell.

Hold a dumbbell across your hip crease. Good for beginners or when barbells aren't available.

### Single-Leg Hip Thrust

Addresses imbalances, increases difficulty.

Same movement, one leg extended or foot off the ground. Much harder than bilateral version—reduce weight significantly.

### Banded Hip Thrust

Band around knees adds hip abduction work.

Place a resistance band just above knees. Push outward against band while thrusting. Extra glute medius activation.

### Elevated Hip Thrust (Feet Elevated)

Increases range of motion.

Place feet on a low platform or step. Allows hips to travel lower, increasing stretch and range.

### Smith Machine Hip Thrust

Easier setup, no bar rolling.

Many find this more comfortable than free barbell. The fixed path also provides stability for learning.

### Pause Hip Thrust

3-5 second pause at the top.

Eliminates momentum and increases time under tension. Use lighter weight—the burn is intense.

### B-Stance Hip Thrust

Unilateral emphasis without full single-leg difficulty.

One foot flat and working, other foot on heel only for balance. Shifts most work to the flat foot side.

## Programming Hip Thrusts

### For Beginners

**Frequency:** 1-2 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 3 sets of 12-15 reps
**Weight:** Start light, master form before adding weight
**Focus:** Mind-muscle connection, squeezing glutes

### For Hypertrophy (Glute Growth)

**Frequency:** 2-3 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
**Weight:** Moderate to heavy
**Focus:** Progressive overload, full range, squeeze at top

### For Strength

**Frequency:** 1-2 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 4-5 sets of 4-6 reps
**Weight:** Heavy
**Focus:** Maximum force production, controlled movement

### Sample Weekly Integration

**Day A (Quad focus):**
- Squat: 4×6
- Hip Thrust: 3×10
- Leg Press: 3×12

**Day B (Glute/Hamstring focus):**
- Romanian Deadlift: 4×8
- Hip Thrust: 4×8
- Leg Curl: 3×12

## Progressing Your Hip Thrust

### Add Weight

The most straightforward progression. Add 5-10 lbs when you can complete all prescribed reps with good form.

### Add Reps

If you can't add weight, add reps until you reach the top of your range, then add weight and drop reps.

### Add Sets

If 3 sets is easy, try 4. More volume equals more stimulus.

### Add Pause

Pause for 2-3 seconds at the top. Dramatically increases difficulty without adding weight.

### Move to Single Leg

When bilateral hip thrusts are easy, single-leg variations provide a significant challenge.

## Common Questions

### How Heavy Should I Hip Thrust?

Eventually, quite heavy. Many people can hip thrust more than they squat once they've built the movement pattern. It's not uncommon for advanced lifters to hip thrust 300+ lbs.

### Do Hip Thrusts Build a Bigger Butt?

Yes. Combined with adequate nutrition, hip thrusts are one of the most effective exercises for glute hypertrophy.

### Can Hip Thrusts Replace Squats?

They serve different purposes. Squats are a more complete lower body exercise; hip thrusts isolate glutes better. Ideally, include both.

### What If It Hurts My Hip Bone?

Use a thick barbell pad. A folded towel can work in a pinch. If discomfort persists, try a different pad or barbell position.

### How Deep Should I Go?

Lower until your hips are near (not touching) the ground. No need to touch the floor between reps.

## The Bottom Line

The hip thrust is unmatched for glute development:

1. **Set up correctly:** Upper back on bench, bar at hip crease, feet positioned for 90° knees at top
2. **Drive through heels:** Not toes
3. **Squeeze hard at top:** Full hip extension, maximal glute contraction
4. **Control the descent:** Don't drop between reps
5. **Progress over time:** More weight, reps, or sets

Start with bodyweight to master the movement, then add load progressively. Your glutes will respond.

The hip thrust might look awkward, but the results speak for themselves. Time to thrust.
