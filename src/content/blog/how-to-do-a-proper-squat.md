The squat is often called the king of exercises—and for good reason. It works more muscles than almost any other movement: quads, glutes, hamstrings, core, and even upper back. It's functional, translating directly to sitting, standing, and picking things up.

But squats are also easy to do poorly. Bad form limits results and risks injury. This guide will teach you proper squat technique from the ground up.

## The Bodyweight Squat

Before adding weight, master the bodyweight squat.

### Setup

1. **Stance:** Feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. Toes pointed out 15-30 degrees (the angle that feels natural for your hips).

2. **Weight distribution:** Even across your whole foot—not on toes, not entirely on heels. Think "tripod": big toe, little toe, heel all in contact.

3. **Posture:** Stand tall. Chest up, shoulders back. Look straight ahead or slightly down.

### The Descent

1. **Initiate with hips:** Push your hips back as if sitting into a chair behind you.

2. **Knees track toes:** Let your knees bend naturally, tracking over (not inside) your toes.

3. **Keep chest up:** Maintain an upright torso. Don't let your chest drop toward the floor.

4. **Depth:** Descend until your hip crease goes below your knee ("parallel" or deeper). If you can't reach depth with good form, work on mobility.

5. **Controlled speed:** Lower in 2-3 seconds with control. Don't drop or bounce.

### The Ascent

1. **Drive through whole foot:** Push the floor away, keeping weight distributed evenly.

2. **Knees out:** Actively push knees outward, tracking over toes.

3. **Hips and chest rise together:** Don't let your hips shoot up first while chest stays down.

4. **Stand fully:** Complete the rep with hips fully extended, standing tall.

### Breathing

**Inhale** during the descent (brace your core).
**Exhale** during the ascent (or at the top).

For heavier weights, you may hold your breath throughout the rep (Valsalva maneuver), exhaling after standing.

## Common Squat Mistakes

### Knees Caving In

When knees collapse inward during the squat.

**Causes:** Weak glutes, poor hip mobility, stance too narrow.

**Fix:** Consciously push knees out over toes. Strengthen glutes with bridges and clamshells. Use a band around knees as a cue during warm-up sets.

### Heels Rising

Coming up onto toes during the descent.

**Causes:** Tight calves or ankles, weight shifting forward, stance issues.

**Fix:** Work on ankle mobility. Try a wider stance with more toe-out angle. Elevate heels slightly (squat shoes or small plates under heels) as a temporary aid.

### Butt Wink

Lower back rounding at the bottom of the squat.

**Causes:** Limited hip mobility, going deeper than your mobility allows, stance issues.

**Fix:** Only squat as deep as you can with neutral spine. Work on hip mobility (90/90 stretch, hip circles). Experiment with stance width and toe angle.

### Forward Lean (Chest Dropping)

Torso tilting excessively forward.

**Causes:** Weak quads, weak upper back, poor ankle mobility.

**Fix:** Strengthen upper back with rows. Improve ankle mobility. Focus on "chest up" cue. Consider tempo squats to build positional strength.

### Shallow Depth

Not reaching at least parallel (hip crease below knee).

**Causes:** Mobility limitations, habit, ego (wanting to use more weight).

**Fix:** Use less weight and prioritize depth. Work on hip and ankle mobility. Use a box or bench as a depth target.

### Looking Up

Craning neck to look at ceiling.

**Causes:** Misguided cue to "keep chest up."

**Fix:** Look straight ahead or slightly down (6-10 feet in front of you). Keep your neck neutral with your spine.

## Squat Progressions

### Bodyweight Squat

Start here. Master form with no weight. Build to 3 sets of 15-20 reps with good depth and control.

### Goblet Squat

Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell at chest height. This reinforces upright posture and is the best way to learn squatting with weight.

**Key points:**
- Hold weight close to chest
- Elbows point down, inside knees at bottom
- Same mechanics as bodyweight squat
- Start with 15-25 lbs, build from there

Build to 3 sets of 12-15 reps with challenging weight.

### Barbell Back Squat

The classic loaded squat. Bar rests on your upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar).

**High bar position:**
- Bar on upper traps
- More upright torso
- Greater knee travel
- Emphasizes quads

**Low bar position:**
- Bar on rear delts (lower on back)
- More forward lean
- Greater hip hinge
- Emphasizes posterior chain

Both are valid. High bar is usually easier to learn.

**Setup:**
1. Duck under bar, positioning it on upper back
2. Grip bar outside shoulders
3. Squeeze shoulder blades together to create a shelf
4. Stand up, take 2-3 steps back
5. Set stance, brace core
6. Squat

### Front Squat

Bar rests on front of shoulders, held in clean grip (fingers under bar) or cross-arm grip.

**Benefits:**
- Forces upright posture
- Emphasizes quads
- Easier on lower back for many people

**Challenge:** Requires good wrist and thoracic mobility for clean grip.

## Mobility for Better Squats

Limited mobility restricts squat depth and quality. Key areas to address:

### Ankle Mobility

Tight calves and ankles prevent knees from tracking forward properly.

**Test:** In a half-kneeling position, can your knee travel 4-5 inches past your toes?

**Improve:**
- Calf stretches (straight leg and bent leg)
- Ankle circles
- Kneeling ankle rocks
- Weighted ankle stretches

### Hip Mobility

Tight hips restrict depth and cause compensation patterns.

**Improve:**
- 90/90 stretch
- Pigeon pose
- Deep squat holds
- Hip circles

### Thoracic Mobility

A stiff upper back makes staying upright difficult.

**Improve:**
- Thoracic extensions on foam roller
- Cat-cow
- Thread the needle

## Squat Variations

### Pause Squat

Pause at the bottom for 2-3 seconds. Builds strength out of the hole and reinforces positioning.

### Tempo Squat

Slow down the descent (3-5 seconds). Builds control and highlights weaknesses.

### Box Squat

Squat down to a box or bench. Teaches depth consistency and builds confidence.

### Bulgarian Split Squat

Single-leg variation with rear foot elevated. Builds unilateral strength and addresses imbalances.

### Heel-Elevated Squat

Place small plates or wedge under heels. Reduces ankle mobility demands and emphasizes quads.

## How Deep Should You Squat?

**The standard:** Hip crease below the top of the knee (parallel or deeper).

Full depth squats (ass-to-grass) are fine if you can maintain a neutral spine. If your lower back rounds (butt wink), stop at the depth where you can maintain form.

Partial squats (above parallel) can be used occasionally for specific purposes but shouldn't be your primary squatting pattern. They limit muscle development and don't build strength through full range.

## Programming Squats

### For Beginners

**Frequency:** 2-3 times per week
**Volume:** 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
**Focus:** Perfect form, progressive weight increases

### For Strength

**Frequency:** 2 times per week
**Volume:** 4-5 sets of 3-6 reps
**Focus:** Heavy weight with good form, adequate rest between sets

### For Muscle Building

**Frequency:** 2 times per week
**Volume:** 3-4 sets of 6-12 reps
**Focus:** Moderate weight, controlled tempo, full range of motion

## Troubleshooting

### "My knees hurt when I squat"

Check form: are knees caving in? Are you going too deep for your current mobility? Are you using too much weight?

Try: Reduce weight, focus on knees tracking over toes, strengthen glutes, improve hip mobility.

If pain persists, see a professional.

### "I can't get deep enough"

Limited depth is usually mobility, not strength.

Try: Wider stance, more toe-out angle, work on ankle and hip mobility, use heel elevation temporarily.

### "I feel it all in my lower back"

Usually means you're losing neutral spine position or hips are shooting up first.

Try: Reduce weight, focus on keeping chest up, film yourself to check form, strengthen your core.

### "I fall forward at the bottom"

Ankle mobility or balance issue, or weight is too heavy.

Try: Elevate heels, widen stance, reduce weight, practice pause squats to build bottom position strength.

## Safety Tips

**Use a rack with safeties:** When back squatting, set the safety pins so you can bail a failed rep.

**Know how to bail:** Practice dumping the bar behind you (back squat) or in front (front squat) with light weight.

**Don't max out alone:** Have a spotter for heavy attempts, or use a rack with proper safeties.

**Check your ego:** Bad reps with too much weight don't build muscle—they build injuries.

## The Bottom Line

The squat is a fundamental movement everyone should master:

1. **Start with bodyweight** and nail the basics
2. **Progress to goblet squats** to learn weighted squatting
3. **Move to barbell squats** when ready
4. **Prioritize form over weight**—always
5. **Work on mobility** to improve depth and quality
6. **Be patient**—squat proficiency develops over months and years

A good squat takes practice. Film yourself, compare to good examples, and keep refining. The investment in learning proper technique pays dividends for a lifetime of strong, safe training.

Now go squat.
