# Hip Hinge and RDL: Master the Movement Pattern That Protects Your Back

Every time you pick something up from the floor, you're hinging at the hips—or you should be. The hip hinge is the foundational movement pattern that allows you to lift safely and powerfully, using your glutes and hamstrings instead of your lower back.

Most people never learn to hinge properly. They bend from the spine, stress their discs, and wonder why their back hurts. Master the hip hinge and you solve this problem permanently.

The Romanian Deadlift (RDL) is the primary exercise for building hip hinge strength. This guide takes you from basic hinge mechanics through loaded RDL variations that build serious posterior chain strength.

## What Is the Hip Hinge?

### The Movement

The hip hinge is bending forward by pushing your hips back while maintaining a neutral spine. Your torso lowers because your hips move backward, not because your spine flexes.

Think of it like a door hinge: the door (torso) swings, but the hinge point (hips) stays stable.

### Why It Matters

**For back health**: Hinging keeps your spine neutral while your hips do the work. This protects discs and reduces lower back stress.

**For strength**: The hip hinge loads your glutes and hamstrings—the strongest muscles in your body. Using them instead of your back means more strength with less injury risk.

**For daily life**: Picking up children, groceries, and objects from the floor all require hip hinging. Do it wrong and you'll pay for it over time.

### Hip Hinge vs. Squat

Both are essential, but they're different:

**Squat**: Knees bend significantly, torso stays more upright, load distributed across quads, glutes, and posterior chain.

**Hip hinge**: Knees bend slightly, hips push far back, torso angles forward, load primarily on glutes and hamstrings.

Many exercises combine both patterns, but the pure hip hinge is a distinct skill.

## Anatomy of the Hip Hinge

### Primary Muscles

- **Glutes**: Hip extensors—the main engine
- **Hamstrings**: Assist hip extension, control descent
- **Erector spinae**: Maintain neutral spine (isometric)

### Supporting Muscles

- **Core**: Braces to protect spine
- **Lats**: Help maintain back position
- **Grip**: Holds whatever you're lifting

### The Posterior Chain

"Posterior chain" refers to the muscles along your backside: glutes, hamstrings, erectors, lats. The hip hinge is the primary movement pattern for developing this entire chain.

## Learning the Hip Hinge

### The Wall Drill

Start here if you're new to hinging:

1. Stand 6 inches from a wall, facing away
2. Feet shoulder-width, slight knee bend
3. Push your hips back until your glutes touch the wall
4. Your torso will angle forward naturally
5. Return by squeezing glutes to stand
6. Repeat 10-15 times

Progress by moving further from the wall. Eventually, you won't need the wall.

### The Dowel Drill

1. Hold a dowel or broomstick behind you, vertically along spine
2. One hand at lower back, one at back of head
3. Dowel should touch your head, upper back, and tailbone
4. Hip hinge while maintaining all three contact points
5. If the dowel loses contact, your spine moved
6. Practice until you can hinge deeply with all contacts maintained

### The RDL Stance

Once you understand the pattern:

1. Feet hip-width apart
2. Slight knee bend (soft knees, not locked, not squatting)
3. Weight in midfoot to heels
4. Arms hanging naturally
5. Core braced, shoulders back

## Bodyweight Hip Hinge Progressions

### Good Morning (Bodyweight)

1. Stand with hands behind head or crossed at chest
2. Hip hinge with control
3. Lower until torso is near parallel to floor (or hamstring limit)
4. Squeeze glutes to return
5. 12-15 reps

Great for learning without load.

### Single-Leg RDL (Bodyweight)

1. Stand on one leg
2. Hip hinge while extending other leg behind
3. Keep hips square (don't rotate)
4. Lower until hamstring tension limits you
5. Squeeze glutes to return
6. 8-10 per leg

This is challenging—excellent for balance and hip stability.

### Hip Hinge with Reach

1. Hip hinge with arms reaching toward floor
2. Touch the floor if flexibility allows
3. Focus on hamstring stretch at bottom
4. Squeeze glutes to stand
5. 10-12 reps

## The Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

### Standard RDL Technique

**Setup**:
1. Hold barbell with overhand grip, hands just outside hips
2. Stand tall, feet hip-width
3. Slight knee bend, core braced

**Descent**:
1. Push hips back (don't just bend forward)
2. Bar slides down thighs, staying close to body
3. Lower until hamstring tension stops you (typically mid-shin to floor)
4. Maintain neutral spine throughout
5. Feel stretch in hamstrings

**Ascent**:
1. Drive through heels
2. Squeeze glutes to extend hips
3. Bar slides up thighs
4. Stand tall, full hip extension at top

### Critical Cues

- **"Push hips back, not bend forward"**: Hips initiate, torso follows
- **"Bar on legs"**: Keep the bar touching your thighs throughout
- **"Soft knees"**: Slight bend, but it doesn't change during the movement
- **"Long spine"**: Neutral from head to tailbone
- **"Squeeze to stand"**: Glutes drive the return

### Common RDL Mistakes

**Rounding the back**: Indicates going too deep or too heavy
**Squatting down**: Too much knee bend makes it a deadlift, not RDL
**Bar drifting forward**: Stresses lower back; keep bar on legs
**Looking up**: Hyperextends neck; keep head neutral
**Locking knees**: Creates hamstring strain; maintain soft bend

## RDL Variations

### Dumbbell RDL

1. Dumbbell in each hand at sides
2. Same hip hinge pattern
3. Dumbbells travel alongside legs
4. 10-12 reps

Good for beginners or when barbells aren't available.

### Kettlebell RDL

1. Single or double kettlebell(s)
2. Held between legs or at sides
3. Same pattern
4. 10-12 reps

The handle position allows deep range of motion.

### Single-Leg RDL (Loaded)

1. Weight in one or both hands
2. Stand on one leg
3. Hip hinge while opposite leg extends back
4. Keep hips square—no rotation
5. 8-10 per leg

Significantly more challenging. Build balance first with bodyweight.

### B-Stance RDL

1. One foot flat (working leg)
2. Other foot kickstand position (heel up, just for balance)
3. ~80% weight on front leg
4. Hip hinge pattern
5. 10-12 per leg

Bridges the gap between bilateral and single-leg.

### Deficit RDL

1. Stand on small platform (1-3 inches)
2. Standard RDL pattern
3. Increased range of motion
4. Greater hamstring stretch
5. 8-10 reps

Only for those with excellent mobility and no back issues.

### Snatch-Grip RDL

1. Wide grip (snatch width) on barbell
2. Standard RDL pattern
3. Increases upper back and lat demand
4. Greater range of motion
5. 8-10 reps

### Paused RDL

1. Standard RDL
2. Pause 2-3 seconds at bottom
3. Eliminates stretch reflex
4. Builds strength at weakest point
5. 6-8 reps

## Stiff-Leg Deadlift vs. RDL

These terms are often confused:

**Romanian Deadlift (RDL)**:
- Starts from top (standing)
- Slight knee bend maintained
- Bar stays on legs
- Lower until hamstring tension limits you
- Never touches floor between reps

**Stiff-Leg Deadlift (SLDL)**:
- Can start from floor
- Legs straighter (but not locked)
- Bar may travel slightly forward
- Often deeper range of motion
- Higher hamstring and low back stress

**Recommendation**: The RDL is safer and sufficient for most people. Reserve SLDL for advanced trainees with excellent mobility.

## Programming the RDL

### For Muscle Building

- 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
- Moderate load (60-75% of deadlift max)
- Controlled tempo (3 seconds down, 1 second up)
- 2-3 times per week

### For Strength

- 4-5 sets of 5-8 reps
- Heavier load (70-85% of deadlift max)
- Full recovery between sets
- 1-2 times per week

### For Deadlift Assistance

- After main deadlift work
- 3 sets of 8-10 reps
- Focus on position and hamstring tension
- Addresses weakness off the floor

### For Hamstring Focus

- RDL as primary exercise
- Add pause variations
- Include single-leg work
- Higher rep ranges (10-15) for hypertrophy

## Level 1: Foundation (Week 1-2)

### 1A: Wall Hip Hinge Drill

10-15 reps, 2-3 sets, daily if possible

### 1B: Dowel Good Morning

10-12 reps with perfect contact points, 2-3 sets

### 1C: Bodyweight Good Morning

12-15 reps, 3 sets, focus on hamstring stretch

## Level 2: Light Loading (Week 3-4)

### 2A: Dumbbell RDL

Light weight, 12-15 reps, 3 sets, perfect form

### 2B: Kettlebell RDL

Moderate weight, 10-12 reps, 3 sets

### 2C: Barbell RDL (Empty Bar or Light)

Focus on bar path on legs, 10-12 reps, 3 sets

## Level 3: Building Load (Week 5-8)

### 3A: Barbell RDL

Progressive loading, 8-10 reps, 3-4 sets

### 3B: B-Stance RDL

Introduction to single-leg pattern, 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

### 3C: Dumbbell Single-Leg RDL

Light load, focus on balance, 8-10 per leg, 2-3 sets

## Level 4: Advanced Variations (Week 9+)

### 4A: Heavy Barbell RDL

Near-max loading, 5-8 reps, 4-5 sets

### 4B: Single-Leg RDL (Loaded)

Challenging load, 8-10 per leg, 3-4 sets

### 4C: Deficit or Paused RDL

Specialized work, 6-8 reps, 3 sets

## Hip Hinge in Daily Life

The gym is practice. Real life is the test.

### Picking Things Up

1. Get close to the object
2. Hip hinge to grip it
3. Brace core
4. Squeeze glutes to stand
5. Keep object close to body

### Lifting From Floor

For very low objects:
1. Hinge + slight squat hybrid
2. Whatever allows neutral spine
3. Never round your back under load

### Repetitive Tasks

Gardening, cleaning, organizing:
1. Use the hinge for every bend
2. Switch to kneeling for prolonged low work
3. Take breaks to extend (stand tall, hands on hips)

## Flexibility Requirements

### Hamstring Mobility

The hip hinge requires sufficient hamstring length. If you can't hinge without rounding your back, work on:

- Standing hamstring stretches
- Lying hamstring stretches
- Active leg lowering drills

### Hip Mobility

Hip flexors and adductors can limit hinge depth:

- Hip flexor stretches
- 90-90 hip switches
- Deep squat holds

## Common Problems and Fixes

### Problem: Can't Keep Spine Neutral

**Causes**: Tight hamstrings, weak back extensors, poor body awareness

**Fixes**: 
- Limit range of motion to where you can stay neutral
- Work on hamstring flexibility separately
- Use dowel drill for feedback
- Strengthen back with supermans and back extensions

### Problem: Knees Bending Too Much

**Causes**: Squatting pattern dominance, not understanding the hinge

**Fixes**:
- Wall drill to learn hip initiation
- Cue "push hips back first"
- Keep shins more vertical

### Problem: Weight Shifts to Toes

**Causes**: Not pushing hips back enough, bar too far forward

**Fixes**:
- Cue "weight in heels"
- Think "push hips toward wall behind you"
- Keep bar on legs

### Problem: Lower Back Pain

**Causes**: Rounding spine, going too heavy, insufficient core bracing

**Fixes**:
- Reduce load immediately
- Limit range of motion
- Focus on core bracing before each rep
- May need to address underlying issues

## The Hip Hinge and Back Pain

Many people with back pain avoid hip hinging—but that's backward. Learning to hinge correctly is often part of the solution.

**Why**: Proper hinging uses glutes and hamstrings, sparing the lower back. It teaches your body the safe movement pattern.

**How to start**:
1. Wall drills with no load
2. Bodyweight good mornings
3. Very light RDLs
4. Progress slowly as confidence builds

**When to get help**: If pain persists or worsens, consult a physical therapist who understands strength training.

## The Bottom Line

The hip hinge is non-negotiable. Every human needs to know how to bend forward safely under load. The RDL is the primary exercise for building this pattern with strength.

Start with bodyweight drills. Master position before adding load. Progress the RDL systematically. Your back will thank you, and your posterior chain will grow.

## Quick Reference

**Hip Hinge**: Push hips back, maintain neutral spine, slight knee bend

**RDL Setup**: Hip-width stance, soft knees, core braced

**RDL Descent**: Hips push back, bar on legs, lower until hamstring tension

**RDL Ascent**: Drive through heels, squeeze glutes to stand

**Cues**: "Hips back," "bar on legs," "long spine," "squeeze to stand"

**Loading**: Start light (just bar), progress 5-10% per week as form allows
