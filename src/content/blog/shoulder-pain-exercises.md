Shoulder pain is frustratingly common, especially among people who lift weights, work at desks, or have physically demanding jobs. The shoulder is incredibly mobile, but that mobility comes at the cost of stability—making it vulnerable to various problems.

The right exercises can often reduce shoulder pain and prevent it from returning. Here's what to do.

## Understanding Shoulder Pain

Before diving into exercises, understand that "shoulder pain" can have many causes:

**Common culprits:**
- Rotator cuff irritation or tendinitis
- Shoulder impingement
- Muscle imbalances (tight front, weak back)
- Poor posture (rounded shoulders)
- Biceps tendinitis
- AC joint problems
- Referred pain from neck

**When to see a professional:**
- Pain after a specific injury or fall
- Severe pain that doesn't improve with rest
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in arm or hand
- Pain that wakes you from sleep consistently
- Visible deformity
- Pain lasting more than 2-3 weeks despite self-care

The exercises below help with general shoulder tightness, mild impingement, and muscle imbalances. They're not a substitute for medical evaluation of significant injuries.

## Mobility and Stretching Exercises

### Doorway Pec Stretch

Tight pecs pull shoulders forward, contributing to impingement and poor posture.

1. Stand in a doorway, forearm against the frame
2. Elbow at shoulder height or slightly below
3. Step through with the same-side foot
4. Rotate body away until you feel chest stretch
5. Hold 30-45 seconds each side
6. Repeat with arm higher and lower to target different pec fibers

### Cross-Body Shoulder Stretch

Stretches the posterior shoulder capsule.

1. Bring one arm across your body at shoulder height
2. Use the other hand to gently pull the arm closer to your chest
3. Keep the stretched arm straight
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Note:** If this causes pinching at the front of your shoulder, skip it—you may have impingement that this exacerbates.

### Sleeper Stretch

Targets the posterior capsule, often tight in those with shoulder problems.

1. Lie on your side with the affected shoulder down
2. Affected arm straight out at 90 degrees from body
3. Bend elbow to 90 degrees (forearm pointing up)
4. Use your other hand to gently push the forearm toward the floor
5. Hold 30 seconds, gentle pressure only

**Caution:** Be very gentle—this is a delicate stretch.

### Thread the Needle

Opens up the thoracic spine and stretches the shoulder.

1. Start on hands and knees
2. Reach one arm under your body, rotating your thoracic spine
3. Let your shoulder lower toward the floor
4. Hold 20-30 seconds, then rotate the other direction
5. Do 5-8 per side

### Lat Stretch

Tight lats can limit overhead mobility and contribute to shoulder issues.

1. Kneel in front of a bench or sturdy surface
2. Place one arm on the bench, palm down
3. Sit your hips back toward your heels
4. Let your chest drop toward the floor
5. Hold 30-45 seconds each side

## Strengthening Exercises

### Face Pulls

Essential for rear deltoids and external rotators—often weak in those with shoulder pain.

1. Set cable at face height with rope attachment
2. Pull toward your face, spreading hands apart at the end
3. Finish with hands beside ears, elbows high
4. Squeeze rear shoulders and upper back
5. Do 15-20 reps with light weight

**Key:** Light weight, high reps, focus on the squeeze.

### Band Pull-Aparts

Strengthens mid-back and rear delts, counteracting forward shoulder posture.

1. Hold a resistance band in front of you, arms extended
2. Pull band apart until it touches your chest
3. Squeeze shoulder blades together at the end
4. Control the return
5. Do 15-20 reps

### External Rotation with Band

Directly strengthens the external rotators of the rotator cuff.

1. Attach band at elbow height, stand sideways to anchor
2. Elbow bent 90 degrees, upper arm at your side
3. Rotate forearm away from your body against band resistance
4. Control the return
5. Do 15-20 reps each side

**Variation:** Lying external rotation with light dumbbell.

### Internal Rotation with Band

Strengthens the internal rotators (subscapularis).

1. Same setup, but rotate forearm toward your body
2. Keep elbow pinned to your side
3. Do 15-20 reps each side

### Prone Y-T-W Raises

Strengthens lower traps, rhomboids, and rear delts—critical for shoulder health.

1. Lie face down on the floor or an incline bench
2. **Y:** Arms overhead at 45-degree angles, thumbs up, lift and hold 5 seconds
3. **T:** Arms straight out to sides, thumbs up, lift and hold 5 seconds
4. **W:** Elbows bent, pull toward hips while squeezing shoulder blades, hold 5 seconds
5. Do 8-10 of each position with no weight or very light weights (1-3 lbs)

### Scapular Push-Ups

Teaches scapular control, often lacking in those with shoulder problems.

1. Get in push-up position (or on knees)
2. Without bending elbows, let your chest sink between your shoulder blades
3. Then push through your hands to spread your shoulder blades apart
4. Only the shoulder blades move—arms stay straight
5. Do 15-20 reps

### Wall Slides

Improves overhead mobility and scapular control.

1. Stand with back against wall, feet a few inches away
2. Arms in "goalpost" position (elbows bent 90°) against wall
3. Slowly slide arms up the wall, keeping contact
4. Go as high as you can while maintaining wall contact
5. Lower with control
6. Do 10-15 reps

## Exercises to Be Careful With

When you have shoulder pain, some exercises tend to aggravate it:

### Behind-the-Neck Press/Pulldown

Puts the shoulder in a vulnerable position. Switch to front versions.

### Upright Rows

Often causes impingement. Replace with face pulls or lateral raises.

### Dips (Full Range)

Deep dips stress the anterior shoulder. Limit depth or avoid until pain-free.

### Bench Press (Very Wide Grip)

Excessively wide grip increases shoulder stress. Use a moderate grip width.

### Heavy Overhead Work

Scale back overhead pressing weight if it causes pain. Focus on high-rep rehab work instead.

## Daily Routine for Shoulder Pain

**Morning (5 minutes):**
- Doorway pec stretch: 30 seconds each side
- Thread the needle: 5 each side
- Arm circles: 10 each direction
- Scapular push-ups: 15 reps

**Before Upper Body Training (5 minutes):**
- Band pull-aparts: 20 reps
- External rotation with band: 15 each side
- Face pulls: 15 reps (light)
- Wall slides: 10 reps

**After Training or Evening (10 minutes):**
- Doorway pec stretch: 45 seconds each side (multiple angles)
- Lat stretch: 30 seconds each side
- Cross-body stretch: 30 seconds each side (if tolerated)
- Sleeper stretch: 30 seconds each side (gentle)
- Band pull-aparts: 20 reps
- Prone Y-T-W: 10 each position
- Face pulls: 20 reps

## Training Modifications

While managing shoulder pain, modify your training:

### Pressing Movements

- Use neutral grip (palms facing each other) when possible
- Lower the weight and increase reps
- Limit range of motion if deep positions cause pain
- Try floor press instead of bench press
- Reduce volume—fewer sets per week

### Pulling Movements

- Neutral grip pull-ups/pulldowns often feel better
- Focus on rowing movements (usually well-tolerated)
- Avoid extreme ranges of motion

### General Principles

- Pain during an exercise = stop or modify
- Mild discomfort that doesn't worsen = usually okay
- Pain after exercise that persists = too much

## Posture and Habits

Exercise alone won't fix shoulder pain if your daily habits perpetuate the problem.

### Workstation Setup

- Monitor at eye level
- Keyboard and mouse at elbow height
- Chair supporting upright posture
- Regular breaks to move

### Phone Use

- Raise phone to eye level
- Avoid sustained overhead reaching
- Take breaks from prolonged use

### Sleeping Position

- Avoid sleeping directly on the painful shoulder
- Side sleepers: pillow between arms
- Back sleepers: pillow under arms for support

### Posture Throughout the Day

- Shoulders back and down, not forward and elevated
- Ears over shoulders, not in front
- Regular posture checks until it becomes habit

## Timeline for Improvement

With consistent work:

**Week 1-2:** May feel worse initially as you work muscles that have been neglected. This is normal if it's muscle soreness, not sharp pain.

**Week 3-4:** Mobility improvements often appear first. Some reduction in pain during daily activities.

**Week 6-8:** Noticeable improvement in pain levels and function for most people with muscle imbalance issues.

**3+ months:** Continued strengthening and habit changes create lasting improvement.

Be patient. Shoulders are complex and don't heal overnight. Consistent, daily attention to these exercises produces results.

## When It's Not Getting Better

If shoulder pain persists despite 4-6 weeks of consistent exercise:

1. **See a professional:** Physical therapist, sports medicine doctor, or orthopedist can provide proper diagnosis
2. **Get imaging if recommended:** MRI or X-ray may reveal issues that need specific treatment
3. **Consider manual therapy:** Massage, dry needling, or other hands-on treatment can complement exercises
4. **Rule out other causes:** Neck problems can refer pain to the shoulder

Don't push through worsening pain or pain that doesn't respond to conservative treatment. Some shoulder problems require professional intervention.

## The Bottom Line

Most shoulder pain improves with:

1. **Stretching** tight muscles (pecs, lats, posterior capsule)
2. **Strengthening** weak muscles (rotator cuff, lower traps, rear delts)
3. **Improving** posture and daily habits
4. **Modifying** training to avoid aggravating movements
5. **Being patient** and consistent

The exercises in this guide address the most common causes of non-traumatic shoulder pain. Done consistently, they work for many people.

Your shoulders are built to move. Give them the strength and mobility they need, and they'll serve you well.
