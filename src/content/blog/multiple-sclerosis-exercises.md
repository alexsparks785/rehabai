# Multiple Sclerosis Exercises: Staying Active with MS

Exercise was once thought risky for people with multiple sclerosis—the concern was that it might worsen symptoms or trigger relapses. We now know the opposite is true: regular exercise is one of the most beneficial things you can do for managing MS. It improves strength, balance, fatigue, mood, and quality of life, and there's no evidence it increases relapse risk.

## Understanding MS and Exercise

Multiple sclerosis is an autoimmune disease affecting the central nervous system. It can cause:
- Fatigue (the most common symptom)
- Weakness
- Numbness or tingling
- Balance and coordination problems
- Spasticity (muscle stiffness)
- Heat sensitivity
- Cognitive changes

Symptoms vary widely between individuals and can fluctuate over time.

## Benefits of Exercise for MS

Research consistently shows exercise:
- **Improves walking ability** and gait quality
- **Increases strength** in affected and unaffected limbs
- **Reduces fatigue** (counterintuitive but well-documented)
- **Improves balance** and reduces fall risk
- **Enhances mood** and reduces depression
- **May slow disease progression** (emerging research)
- **Improves cognitive function** in some studies
- **Increases overall quality of life**

## Exercise Principles for MS

### Start Low, Go Slow
Begin with less than you think you can do. It's easier to add more than to recover from overdoing it.

### Listen to Your Body
MS symptoms fluctuate. Adjust exercise based on how you feel each day.

### Manage Heat
Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon) is common. Cooling strategies allow safe exercise.

### Rest and Recovery
Recovery time may be longer than for people without MS. Build rest into your program.

### Consistency Matters
Regular, moderate exercise provides better results than sporadic intense workouts.

## Managing Heat Sensitivity

Heat can temporarily worsen MS symptoms. Strategies include:
- Exercise in air-conditioned environments
- Use cooling vests, neck wraps, or cooling towels
- Exercise in water (pool temperature 80-84°F/27-29°C ideal)
- Exercise during cooler parts of the day
- Have cold water available to drink
- Use fans
- Take cool showers before or after exercise

Remember: Heat-related symptom changes are temporary and don't indicate damage.

## Managing Fatigue

MS fatigue is different from normal tiredness. Strategies:
- **Exercise in the morning** when energy is typically highest
- **Shorter, more frequent sessions** (10-15 minutes multiple times daily vs. one long session)
- **Pace yourself** — don't push to exhaustion
- **Rest before you're exhausted** — conserve energy throughout the day
- **Aerobic exercise actually helps** — though it seems counterintuitive

## Types of Exercise for MS

### 1. Aerobic Exercise

Cardiovascular exercise improves endurance, reduces fatigue, and benefits heart and lung health.

**Good options:**
- Walking (with or without assistive device)
- Stationary cycling (recumbent is often easier)
- Swimming or water aerobics (temperature-controlled pool)
- Elliptical trainer
- Arm ergometer (if leg weakness is significant)

**Guidelines:**
- Start with 10-15 minutes
- Progress to 20-30 minutes, 3-5 times per week
- Moderate intensity (somewhat hard, but can still talk)
- Adapt based on daily symptoms

### 2. Strength Training

Maintaining muscle strength helps with mobility and daily activities.

**Upper Body:**

*Seated Rows (with band):*
1. Sit with band around feet
2. Pull handles toward waist
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 10-15 reps

*Wall Push-Ups:*
1. Stand arm's length from wall
2. Lean in, bend elbows
3. Push back
4. 10-15 reps

*Bicep Curls:*
1. Hold light weights or band
2. Curl toward shoulders
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps

**Lower Body:**

*Sit-to-Stand:*
1. Sit at edge of chair
2. Stand up without using arms (if possible)
3. Sit with control
4. 10-15 reps

*Seated Leg Extensions:*
1. Sit in chair
2. Straighten one leg
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps each leg

*Standing Hip Abduction:*
1. Hold onto support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg

*Heel Raises:*
1. Hold support
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 reps

### 3. Balance Training

Balance problems are common in MS and significantly increase fall risk.

**Static Balance Progression:**
1. Stand with feet together, eyes open (use support as needed)
2. Stand with feet together, eyes closed
3. Tandem stance (heel to toe)
4. Single-leg stance

Hold each position 10-30 seconds, progress to less support.

**Dynamic Balance:**

*Weight Shifts:*
1. Stand with support nearby
2. Shift weight side to side
3. Shift forward to backward
4. 1-2 minutes

*Step and Stop:*
1. Take a step forward
2. Stop in balanced position
3. Step back
4. Repeat in all directions

*Obstacle Negotiation:*
1. Practice stepping over objects
2. Vary heights as able

### 4. Flexibility and Stretching

Spasticity and stiffness are common. Daily stretching helps maintain mobility.

**Hamstring Stretch:**
1. Lie on back
2. Use strap around foot
3. Straighten leg toward ceiling
4. Hold 30 seconds each leg

**Calf Stretch:**
1. Stand facing wall
2. Step one foot back
3. Keep heel down, lean forward
4. Hold 30 seconds each leg

**Hip Flexor Stretch:**
1. Half-kneeling (use support)
2. Tuck pelvis under
3. Lean forward gently
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Chest and Shoulder Stretch:**
1. Clasp hands behind back
2. Lift arms slightly
3. Hold 30 seconds

**Spasticity-Specific:**
If certain muscles are particularly tight or spastic, hold stretches longer (60+ seconds) and repeat more frequently.

### 5. Aquatic Exercise

Water exercise is particularly beneficial for MS:
- Cooling effect (appropriate temperature pool)
- Buoyancy reduces fall risk
- Less stress on joints
- Resistance for strengthening
- Often more comfortable than land exercise

**Pool Temperature:** 80-84°F (27-29°C) ideal—warm enough for comfort, cool enough to prevent overheating.

## Sample Weekly Schedule

**Daily (5-10 minutes):**
- Stretching routine
- Brief balance practice

**3-5x per Week (15-30 minutes):**
- Aerobic exercise (walking, cycling, swimming)

**2-3x per Week (15-20 minutes):**
- Strength training (alternate upper/lower body days if easier)

**Adjust based on:**
- Daily symptom level
- Energy availability
- Recovery needs

## Exercise During Relapses

During an acute relapse:
- Reduce exercise intensity and duration significantly
- Focus on gentle stretching and range of motion
- Rest is important for recovery
- Resume exercise gradually as relapse resolves
- Consult your medical team about appropriate activity

## Working with Professionals

Consider working with:
- **Physical therapist**: Especially one familiar with MS for individualized program
- **Occupational therapist**: For adapting daily activities
- **Exercise physiologist**: For fitness programming
- **MS specialist**: For overall disease management

Many MS centers offer specialized exercise programs.

## Adaptive Equipment

Depending on your needs, consider:
- Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) for foot drop
- Walking aids (canes, walkers) for balance
- Recumbent bikes or arm ergometers
- Pool noodles or flotation devices for water exercise
- Seated exercise options
- Resistance bands (easier to control than free weights)

## Signs to Modify or Stop

**Modify exercise if:**
- Unusual fatigue that doesn't resolve with rest
- Symptoms temporarily worsen (wait for them to settle)
- Heat-related symptom changes
- Feeling "off" that day

**Stop and consult healthcare provider if:**
- New symptoms develop
- Significant worsening of existing symptoms
- Symptoms don't return to baseline after exercise
- Signs of relapse

## Key Takeaways

1. **Exercise is safe and beneficial** for people with MS—it doesn't increase relapse risk
2. **Fatigue often improves** with regular exercise, despite seeming counterintuitive
3. **Manage heat** with cooling strategies, cool environments, and water exercise
4. **Listen to your body** and adjust based on daily symptoms
5. **Consistency beats intensity** — regular moderate exercise works best
6. **Balance training is essential** — reduces fall risk significantly
7. **Aquatic exercise is excellent** — cooling, supportive, and effective
8. **Start slowly and progress gradually** — you can always do more later

MS doesn't mean you have to stop moving. With appropriate modifications and consistent effort, exercise can significantly improve your function, energy, and quality of life.
