The deadlift is arguably the most functional exercise there is—you're literally picking weight up off the ground. It builds total body strength like nothing else: back, legs, grip, core, and even shoulders all work together.

But deadlifts have a reputation for being dangerous. This reputation is undeserved when performed correctly. The key is learning proper technique before chasing heavy weights.

Here's everything you need to deadlift safely and effectively.

## Why Deadlift?

### Builds Total Body Strength

The deadlift works more muscles than almost any other exercise:
- **Posterior chain:** Glutes, hamstrings, lower back
- **Back:** Lats, traps, rhomboids
- **Legs:** Quads (off the floor)
- **Core:** Entire midsection stabilizes the spine
- **Grip:** Forearms and hands

### Functional Carryover

Picking things up off the ground is a fundamental human movement. Deadlift strength transfers directly to daily life.

### Hormonal Response

Heavy compound movements like deadlifts trigger the release of testosterone and growth hormone, supporting muscle building and fat loss.

### Posture and Back Health

Despite fears about back injury, deadlifts actually strengthen the muscles that protect the spine—when done correctly with appropriate weight.

## The Conventional Deadlift Setup

Proper setup is 90% of a good deadlift.

### Stance

- Feet roughly hip-width apart (narrower than squat stance)
- Toes pointing forward or slightly out (up to 15 degrees)
- Bar over mid-foot (about 1 inch from shins when viewed from above)

### Grip

- Hands just outside your legs
- Double overhand grip for lighter weights
- Mixed grip (one palm forward, one back) or hook grip for heavier weights
- Arms straight—no bending elbows

### The Hinge

1. Push your hips back (like closing a car door with your butt)
2. Bend your knees until your shins touch the bar
3. Shins should be vertical or nearly vertical
4. Keep chest up and back flat

### Setting the Back

1. Take a deep breath into your belly
2. Brace your core (like preparing for a punch)
3. Pull your shoulder blades down and back
4. Create tension through your lats (protect your armpits)
5. Your back should be flat—neutral spine, no rounding

### Head Position

Look at a spot on the floor about 10-15 feet in front of you. Keep your neck neutral with your spine—don't crane up or tuck chin excessively.

## Executing the Lift

### The Pull

1. Drive through your whole foot (not just heels or toes)
2. Push the floor away rather than pulling the bar up
3. Keep the bar close to your body—it should drag up your shins and thighs
4. Hips and shoulders rise at the same rate
5. Once the bar passes your knees, drive your hips forward
6. Stand tall at the top—fully extend hips without hyperextending lower back
7. Squeeze your glutes at lockout

### The Descent

1. Push hips back first (hip hinge)
2. Once bar passes knees, bend knees to lower bar to floor
3. Control the descent—don't just drop the weight
4. Reset completely before the next rep (no bouncing)

### Breathing

For lighter weights: Inhale during descent, exhale during ascent

For heavier weights: Take a big breath at the top, brace, hold throughout the rep, exhale at lockout

## Common Deadlift Mistakes

### Rounding the Lower Back

**The problem:** Spine flexes under load, risking disc injury.

**The cause:** Insufficient bracing, weak back, hips rising too fast, weight too heavy.

**The fix:** Brace harder before each rep. Strengthen your back with lighter deadlifts and accessory work. Drop weight if you can't maintain a flat back.

### Bar Drifting Away from Body

**The problem:** Bar swings forward, increasing lower back stress and making the lift harder.

**The cause:** Not engaging lats, starting with bar too far from shins.

**The fix:** Keep bar touching your body throughout. "Protect your armpits" by engaging lats. Start with bar over mid-foot.

### Hips Rising First (Stiff-Leg Start)

**The problem:** Hips shoot up before bar moves, turning the lift into a back exercise.

**The cause:** Weak quads, not pushing through legs, improper setup.

**The fix:** Drive through the floor with legs. Think "leg press" the ground away initially. Strengthen quads.

### Hyperextending at Lockout

**The problem:** Leaning back excessively at the top, stressing the lower back.

**The cause:** Trying to show complete lockout, weak glutes.

**The fix:** Stand tall but don't lean back. Squeeze glutes to finish the hip extension.

### Bouncing Reps

**The problem:** Using momentum instead of resetting, increasing injury risk.

**The cause:** Fatigue, trying to get more reps, improper breathing.

**The fix:** Dead stop each rep. Reset your breath and brace. Each rep starts from a dead stop.

### Looking Up

**The problem:** Cranking neck back, causing neck strain and misalignment.

**The cause:** Misguided cue to "keep chest up."

**The fix:** Keep neck neutral with spine. Look at floor 10-15 feet ahead.

### Shrugging at the Top

**The problem:** Shoulders elevate at lockout, wasting energy and missing the point.

**The cause:** Misunderstanding of lockout position.

**The fix:** Shoulders stay down and back. Don't shrug—just stand tall with glutes squeezed.

## Deadlift Variations

### Sumo Deadlift

Wide stance with hands inside the legs.

**Benefits:** Shorter range of motion, more upright torso, less lower back stress
**Best for:** Those with long torsos, hip mobility that allows wide stance, lifters who struggle with conventional

**Setup differences:**
- Feet wide (pointing out 30-45 degrees)
- Grip inside legs
- More upright torso
- Push knees out over toes

### Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

Starts from standing, emphasizes the eccentric.

**Benefits:** Excellent hamstring and glute builder, teaches hip hinge
**Best for:** Accessory work, hamstring development, learning the hip hinge pattern

**Execution:**
- Start standing with weight
- Push hips back, lowering weight along legs
- Keep knees slightly bent (but don't bend more as you descend)
- Go until you feel hamstring stretch (usually mid-shin)
- Drive hips forward to stand

### Trap Bar Deadlift

Uses a hexagonal bar you stand inside.

**Benefits:** More quad involvement, easier on lower back, more natural grip position
**Best for:** Beginners, those with back issues, athletes focused on leg development

### Deficit Deadlift

Standing on a small platform (1-3 inches).

**Benefits:** Increased range of motion, builds strength off the floor
**Best for:** Intermediate/advanced lifters working on weak point

### Rack Pull

Bar starts elevated on pins or blocks.

**Benefits:** Allows heavier loading, focuses on lockout strength
**Best for:** Working on lockout weakness, back strength, grip training

## Programming Deadlifts

### For Beginners

**Frequency:** 1-2 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 3-4 sets of 5-8 reps
**Focus:** Perfect form with moderate weight, gradual progression

### For Strength

**Frequency:** 1-2 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 4-6 sets of 1-5 reps
**Focus:** Heavy weight with good form, full recovery between sets

### For Muscle Building

**Frequency:** 1-2 times per week
**Sets/Reps:** 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps
**Focus:** Moderate weight, controlled tempo, feel the muscles working

### Recovery Considerations

Deadlifts are highly fatiguing. They require more recovery than most exercises. If you're squatting heavy twice a week, once-weekly deadlifts may be sufficient.

## Grip Options

### Double Overhand

Both palms facing you. Best for developing grip strength. Limited by grip on heavier weights.

### Mixed Grip

One palm forward, one back. Prevents bar rolling. Can create asymmetric loading—alternate which hand is over/under, or use for max attempts only.

### Hook Grip

Thumb wrapped around bar, fingers over thumb. Very secure, but painful until you adapt. Used in Olympic lifting.

### Straps

Fabric straps wrap around bar. Removes grip as a limiting factor. Good for high-rep work or when training back without grip interference.

**Recommendation:** Use double overhand as long as possible. Switch to mixed grip or straps only when grip limits the lift. This builds grip strength over time.

## Deadlift Safety

### Use Appropriate Weight

Your back should stay flat throughout the lift. If it rounds under load, the weight is too heavy—regardless of what your ego says.

### Don't Train to Failure

Deadlift failure is ugly and potentially dangerous. Leave 1-2 reps in reserve on most sets.

### Rest Adequately

Take 2-3+ minutes between heavy sets. You need full recovery for safe technique.

### Know When to Bail

If you feel your back rounding during a rep, abort. Either lower the weight controlled or drop it if needed.

### Film Yourself

You can't see your own back during deadlifts. Video review helps identify form issues before they cause problems.

## Building Your Deadlift

### Accessory Work

Strengthen the muscles involved:
- **Romanian deadlifts:** Hamstrings and hip hinge pattern
- **Barbell rows:** Upper back
- **Planks and carries:** Core stability
- **Hip thrusts:** Glutes
- **Farmer's carries:** Grip

### Mobility Work

If you can't get into a good starting position, work on:
- Hamstring flexibility
- Hip mobility
- Thoracic extension

### Progressive Overload

Add weight gradually—2.5-5 lbs per session when possible. Small, consistent increases build big totals over time.

## The Bottom Line

The deadlift is a foundational movement that builds total body strength:

1. **Set up correctly:** Bar over mid-foot, flat back, braced core
2. **Execute properly:** Push floor away, keep bar close, hips and shoulders rise together
3. **Avoid common mistakes:** No rounding, no bouncing, no hyperextending
4. **Progress gradually:** Form first, weight second

Deadlifts aren't dangerous—poor technique with too much weight is dangerous. Master the basics with moderate loads before chasing heavy singles.

Build your deadlift properly and it becomes one of the safest, most effective exercises you can do.
