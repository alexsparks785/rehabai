# Hip Labral Tear Exercises: Conservative Treatment and Post-Surgical Rehab

Hip labral tears have become increasingly recognized as a source of hip pain, especially in active adults. The good news: many labral tears can be managed conservatively, and even surgical repairs have excellent outcomes with proper rehabilitation. Understanding the right exercises—and which to avoid—is essential for recovery.

## Understanding Hip Labral Tears

The labrum is a ring of cartilage that surrounds the hip socket (acetabulum), deepening it and providing stability. Tears can occur from:

- **Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI)**: Abnormal bone shapes cause pinching
- **Trauma**: Falls, accidents, or sudden twisting
- **Repetitive motion**: Sports with pivoting, squatting, or extreme hip motion
- **Degeneration**: Wear and tear over time
- **Hip dysplasia**: Shallow hip socket increases labral stress

**Common symptoms:**
- Groin pain (most common location)
- Clicking, catching, or locking
- Stiffness after sitting
- Pain with deep squatting or pivoting
- Limited hip motion
- Pain that worsens with activity

## Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

### Conservative Treatment May Work For:
- Smaller tears
- Minimal mechanical symptoms (catching, locking)
- Older patients or lower activity demands
- No significant FAI or structural issues
- Pain that responds to initial therapy

### Surgery May Be Needed For:
- Large tears with mechanical symptoms
- Significant FAI requiring bone reshaping
- Failed conservative treatment (3-6 months)
- High-level athletes requiring full hip function
- Substantial functional limitation

**Success rates:** Conservative treatment succeeds in 40-60% of cases. Surgical outcomes are generally good, with 85%+ returning to sport.

## Conservative Treatment Protocol

### Phase 1: Pain and Inflammation Control (Weeks 1-4)

**Activity modification:**
- Avoid deep hip flexion (>90°)
- Avoid pivoting and twisting
- Limit prolonged sitting
- Reduce or stop aggravating activities

**Gentle range of motion:**

*Pendulums (Hip):*
1. Stand holding support
2. Let affected leg swing gently
3. Forward/back, side to side
4. 2-3 minutes, several times daily

*Supine Hip Rotation (Gentle):*
1. Lie on back, knee bent
2. Let knee fall gently outward
3. Return to center
4. Let knee fall gently inward
5. Stay in pain-free range
6. 10-15 reps

*Heel Slides:*
1. Lie on back
2. Slide heel toward buttocks (don't force flexion)
3. Slide back out
4. 15-20 reps

**Avoid in this phase:**
- Deep squats or lunges
- Hip flexor stretching (can impinge labrum)
- Pivoting activities
- Impact exercise

### Phase 2: Stability and Strength (Weeks 4-8)

**Core Strengthening:**

*Transverse Abdominis Activation:*
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Draw belly button toward spine gently
3. Hold 10 seconds, breathe normally
4. 10-15 reps

*Dead Bug (Modified):*
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Engage core
3. Slowly lift one foot off floor
4. Lower, alternate
5. 10 reps each side

*Bridge:*
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10-15 reps

**Hip Strengthening:**

*Side-Lying Hip Abduction:*
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Lift top leg (keep it straight)
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 reps

*Clamshells:*
1. Lie on side, knees bent 45°
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 reps

*Prone Hip Extension:*
1. Lie face down
2. Lift one leg (keep knee straight)
3. Don't arch lower back
4. 10-15 reps each leg

*Standing Hip Abduction:*
1. Hold support
2. Lift affected leg to side
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps

### Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-12)

**Progress hip exercises with resistance bands or weights.**

*Monster Walks:*
1. Band around ankles
2. Slight squat position
3. Step sideways
4. 15 steps each direction

*Single-Leg Bridge:*
1. Lie on back, one leg extended
2. Bridge up on one leg
3. 10 reps each side

*Single-Leg Deadlift (Light):*
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Hinge forward, other leg extends back
3. Return to standing
4. 10 reps each side

*Lateral Step-Downs:*
1. Stand on step with affected leg
2. Lower other foot toward floor
3. Touch lightly, return up
4. 10-15 reps

**Limited squatting:**
- Shallow squats only (above 90° hip flexion)
- Focus on form, not depth
- Progress slowly

### Phase 4: Return to Activity (Weeks 12+)

**Criteria to progress:**
- Minimal or no pain with daily activities
- Good strength symmetry
- Full pain-free range of motion
- No catching or locking

**Gradual return:**
- Sport-specific movements at low intensity
- Progress intensity over 4-8 weeks
- Maintain strength program

## Post-Surgical Rehabilitation (Hip Arthroscopy)

### Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0-4)

**Weight bearing:** Often partial weight bearing with crutches (varies by surgery)

**Precautions:**
- Limit hip flexion based on surgeon's protocol
- Avoid active hip flexion initially
- No external rotation with flexion
- Use CPM machine if prescribed

**Exercises:**

*Ankle Pumps:*
- Frequently throughout day

*Quad Sets:*
1. Tighten thigh
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 20+ reps, multiple times daily

*Gluteal Squeezes:*
1. Squeeze buttocks together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 15-20 reps

*Passive Range of Motion:*
- Performed by therapist or with assistance
- Within prescribed limits

*Stationary Bike (When Cleared):*
- High seat, low resistance
- Focus on motion, not workout
- Usually starts week 2-3

### Phase 2: Progressive Motion (Weeks 4-8)

**Weight bearing:** Usually full by week 4-6

**Goals:**
- Restore normal gait (no limp)
- Progress range of motion
- Begin active hip strengthening

**Exercises:**

*Active Range of Motion:*
- Hip flexion, extension, rotation within limits
- Gradually increase range

*Bridges:*
- Progress to single-leg when able

*Side-Lying Hip Exercises:*
- Abduction, adduction
- Progress resistance

*Standing Exercises:*
- Hip abduction, extension
- Weight shifts
- Mini squats (shallow)

*Aquatic Exercise (if cleared):*
- Walking in pool
- Range of motion exercises

### Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 8-16)

**Goals:**
- Build hip and core strength
- Improve balance and proprioception
- Progress to functional activities

**Exercises:**

*Progressive Squats:*
- Increase depth as tolerated (discuss limits with surgeon)
- Add resistance gradually

*Lunges (Forward, Lateral):*
- Start with small range
- Progress depth and resistance

*Step-Ups:*
- Increase step height progressively
- Add resistance

*Single-Leg Balance:*
- Progress to unstable surfaces

*Hip Strengthening with Resistance:*
- All planes of motion
- Bands, cables, weights

### Phase 4: Return to Sport (Months 4-6+)

**Criteria:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength symmetry (>85% of other side)
- Normal movement patterns
- Clearance from surgeon

**Progression:**
- Sport-specific drills at 50% → 75% → 100%
- Gradual return to practice
- Full competition typically 4-6 months

## Exercises to Avoid or Modify

**Avoid (especially early):**
- Deep squats past 90°
- Aggressive hip flexor stretching
- Hip circles at end range
- Leg press with deep flexion
- Activities causing catching or clicking

**Modify:**
- Reduce squat depth
- Avoid prolonged sitting in deep flexion
- Use higher seats and raised toilet
- Avoid crossing legs

## Long-Term Hip Health

**Maintain:**
- Hip and core strength
- Flexibility (within safe ranges)
- Good movement patterns

**Consider:**
- Avoiding repeated deep hip flexion activities if problematic
- Modifying sports technique if FAI is present
- Ongoing strengthening program

## Sample Conservative Treatment Schedule

**Daily:**
- Gentle range of motion (5 minutes)
- Core activation exercises

**3-4x per week:**
- Hip strengthening routine (20-25 minutes)
- Low-impact cardio (cycling, swimming) (20-30 minutes)

**Progress based on:**
- Pain levels
- Function improvement
- Physical therapist guidance

## Key Takeaways

1. **Many labral tears respond to conservative treatment** — surgery isn't always needed
2. **Avoid deep hip flexion** — especially early in treatment
3. **Hip and core strength are essential** — support the joint
4. **Patience is required** — recovery takes months, not weeks
5. **Post-surgical rehab follows strict protocols** — respect the healing tissue
6. **Return to sport is typically 4-6 months** after surgery
7. **Some activities may need permanent modification** — especially if FAI is present
8. **Work with professionals** — physical therapy guidance improves outcomes

Hip labral tears can be frustrating, but with the right approach, most people return to their desired activities—whether through conservative care or surgical repair followed by dedicated rehabilitation.
