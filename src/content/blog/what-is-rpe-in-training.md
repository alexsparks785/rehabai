"Do 3 sets of 5 at RPE 8."

If you've seen this in a training program and wondered what it means, you're not alone. RPE—Rate of Perceived Exertion—is a powerful tool for managing training intensity, but it's often poorly explained.

Here's everything you need to know about RPE and how to use it.

## What Is RPE?

RPE stands for Rate of Perceived Exertion. It's a subjective measure of how hard a set feels, typically on a scale of 1-10.

Instead of prescribing exact weights, RPE prescribes effort levels. This allows training to adapt to your daily readiness—good days and bad days alike.

## The RPE Scale for Strength Training

The modern RPE scale used in strength training is based on Reps in Reserve (RIR):

| RPE | Reps in Reserve | Description |
|-----|-----------------|-------------|
| 10 | 0 | Maximum effort. Could not do another rep. True failure. |
| 9.5 | 0 | Max effort. Maybe could do another, but not confident. |
| 9 | 1 | Could definitely do 1 more rep. |
| 8.5 | 1-2 | Could do 1 more, maybe 2. |
| 8 | 2 | Could do 2 more reps. |
| 7.5 | 2-3 | Could do 2 more, maybe 3. |
| 7 | 3 | Could do 3 more reps. |
| 6 | 4 | Could do 4 more reps. Light working weight. |
| 5 | 5+ | Could do 5+ more reps. Warm-up territory. |

Most training lives in the RPE 6-9 range. Below RPE 6 is warm-up weight. RPE 10 (true failure) is rarely programmed due to its recovery cost.

## Why Use RPE?

### Autoregulation

Your capacity changes daily based on sleep, stress, nutrition, and recovery. RPE allows your training to match your actual readiness.

**Example:** Your program says "Squat 275x5 at RPE 8."

- On a good day, 275 feels like RPE 7—add weight
- On a bad day, 275 feels like RPE 9—reduce weight

You hit the intended effort level regardless of daily fluctuations.

### Progress Without Specific Numbers

Early in training, you don't know your maxes. RPE lets you train at appropriate intensities while figuring out what weights work for you.

### Long-Term Sustainability

Training to failure (RPE 10) every session is unsustainable and counterproductive. RPE helps regulate intensity to manageable levels that allow consistent progress.

### Better Communication

When you log "275x5 at RPE 8," you know exactly how hard that was. If next week 275x5 feels like RPE 7, you've gotten stronger—even if the numbers are the same.

## How to Estimate RPE

Estimating RPE is a skill that improves with practice.

### Method 1: Ask "How Many More?"

After each set, ask yourself: "How many more reps could I have done with good form?"

- Could do 0 more → RPE 10
- Could do 1 more → RPE 9
- Could do 2 more → RPE 8
- Could do 3 more → RPE 7

### Method 2: Watch for Technical Breakdown

As you approach failure:
- Bar speed slows significantly
- Form starts to break down
- You have to grind through reps

If the last rep was slow and grindy but you completed it, you're likely at RPE 9-10. If the last rep moved well and you felt controlled, you had more in the tank.

### Method 3: Use the "Gun to Head" Test

Could you absolutely do one more rep if your life depended on it? If yes, it wasn't RPE 10.

This sounds dramatic, but it clarifies the difference between "this is hard" and "I literally cannot complete another rep."

## Common RPE Mistakes

### Underestimating Effort (Sandbagging)

New lifters often think they're at RPE 8 when they're really at RPE 6. They stop when it gets uncomfortable, not when they're actually near failure.

**Fix:** Occasionally test true failure (carefully, with appropriate exercises) to calibrate what RPE 10 actually feels like.

### Overestimating Effort

Some lifters go too hard, thinking everything is RPE 8 when they're really hitting 9.5 every set.

**Fix:** Be honest about bar speed and form. If the weight is flying up, it's not high RPE regardless of sensation.

### Inconsistent Application

RPE for squats might feel different than RPE for curls. You need exercise-specific calibration.

**Fix:** Pay attention to how different exercises feel at various RPE levels. Build mental references for each major lift.

### Ignoring Form

RPE should be based on good reps. A grinding rep with terrible form doesn't count the same as a clean rep.

**Fix:** Define RPE as reps you could do with maintained technique, not just any reps.

## Applying RPE to Training

### In Your Program

A well-designed program might look like:

**Week 1:**
- Squat: Work up to 5 reps at RPE 7
- Bench: 4 sets of 6 at RPE 8

**Week 2:**
- Squat: Work up to 5 reps at RPE 8
- Bench: 4 sets of 5 at RPE 8

**Week 3:**
- Squat: Work up to 3 reps at RPE 9
- Bench: 4 sets of 4 at RPE 8.5

The weights aren't prescribed—you work up to the RPE target with whatever weight gets you there.

### Determining Working Weight

**For "work up to" prescriptions:**
1. Warm up gradually
2. Increase weight each set
3. Stop when you hit the target RPE for prescribed reps

**For "multiple sets at" prescriptions:**
1. Estimate the weight that will hit your RPE target
2. Do set 1, evaluate RPE
3. Adjust weight for subsequent sets if needed

### Logging

Record both weight and RPE for meaningful data:

"Squat: 275x5 @8, 275x5 @8.5, 275x5 @9"

This tells you the weight was appropriate for the first set but accumulated fatigue made it harder. Next week, you might start slightly lighter or expect the same progression.

## RPE vs. Percentage-Based Training

Traditional programs prescribe weights based on a percentage of your 1-rep max (e.g., "80% of 1RM for 5 reps").

**Advantages of percentage-based:**
- Objective and precise
- Easy to follow
- Removes subjective assessment

**Disadvantages of percentage-based:**
- Requires knowing your max
- Doesn't adapt to daily readiness
- Your max changes over time

**RPE advantages:**
- Adapts to daily fluctuations
- Works without knowing maxes
- Self-regulating for recovery

**RPE disadvantages:**
- Subjective and requires practice
- Can be inconsistent if you're not honest
- Harder for beginners who lack calibration

Many effective programs combine both: percentage-based targets with RPE as a reality check.

## RPE for Different Rep Ranges

RPE feels different across rep ranges:

**Low reps (1-5):** RPE is largely determined by whether you could complete another rep. Clear distinction between RPE 9 and 10.

**Moderate reps (6-12):** More gradual buildup of fatigue. Harder to distinguish RPE 7 from 8. Focus on bar speed and form degradation.

**High reps (12+):** Metabolic fatigue (burning, breathing) becomes a factor. RPE can be influenced by cardiovascular demand as much as muscular strength.

## RPE for Cardio

The original RPE scale (Borg scale) was designed for cardio, using 6-20 instead of 1-10:

| RPE | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| 6 | No exertion (sitting) |
| 9 | Very light (easy walking) |
| 11 | Light (comfortable pace) |
| 13 | Somewhat hard (moderate effort) |
| 15 | Hard (vigorous) |
| 17 | Very hard (very intense) |
| 19-20 | Maximum effort |

For simplicity, many people use a 1-10 scale for cardio too:
- 4-5: Easy, conversational pace
- 6-7: Moderate, can talk but with effort
- 8-9: Hard, difficult to talk
- 10: Maximum sprint

## Building Your RPE Skill

### Keep a Training Log

Write down RPE for every working set. Over time, you'll see patterns and improve calibration.

### Occasionally Test Limits

Every few weeks, take one set to true failure (on a safe exercise, with spotter if needed). This recalibrates your understanding of RPE 10.

### Use Video

Film your sets. Watch bar speed on different RPE levels. Build visual references for what RPE 7, 8, and 9 look like for your major lifts.

### Be Honest

RPE only works if you're truthful with yourself. Ego-lifting defeats the purpose. If it was hard, log it as hard.

## The Bottom Line

RPE is a tool for managing training intensity based on effort rather than fixed weights:

- **RPE 7:** Could do 3 more reps
- **RPE 8:** Could do 2 more reps
- **RPE 9:** Could do 1 more rep
- **RPE 10:** True failure

**Benefits:**
- Adapts to your daily readiness
- Self-regulating for recovery
- Works without knowing maxes
- Provides meaningful training data

**It takes practice to use well.** Calibrate through experience, be honest with yourself, and log consistently.

When used correctly, RPE helps you train hard enough to progress without constantly overreaching. It's a skill worth developing.
