# Exercises for Osteoarthritis: Movement as Medicine for Joint Pain

Osteoarthritis (OA) affects over 500 million people worldwide. If you have it, you've probably heard "exercise is good for arthritis"—but when your joints hurt, that advice can feel useless or even cruel. The truth is more nuanced: the *right* exercise, done *correctly*, is one of the most effective treatments we have.

## Why Exercise Helps (Not Hurts) Arthritis

Your joints don't have a direct blood supply. Cartilage gets its nutrition through a pumping action—compression and release during movement pushes fluid in and out. Without movement, cartilage literally starves.

Research consistently shows that appropriate exercise:
- **Reduces pain** (often as effectively as medication)
- **Improves function** and mobility
- **Slows disease progression**
- **Strengthens muscles** that support and protect joints
- **Reduces inflammation** (yes, exercise is anti-inflammatory)
- **Improves mental health** and quality of life

The key word is *appropriate*. Let's break down what that means.

## General Principles for Exercising with OA

### The 24-Hour Rule
Some discomfort during exercise is normal. But if pain is significantly worse 24 hours after exercise, you did too much. Scale back next time.

### Start Low, Progress Slow
Begin with less than you think you can do. It's easier to add more than to recover from a flare-up.

### Consistency Over Intensity
Three 10-minute sessions beat one 30-minute session that leaves you unable to move tomorrow.

### Warm Up Thoroughly
Arthritic joints need more warm-up time. Start with gentle range of motion before any strengthening.

### Listen to Sharp Pain
Dull aching is often okay to work through gently. Sharp, stabbing pain is a stop sign.

## Exercises for Knee Osteoarthritis

Knee OA is the most common form. Focus on quadriceps strength and overall leg stability.

### Quad Sets
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Tighten your thigh muscle, pressing the back of your knee down
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3x daily

This is the foundation—safe, effective, and you can do it anywhere.

### Straight Leg Raises
1. Lie on your back, one knee bent, one leg straight
2. Tighten the thigh of the straight leg
3. Lift leg to height of bent knee (about 12 inches)
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps each leg, 2-3 sets

### Mini Squats
1. Stand with support nearby (counter, chair)
2. Feet shoulder-width apart
3. Bend knees 30-45 degrees (partial squat)
4. Keep weight in heels, knees tracking over toes
5. Return to standing
6. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

Progress depth gradually as strength improves.

### Step-Ups
1. Use a low step (4-6 inches to start)
2. Step up with affected leg
3. Bring other foot up
4. Step down leading with unaffected leg
5. 10-15 reps each leg

### Hamstring Curls
1. Stand holding a support
2. Bend knee, bringing heel toward buttock
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg, 2-3 sets

## Exercises for Hip Osteoarthritis

Hip OA benefits from strengthening the muscles around the joint and maintaining range of motion.

### Hip Circles
1. Stand holding a support
2. Lift one leg slightly
3. Make small circles with your knee (like stirring a pot)
4. 10 circles each direction, each leg

### Clamshells
1. Lie on your side, knees bent 90 degrees
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Don't let hips roll backward
4. Lower slowly
5. 15-20 reps each side, 2-3 sets

### Hip Abduction (Side Leg Raises)
1. Lie on your side, legs straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling, keeping it straight
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each side, 2-3 sets

### Bridges
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips toward ceiling
3. Hold 5 seconds at top
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Seated Hip Flexion
1. Sit in a chair
2. Lift one knee toward chest
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg

## Exercises for Hand Osteoarthritis

Hand OA can significantly impact daily function. These exercises maintain dexterity and reduce stiffness.

### Finger Spreads
1. Start with fingers together
2. Spread fingers as wide as possible
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10 reps, several times daily

### Fist Making
1. Start with fingers extended
2. Slowly curl into a fist (don't force it)
3. Open slowly
4. 10 reps, several times daily

Do this in warm water for easier movement.

### Finger Touches
1. Touch thumb to each fingertip in sequence
2. Make an "O" shape with each touch
3. Reverse direction
4. 5-10 cycles, several times daily

### Wrist Circles
1. Make gentle circles with your wrist
2. 10 circles each direction, each wrist

### Grip Strengthening
1. Squeeze a soft ball or therapy putty
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 10 reps, 2-3x daily

Start very gentle—grip strength exercises can aggravate hand OA if too intense.

## Exercises for Spine Osteoarthritis

Spinal OA affects the facet joints and can cause back or neck stiffness and pain.

### Cat-Cow Stretch
1. On hands and knees
2. Arch back up like a cat, tucking chin
3. Then drop belly, lift head (cow)
4. Move slowly and gently
5. 10 cycles, 1-2x daily

### Pelvic Tilts
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Flatten lower back against floor (posterior tilt)
3. Then arch slightly (anterior tilt)
4. Move between positions slowly
5. 10-15 reps

### Knee-to-Chest Stretch
1. Lie on back
2. Bring one knee toward chest
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. Repeat other side
5. Then both knees together

### Chin Tucks
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Draw chin straight back (not down)
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10-15 reps, several times daily

## Low-Impact Cardio Options

Aerobic exercise reduces OA symptoms, but high-impact activities can aggravate joints.

**Best options:**
- **Walking**: Start with 10-15 minutes, progress gradually
- **Swimming/Water aerobics**: Buoyancy reduces joint stress by 90%
- **Cycling**: Stationary or outdoor, low impact on knees and hips
- **Elliptical**: Smooth motion, no impact

**Aim for:** 150 minutes of moderate activity per week (can be broken into 10-minute sessions)

## Sample Weekly Routine

**Daily:**
- Range of motion exercises (5 minutes morning)
- Walking or other low-impact cardio (20-30 minutes)

**3x per week:**
- Strengthening exercises for affected joints (15-20 minutes)

**As needed:**
- Gentle stretching when stiff
- Heat before exercise, ice after if sore

## Managing Flare-Ups

Even with perfect programming, flare-ups happen. When they do:

1. **Reduce intensity, don't stop completely**—gentle range of motion maintains nutrition to the joint
2. **Use heat** for stiffness, **ice** for swelling
3. **Modify activities** temporarily
4. **Return gradually** once the flare settles

## When to Seek Help

Consult a healthcare provider if:
- Pain is severe or getting progressively worse
- You have significant swelling, redness, or warmth
- Joint feels unstable or gives way
- You're unsure what exercises are safe for you

A physical therapist can design a personalized program and ensure proper form.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Movement is medicine**—appropriate exercise is one of the best treatments for OA
2. **Start gentle** and progress slowly
3. **Consistency matters** more than intensity
4. **Strengthen the muscles** around affected joints
5. **Include low-impact cardio** for overall health and inflammation reduction
6. **The 24-hour rule** guides your intensity—if pain is worse tomorrow, you did too much

Living well with osteoarthritis means finding the right balance of activity and rest. With patience and consistency, most people can significantly improve their function and quality of life through exercise.
