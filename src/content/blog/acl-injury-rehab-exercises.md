# ACL Injury Rehab: Exercises for Recovery With or Without Surgery

An ACL tear is one of the most common—and most feared—sports injuries. Whether you're pursuing surgical reconstruction or non-operative treatment, your rehabilitation determines your outcome. This guide covers both paths with evidence-based exercise progressions.

## Understanding ACL Injuries

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) prevents your tibia from sliding forward and controls rotational stability. Tears typically occur during:
- Cutting or pivoting movements
- Landing from jumps
- Sudden deceleration
- Direct contact to the knee

**The decision: Surgery vs. no surgery** depends on:
- Activity level and goals
- Presence of other injuries (meniscus, other ligaments)
- Knee instability symptoms
- Age and overall health

Many people—especially those who don't play cutting/pivoting sports—do very well without surgery. This approach is called "conservative management" or "ACL-deficient rehabilitation."

## Non-Operative (Conservative) Treatment

### Who It's For
- Lower-demand activities (walking, cycling, swimming, straight-line running)
- No significant instability in daily life
- Willing to modify activities or wear a brace for higher-risk activities
- Strong quadriceps and hamstrings before injury

### Goals
- Restore full range of motion
- Build exceptional strength (quadriceps, hamstrings, hip)
- Achieve dynamic stability through neuromuscular training
- Return to desired activities with or without bracing

### Phase 1: Acute (Weeks 1-2)

**Control swelling:**
- Ice 15-20 minutes, multiple times daily
- Compression wrap or sleeve
- Elevation above heart when resting

**Restore extension:**
Getting your leg fully straight is critical.

*Prone Hangs:*
1. Lie face down with kneecap just off edge of bed
2. Let gravity straighten the knee
3. 10-15 minutes, 3-4x daily

*Heel Props:*
1. Sit with heel on pillow or rolled towel
2. Let knee straighten with gravity
3. Add light weight on thigh if needed
4. 10-15 minutes, several times daily

**Basic strengthening:**

*Quad Sets:*
1. Straighten leg, tighten thigh
2. Press back of knee down
3. Hold 10 seconds, 20+ reps, multiple times daily

*Straight Leg Raises:*
1. Lying on back, tighten quad first
2. Lift leg 12 inches, keeping knee straight
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 3 sets

*Heel Slides:*
1. Slide heel toward buttocks, bending knee
2. Slide back out
3. 15-20 reps, 3x daily

### Phase 2: Strength Building (Weeks 2-8)

**Range of motion:** Work toward full flexion (heel to buttocks).

*Wall Slides:*
1. Lie on back, feet on wall
2. Let foot slide down, bending knee
3. Hold at end range 30-60 seconds

**Strengthening progression:**

*Mini Squats → Squats:*
- Start with 30-degree knee bend
- Progress depth as strength improves
- Add weight gradually

*Step-Ups:*
1. Start with 4-inch step
2. Progress to 8-inch
3. 10-15 reps, 3 sets each leg

*Leg Press (if available):*
- Start light, controlled motion
- Progress load gradually

*Hamstring Curls:*
- Standing, seated, or lying
- Critical for ACL-deficient knee stability

*Nordic Hamstring Curls (advanced):*
1. Kneel with ankles anchored
2. Lower body forward slowly
3. Catch yourself with hands
4. Return using hamstrings
5. Build to 8-12 reps

**Hip strengthening:**

*Side-Lying Hip Abduction:*
- 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

*Clamshells:*
- With resistance band: 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

*Single-Leg Bridges:*
- 10-15 reps each leg

### Phase 3: Neuromuscular Training (Weeks 6-12+)

*Single-Leg Balance:*
- Progress from firm surface to foam pad to wobble board
- Eyes open → eyes closed
- 30-60 seconds each leg

*Single-Leg Squats:*
- Start with minimal depth
- Focus on knee tracking over toes
- 10 reps each leg

*Lateral Band Walks:*
- Maintain athletic stance
- 15 steps each direction

*Perturbation Training:*
- Stand on one leg while partner provides gentle pushes
- React and stabilize

### Return to Activity (Non-Operative)

**Criteria to progress:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Quadriceps strength >90% of other leg
- Hamstring strength >90%
- Single-leg hop tests >90%
- Confidence and no instability

**Consider a brace for:**
- Pivoting or cutting sports
- Activities with uneven terrain
- Any situation where unexpected rotation might occur

## Post-Surgical ACL Reconstruction

### Graft Types
- **Patellar tendon** (bone-tendon-bone): Often for high-level athletes
- **Hamstring tendon**: Common choice, less anterior knee pain
- **Quadriceps tendon**: Increasingly popular
- **Allograft** (donor tissue): Longer healing, often for older/less active patients

Each graft has slightly different precautions—follow your surgeon's protocol.

### Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0-2)

**Goals:** Control swelling, restore extension, protect graft

**Weight bearing:** Usually full weight with crutches and brace locked in extension

**Critical: Extension, extension, extension**

*Prone Hangs:*
- 15-20 minutes, 4-5x daily
- The most important early exercise

*Heel Props:*
- Constant focus on getting leg straight

**Exercises:**

*Quad Sets:*
- 20+ reps, every hour if possible
- Electrical stimulation may help if quad won't fire

*Straight Leg Raises:*
- Keep brace on initially if prescribed
- 10-15 reps, 3 sets, 3x daily

*Ankle Pumps:*
- Constantly throughout day

*Patellar Mobilizations:*
- Gently move kneecap up/down and side to side
- Prevents scar tissue adhesion

### Phase 2: Early Rehabilitation (Weeks 2-6)

**Goals:** Full extension, progress flexion to 120°+, basic strength

*Heel Slides (progressing):*
- Work toward full flexion
- May use strap to assist

*Stationary Bike:*
- Usually starts week 2-3
- Seat high, rock back and forth initially
- Progress to full rotations

*Closed Chain Exercises:*
- Mini squats (progress depth)
- Leg press (light weight)
- Step-ups (low step)

*Open Chain (if allowed by protocol):*
- Short-arc quads
- Some protocols delay open chain knee extension

*Balance:*
- Double-leg balance activities
- Progress to single-leg as tolerated

### Phase 3: Strength and Neuromuscular (Weeks 6-12)

**Goals:** Build strength, improve balance, begin sport-specific patterns

*Progressive Squats:*
- Increase depth and load
- Single-leg variations when ready

*Deadlifts:*
- Romanian deadlifts for hamstrings
- Progress load gradually

*Step-Downs:*
- Excellent for quadriceps and control
- Focus on slow, controlled movement

*Single-Leg Balance Progressions:*
- Unstable surfaces
- Eyes closed
- Perturbation training

*Pool Running:*
- Low-impact cardiovascular conditioning
- Sport-specific movements in water

### Phase 4: Running and Agility (Weeks 12-20)

**Criteria to start running:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- No swelling after activity
- Quad strength >70% of other leg
- Normal walking pattern
- Cleared by surgeon/physical therapist

*Running Progression:*
1. Walk/jog intervals on flat surface
2. Progress jog duration
3. Increase speed gradually
4. Add inclines
5. Introduce direction changes (large radius first)

*Agility Progression:*
- Figure-8s (large → small)
- Lateral shuffles
- Carioca
- Gradual cutting and pivoting

### Phase 5: Return to Sport (Months 6-12+)

**Return-to-sport criteria (all should be met):**
- Time since surgery: Minimum 9 months (many recommend 12)
- Quad strength: >90% of other leg (ideally symmetrical)
- Hamstring strength: >90%
- Hop tests: >90% symmetry (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, timed hop)
- Y-balance test: Within normal limits
- Psychological readiness: Confidence in knee
- Clearance: From surgeon and physical therapist

**Sport-specific progression:**
1. Practice skills at 50% speed
2. Progress to 75%
3. Progress to full speed
4. Non-contact practice
5. Limited contact practice
6. Full practice
7. Return to competition

## Exercises for Injury Prevention

After returning to sport—or if you've never had an ACL injury—these exercises reduce risk:

**The FIFA 11+ Program** (proven to reduce ACL injuries by 50%+):
- Running drills
- Strength exercises (squats, Nordic hamstrings)
- Balance exercises
- Jumping/landing mechanics

**Key landing mechanics:**
- Land with soft knees (not locked)
- Knees tracking over toes (not collapsing inward)
- Hips back, chest up
- Absorb force gradually

**Essential strength:**
- Strong quadriceps AND hamstrings
- Hip external rotators and abductors
- Core stability

## Key Takeaways

1. **Full extension is the #1 priority** after injury and surgery—work on it relentlessly
2. **Non-operative treatment works** for many people, especially those not doing cutting/pivoting sports
3. **Rehabilitation takes 9-12 months** after surgery—don't rush return to sport
4. **Quad and hamstring strength** are critical for knee stability
5. **Meet objective criteria** before returning to sport (hop tests, strength testing)
6. **Prevention programs work**—FIFA 11+ reduces ACL injuries significantly
7. **Psychological readiness matters**—fear of re-injury is common and should be addressed

Your ACL recovery is a marathon, not a sprint. Trust the process, do the work, and you'll give yourself the best chance of returning to the activities you love.
