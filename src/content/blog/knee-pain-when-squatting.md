Knee pain during squats is frustrating. The squat is one of the best exercises for building leg strength, but if your knees hurt every time you do it, something needs to change.

The good news: most squat-related knee pain is fixable. It's usually a technique issue, mobility limitation, or muscle imbalance—not a sign you should never squat again.

Here's how to identify and fix the problem.

## Common Causes of Knee Pain When Squatting

### 1. Knees Caving Inward (Valgus)

**What it looks like:** Knees collapse toward each other during the descent or ascent.

**Why it hurts:** Creates shearing forces on the knee joint, stresses the MCL and patella.

**Common causes:**
- Weak glutes (especially glute medius)
- Tight adductors
- Poor motor control
- Stance too narrow

**The fix:**
- Strengthen glutes with bridges, clamshells, and banded walks
- Cue "knees out" or "spread the floor" during squats
- Use a light band around knees as a tactile cue
- Try a slightly wider stance

### 2. Knees Shooting Too Far Forward

**What it looks like:** Knees travel excessively past toes, heels may rise.

**Why it hurts:** Increases pressure on the patellar tendon and kneecap.

**Common causes:**
- Limited ankle mobility
- Weak posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings)
- Too much forward lean avoided by excessive knee bend
- Stance too narrow

**The fix:**
- Improve ankle mobility (stretches, mobilizations)
- Elevate heels temporarily (squat shoes or small plates)
- Focus on sitting back more into the squat
- Strengthen glutes and hamstrings
- Try a wider stance with more toe-out

### 3. Patellar Tendinitis (Jumper's Knee)

**What it looks like:** Pain at the front of the knee, just below the kneecap, especially at the bottom of the squat.

**Why it happens:** Overuse, sudden volume increases, or poor load management.

**The fix:**
- Reduce squat volume temporarily
- Avoid deep squats until pain subsides
- Do isometric wall sits (pain-free angle)
- Gradually rebuild with slow eccentrics
- Address any technique issues contributing to tendon overload

### 4. IT Band Syndrome

**What it looks like:** Pain on the outside of the knee, may feel like a snap or pop.

**Why it happens:** Tight IT band rubbing over the lateral femoral condyle, often due to weak glutes.

**The fix:**
- Strengthen glute medius (side-lying leg raises, clamshells, band walks)
- Foam roll the quads and TFL (not the IT band directly)
- Stretch hip flexors
- Address running volume if applicable

### 5. Meniscus Irritation

**What it looks like:** Deep knee pain, possibly with clicking, catching, or locking. Often worse at full depth.

**Why it happens:** Can be acute injury or degenerative wear.

**The fix:**
- Limit squat depth to pain-free range
- See a professional for proper diagnosis
- May require medical imaging
- Strengthening the surrounding muscles helps long-term

### 6. Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)

**What it looks like:** Diffuse pain around or behind the kneecap, worse with squatting, stairs, or prolonged sitting.

**Why it happens:** Poor patellar tracking, often due to muscle imbalances (weak VMO, tight lateral structures).

**The fix:**
- Strengthen the entire quad, especially inner quad (VMO)
- Strengthen glutes
- Foam roll and stretch lateral quad and IT band
- Temporary reduction in squat depth
- Focus on controlled movement

## Form Corrections for Pain-Free Squats

### Check Your Stance

**Width:** Too narrow forces more forward knee travel. Try shoulder-width or slightly wider.

**Toe angle:** 15-30 degrees out is typical. Your knees should track over your toes—if they can't, adjust toe angle.

**Bar position (if barbell squatting):** High bar allows more upright torso; low bar requires more forward lean. Experiment to find what feels better on your knees.

### Control the Descent

Dropping quickly into the squat and bouncing out of the bottom stresses the knees.

**Instead:**
- Lower with control (2-3 seconds)
- Own the bottom position
- Drive up without bouncing

### Drive Through the Whole Foot

Shifting weight to toes increases knee stress. Keep weight distributed across your entire foot—heel, big toe, and little toe all in contact.

### Keep Knees Tracking Over Toes

Knees should travel in the same direction your toes point. Not inward, not excessively outward.

### Find Your Depth

Deep squats aren't mandatory. If your knees hurt at full depth, squat to a comfortable depth and work on mobility separately.

Squatting to parallel (hip crease at knee level) is sufficient for most goals.

## Mobility Work for Better Squats

### Ankle Mobility

Limited ankle mobility forces compensations that stress the knees.

**Test:** In a half-kneeling position, can your knee travel 4-5 inches past your toes while keeping heel down?

**Improve with:**
- Kneeling ankle rocks
- Calf stretches (straight and bent knee)
- Ankle circles
- Elevated heel squats as a temporary aid

### Hip Mobility

Tight hips limit squat depth and can alter knee mechanics.

**Improve with:**
- 90/90 stretch
- Pigeon pose
- Deep squat holds (goblet squat position)
- Hip circles

### Quad and Hip Flexor Flexibility

Tight quads and hip flexors can affect patellar tracking.

**Improve with:**
- Couch stretch
- Kneeling hip flexor stretch
- Foam rolling quads

## Strengthening for Knee Health

### Glute Strengthening

Weak glutes are behind many knee problems.

**Key exercises:**
- Glute bridges: 3×15-20
- Clamshells with band: 3×15 each side
- Banded lateral walks: 3×10-15 steps each direction
- Single-leg glute bridges: 3×10-12 each side

### VMO (Inner Quad) Strengthening

The vastus medialis oblique helps with patellar tracking.

**Key exercises:**
- Terminal knee extensions with band
- Step-downs (slow and controlled)
- Spanish squats (band around knees, lean back)
- Split squats with focus on controlled knee bend

### Hamstring Strengthening

Balance the quad-dominant squat with hamstring work.

**Key exercises:**
- Romanian deadlifts
- Nordic curls (or assisted versions)
- Leg curls
- Glute-ham raises

## Squat Modifications for Knee Pain

### Box Squats

Sitting back to a box reduces forward knee travel and teaches proper hip mechanics.

### Goblet Squats

The front-loaded position often feels better on knees than barbell squats.

### Heel-Elevated Squats

Plates or squat shoes under heels reduce ankle mobility demands and allow a more upright torso.

### Partial Range Squats

Squat to a pain-free depth while working on mobility and strength. Gradually increase depth as tolerated.

### Tempo Squats

Slow descent (3-5 seconds) builds control and strength without heavy loads.

### Leg Press

If squats are too painful temporarily, the leg press can maintain leg strength while you address the underlying issue.

## When to See a Professional

See a doctor or physical therapist if:

- Pain is severe or getting worse
- You have significant swelling
- The knee locks, catches, or gives way
- Pain persists despite 2-3 weeks of modifications and self-care
- You heard a pop followed by immediate pain and swelling
- Pain is present at rest, not just during squats

Some knee issues require professional diagnosis and treatment beyond what self-correction can address.

## Return to Squatting Protocol

After addressing pain:

**Week 1-2:** Bodyweight squats only, pain-free depth, focus on perfect form. High reps (15-20), low intensity.

**Week 3-4:** Add light weight (goblet squat). Still high reps (12-15), controlled tempo.

**Week 5-6:** Gradually increase weight while maintaining form. Can reduce reps (8-12).

**Week 7+:** Return to normal training, but continue mobility and strengthening work as maintenance.

Increase intensity only if you remain pain-free. Any return of pain means scaling back.

## Prevention: Keeping Knees Healthy

### Warm Up Properly

- 5 minutes light cardio
- Bodyweight squats
- Glute activation (bridges, band walks)
- Ankle and hip mobility work

### Don't Neglect Accessory Work

Regularly include:
- Glute strengthening
- Hamstring work
- Single-leg exercises
- Mobility maintenance

### Progress Gradually

Sudden jumps in weight or volume are common causes of knee pain. Increase loads by 5-10% per week maximum.

### Listen to Your Body

Mild discomfort that goes away is normal. Pain that persists or worsens is a warning sign. Don't push through significant knee pain.

### Vary Your Training

Don't squat heavy every session. Include different rep ranges, squat variations, and other leg exercises.

## The Bottom Line

Knee pain when squatting usually stems from:
- Technique issues (knees caving, excessive forward travel)
- Mobility limitations (ankles, hips)
- Muscle weaknesses (glutes, VMO)
- Overuse or rapid volume increases

The fix involves:
1. **Correcting form** (stance, depth, knee tracking)
2. **Improving mobility** (ankles, hips)
3. **Strengthening** weak muscles (glutes, hamstrings, quads)
4. **Progressing gradually** and listening to your body

Most people can squat pain-free with the right adjustments. Don't accept knee pain as inevitable—address it systematically and get back to building strong legs.
