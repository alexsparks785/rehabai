# Vertigo and Vestibular Exercises: Regaining Your Balance

Vertigo—the sensation that you or your surroundings are spinning—can be terrifying and debilitating. But here's the encouraging news: most vestibular disorders respond remarkably well to specific exercises. In fact, the treatment for the most common cause of vertigo (BPPV) works in just a few minutes.

## Understanding Vestibular Problems

Your vestibular system (inner ear + brain connections) controls balance and spatial orientation. When it malfunctions, you may experience:
- Vertigo (spinning sensation)
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Balance problems
- Nausea
- Visual disturbances
- Difficulty concentrating

**Common causes:**
- **BPPV** (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo): Displaced crystals in inner ear
- **Vestibular neuritis/labyrinthitis**: Viral infection of vestibular nerve
- **Meniere's disease**: Fluid buildup in inner ear
- **Vestibular migraine**: Migraine affecting vestibular system
- **Concussion/head injury**: Trauma affecting vestibular function

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**Seek immediate care if vertigo accompanies:**
- Sudden severe headache
- Difficulty speaking or understanding speech
- Vision changes or double vision
- Weakness or numbness (especially one-sided)
- Difficulty walking
- Chest pain
- Loss of consciousness

These could indicate stroke or other serious conditions.

## BPPV: The Most Common Cause

BPPV causes brief episodes of intense vertigo triggered by head movements—typically rolling over in bed, looking up, or bending down. It accounts for about 50% of vertigo cases.

**What's happening:** Tiny calcium crystals (otoconia) have dislodged from their normal location and moved into the semicircular canals, sending false signals when you move your head.

### Diagnosing BPPV: The Dix-Hallpike Test

A healthcare provider can diagnose BPPV by:
1. You sit on a table
2. They quickly lower you backward with head turned 45° to one side
3. If BPPV is present in that ear, vertigo and characteristic eye movements (nystagmus) occur

### Treating BPPV: The Epley Maneuver

The Epley maneuver repositions the crystals. It's highly effective—success rates of 80-90% with a single treatment.

**For right-sided BPPV:**

1. **Start position**: Sit on bed, turn head 45° to the right

2. **Position 1**: Quickly lie back with head hanging slightly over edge, still turned right
   - Wait 30 seconds (or until vertigo stops)

3. **Position 2**: Turn head 90° to the left (now looking 45° left)
   - Wait 30 seconds

4. **Position 3**: Roll onto your left side while turning head to look at the floor
   - Wait 30 seconds

5. **Position 4**: Slowly sit up, keeping head tilted down

**For left-sided BPPV**: Reverse the directions (start head turned left, roll onto right side).

**After the maneuver:**
- Avoid lying flat for the rest of the day
- Sleep propped up at 45° for 1-2 nights (some practitioners)
- Avoid head movements that trigger symptoms
- Repeat maneuver if symptoms return

### Other BPPV Maneuvers

**Semont Maneuver**: Alternative to Epley
**BBQ Roll**: For horizontal canal BPPV (less common)
**Brandt-Daroff Exercises**: Habituation exercises for persistent cases

## Vestibular Rehabilitation Exercises

For vestibular neuritis, chronic dizziness, or BPPV that doesn't fully resolve, vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) helps your brain compensate for inner ear dysfunction.

### Gaze Stabilization Exercises

These train your vestibular system to keep vision stable during head movement.

**VOR x1 (Basic):**
1. Hold a card with a letter at arm's length
2. Keep eyes fixed on the letter
3. Turn head side to side while maintaining focus
4. Start slow, increase speed as tolerated
5. 1-2 minutes, 3x daily

**VOR x1 Vertical:**
- Same exercise but nod head up and down

**VOR x2 (Advanced):**
1. Hold card at arm's length
2. Move card one direction while moving head opposite direction
3. Keep focus on the letter
4. 1-2 minutes, 3x daily

**Important:** These may initially increase dizziness—that's expected. Stop if severe, but mild symptoms mean it's working.

### Habituation Exercises

These reduce dizziness by repeatedly exposing your brain to movements that trigger symptoms.

**Brandt-Daroff Exercises:**
1. Sit on edge of bed
2. Quickly lie on one side, nose pointed 45° up
3. Stay 30 seconds (or until dizziness stops)
4. Return to sitting (30 seconds)
5. Lie on opposite side
6. Repeat 5x each side, 2-3x daily

**Position Changes:**
Practice movements that trigger mild symptoms:
- Bending forward
- Looking up
- Turning head quickly
- Rolling in bed

Start gently, progress as tolerance improves.

### Balance Exercises

Progress through these as able:

**Level 1 (Basic):**
- Stand feet together, eyes open (30 seconds)
- Stand feet together, eyes closed (30 seconds)
- Stand on one foot, holding support (30 seconds each)

**Level 2 (Intermediate):**
- Stand feet together, eyes closed, on pillow
- Stand on one foot, eyes open, no support
- Walk heel-to-toe in a line (10 steps)
- Walk while turning head side to side

**Level 3 (Advanced):**
- Single-leg stance, eyes closed
- Stand on foam pad with head movements
- Walk figure-8 patterns
- Catch and throw ball while standing on one foot

### Walking Exercises

**Head movements while walking:**
1. Walk at comfortable pace
2. Turn head side to side every few steps
3. Progress to up/down head movements
4. Practice in different environments (hall, store, outdoors)

**Gait variations:**
- Walk backward (safely, with clear path)
- Sidestep walking
- Walk on different surfaces (carpet, tile, grass)

## Exercise Progression Guidelines

**Start where you are:**
- If very symptomatic, begin with bed-level exercises
- Progress to sitting, then standing, then walking

**Expect some symptoms:**
- Mild dizziness during exercises is okay
- Should settle within 15-20 minutes after stopping
- Reduce intensity if symptoms are severe or lasting

**Frequency:**
- Most programs: 2-3x daily
- Consistency matters more than duration

**Progress when:**
- Current level no longer provokes symptoms
- You can complete exercises with good balance
- Typically advance every 1-2 weeks

## Lifestyle Modifications

**During acute episodes:**
- Move slowly, especially when getting up
- Hold onto stable objects when walking
- Avoid sudden head movements
- Stay hydrated
- Get adequate sleep

**Long-term:**
- Continue maintenance exercises
- Stay physically active
- Address vision problems
- Review medications (some worsen dizziness)
- Limit caffeine and alcohol if they trigger symptoms

## Recovery Expectations

**BPPV:**
- Often resolved with 1-3 Epley maneuvers
- May recur (30% within one year)
- Can self-treat if it recurs

**Vestibular neuritis:**
- Acute phase: 1-2 weeks
- Significant improvement: 4-6 weeks
- Full compensation: 3-6 months
- Some have residual symptoms long-term

**General vestibular rehabilitation:**
- Noticeable improvement: 2-4 weeks
- Significant improvement: 6-8 weeks
- Maximum benefit: 3-6 months

## Sample Daily Program

**Morning (5-10 minutes):**
- Gaze stabilization: VOR x1 horizontal and vertical (1 minute each)
- Brandt-Daroff: 5 reps each side
- Balance: Feet together, eyes closed (30 seconds x 3)

**Midday (5 minutes):**
- Gaze stabilization exercises
- Walking with head turns

**Evening (5-10 minutes):**
- Gaze stabilization
- Balance progression exercises
- Walking exercises

## When Exercises Aren't Enough

Seek professional evaluation if:
- Symptoms persist beyond 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise
- Episodes are becoming more frequent or severe
- New symptoms develop
- Daily function is significantly impacted

**Specialists who can help:**
- Vestibular physical therapist
- ENT (otolaryngologist)
- Neurologist
- Audiologist

## Key Takeaways

1. **BPPV is the most common cause** and often cured with the Epley maneuver in minutes
2. **Exercises may initially increase dizziness** — that's part of the process
3. **Gaze stabilization and balance exercises** help your brain compensate
4. **Consistency is essential** — do exercises daily, even when feeling better
5. **Most people improve significantly** within weeks to months
6. **Some symptoms during exercises are expected**; severe or lasting symptoms warrant medical attention
7. **Recurrence is possible** — know your exercises so you can self-treat

Vestibular disorders are treatable. With the right diagnosis and consistent rehabilitation, most people regain their balance and return to normal activities.
