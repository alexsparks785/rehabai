# Rotator Cuff Tear Exercises: Conservative Treatment and Post-Surgical Rehab

Rotator cuff tears are incredibly common—affecting up to 30% of people over 60—yet many people function well without surgery. Whether you're pursuing conservative treatment or recovering from repair, the right exercises are essential for shoulder function.

## Understanding Rotator Cuff Tears

The rotator cuff consists of four muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) that stabilize your shoulder and control arm movement. Tears can be:

**By cause:**
- **Traumatic**: From a fall, lifting injury, or sudden force
- **Degenerative**: Gradual wear and tear over time (most common)

**By severity:**
- **Partial thickness**: Some fibers torn, not all the way through
- **Full thickness**: Complete tear through the tendon
- **Massive**: Large or multiple tendon involvement

**Important:** Many tears are asymptomatic. Studies show ~25% of people over 50 have rotator cuff tears with no pain.

## Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

### Conservative Treatment Often Works For:
- Partial-thickness tears
- Degenerative tears in older adults
- Tears without significant weakness
- Patients who respond to initial physical therapy
- Those with lower activity demands

### Surgery May Be Needed For:
- Acute traumatic tears (especially in younger, active patients)
- Complete tears with significant weakness
- Failed conservative treatment after 3-6 months
- Large or massive tears that may retract further
- Athletes or workers requiring full shoulder strength

### The Research
Studies show 75-80% of patients with rotator cuff tears improve with conservative treatment. Even some full-thickness tears can be managed non-operatively with excellent outcomes.

## Non-Operative Treatment Protocol

### Phase 1: Pain Control and Protection (Weeks 1-3)

**Rest from aggravating activities:**
- Avoid overhead reaching
- No lifting away from body
- Sleep modifications (pillow support, avoid lying on affected side)

**Pain management:**
- Ice for 15-20 minutes after activity
- NSAIDs if appropriate (consult doctor)
- Activity modification

**Gentle range of motion:**

*Pendulum Exercises:*
1. Lean forward, supporting yourself with unaffected arm
2. Let affected arm hang
3. Make small circles (clockwise and counter)
4. Let momentum move the arm, not muscles
5. 2-3 minutes, 3-4x daily

*Passive Forward Flexion:*
1. Lie on back
2. Use unaffected arm to lift affected arm overhead
3. Go only as far as comfortable
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3x daily

*Passive External Rotation:*
1. Hold a stick with both hands
2. Use unaffected arm to push affected arm into external rotation
3. Keep elbow at side
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3x daily

### Phase 2: Active Motion and Early Strengthening (Weeks 3-8)

**Active-assisted exercises:**

*Supine Flexion with Dowel:*
1. Lie on back, hold stick with both hands
2. Raise arms overhead together
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

*Wall Walks:*
1. Face wall, place fingertips on wall
2. Walk fingers up the wall
3. Go as high as comfortable
4. Walk back down
5. 10-15 reps

**Isometric strengthening:**

*Isometric External Rotation:*
1. Stand with elbow bent 90°, arm at side
2. Press back of wrist against doorframe
3. Hold 10 seconds (no movement)
4. 10 reps, 2-3 sets

*Isometric Internal Rotation:*
1. Stand with elbow bent 90°, arm at side
2. Press palm against doorframe
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 10 reps, 2-3 sets

*Isometric Abduction:*
1. Stand with arm at side against wall
2. Press arm outward into wall
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 10 reps, 2-3 sets

**Scapular strengthening:**

*Scapular Squeezes:*
1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

*Prone Rows (light or no weight):*
1. Lie face down on bench or bed
2. Let arm hang
3. Pull elbow toward ceiling, squeezing shoulder blade
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-16)

**Rotator cuff exercises with resistance:**

*Side-Lying External Rotation:*
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Affected elbow bent 90°, resting on side
3. Hold light weight, rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

*Standing External Rotation (Band):*
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Hold resistance band
3. Rotate forearm outward
4. Return slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

*Standing Internal Rotation (Band):*
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Hold band anchored to side
3. Rotate forearm across body
4. Return slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

*Standing Rows:*
1. Hold band or cable
2. Pull elbows back, squeezing shoulder blades
3. Return slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

**Progress deltoid strengthening:**

*Lateral Raises:*
- Start with light weight
- Only lift to shoulder height
- 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

*Front Raises:*
- Light weight, controlled motion
- Thumb up position may be more comfortable
- 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Phase 4: Functional Progression (Weeks 16+)

- Progressive resistance training
- Sport or work-specific movements
- Gradual return to activities
- Maintain rotator cuff and scapular strength

## Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

Protocols vary by surgeon, repair size, and tissue quality. **Always follow your surgeon's specific guidelines.**

### Phase 1: Maximum Protection (Weeks 0-6)

**Sling wear:** Usually 4-6 weeks (sometimes longer for large repairs)

**Motion restrictions:** Often no active shoulder movement initially

**Allowed exercises:**

*Elbow, wrist, and hand exercises:*
- Elbow flexion/extension
- Wrist circles
- Ball squeezes

*Pendulums (when cleared):*
- Very gentle, small circles
- Arm hangs, gravity assists

*Passive range of motion (performed by therapist or self):*
- Forward flexion (often limited range)
- External rotation (often limited range)
- Usually no internal rotation initially

### Phase 2: Protected Motion (Weeks 6-12)

**Sling:** Usually discontinued by week 6

**Goals:** Progress passive and active-assisted range of motion

*Active-assisted exercises:*
- Pulley exercises
- Cane/stick exercises
- Wall walks

*Isometrics (when cleared):*
- Submaximal, pain-free
- Progress gradually

*Scapular exercises:*
- Scapular squeezes
- Light rows

### Phase 3: Active Strengthening (Weeks 12-20)

**Goals:** Restore active motion and begin strengthening

*Active range of motion:*
- Progress to full motion without assistance

*Rotator cuff strengthening:*
- Start with gravity-eliminated positions
- Progress to resistance bands
- Light weights

*Deltoid and periscapular strengthening:*
- Progressive resistance

### Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 20-26+)

*Progressive resistance training:*
- Continue rotator cuff strengthening
- Advance deltoid work
- Functional movement patterns

### Phase 5: Return to Activity (6 months+)

*Criteria for return:*
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength at least 80-90% of other side
- No pain with sport or work-specific movements
- Cleared by surgeon

**Timeline varies:** Simple repairs may return to activity at 6 months; large or massive repairs may take 9-12 months.

## Exercises to Avoid

**During conservative treatment:**
- Overhead pressing (until pain-free)
- Heavy lifting
- Movements that reproduce pain

**After surgery (until cleared):**
- Active shoulder motion (early phases)
- Lifting anything
- Behind-back movements
- Reaching across body

**Long-term caution:**
- Heavy overhead lifting
- High-volume throwing without proper conditioning

## Preventing Future Problems

*Maintain rotator cuff strength:*
- External rotation exercises 2-3x weekly
- Scapular strengthening

*Warm up shoulders before activity*

*Use good mechanics:*
- Don't reach and lift
- Keep loads close to body

*Address posture:*
- Forward head and rounded shoulders increase impingement risk

## Key Takeaways

1. **Most rotator cuff tears improve without surgery** (75-80% with proper rehabilitation)
2. **Surgery is important for** acute traumatic tears, significant weakness, or failed conservative care
3. **Start with pain control and gentle motion**, progress to strengthening
4. **Scapular strength is as important as rotator cuff strength**
5. **Post-surgical rehab requires patience** — protect the repair in early weeks
6. **Full recovery takes 6-12 months** after surgery
7. **Maintain shoulder strength** long-term to prevent future problems

Whether you're healing conservatively or recovering from repair, commitment to your exercises is the key to restoring shoulder function and returning to the activities you enjoy.
