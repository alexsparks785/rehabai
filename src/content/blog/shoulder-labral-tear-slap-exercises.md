# Shoulder Labral Tear and SLAP Lesion Exercises: Recovery Guide

Shoulder labral tears, including SLAP (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior) lesions, are common in overhead athletes and can cause significant pain and dysfunction. Whether you're pursuing conservative treatment or recovering from surgery, the right rehabilitation approach is essential for returning to full function.

## Understanding Shoulder Labral Tears

The labrum is a ring of cartilage that surrounds the shoulder socket (glenoid), deepening it and providing stability. The biceps tendon attaches at the top of the labrum.

**Types of labral tears:**
- **SLAP lesions**: Tears at the top of the labrum where biceps attaches (Types I-IV)
- **Bankart lesions**: Tears at the front-bottom, often from dislocations
- **Posterior labral tears**: Tears at the back of the labrum

**Common causes:**
- Repetitive overhead motions (throwing, swimming, tennis)
- Traumatic injury or fall
- Shoulder dislocation or instability
- Wear and tear over time
- Heavy lifting

**Symptoms:**
- Deep shoulder pain (often hard to localize)
- Catching, clicking, or popping
- Pain with overhead activities
- Feeling of instability
- Decreased throwing velocity or accuracy (athletes)
- Night pain

## Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

### Conservative Treatment May Work For:
- Type I SLAP tears (fraying without detachment)
- Older patients (40+) with degenerative tears
- Lower activity demands
- Tears without significant instability
- Those willing to modify activities

### Surgery Often Recommended For:
- Type II SLAP tears (biceps anchor detached)
- Young, overhead athletes wanting to return to sport
- Failed conservative treatment (3-6 months)
- Significant instability (Bankart repairs)
- Mechanical symptoms limiting function

## Conservative Treatment Protocol

### Phase 1: Pain Control and Protection (Weeks 1-4)

**Activity modification:**
- Avoid overhead reaching
- No throwing or overhead sports
- Limit lifting to below shoulder height
- Sleep modifications (avoid lying on affected side)

**Gentle range of motion:**

*Pendulum Exercises:*
1. Lean forward, support yourself with unaffected arm
2. Let affected arm hang and swing gently
3. Circles, forward/back, side to side
4. 2-3 minutes, several times daily

*Passive Forward Flexion:*
1. Lie on back
2. Use unaffected arm to lift affected arm overhead
3. Go only as far as comfortable
4. 10-15 reps

*Passive External Rotation:*
1. Hold stick with both hands
2. Elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Use unaffected arm to push affected arm into external rotation
4. 10-15 reps

### Phase 2: Active Motion and Early Strengthening (Weeks 4-8)

**Active range of motion:**

*Active-Assisted Flexion:*
1. Use pulley or stick
2. Progress to active motion without assistance
3. 10-15 reps

*Supine External Rotation (Active):*
1. Lie on back, elbow supported
2. Rotate forearm outward
3. 10-15 reps

**Scapular strengthening (critical):**

*Scapular Squeezes:*
1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 reps

*Prone Y's, T's, W's:*
1. Lie face down on bench or bed
2. Y: Raise arms overhead in Y position
3. T: Raise arms out to sides
4. W: Arms bent, squeeze shoulder blades, rotate arms up
5. 10-15 reps each

*Low Rows:*
1. With band or cable
2. Pull elbows back, squeeze shoulder blades
3. 10-15 reps

**Rotator cuff (submaximal):**

*Isometric External Rotation:*
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Press back of wrist into wall or doorframe
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 10 reps

*Isometric Internal Rotation:*
1. Same position
2. Press palm into wall
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 10 reps

### Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-12+)

**Rotator cuff with resistance:**

*Side-Lying External Rotation:*
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Elbow bent 90°, resting on side
3. Light weight, rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. 10-15 reps

*Standing External Rotation (Band):*
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Pull band outward
3. 10-15 reps

*Standing Internal Rotation (Band):*
1. Elbow at side
2. Pull band across body
3. 10-15 reps

**Shoulder strengthening:**

*Shoulder Flexion:*
- Light weight, raise arm forward
- Stay below pain threshold
- 10-15 reps

*Scaption:*
1. Raise arms at 45° angle (between front and side)
2. Thumbs up position
3. 10-15 reps

*Rows:*
- Progress resistance
- Focus on scapular control

### Phase 4: Functional Progression

**Criteria to progress:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Good rotator cuff and scapular strength
- No catching or clicking

**Sport-specific preparation:**
- Interval throwing program (for throwers)
- Swimming progression (for swimmers)
- Gradual return to overhead activities

## Post-Surgical Rehabilitation (SLAP Repair)

Protocols vary significantly based on repair type and surgeon preference. **Follow your surgeon's specific guidelines.**

### Phase 1: Maximum Protection (Weeks 0-4)

**Sling wear:** Typically 4-6 weeks

**Precautions:**
- No active biceps contraction (protects repair)
- No shoulder external rotation beyond neutral initially
- No lifting

**Exercises:**

*Elbow, Wrist, Hand:*
- Bend and straighten elbow (outside sling)
- Wrist circles
- Ball squeezes

*Pendulums (When Cleared):*
- Very gentle, small movements

*Passive ROM (by therapist or self):*
- Forward flexion (limited range)
- External rotation (limited or none initially)

### Phase 2: Protected Motion (Weeks 4-8)

**Sling:** Usually discontinued around week 6

**Goals:**
- Progress passive and active-assisted ROM
- Begin gentle active motion

*Active-Assisted Exercises:*
- Pulleys
- Cane/stick exercises
- Progress within allowed ranges

*Isometrics (When Cleared):*
- Submaximal, pain-free
- Rotator cuff and deltoid

*Scapular Exercises:*
- Scapular squeezes
- Gentle rows

### Phase 3: Active Strengthening (Weeks 8-14)

**Goals:**
- Restore full active motion
- Begin rotator cuff strengthening

*Active ROM:*
- Progress to full motion
- All planes

*Rotator Cuff:*
- Light resistance
- Progress gradually
- Include biceps strengthening (when cleared)

*Scapular Strengthening:*
- Progress resistance
- Include Y's, T's, W's

### Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 14-20)

*Progressive Resistance:*
- Continue rotator cuff
- Deltoid strengthening
- Biceps (typically cleared by now)

*Functional Movements:*
- Simulated sport activities
- Light plyometrics (when cleared)

### Phase 5: Return to Sport (Months 5-9+)

**For Throwers:**
- Interval throwing program (typically starts month 4-5)
- Very gradual progression over 2-3 months
- Full throwing typically 6-9 months

**Criteria:**
- Full, pain-free ROM
- Strength symmetry (>90%)
- Successful completion of throwing program
- Surgeon clearance

## Interval Throwing Program (Overview)

For throwers returning after SLAP repair:

**Phase 1:** 45-foot throws, flat ground
**Phase 2:** Progress distance to 60, 90, 120 feet
**Phase 3:** Long toss to 150+ feet
**Phase 4:** Throwing from mound at 50%
**Phase 5:** Progress intensity on mound
**Phase 6:** Live batting practice
**Phase 7:** Game situations

Each phase takes 1-2 weeks minimum. **Any pain = step back.**

## Exercises to Avoid

**During conservative treatment:**
- Overhead pressing
- Dips (stress biceps anchor)
- Behind-neck exercises
- Heavy bicep curls
- Throwing or overhead sports

**After surgery:**
- All of above until cleared
- Active biceps (early phase)
- External rotation beyond limits
- Any exercise causing pain

## Long-Term Shoulder Health

**Maintain:**
- Rotator cuff strength
- Scapular stability
- Posterior shoulder flexibility
- Proper throwing/overhead mechanics

**For throwers:**
- Year-round arm care program
- Pitch count awareness
- Off-season maintenance

## Key Takeaways

1. **Conservative treatment works for many labral tears** — especially degenerative and Type I SLAP
2. **Scapular strength is as important as rotator cuff** — don't neglect it
3. **Biceps protection is critical** after SLAP repair — follow precautions strictly
4. **Return to throwing takes 6-9 months** after surgery — don't rush
5. **Progress based on function** — not just time
6. **Interval programs are essential** for overhead athletes
7. **Pain is a signal** — step back if exercises cause pain
8. **Long-term maintenance matters** — keep shoulders strong

Shoulder labral injuries require patience, but with proper rehabilitation, most people return to their desired activities—even high-level throwing sports.
