Neck pain affects nearly everyone at some point. Whether from poor posture, sleeping wrong, stress, or staring at screens all day, that stiff, achy neck can make everything harder.

The right exercises can provide significant relief and help prevent future episodes. Here's what works.

## Understanding Neck Pain

Most neck pain comes from:

**Muscle tension:** Tight muscles from stress, posture, or overuse
**Poor posture:** Forward head position strains neck muscles
**Prolonged positions:** Holding any position too long causes stiffness
**Weakness:** Weak supporting muscles lead to strain
**Sleeping position:** Wrong pillow or position causes morning stiffness

Less commonly, neck pain can indicate disc problems, arthritis, or other conditions requiring medical attention.

### When to See a Doctor

Seek medical evaluation if you have:
- Pain after trauma (accident, fall)
- Pain radiating down your arm
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in arms/hands
- Severe pain that doesn't improve with rest
- Pain accompanied by headache, fever, or other symptoms
- Pain lasting more than 2-3 weeks despite self-care

The exercises below are for general muscular neck pain, not injuries or pathology.

## Neck Stretches

### Upper Trapezius Stretch

Releases the commonly tight muscle running from shoulder to skull.

1. Sit or stand tall
2. Hold the bottom of your chair (or put hand behind back)
3. Tilt your head toward the opposite shoulder
4. For added stretch, gently use your free hand to guide your head
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Feel it:** Side of neck, from shoulder to behind ear

### Levator Scapulae Stretch

Targets the muscle from shoulder blade to neck—often a major tension culprit.

1. Sit tall, hold chair with one hand
2. Turn your head 45 degrees toward opposite side
3. Tilt your chin down toward your armpit
4. Use free hand to gently increase stretch
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Feel it:** Back/side of neck, toward shoulder blade

### Chin Tuck

Stretches suboccipital muscles and retrains head position.

1. Sit or stand tall
2. Without tilting your head, draw your chin straight back
3. Make a "double chin"
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Repeat 10-15 times

**Feel it:** Stretch at base of skull, front of neck muscles working

### Neck Flexion Stretch

Stretches the back of the neck.

1. Sit tall
2. Gently drop your chin toward your chest
3. Let gravity provide the stretch
4. Optionally, gently add pressure with hands behind head
5. Hold 20-30 seconds

**Feel it:** Back of neck, upper back

### Neck Extension Stretch

Stretches the front of the neck.

1. Sit tall
2. Slowly tilt head back, looking up
3. Open your mouth slightly for a deeper stretch
4. Hold 10-15 seconds

**Caution:** Skip if looking up causes pain or dizziness.

### Scalene Stretch

Stretches muscles at the front/side of neck.

1. Sit tall, place one hand on collarbone
2. Tilt head away from that side
3. Rotate head slightly upward
4. Hold 20-30 seconds each side

**Feel it:** Front/side of neck

## Strengthening Exercises

### Chin Tuck with Resistance

Strengthens deep neck flexors—often weak in those with neck pain.

1. Place your fist against your chin
2. Perform a chin tuck while resisting with your fist
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Do 10-15 reps

### Neck Flexion Strengthening

1. Lie on your back, no pillow
2. Perform a chin tuck
3. Lift your head 1 inch off the floor (keep chin tucked)
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Lower with control
6. Do 10-12 reps

### Prone Neck Extension

Strengthens back of neck.

1. Lie face down, forehead on rolled towel
2. Lift head slightly while maintaining neutral neck
3. Don't crane neck back—just lift
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. Do 10-12 reps

### Isometric Resistance (All Directions)

Build strength without movement.

**Flexion:** Place palm on forehead, push head forward against hand resistance. Hold 5 seconds.

**Extension:** Clasp hands behind head, push head back against hand resistance. Hold 5 seconds.

**Lateral:** Place palm on side of head, push head sideways against resistance. Hold 5 seconds each side.

**Rotation:** Place palm on side of face, try to turn head against resistance. Hold 5 seconds each side.

Do each direction 5-10 times.

### Scapular Squeezes

Strengthens muscles that support neck posture.

1. Sit or stand tall
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Release
5. Do 15-20 reps

### Prone Y-T-W Raises

Strengthens upper back muscles that support good neck position.

1. Lie face down
2. Y: Arms overhead at 45°, thumbs up, lift and hold 5 seconds
3. T: Arms to sides, thumbs up, lift and hold 5 seconds
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades, hold 5 seconds
5. Do 8-10 of each

## Mobility Exercises

### Neck Circles

Gentle movement through full range.

1. Let head drop forward
2. Slowly roll head in a circle
3. Go gently—don't force
4. Do 5-10 circles each direction

**Note:** Skip if circles cause pain or dizziness.

### Neck Rotations

1. Look straight ahead
2. Slowly turn head to look over one shoulder
3. Hold briefly
4. Return and rotate to other side
5. Do 10-12 total

### Neck Side Bends

1. Look straight ahead
2. Tilt head toward one shoulder (ear toward shoulder)
3. Hold briefly
4. Return and tilt to other side
5. Do 10-12 total

## Daily Routine for Neck Pain

### Morning (5 minutes)

- Chin tucks: 10 reps
- Neck rotations: 10 total
- Upper trap stretch: 20 seconds each side
- Levator scapulae stretch: 20 seconds each side
- Scapular squeezes: 15 reps

### At Work (Every 1-2 Hours, 2 minutes)

- Chin tucks: 5-10 reps
- Neck rotations: 5 each direction
- Shoulder rolls: 10 each direction
- Quick upper trap stretch: 15 seconds each side

### Evening (10 minutes)

- All morning stretches with longer holds (30 seconds)
- Isometric strengthening: All 4 directions, 5 reps each
- Prone Y-T-W: 8 reps each position
- Chin tuck strengthening: 10 reps
- Gentle neck circles: 5 each direction

## Posture and Habits

Exercises help, but habits matter more for long-term relief.

### Screen Position

- Computer monitor at eye level
- Top of screen at or slightly below eye level
- Screen 20-26 inches from eyes
- Phone at eye level when possible (not looking down)

### Workstation Setup

- Chair supporting lower back
- Feet flat on floor
- Keyboard at elbow height
- Avoid hunching forward

### Sleeping Position

**Best for neck:** Back or side sleeping

**Pillow guidance:**
- Side sleepers: Pillow should fill gap between shoulder and head, keeping neck neutral
- Back sleepers: Pillow should support natural curve without pushing head forward
- Avoid stomach sleeping (forces neck rotation)

### Movement Breaks

- Change position every 30-60 minutes
- Stand, stretch, walk around
- Don't hold any position for extended periods

### Stress Management

Stress creates tension that accumulates in the neck and shoulders. Address stress through:
- Regular exercise
- Deep breathing
- Adequate sleep
- Whatever relaxation techniques work for you

## Heat vs. Ice

**Heat:**
- Good for muscle tension and stiffness
- Increases blood flow
- Relaxes tight muscles
- Use warm compress for 15-20 minutes

**Ice:**
- Better for acute inflammation
- Use for new injury or acute flare-up
- 15-20 minutes at a time
- Wrap ice in towel—don't apply directly to skin

**General rule:** Heat for chronic tension, ice for acute injury or inflammation.

## What to Avoid

### Quick Neck Rolls with Force

Aggressive, fast neck rotations can strain muscles or irritate joints. Keep movements slow and controlled.

### Cracking Your Own Neck

Habitual self-manipulation can create hypermobility and dependency. If you feel the need to constantly crack your neck, address the underlying tightness instead.

### Heavy Neck Exercises When Painful

If you're in acute pain, focus on gentle stretching and mobility. Add strengthening once pain subsides.

### Ignoring Persistent Pain

Pain that doesn't improve with self-care deserves professional evaluation.

## When It's Not Getting Better

If neck pain persists despite 2-3 weeks of consistent self-care:

1. **See a professional:** Doctor, physical therapist, or chiropractor can properly diagnose the issue
2. **Consider imaging:** X-ray or MRI may reveal structural problems
3. **Try manual therapy:** Hands-on treatment can address issues exercises can't
4. **Evaluate ergonomics:** Have someone assess your workstation setup
5. **Check your pillow:** It may be time for a new one

## The Bottom Line

Most neck pain improves with:

1. **Regular stretching** of tight muscles (traps, levator, scalenes)
2. **Strengthening** of deep neck flexors and upper back
3. **Mobility work** to maintain range of motion
4. **Posture improvement** at work and while using devices
5. **Movement breaks** to avoid prolonged positioning
6. **Stress management** to reduce tension

Start with the daily routine above. Be consistent—a few minutes daily beats occasional long sessions.

Your neck supports your head all day, every day. Give it the care it deserves, and the pain will subside.
