# Exercises After Hip Replacement: Complete Recovery Guide

Hip replacement surgery can be life-changing, relieving years of pain and restoring mobility. But the surgery is just the beginning—your rehabilitation determines how well you'll move for years to come. This guide covers what to expect and how to exercise safely based on your surgical approach.

## Understanding Your Precautions

Your hip replacement was done through one of several approaches, each with different precautions:

### Posterior Approach (Most Common)
**Precautions (typically 6-12 weeks):**
- No bending hip past 90 degrees
- No crossing legs or ankles
- No turning toes inward (internal rotation)
- No twisting at the hip

### Anterior Approach
**Precautions (often fewer/shorter):**
- No excessive extension (leg going behind you)
- No external rotation combined with extension
- Many surgeons allow more freedom earlier

### Lateral Approach
**Precautions vary** — follow your surgeon's specific guidelines.

**Critical:** Always confirm your specific precautions with your surgical team. The guidelines below assume posterior approach precautions unless noted.

## Goals After Hip Replacement

**Primary goals:**
- Walk safely and independently
- Manage stairs
- Return to daily activities without pain
- Maintain hip precautions until cleared

**Typical timeline:**
- Walking with walker: Days 1-7
- Walking with cane: Weeks 2-4
- Walking unassisted: Weeks 4-8
- Driving (if right hip, automatic): 4-6 weeks
- Return to most activities: 3-6 months

## Phase 1: Hospital Phase (Days 1-3)

### Ankle Pumps
1. Pump feet up and down vigorously
2. 20-30 reps, every hour while awake
3. Prevents blood clots

### Quad Sets
1. Lying flat, tighten thigh muscle
2. Press back of knee into bed
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10-15 reps, hourly

### Glute Squeezes
1. Lying flat, squeeze buttocks together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 10-15 reps, several times daily

### Heel Slides (Modified)
1. Lying flat, slide heel toward buttocks
2. **Stop before hip reaches 90 degrees**
3. Slide back out
4. 10-15 reps, 3-4x daily

### Abduction/Adduction (In Bed)
1. Lying flat, slide operated leg out to side
2. Keep toes pointing up (not turned in)
3. Return to center (don't cross midline)
4. 10-15 reps, 3-4x daily

### Standing and Walking
- Stand with walker, weight bear as allowed
- Short walks in hallway
- Focus on heel-toe pattern
- Keep toes pointing forward

## Phase 2: Early Home Phase (Weeks 1-3)

Continue all hospital exercises with increased repetitions.

### Standing Hip Abduction
1. Hold walker or counter
2. Lift operated leg out to side
3. Keep toes forward, trunk upright
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Standing Hip Extension
1. Hold onto support
2. Move operated leg backward
3. Don't lean forward or arch back
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Standing Marching
1. Hold onto support
2. Lift knee up (staying under 90 degrees)
3. Lower with control
4. 10-15 reps each leg

### Seated Knee Extension
1. Sit in chair (use raised seat to maintain precautions)
2. Straighten operated leg
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Walking Program
- Increase distance gradually
- 4-6 short walks daily (10-15 minutes each)
- Progress from walker to cane when stable
- Ice hip after walking if sore

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 3-6)

### Bridging
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. **Keep knees no closer than hip-width** (use pillow between knees if needed)
3. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. Lower slowly
6. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Standing Hip Flexion
1. Hold onto support
2. Lift knee toward chest (respect 90-degree limit if still on precautions)
3. Lower with control
4. 10-15 reps each leg

### Heel Raises
1. Hold onto support
2. Rise up onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Partial Squats
1. Hold onto support, feet hip-width apart
2. Bend knees 30-45 degrees
3. Keep weight even on both legs
4. Return to standing
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Step-Ups (Low Step)
1. Use 4-inch step
2. Step up with operated leg
3. Bring other foot up
4. Step down with non-operated leg first
5. 10 reps, 2 sets

### Stationary Bike
- Raise seat high (hip stays above 90 degrees)
- Low resistance
- 10-15 minutes, 1-2x daily
- Excellent for hip mobility and endurance

## Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 6-12+)

As precautions are lifted (confirm with your surgeon):

### Side-Lying Hip Abduction
1. Lie on non-operated side
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. Keep leg in line with body (not forward)
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Clamshells
1. Lie on non-operated side, knees bent
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Don't let hips roll backward
4. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

### Single-Leg Balance
1. Stand near support
2. Balance on operated leg
3. Use support as needed, progress to less support
4. 30 seconds, 3-5 reps

### Step-Downs
1. Stand on step with operated leg
2. Lower non-operated foot toward floor
3. Touch lightly, return up
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Lateral Band Walks
1. Place resistance band around ankles
2. Step sideways with operated leg
3. Follow with other leg
4. 10-15 steps each direction, 2-3 sets

### Lunges (When Cleared)
1. Hold onto support initially
2. Step forward, lower gently
3. Keep operated hip from internally rotating
4. 10 reps each leg

## Precautions in Daily Life

### Sitting
- Use chairs with armrests
- Keep hips above knees (raised toilet seat, cushions)
- Don't cross legs
- Stand by pushing through arms, not leaning forward

### Sleeping
- Sleep on your back or non-operated side
- If on side, place pillow between knees
- Don't roll operated leg across body

### Dressing
- Use long-handled shoe horn and sock aid
- Dress operated leg first
- Don't bend to tie shoes (use slip-ons or elastic laces)

### Getting In/Out of Bed
- Back up to bed, sit, then swing legs up together
- Keep legs apart (abduction pillow if needed)
- Reverse to get out

### Bathing
- Use shower chair and handheld sprayer initially
- Don't reach down to wash feet (use long-handled sponge)
- Non-slip mat in tub/shower

## Returning to Activities

**Usually safe after recovery:**
- Walking (unlimited)
- Swimming
- Cycling
- Golf
- Dancing (low-impact)
- Hiking (moderate terrain)
- Gardening (with precautions for bending)

**Discuss with your surgeon:**
- Tennis, pickleball (doubles may be okay)
- Skiing (downhill with caution)
- Horseback riding

**Generally not recommended:**
- Running/jogging
- High-impact aerobics
- Contact sports
- Heavy lifting with squatting

## Red Flags: Contact Your Doctor

- Fever over 101°F (38.3°C)
- Increased redness, warmth, or drainage at incision
- Calf pain, swelling, or redness (blood clot concern)
- Sudden popping sensation with pain (possible dislocation)
- Feeling like hip is "giving way"
- Leg length feels significantly unequal

## Sample Schedule (Weeks 2-6)

**Morning:**
- Ankle pumps (in bed)
- Lying exercises (heel slides, abduction)
- Standing exercises at counter
- Walk 10-15 minutes
- Ice

**Midday:**
- Seated exercises
- Walk 10-15 minutes
- Stationary bike 10 minutes

**Evening:**
- Full exercise routine
- Walk 10-15 minutes
- Ice and elevate

## Long-Term Success

**Keep your hip healthy:**
- Maintain healthy weight
- Stay active with appropriate activities
- Report any new pain or changes to your doctor
- Inform all healthcare providers about your hip replacement
- Take antibiotics before dental procedures if recommended

**Implant longevity:** Modern hip replacements typically last 20-25+ years with appropriate activity levels.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Know your surgical approach** — precautions vary significantly
2. **Respect the precautions** — dislocation is preventable
3. **Start exercises immediately** and progress consistently
4. **Use assistive devices** (raised seats, reachers) during precaution period
5. **Walking is your best exercise** — do it frequently
6. **Be patient** — full recovery takes 3-6 months, maximum benefit at 1 year
7. **Stay active for life** — your hip is designed to move

Your dedication to rehabilitation will determine how much you get out of your new hip. The work is worth it—most people return to an active, pain-free life.
