# Parkinson's Disease Exercises: Maintaining Movement and Function

Exercise isn't just helpful for Parkinson's disease—it may be one of the most powerful interventions available. Research increasingly shows that regular, intensive exercise can slow disease progression, improve symptoms, and maintain quality of life. The key is finding the right types of exercise and making them part of your routine.

## Why Exercise Is Essential for Parkinson's

Parkinson's disease affects the brain's ability to produce dopamine, leading to motor symptoms like tremor, rigidity, slowness (bradykinesia), and balance problems. Exercise helps by:

- **Promoting neuroplasticity**: Exercise stimulates brain changes that may protect against further decline
- **Improving dopamine efficiency**: Movement helps remaining dopamine work better
- **Maintaining muscle strength**: Counteracts the tendency toward weakness
- **Preserving flexibility**: Fights rigidity and stiffness
- **Improving balance**: Reduces fall risk
- **Enhancing mood**: Reduces depression and anxiety common in Parkinson's
- **Slowing progression**: Some studies suggest intensive exercise may slow disease advancement

## The Evidence: Exercise Changes Outcomes

Research highlights:
- People who exercise regularly have slower symptom progression
- High-intensity exercise shows greater benefits than low-intensity
- Exercise improves gait speed, balance, and quality of life
- Benefits are seen at all stages of the disease
- It's never too late to start

## Key Exercise Principles for Parkinson's

### Amplitude Matters (Think BIG)
Parkinson's causes movements to become smaller over time. Consciously making movements **larger than feels natural** helps counteract this. This is the foundation of LSVT BIG therapy.

### Intensity Counts
Higher-intensity exercise shows greater benefits. Aim for challenging workouts, not just gentle movement.

### Dual-Tasking
Practice doing two things at once (walking while talking, counting while moving) since this becomes more difficult with Parkinson's.

### Consistency Is Key
Regular exercise (ideally daily) provides the greatest benefits. Sporadic intense sessions are less effective than consistent moderate ones.

## Exercise Types for Parkinson's

### 1. Amplitude Training (LSVT BIG-Based)

LSVT BIG is a specialized therapy that emphasizes large, exaggerated movements. You can apply these principles:

**Maximal Daily Exercises:**

*Big Arm Reaches:*
1. Stand tall
2. Reach both arms overhead as HIGH as possible
3. Really stretch—bigger than feels natural
4. Lower and repeat
5. 10 reps, emphasis on amplitude

*Big Steps:*
1. Stand tall
2. Take an exaggeratedly large step forward
3. Drive arms in big motions
4. 10 steps forward, turn, 10 steps back

*Big Sit-to-Stand:*
1. Sit at edge of chair
2. Lean forward
3. Stand up FAST and tall
4. Sit down slowly
5. 10 reps

*Big Turning:*
1. Stand tall
2. Take large steps to turn in a circle
3. Exaggerate arm swing
4. 5 turns each direction

*Floor to Standing (if able):*
1. Lie on floor
2. Get up using large, purposeful movements
3. Practice the sequence regularly

### 2. Boxing and Combat Exercise

Boxing for Parkinson's (like Rock Steady Boxing) has become popular because it:
- Requires large, powerful movements
- Involves multidirectional movement
- Includes cognitive demands (combinations)
- Is engaging and motivating
- Improves balance and reaction time

**At-Home Boxing Moves:**

*Jab-Cross:*
1. Boxing stance (feet staggered)
2. Punch forward with lead hand (jab)
3. Rotate and punch with rear hand (cross)
4. Make movements BIG and powerful
5. 30 seconds, rest, repeat

*Hooks:*
1. From boxing stance
2. Swing arm in arc at shoulder height
3. Rotate hips and torso
4. Alternate sides
5. 30 seconds

*Speed Bag Simulation:*
1. Circle fists around each other
2. Keep elbows up
3. 30-60 seconds

### 3. Balance Training

Falls are a major concern in Parkinson's. Balance training should be a priority.

**Stance Progressions:**
1. Feet together (30 seconds)
2. Semi-tandem (heel to arch) (30 seconds each foot forward)
3. Tandem (heel to toe) (30 seconds each)
4. Single leg (work up to 30 seconds each)

Use support as needed, progress to less support.

**Weight Shifts:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart
2. Shift weight to right foot
3. Shift to left foot
4. Shift forward onto toes
5. Shift backward onto heels
6. 2-3 minutes

**Reaching Activities:**
1. Stand near support
2. Reach forward, sideways, and diagonally
3. Challenge your limits of stability
4. 2-3 minutes

### 4. Gait Training

Walking changes with Parkinson's (shuffling, freezing, reduced arm swing). Practice:

**Big Walking:**
1. Focus on heel strike first
2. Take longer steps than feel natural
3. Swing arms deliberately and largely
4. Practice daily

**Obstacle Course:**
Set up household items to step over and around. This challenges attention and adaptability.

**Metronome Walking:**
Use a metronome app to cue steps. This can help with freezing and improve rhythm.

**Backward and Sideways Walking:**
Practice in safe environment. Improves multidirectional mobility.

### 5. Flexibility and Stretching

Rigidity is a hallmark of Parkinson's. Daily stretching helps maintain mobility.

**Trunk Rotation:**
1. Sit in chair
2. Rotate torso to one side
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat other side

**Chest Stretch:**
1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearms on doorframe
3. Step forward, feeling stretch across chest
4. Hold 30 seconds

**Hip Flexor Stretch:**
1. Half-kneeling (one knee down)
2. Tuck pelvis under
3. Lean forward gently
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Hamstring Stretch:**
1. Sit on edge of chair
2. Extend one leg, heel on floor
3. Lean forward with straight back
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### 6. Strength Training

Maintaining muscle strength supports mobility and reduces fall risk.

**Squats:**
- Bodyweight to start
- Emphasize standing up quickly (power)
- 10-15 reps

**Heel Raises:**
- Hold support if needed
- Rise up quickly, lower slowly
- 15-20 reps

**Step-Ups:**
- Use sturdy step
- Alternate legs
- 10 reps each leg

**Wall Push-Ups:**
- Progress to regular push-ups if able
- 10-15 reps

### 7. Aerobic Exercise

Cardiovascular exercise provides significant benefits.

**Best options:**
- Cycling (stationary or regular): High-cadence cycling shows particular benefits
- Walking: With conscious attention to gait quality
- Swimming: Full-body, low-impact
- Dancing: Combines physical and cognitive challenges
- Treadmill: Can use handrails for safety

**Intensity goal:** Moderate to vigorous (where talking becomes difficult)

**Duration goal:** 30-45 minutes, 3-5 times per week

## Addressing Specific Symptoms

### For Freezing of Gait
- Visual cues: Step over lines on floor, use laser pointer
- Auditory cues: Metronome, music with strong beat
- Mental strategies: Count "1-2-3, step"
- Shifting weight side to side before stepping

### For Reduced Facial Expression
- Practice exaggerated facial movements daily
- Raise eyebrows high, smile wide, open mouth big
- Practice in mirror

### For Soft Voice
- LSVT LOUD therapy (speech-specific)
- Practice speaking loudly every day
- Read aloud with exaggerated projection

## Sample Weekly Schedule

**Daily (15-20 minutes):**
- Amplitude exercises (BIG movements): 10 minutes
- Balance practice: 5 minutes
- Stretching: 5 minutes

**3-4x Per Week (30-45 minutes):**
- Aerobic exercise (cycling, walking, swimming)

**2-3x Per Week (20-30 minutes):**
- Strength training
- Boxing exercises

**Consider adding:**
- Dance class (weekly)
- Tai chi (1-2x weekly)
- Group exercise for Parkinson's

## Safety Considerations

- Exercise when medications are working well ("on" time)
- Stay near support surfaces during balance work
- Clear clutter from exercise area
- Use proper footwear
- Stay hydrated
- Stop if you feel faint or unwell
- Have a plan for falls

## Finding Programs

- **Rock Steady Boxing**: Boxing-based program for Parkinson's
- **LSVT BIG**: Intensive amplitude-based therapy (requires certified clinician)
- **PWR! Moves**: Parkinson's-specific exercise approach
- **Dance for PD**: Dance classes designed for Parkinson's
- Local Parkinson's support groups often organize exercise classes

## Key Takeaways

1. **Exercise may slow Parkinson's progression** — it's that important
2. **Think BIG** — exaggerate movements to counteract motor changes
3. **Higher intensity shows greater benefits** — challenge yourself
4. **Balance training is essential** — reduces fall risk
5. **Consistency matters** — daily exercise beats sporadic intense workouts
6. **Multiple types are beneficial** — combine amplitude, strength, balance, cardio
7. **It's never too late** — benefits seen at all disease stages
8. **Find what you enjoy** — boxing, dancing, cycling—exercise you'll actually do

Parkinson's disease changes many things, but it doesn't have to take away your ability to move. With dedicated, intensive exercise, many people maintain function, independence, and quality of life far longer than previously thought possible.
