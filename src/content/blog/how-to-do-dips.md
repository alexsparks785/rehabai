Dips are one of the most effective upper body exercises you can do. They build serious chest, tricep, and shoulder strength using just your bodyweight—and they scale from beginner to advanced with ease.

But dips also have a reputation for causing shoulder problems when done incorrectly. Here's how to do them right.

## Why Dips?

### Compound Upper Body Builder

Dips work multiple muscle groups simultaneously:
- Triceps (primary for tricep dips)
- Chest (primary for chest dips)
- Front deltoids (shoulders)
- Core (stabilization)

### Progressive and Scalable

Unlike fixed machines, dips allow natural movement patterns. They scale from assisted (easier) to weighted (harder), providing years of progressive challenge.

### Functional Strength

The pushing pattern in dips transfers to real-world activities—pushing yourself up from a chair, off the ground, or over obstacles.

### Minimal Equipment

All you need is a dip station, parallel bars, or even two sturdy chairs.

## Tricep Dips vs. Chest Dips

The two main dip variations emphasize different muscles based on body position.

### Tricep-Focused Dips

**Body position:** Upright torso, minimal forward lean
**Elbow path:** Straight back, close to body
**Best for:** Tricep development, arm strength

### Chest-Focused Dips

**Body position:** Forward lean (about 30-45 degrees)
**Elbow path:** Flare out slightly wider
**Best for:** Chest development, overall mass

Both variations work the same muscles—the difference is in emphasis. Most people benefit from a moderate forward lean that trains both effectively.

## How to Do Dips

### Setup

1. Grip the bars (or handles) with palms facing in
2. Jump or push up to the starting position
3. Arms fully extended (not locked)
4. Shoulders down and back—don't shrug
5. Core engaged, legs crossed or straight

### The Descent

1. Begin lowering by bending elbows
2. Control the descent—don't drop
3. Allow elbows to track naturally (back for tricep focus, slightly out for chest)
4. Lower until upper arms are roughly parallel to ground (90° elbow bend)
5. Keep shoulders from rolling forward excessively

### The Ascent

1. Push through palms to drive back up
2. Extend arms fully without locking
3. Keep core engaged throughout
4. Don't let shoulders shrug up

### Breathing

- Inhale on the way down
- Exhale on the way up (during the push)

## Common Dip Mistakes

### Going Too Deep

**The problem:** Dropping past 90° puts extreme stress on shoulder joints.

**The fix:** Stop when upper arms are parallel to the ground. Deeper isn't better—it's riskier.

### Shoulders Rolling Forward

**The problem:** Shoulders round forward and roll up, increasing shoulder impingement risk.

**The fix:** Keep shoulder blades down and back throughout. Maintain a proud chest position.

### Flaring Elbows Too Wide

**The problem:** Excessive elbow flare increases shoulder stress.

**The fix:** Keep elbows at a moderate angle—not pinned to your sides, not flared out to 90°.

### Swinging or Kipping

**The problem:** Using momentum instead of muscle strength.

**The fix:** Control both the descent and ascent. No swinging or bouncing.

### Shrugging Shoulders

**The problem:** Shoulders rise up toward ears, reducing stability and increasing injury risk.

**The fix:** Actively depress shoulders (pull them down) before and during the movement.

### Half Reps

**The problem:** Only going partway down or up.

**The fix:** Full range of motion—lower to 90° elbow bend, rise to full extension.

## Dip Progressions

### Can't Do Dips Yet? Start Here:

#### Bench Dips

Easier version using a bench.

1. Sit on bench edge, hands beside hips
2. Walk feet forward, lifting butt off bench
3. Lower by bending elbows (to 90°)
4. Push back up

**Note:** Keep feet closer for easier, further for harder. Don't go too deep—this position is hard on shoulders.

#### Band-Assisted Dips

Use a resistance band for support.

1. Loop band over dip bars
2. Place knee(s) in band
3. Perform dip with band providing assistance
4. Use thinner bands as you get stronger

#### Negative Dips

Builds strength through the lowering phase.

1. Jump or step up to top position
2. Lower yourself as slowly as possible (5-10 seconds)
3. Step back up and repeat
4. Progress to full dips when you can do 3x5 slow negatives

#### Machine-Assisted Dips

Many gyms have assisted dip machines.

1. Select weight for assistance (more weight = more help)
2. Perform dips with machine support
3. Decrease assistance weight over time

### Full Dips

Once you can do negatives or assisted dips easily, try full dips. Even one or two reps is progress.

### Advanced: Weighted Dips

When bodyweight dips become easy (12-15+ reps), add weight.

**Options:**
- Dip belt with plates
- Weight vest
- Dumbbell between feet
- Chains around neck

Start with 5-10 lbs and progress gradually.

## Dip Variations

### Ring Dips

Dips on gymnastic rings—much harder due to instability.

Requires significant shoulder stability. Master bar dips thoroughly before attempting.

### Korean Dips

Hands behind you on a bar, facing away.

Extremely challenging variation that builds incredible tricep and shoulder strength.

### Straight Bar Dips

Dips on a single straight bar (like a pull-up bar).

Requires more forward lean and different technique. Great for calisthenics progression.

### Gironda Dips

Wide grip, extreme forward lean, elbows out.

Emphasizes chest heavily. Named after bodybuilding legend Vince Gironda.

### Close-Grip Dips

Hands closer together, elbows tight to body.

Maximum tricep emphasis.

## Programming Dips

### For Beginners

**Goal:** Build to 3 sets of 10 bodyweight dips

- Start with progression exercises (bench dips, negatives, assisted)
- Practice 3x per week
- Add reps each session when possible
- Move to next progression when current becomes easy

### For Hypertrophy (Muscle Building)

**Volume:** 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
**Frequency:** 2-3x per week
**Progression:** Add weight when you can do 3x12

### For Strength

**Volume:** 4-5 sets of 4-6 reps
**Frequency:** 1-2x per week
**Progression:** Add weight gradually (2.5-5 lbs)
**Rest:** 2-3 minutes between sets

### Integrating Dips into Your Routine

**Push day:** Pair with bench press and overhead press
**Upper body day:** Use as a primary or secondary movement
**Full body:** Include as upper body pushing exercise
**Superset:** Pair with pull-ups for efficient training

## Shoulder Safety

Dips can aggravate shoulders when done incorrectly. Protect yourself:

### Warning Signs

- Sharp pain during the movement
- Pain that persists after training
- Clicking or grinding with discomfort
- Feeling like shoulder might "pop out"

If you experience these, stop and assess.

### Shoulder-Safe Dip Tips

1. **Warm up thoroughly:** Include shoulder circles, band pull-aparts, push-ups before dips
2. **Don't go too deep:** Stop at 90° elbow bend
3. **Keep shoulders down and back:** No shrugging or rolling forward
4. **Control the movement:** No bouncing or dropping
5. **Progress gradually:** Don't jump to weighted dips too quickly
6. **Listen to your body:** Skip dips if your shoulders feel off

### If Dips Bother Your Shoulders

- Try different grip widths
- Reduce depth
- Use more forward lean (often more comfortable)
- Strengthen rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers
- Consider substituting close-grip bench press or push-ups temporarily
- See a professional if pain persists

## Common Questions

### How Deep Should I Go?

Until your upper arms are roughly parallel to the ground (90° elbow bend). Going deeper increases shoulder stress without significant benefit.

### Dips vs. Bench Press?

Both are excellent. Dips offer more natural movement pattern and core engagement. Bench allows heavier loading. Include both if possible.

### Can Dips Build a Big Chest?

Yes, especially chest-focused dips with forward lean. Combined with progressive overload (adding weight), they're excellent chest builders.

### How Many Dips Should I Be Able to Do?

There's no "should," but here are benchmarks:
- Beginner: 1-5 reps
- Intermediate: 10-20 reps
- Advanced: 20+ reps or significant added weight

### Why Can I Bench Press a Lot But Can't Do Dips?

Dips require more shoulder stability and core control. They're also a different movement pattern. Both strength and technique need development.

## The Bottom Line

Dips are a foundational upper body exercise:

1. **Setup properly:** Shoulders down, core tight, full grip on bars
2. **Control the descent:** Lower to 90° elbow bend, no deeper
3. **Push through:** Full extension without locking
4. **Progress appropriately:** Master bodyweight before adding weight
5. **Protect your shoulders:** Warm up, use good form, listen to your body

Build from assisted or negative dips to full bodyweight dips to weighted dips. Your chest and triceps will grow impressively with this one movement.

Time to dip.
