# Hamstring Exercises: Build Strong, Injury-Resistant Legs

The hamstrings are among the most commonly injured muscles, especially in athletes. Yet they're often undertrained compared to quads. Strong, flexible hamstrings protect your knees, improve athletic performance, and prevent the dreaded hamstring strain.

This guide covers everything you need for complete hamstring development.

## Hamstring Anatomy

The hamstrings consist of three muscles on the back of your thigh:

### Biceps Femoris
- Two heads: long and short
- Located on the outer portion
- The long head crosses both hip and knee

### Semitendinosus
- Long, thin muscle on the inner back thigh
- Crosses both hip and knee
- Often involved in strains

### Semimembranosus
- Deep muscle, underneath semitendinosus
- Crosses both hip and knee
- Provides thickness to inner hamstring

**Key insight:** Because most hamstring muscles cross both the hip and knee, they need to be trained through both joints for complete development.

## Functions of the Hamstrings

Understanding function improves training:

- **Knee flexion:** Bending the knee (leg curls)
- **Hip extension:** Driving the leg backward (deadlifts, bridges)
- **Deceleration:** Slowing down running, controlling descent

The deceleration function is why hamstring injuries often occur when sprinting or suddenly stopping—the muscles eccentrically contract under high force.

## Why Hamstring Strength Matters

### Injury Prevention
Strong hamstrings resist strains. They handle the forces of sprinting, jumping, and sudden stops better when properly trained.

### Knee Health
Hamstrings balance the quads and protect the ACL. Quad-dominant athletes with weak hamstrings face higher ACL injury risk.

### Athletic Performance
Hamstrings power:
- Sprinting (especially acceleration)
- Jumping
- Kicking
- Cutting and direction changes

### Lower Back Health
Weak or tight hamstrings force the lower back to compensate during bending and lifting.

## Best Hamstring Exercises

### Hip Hinge Movements

These train the hamstrings at the hip:

**Romanian Deadlift (RDL):**
1. Hold barbell or dumbbells at hip level
2. Slight knee bend, push hips back
3. Lower weights along legs
4. Feel stretch in hamstrings
5. Drive hips forward to stand
6. 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps

**Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift:**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Hinge at hip, reaching opposite leg back
3. Touch dumbbells toward floor
4. Return to standing
5. 3 sets of 8-10 per leg

**Good Mornings:**
1. Bar across upper back (like squat)
2. Slight knee bend, hinge at hips
3. Lower torso until hamstring stretch
4. Return to standing
5. 3 sets of 10-12 reps

**Stiff-Leg Deadlift:**
1. Similar to RDL but minimal knee bend
2. More hamstring stretch
3. Lower weight than RDL
4. 3 sets of 10-12 reps

### Knee Flexion Movements

These train the hamstrings at the knee:

**Lying Leg Curl:**
1. Lie face down on machine
2. Curl heels toward glutes
3. Squeeze at top
4. Lower with control
5. 3 sets of 10-15 reps

**Seated Leg Curl:**
1. Sit in machine, pad behind ankles
2. Curl heels under seat
3. Squeeze at full contraction
4. Control the return
5. 3 sets of 10-15 reps

**Nordic Hamstring Curl:**
The gold standard for injury prevention:
1. Kneel, ankles secured (partner or pad)
2. Keep body straight from knee to head
3. Lower slowly toward ground (as slow as possible)
4. Catch yourself at bottom, push back up
5. 3 sets of 3-6 reps (build up slowly)

**Sliding Leg Curl:**
1. Lie on back, heels on sliders or towel
2. Bridge up (hips off ground)
3. Curl heels toward glutes while staying bridged
4. Extend legs back out
5. 3 sets of 8-12 reps

### Compound Movements

**Conventional Deadlift:**
1. Bar over mid-foot, hip-width stance
2. Hinge and grip bar
3. Drive through floor, extending hips and knees
4. 3-4 sets of 5-8 reps

**Sumo Deadlift:**
1. Wide stance, toes out
2. Grip inside legs
3. Drive through heels
4. 3-4 sets of 5-8 reps

**Glute-Ham Raise:**
1. Set up in GHD or GHR machine
2. Lower body forward from knees
3. Pull yourself back using hamstrings
4. 3 sets of 6-10 reps

**Hip Thrust:**
Also trains glutes heavily:
1. Upper back on bench
2. Drive through heels to lift hips
3. Squeeze glutes and hamstrings at top
4. 3 sets of 10-12 reps

### Stability Ball Exercises

**Stability Ball Hamstring Curl:**
1. Lie on back, heels on ball
2. Bridge up
3. Curl ball toward you
4. Extend back out
5. 3 sets of 12-15 reps

**Single-Leg Ball Curl:**
1. Same setup, one leg at a time
2. Much harder
3. 3 sets of 8-10 per leg

## Sample Hamstring Workouts

### Quick Routine (15 minutes)

1. RDLs: 3 × 10
2. Lying leg curls: 3 × 12
3. Single-leg RDLs: 2 × 8 per leg

### Complete Hamstring Session

1. Romanian deadlifts: 4 × 10
2. Nordic curls (or lying curls): 3 × 6-8
3. Single-leg RDLs: 3 × 8 per leg
4. Sliding leg curls: 3 × 12
5. Good mornings: 2 × 12

### Injury Prevention Focus

1. Nordic curls: 3 × 4-6 (build up slowly)
2. RDLs: 3 × 10
3. Single-leg RDLs: 3 × 8 per leg
4. Eccentric leg curls (slow lowering): 3 × 8
5. Glute bridges: 3 × 15

### Athlete/Speed Focus

1. RDLs: 4 × 8
2. Nordic curls: 4 × 4-6
3. Single-leg RDLs: 3 × 8 per leg
4. Good mornings: 3 × 10
5. Hip thrusts: 3 × 10

### Home Workout

1. Single-leg RDLs: 4 × 10 per leg
2. Sliding leg curls: 3 × 12
3. Glute bridges (feet elevated): 3 × 15
4. Nordic curls (assisted): 3 × 5
5. Good mornings (bodyweight): 3 × 15

## Nordic Curl Progressions

Nordics are challenging but extremely effective. Build up gradually:

**Level 1: Negative Only**
- Use hands to push back up
- Focus on slow, controlled lowering (5+ seconds)

**Level 2: Band Assisted**
- Loop band around chest, attached in front
- Band helps on the way up

**Level 3: Partial Reps**
- Lower only halfway
- Pull yourself back up

**Level 4: Full Nordic**
- Full range of motion
- Control entire movement

**Level 5: Weighted Nordic**
- Hold weight at chest
- Only for advanced

## Form Tips

### For RDLs
- Push hips BACK, not down
- Keep bar/weights close to legs
- Feel stretch in hamstrings, not lower back
- Maintain neutral spine throughout

### For Leg Curls
- Don't hyperextend at the bottom
- Full contraction at top
- Control the negative (don't let weight drop)
- Keep hips down on lying curls

### For Nordics
- Keep body straight from knee to head
- Don't bend at hips
- Go as slow as possible on the way down
- Build up gradually—very challenging

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Rounding the Back on Deadlifts/RDLs
A rounded spine shifts stress to the lower back. Keep chest up and spine neutral.

### Quad Dominance
If you always feel squats in quads but never deadlifts in hamstrings, focus on the hip hinge pattern. Push hips back.

### Ignoring Eccentric Training
Hamstring injuries happen during eccentric (lengthening) contractions. Nordic curls and slow negatives are essential.

### Only Doing Leg Curls
Leg curls train knee flexion but not hip extension. Include RDLs and hip hinges.

### Skipping Single-Leg Work
Bilateral exercises hide imbalances. Include single-leg RDLs to expose and fix asymmetries.

### Stretching Cold Hamstrings
Don't aggressively stretch cold muscles. Warm up first, then stretch.

## Hamstring Stretches

### Standing Hamstring Stretch
1. Foot elevated on step or bench
2. Hinge at hip, reaching toward toes
3. Keep back flat
4. Hold 30-45 seconds per leg

### Lying Hamstring Stretch
1. Lie on back
2. Pull one leg toward chest, keeping it straight
3. Use strap or hands behind thigh
4. Hold 30-45 seconds per leg

### Seated Forward Fold
1. Sit with legs extended
2. Reach toward toes
3. Keep back as flat as possible
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Doorway Hamstring Stretch
1. Lie in doorway
2. Leg up against doorframe
3. Scoot closer to deepen stretch
4. Hold 60 seconds per leg

## Programming Tips

### Frequency
- 2-3 hamstring sessions per week
- Can train hamstrings with back or leg days
- Allow 48-72 hours between heavy sessions

### Volume
- 8-12 direct sets per week minimum
- Athletes may need more
- Quality over quantity

### Exercise Selection
Include at least:
- One hip hinge (RDL, good morning)
- One knee flexion (leg curl, Nordic)
- One single-leg movement

### Balance with Quads
Hamstring-to-quad strength ratio should be at least 60%. If quads are much stronger, prioritize hamstrings.

## For Specific Goals

### For Injury Prevention
- Prioritize Nordic curls (build up slowly)
- Include eccentric training
- Single-leg work for imbalance correction
- Don't neglect flexibility

### For Running/Sprinting
- Nordics are essential
- Single-leg RDLs for stability
- Hip extension power (RDLs, hip thrusts)
- Build volume gradually

### For Muscle Size
- Higher volume (12-15 sets/week)
- Include both hip hinge and knee flexion
- Progressive overload over time
- Full range of motion

### For Lower Back Pain
- Strengthen without aggravating back
- Good mornings with light weight
- Leg curls (no spinal load)
- Build hip hinge pattern slowly

## Conclusion

Strong hamstrings are essential for athletic performance, injury prevention, and balanced leg development. The key is training both functions—hip extension (RDLs) and knee flexion (curls)—while including eccentric work like Nordic curls.

Don't neglect single-leg exercises to catch imbalances. Stretch after training, not before. And if you're an athlete, Nordic curls should be a staple in your program.

Train hamstrings 2-3 times per week, focus on quality movement, and progress gradually. Your knees, back, and athletic performance will thank you.
