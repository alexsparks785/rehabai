# Lunge Progressions: Build Single-Leg Strength and Stability

Squats are essential—but they hide your weaknesses. When both legs work together, the strong side compensates for the weak. Lunges expose the truth: imbalances, instability, and single-leg weakness.

That's why lunges matter. They build the unilateral strength and stability that transfers to walking, running, climbing stairs, and virtually every athletic movement.

This guide takes you from assisted split squats through advanced loaded lunges, building real single-leg strength at every level.

## Why Lunges?

### Exposing Imbalances

Most people have one leg stronger than the other. Bilateral exercises (squats, leg press) mask this. Lunges reveal it immediately—and fix it.

### Athletic Transfer

Sports happen one leg at a time. Running, jumping, cutting, kicking—all single-leg dominant. Lunges build the specific strength patterns athletes need.

### Functional Strength

Getting up from the floor. Climbing stairs. Stepping over obstacles. Life is full of single-leg movements. Lunges prepare you.

### Benefits

- Identifies and corrects leg imbalances
- Builds balance and proprioception
- Develops hip stability
- Strengthens through full range of motion
- Improves mobility while building strength
- Transfers directly to athletic movement

## Lunge Fundamentals

### The Movement Pattern

All lunges share common elements:

1. **Split stance**: One foot forward, one back
2. **Descent**: Lower until back knee approaches floor
3. **Front shin**: Relatively vertical (knee over ankle)
4. **Torso**: Upright, core engaged
5. **Ascent**: Drive through front foot to return

### Key Positions

**Top**: Standing tall, feet together or in split stance

**Bottom**: Back knee 1-2 inches from floor, front thigh parallel to floor, torso upright

### Common Limiting Factors

- **Balance**: Can't maintain position without wobbling
- **Hip flexor tightness**: Back hip can't extend fully
- **Ankle mobility**: Front shin can't stay vertical
- **Knee stability**: Front knee caves or tracks poorly
- **Strength**: Can't control the descent or drive up

## Level 1: Foundation (Week 1-2)

### 1A: Split Squat Hold

Before moving, own the position:

1. Split stance, both feet flat
2. Lower to bottom position
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. Focus on balance and position
5. Both legs, 3-4 holds each

### 1B: Assisted Split Squat

1. Hold doorframe, TRX, or sturdy object
2. Split stance with support for balance
3. Lower until back knee nearly touches floor
4. Use assistance as needed
5. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

### 1C: Split Squat (Static)

The foundation lunge—feet don't move:

1. Split stance, front foot flat, back foot on ball
2. Lower straight down until back knee nearly touches
3. Drive through front foot to stand
4. All reps on one leg, then switch
5. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

**Key cue**: "Straight down, straight up"—don't lunge forward.

### 1D: Elevated Front Foot Split Squat

1. Front foot on low step (2-4 inches)
2. Increases range of motion slightly
3. Same split squat pattern
4. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

## Level 2: Basic Lunges (Week 3-4)

### 2A: Reverse Lunge

Easier to balance than forward lunges:

1. Start standing, feet together
2. Step back with one foot
3. Lower until back knee nearly touches
4. Push through front foot to return to standing
5. Alternate legs or all reps one side
6. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

**Why reverse first**: Stepping back is more controlled than stepping forward. Master this before forward lunges.

### 2B: Forward Lunge

1. Start standing, feet together
2. Step forward with one foot
3. Lower until back knee nearly touches
4. Push through front foot to return
5. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

Requires more deceleration control than reverse lunges.

### 2C: Lateral Lunge

1. Start standing, feet together
2. Step wide to one side
3. Sit back and down into stepping leg
4. Keep trailing leg straight
5. Push back to start
6. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

Trains the frontal plane—essential for athletic movement.

### 2D: Curtsy Lunge

1. Step one foot behind and across your body
2. Lower into lunge position
3. Emphasizes glute medius
4. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

## Level 3: Rear Foot Elevated (Week 5-7)

### 3A: Bulgarian Split Squat

The king of single-leg exercises:

1. Back foot on bench behind you
2. Front foot far enough forward for vertical shin at bottom
3. Lower until front thigh parallel or below
4. Drive through front foot
5. 8-12 per leg, 3-4 sets

**Setup tips**:
- Start close to bench, adjust forward as needed
- Back foot: laces down or ball of foot—personal preference
- Most of the weight stays on front leg

### 3B: Bulgarian with Pause

1. Same as above
2. Pause 2-3 seconds at bottom
3. Eliminates stretch reflex
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

### 3C: Bulgarian with Deficit

1. Front foot on small platform (2-4 inches)
2. Increases range of motion
3. Greater challenge
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

### 3D: Front Foot Elevated Reverse Lunge

Alternative to Bulgarian:

1. Front foot on 4-6 inch step
2. Reverse lunge from elevated position
3. Increased range of motion
4. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

## Level 4: Loaded Lunges (Week 8-10)

### 4A: Goblet Split Squat

1. Hold dumbbell or kettlebell at chest
2. Standard split squat pattern
3. 10-12 per leg, 3-4 sets

### 4B: Dumbbell Lunges

1. Dumbbells at sides (farmer carry position)
2. Forward, reverse, or walking lunges
3. 10-12 per leg, 3-4 sets

### 4C: Dumbbell Bulgarian Split Squat

1. Dumbbells at sides
2. Bulgarian split squat pattern
3. 8-10 per leg, 3-4 sets

This is a primary strength movement—can be loaded heavy.

### 4D: Barbell Reverse Lunge

1. Barbell on back (back squat position)
2. Reverse lunge pattern
3. 8-10 per leg, 3-4 sets

### 4E: Front Rack Lunges

1. Barbell or kettlebells in front rack position
2. Any lunge variation
3. Increased core demand
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

## Level 5: Dynamic Variations (Week 11-13)

### 5A: Walking Lunge

1. Forward lunge, but don't return to start
2. Bring back foot through to next lunge
3. Continuous walking pattern
4. 10-12 per leg (20-24 total steps), 3 sets

### 5B: Reverse Lunge to Knee Drive

1. Reverse lunge
2. Drive back leg up into high knee
3. Return to reverse lunge
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

Adds explosive hip flexion.

### 5C: Jumping Lunges

1. Lunge position
2. Jump and switch legs in air
3. Land in opposite lunge
4. Continue alternating
5. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

Plyometric power development.

### 5D: Lateral Lunge to Balance

1. Lateral lunge to one side
2. Push back to single-leg stand on opposite leg
3. Brief balance hold
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

## Level 6: Advanced Variations (Week 14+)

### 6A: Deficit Bulgarian Split Squat (Loaded)

1. Front foot elevated, back foot on bench
2. Heavy dumbbell load
3. Full range of motion
4. 6-8 per leg, 4 sets

### 6B: Barbell Walking Lunge (Heavy)

1. Barbell on back
2. Walking lunge pattern
3. Challenging load
4. 8-10 per leg, 3-4 sets

### 6C: Single-Leg Squat (Pistol Progression)

1. Assisted pistol with TRX or doorframe
2. Box pistol (squat to low box on one leg)
3. Full pistol squat
4. 5-8 per leg, 3 sets

Requires significant mobility and strength.

### 6D: Skater Squat

1. Single-leg squat, trailing leg goes behind
2. Touch trailing knee to floor (or pad)
3. Stand on single leg
4. 6-8 per leg, 3 sets

Similar demand to pistol with less mobility requirement.

## Step-Up Progressions

Step-ups complement lunges for complete single-leg training.

### Basic Step-Up

1. Step onto box with one foot
2. Stand fully on box
3. Lower with control
4. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

### Lateral Step-Up

1. Box at your side
2. Step up sideways
3. 10-12 per leg, 3 sets

### High Box Step-Up

1. Box at knee height or higher
2. Same pattern
3. Greater range of motion
4. 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

### Loaded Step-Up

1. Add dumbbells or barbell
2. 8-10 per leg, 3-4 sets

## Programming Lunges

### For Beginners

- 2 times per week
- Split squats: 3x12 per leg
- Reverse lunges: 3x10 per leg
- Focus on balance and control

### For Strength

- 2 times per week
- Bulgarian split squat: 4x6-8 per leg (loaded)
- Walking lunges: 3x10 per leg
- Include with or after bilateral work

### For Athletes

- 2-3 times per week
- Include dynamic variations
- Lateral and rotational lunges
- Plyometric lunges for power

### For Muscle Building

- 2 times per week
- Higher volume: 3-4 sets of 10-15
- Multiple variations per session
- Controlled tempo (3-1-2)

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

### Mistake: Knee Caving

**Why**: Weak glute medius, poor motor control

**Fix**: Cue "knee out." Strengthen with lateral band walks. Reduce load.

### Mistake: Torso Leaning Forward

**Why**: Tight hip flexors, weak quads, habit

**Fix**: Cue "chest up." Work hip flexor mobility. Reduce range initially.

### Mistake: Front Knee Too Far Forward

**Why**: Stepping too short, poor setup

**Fix**: Step further. Keep shin more vertical. Lower "down" not "forward."

### Mistake: Wobbly Balance

**Why**: Weak stabilizers, moving too fast

**Fix**: Slow down. Hold bottom position. Practice split squat holds.

### Mistake: Rising on Front Toes

**Why**: Ankle mobility limitation, weight too far forward

**Fix**: Weight in whole foot. Work ankle mobility. Elevate heel temporarily.

### Mistake: Asymmetric Strength

**Why**: Pre-existing imbalance

**Fix**: Start with weaker leg. Match reps exactly. May need extra weak-side work.

## Testing Single-Leg Strength

### Basic Standards

- 10 bodyweight reverse lunges each leg without balance loss
- 10 Bulgarian split squats each leg (bodyweight)
- Step onto knee-height box with control

### Strength Standards

- Bulgarian split squat with 50% bodyweight (dumbbells) for 8 reps
- Walking lunges with 25% bodyweight (dumbbells) for 20 steps
- Single-leg squat to low box (parallel thigh)

### Balance Check

Can you:
- Hold bottom of split squat for 30 seconds?
- Perform 10 slow reverse lunges without touching anything?
- Stand on one leg with eyes closed for 30 seconds?

## Lunges vs. Squats

Both essential—they're different tools:

**Squats**:
- Bilateral—both legs share load
- Higher loading potential
- Easier to learn
- Hides imbalances

**Lunges**:
- Unilateral—one leg dominates
- Exposes and fixes imbalances
- Greater balance demand
- Better athletic transfer

**Programming**: Include both. Squats as primary strength builder, lunges for balance and single-leg development.

## The Bottom Line

Lunges aren't optional. They build the single-leg strength, stability, and balance that bilateral movements can't develop. They expose weaknesses and fix them.

Start with split squats. Progress to reverse lunges. Build toward Bulgarian split squats. Your legs—and your athletic performance—will thank you.

## Quick Reference

**Level 1**: Split squat holds → Assisted split squats → Static split squats
**Level 2**: Reverse lunges → Forward lunges → Lateral lunges
**Level 3**: Bulgarian split squats (and progressions)
**Level 4**: Loaded variations (dumbbells, barbell)
**Level 5**: Walking lunges, jump lunges, dynamic variations
**Level 6**: Heavy loaded, pistol progressions, skater squats

**Form cues**: Torso upright, front shin vertical, knee tracks over toes, lower straight down

**Progression rule**: 12 perfect reps before adding load or difficulty
