The push-up is the most accessible upper body exercise there is. No equipment needed, can be done anywhere, and scales from complete beginner to advanced athlete.

Yet most people do push-ups wrong—poor form that limits results and can cause shoulder or wrist pain. Here's how to do them right.

## The Perfect Push-Up

### Starting Position

1. **Hands:** Slightly wider than shoulder-width, fingers spread and pointing forward (or slightly outward)
2. **Arms:** Straight but not locked, elbows fully extended
3. **Body:** Straight line from head to heels—no sagging hips, no piked butt
4. **Core:** Braced tight, glutes squeezed
5. **Feet:** Together or up to hip-width apart
6. **Head:** Neutral, looking at the floor slightly ahead of your hands

### The Descent

1. Bend your elbows to lower your body
2. Elbows at approximately 45 degrees from your body (not flared out at 90°, not pinned at your sides)
3. Lower until your chest nearly touches the floor (or touches lightly)
4. Keep your body in a straight line throughout
5. Control the descent—2 seconds down is a good tempo

### The Ascent

1. Push through your palms to extend your arms
2. Drive the floor away from you
3. Keep your core tight—don't let your hips sag or rise first
4. Fully extend your arms at the top
5. Maintain the straight body line throughout

### Breathing

- **Inhale** on the way down
- **Exhale** on the way up (during the effort)

## Common Push-Up Mistakes

### Sagging Hips

**The problem:** Lower back arches, hips drop toward the floor.

**Why it happens:** Weak core, fatigue, not engaging glutes.

**The fix:** Squeeze your glutes and brace your abs throughout. Think about keeping your body rigid like a plank. If you can't maintain position, do an easier variation.

### Pike Position (Butt Too High)

**The problem:** Hips pike up, turning it into a shoulder exercise rather than chest.

**Why it happens:** Trying to make it easier, weak core, habit.

**The fix:** Maintain a straight line. Have someone check your position or film yourself.

### Flared Elbows (T-Shape)

**The problem:** Elbows point straight out at 90 degrees from body.

**Why it happens:** Feels natural to many people, habit from seeing incorrect demos.

**The fix:** Keep elbows at 45-degree angle from your body. This protects your shoulders and engages your chest better.

### Partial Range of Motion

**The problem:** Only going halfway down.

**Why it happens:** Weakness, not knowing what full range feels like, ego (wanting more reps).

**The fix:** Lower until your chest nearly touches the floor. If you can't do full range, use an easier progression.

### Head Dropping or Craning

**The problem:** Looking up or letting head hang down.

**Why it happens:** Fatigue, trying to reach the floor with face rather than chest.

**The fix:** Keep neck neutral with spine. Look at a spot on the floor slightly ahead of your hands.

### Wrists Caving or Hurting

**The problem:** Wrist discomfort or wrists not staying neutral.

**Why it happens:** Limited wrist mobility, weak grip, poor hand placement.

**The fix:** Grip the floor with your fingers, ensure weight is distributed across entire palm, work on wrist mobility. Push-up handles or dumbbells can help if wrist mobility is limited.

## Push-Up Progressions

Can't do a standard push-up yet? Here's how to build up.

### Level 1: Wall Push-Ups

1. Stand facing a wall, arms extended
2. Place hands on wall at shoulder height
3. Lean in, bending elbows, then push back
4. Keep body straight

**Progress when:** You can do 3 sets of 15-20 easily.

### Level 2: Incline Push-Ups

1. Hands on a raised surface (bench, step, sturdy table)
2. Standard push-up form
3. Lower height as you get stronger

**Progress when:** You can do 3 sets of 12-15 at a moderate incline.

### Level 3: Knee Push-Ups

1. Standard push-up position but knees on the ground
2. Still maintain straight line from head to knees
3. Cross ankles and lift feet off ground

**Progress when:** You can do 3 sets of 12-15 with good form.

### Level 4: Negative Push-Ups

1. Start at top of push-up position
2. Lower yourself slowly (5 seconds) to the ground
3. Reset at the top however you need to (knees, stand up)
4. Repeat

**Progress when:** You can do 3 sets of 8 slow negatives.

### Level 5: Standard Push-Ups

Full push-ups with proper form. You've arrived.

### Level 6+: Advanced Variations

Once standard push-ups are easy, progress with:
- Close-grip push-ups
- Wide push-ups
- Decline push-ups (feet elevated)
- Diamond push-ups
- Archer push-ups
- One-arm push-up progressions
- Weighted push-ups (vest or plate on back)

## Push-Up Variations

### Close-Grip (Tricep Focus)

Hands closer together (inside shoulder width). Targets triceps more.

### Wide-Grip (Chest Focus)

Hands wider than shoulders. More chest emphasis, less tricep.

### Diamond Push-Ups

Hands together forming a diamond shape. Intense tricep focus. Ensure elbows stay close to body.

### Decline Push-Ups

Feet elevated on a bench or step. Increases difficulty and shifts emphasis to upper chest and shoulders.

### Incline Push-Ups

Hands elevated. Easier than standard, more lower chest emphasis.

### Pause Push-Ups

Pause at the bottom for 2-3 seconds. Eliminates momentum and builds strength.

### Tempo Push-Ups

Slow descent (3-5 seconds) or slow ascent. Increases time under tension.

### Explosive/Clap Push-Ups

Push up explosively so hands leave the ground. Builds power. Only for those who have mastered standard push-ups.

### Spiderman Push-Ups

Bring knee toward elbow at the bottom of each rep. Adds core and hip flexor work.

### Pike Push-Ups

Hips high in a pike position, lower head toward floor. Shoulder-focused, builds toward handstand push-ups.

## Programming Push-Ups

### For Beginners

**Goal:** Build to 3 sets of 10 standard push-ups

- Do push-ups 3x per week
- Use appropriate progression level
- Add reps each session when possible
- Move to harder progression when current becomes easy

### For General Fitness

**Maintenance:** 3 sets of 15-20, 2-3x per week

**Improvement:** 
- Pyramid: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 (25 total)
- Or: Max reps, rest 1 min, repeat 3-4 times

### For Strength

- Use harder variations (decline, weighted, one-arm progressions)
- Lower reps (5-10) with challenging variation
- Rest 2-3 minutes between sets

### Grease the Groove

Do push-ups throughout the day—never to failure, just easy sets whenever you pass a good spot. Example: 5 push-ups every hour. Builds volume without fatigue.

## Push-Ups in a Workout

### As Primary Exercise

Do push-ups early in workout when fresh:
- 4 sets of 10-15
- Or 3 sets to near-failure
- Progress by adding reps, slowing tempo, or harder variations

### As Accessory

After bench press or other pressing:
- 2-3 sets of 12-20
- Focus on pump and form
- Can use easier variations when fatigued

### Superset Partner

Push-ups pair well with:
- Rows (opposing muscle group)
- Squats (different body part)
- Core exercises

### Finisher

End a pushing workout with:
- One max set
- Drop set (standard → incline → wall)
- AMRAP in 2 minutes

## Troubleshooting

### "My wrists hurt"

- Work on wrist mobility (circles, stretches)
- Use push-up handles or fists
- Ensure weight is distributed, not all on heel of palm

### "My shoulders hurt"

- Don't flare elbows—keep them at 45°
- Don't go too deep if it causes pain
- Strengthen rotator cuff with external rotation exercises
- Check that you're not shrugging shoulders toward ears

### "I can't do any yet"

- Start with wall push-ups
- Progress through the stages above
- Be patient—strength takes time

### "I'm stuck at the same number"

- Try a harder variation for fewer reps
- Try grease the groove approach
- Add pause or tempo variations
- Check form—are you doing full range?

### "I feel it in my lower back"

- Your core is likely giving out
- Squeeze glutes harder
- Brace abs like expecting a punch
- Do fewer reps with better form

## The Bottom Line

The push-up is simple but not easy to master:

1. **Maintain a straight body line** from head to heels
2. **Lower until chest nearly touches floor** (full range)
3. **Keep elbows at 45 degrees** from body
4. **Control the movement** both directions
5. **Progress appropriately** for your level

Start where you are. Progress gradually. Master the basics before advancing to fancy variations.

Push-ups have built strong, athletic bodies for centuries. They'll work for you too.
