# Diastasis Recti Exercises: Healing Abdominal Separation

Diastasis recti—separation of the abdominal muscles—affects up to 60% of women postpartum and can also occur in men and non-pregnant individuals. If you've noticed a ridge or bulge down your midline, difficulty with core tasks, or a persistent "pooch" despite exercise, diastasis recti may be the cause. The good news: targeted exercises can significantly improve both function and appearance.

## Understanding Diastasis Recti

Diastasis recti occurs when the linea alba (the connective tissue between your "six-pack" muscles) stretches and thins, allowing the two sides of the rectus abdominis to separate.

**Common causes:**
- Pregnancy (most common)
- Rapid weight gain/loss
- Improper exercise technique
- Repeated heavy lifting
- Genetics (some people are predisposed)

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Visible bulge or ridge when doing a sit-up motion
- "Doming" of the belly with exertion
- Lower back pain
- Poor posture
- Pelvic floor dysfunction
- Difficulty with core-intensive activities
- Persistent belly bulge despite fitness efforts

## How to Check for Diastasis Recti

**Self-assessment:**
1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Place fingers horizontally across your midline at belly button level
3. Lift your head and shoulders slightly (mini crunch)
4. Feel for a gap between the muscles and note:
   - **Width**: How many fingers fit in the gap?
   - **Depth**: Do fingers sink in deeply or is there tension?
5. Check above and below belly button too

**Interpretation:**
- 1-2 finger width with tension underneath: Normal/mild
- 2+ finger width and/or soft, deep gap: Significant diastasis
- The ability to generate tension matters as much as gap size

**Note:** A professional assessment from a pelvic floor physical therapist is ideal.

## The Goal: Function Over Gap Size

Research shows that **functional recovery** (ability to generate tension across the midline) matters more than gap closure. Some people have a small gap but poor function; others have a larger gap but can generate excellent tension and have no symptoms.

Your goals:
1. Restore ability to create tension across the linea alba
2. Improve core stability and function
3. Reduce symptoms (back pain, bulging, weakness)
4. Minimize gap width (though complete closure isn't always necessary)

## Phase 1: Foundation and Awareness

### Diaphragmatic Breathing

Proper breathing is the foundation. Many people with diastasis recti have dysfunctional breathing patterns.

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Place one hand on chest, one on belly
3. Breathe in through nose—belly rises, chest stays relatively still
4. Exhale slowly through pursed lips—belly falls
5. 5-10 minutes daily

### Connection Breath

Links breath to core and pelvic floor activation.

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Inhale: Let belly and pelvic floor relax and expand
3. Exhale: Gently draw belly button toward spine AND lift pelvic floor (like stopping urine flow)
4. Think "exhale and engage"
5. 10-15 breaths, 2-3x daily

### Finding Neutral Spine

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Tilt pelvis to flatten low back against floor
3. Tilt pelvis to arch low back off floor
4. Find the middle—natural curve with small space under low back
5. This is your "neutral" position for exercises

## Phase 2: Core Activation

### Dead Bug Preparation

1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. On exhale, engage core (connection breath)
3. Maintaining engagement, lift one foot just off the floor
4. Inhale, lower foot
5. Alternate sides
6. 10 reps each side

**Watch for:** Doming or bulging along midline. If it happens, you're progressing too fast.

### Heel Slides

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Slowly slide one heel away from you, straightening leg
4. Only go as far as you can while maintaining core engagement
5. Slide back
6. 10 reps each side

### Toe Taps

1. Lie on back, legs in tabletop (hips and knees at 90°)
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Slowly lower one foot to tap floor
4. Return to tabletop
5. 10 reps each side

**Key:** Keep pelvis stable. No rocking or arching.

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening

### Dead Bug (Full)

1. Lie on back, arms reaching toward ceiling, legs in tabletop
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Slowly extend opposite arm overhead and opposite leg straight
4. Inhale, return to start
5. Alternate sides
6. 10 reps each side

### Bird Dog

1. On hands and knees, neutral spine
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Extend opposite arm and leg
4. Keep spine stable (imagine balancing a cup of water on your back)
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. 10 reps each side

### Pallof Press

1. Attach resistance band at chest height
2. Stand sideways to anchor, hold band at chest
3. Exhale, engage core
4. Press band straight out in front
5. Hold 5 seconds, resisting rotation
6. Return to chest
7. 10 reps each side

### Side Plank (Modified)

1. Lie on side, knees bent, elbow under shoulder
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Lift hips off ground, keeping knees down
4. Hold 10-30 seconds
5. 3-5 reps each side

Progress to full side plank (on feet) when ready.

## Phase 4: Functional Integration

### Squat with Engagement

1. Stand feet shoulder-width apart
2. Exhale, engage core before descending
3. Squat down, maintaining engagement
4. Exhale to stand
5. 10-15 reps

### Farmer's Carry

1. Hold weights at sides (start light)
2. Engage core, maintain tall posture
3. Walk with control
4. 30-60 seconds

### Step-Ups with Load

1. Hold light weights
2. Step up onto bench/step
3. Maintain core engagement throughout
4. 10 reps each leg

## Exercises to Avoid Initially

**Avoid until you can control midline:**
- Traditional crunches and sit-ups
- Leg lifts (both legs straight)
- V-ups
- Bicycle crunches
- Full planks (if doming occurs)
- Double-leg lowers
- Heavy overhead pressing

**Avoid always:**
- Any exercise that causes visible doming
- Breath-holding during exertion
- Jackknife movements

These aren't forever forbidden—many can be safely reintroduced once you have midline control. The test: if it causes doming, you're not ready.

## Daily Habits That Help

### Posture
- Avoid "rib thrust" (flaring ribs forward)
- Stack ribs over pelvis
- Maintain neutral spine

### Getting Up from Lying
- Roll to your side first
- Push up with arms
- Don't "jackknife" straight up

### Lifting
- Exhale on exertion
- Engage core before lifting
- Keep loads close to body

### Bathroom
- Don't strain or hold breath
- Address constipation if present

## Common Mistakes

**Sucking in vs. engaging:**
Sucking your belly button in is different from engaging your core. Engagement creates tension while allowing normal breathing; sucking in creates rigidity and restricts breath.

**Gripping:**
Over-engaging or "bracing" too hard can be counterproductive. Aim for about 30% effort—enough to create tension without restricting movement.

**Moving too fast:**
Progression should be based on control, not time. If you can't prevent doming, you're not ready for that exercise.

**Ignoring pelvic floor:**
Core and pelvic floor work together. If you have pelvic floor symptoms (leakage, pressure, pain), see a pelvic floor PT.

## Recovery Timeline

**Realistic expectations:**
- Awareness and activation: Weeks 1-4
- Noticeable functional improvement: Weeks 4-12
- Significant progress: 3-6 months
- Maximum improvement: 6-12 months (sometimes longer)

Factors affecting recovery:
- Severity of separation
- Tissue quality
- Consistency with exercises
- Presence of other conditions (pelvic floor dysfunction, hernia)
- Number of pregnancies

## When to Seek Professional Help

**See a pelvic floor physical therapist if:**
- Gap is greater than 2.5-3 finger widths
- You have pelvic floor symptoms
- You're not seeing progress after 8-12 weeks
- You're unsure if you're doing exercises correctly
- You have pain with exercises

**Consider surgical evaluation if:**
- Significant functional impairment despite consistent rehab
- Hernia present
- Large separation (usually >3 cm) that doesn't improve

## Key Takeaways

1. **Function matters more than gap size** — focus on generating tension
2. **Breathing is fundamental** — master diaphragmatic breathing first
3. **Exhale on exertion** — link breath to core engagement
4. **Progress based on control** — no doming means you can advance
5. **Avoid traditional crunches** until you have midline control
6. **Be patient** — significant improvement takes months
7. **Seek help if needed** — pelvic floor PT can make a huge difference

Diastasis recti is common and treatable. With the right approach, most people significantly improve their core function and reduce symptoms, even if some separation remains.
