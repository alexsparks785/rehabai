# Exercises After Knee Replacement: Your Recovery Roadmap

Knee replacement surgery gives you a new joint, but exercises give you a new life. The success of your surgery depends heavily on your commitment to rehabilitation. This guide walks you through what to expect and do from day one through full recovery.

## The Importance of Early Movement

Your new knee needs movement to:
- Prevent blood clots
- Reduce swelling
- Prevent scar tissue from limiting motion
- Restore muscle strength
- Achieve the best possible outcome

Starting exercises early—often the same day as surgery—sets you up for success.

## Goals After Knee Replacement

**Primary goals:**
- Achieve 0 degrees extension (leg fully straight)
- Achieve 120+ degrees flexion (knee fully bent)
- Walk without assistive devices
- Return to normal daily activities
- Reduce pain to minimal or none

**Typical timeline:**
- Walking with walker: Days 1-2
- Walking with cane: Weeks 2-4
- Walking unassisted: Weeks 4-8
- Return to most activities: 3-6 months
- Maximum improvement: 6-12 months

## Phase 1: Hospital Phase (Days 1-3)

You'll start exercises the day of or day after surgery. Pain medication helps make this manageable.

### Ankle Pumps
1. Pump your feet up and down (like pressing a gas pedal)
2. Do this constantly throughout the day
3. 20-30 reps every hour while awake

Prevents blood clots and reduces swelling.

### Quad Sets
1. Lying down, tighten your thigh muscle
2. Press the back of your knee toward the bed
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10-15 reps, every hour

Reactivates your quadriceps—critical for knee function.

### Straight Leg Raises
1. Tighten thigh (quad set)
2. Lift entire leg 6-12 inches off bed
3. Lower slowly
4. 10 reps, 3-4x daily

If you can't lift yet, have someone help while you focus on keeping the quad tight.

### Heel Slides
1. Lying down, slide heel toward buttocks
2. Bend knee as far as comfortable
3. Slide back out
4. 10-15 reps, 3-4x daily

Key exercise for regaining flexion.

### Standing (with Walker)
1. Stand at bedside with walker
2. Weight bear as allowed (usually full weight)
3. Practice weight shifting
4. First day: Stand 1-2 minutes
5. Progress to short walks in hallway

## Phase 2: Early Home Phase (Weeks 1-2)

Continue all hospital exercises, increasing repetitions.

### Seated Knee Extension
1. Sit in chair with feet flat
2. Straighten operated leg
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps, 3x daily

### Seated Knee Flexion
1. Sit in chair
2. Slide foot back, bending knee
3. Use other foot to gently push further if needed
4. Hold 10-30 seconds at end range
5. 10-15 reps, 3x daily

### Standing Knee Flexion
1. Hold onto counter or walker
2. Bend knee, bringing heel toward buttocks
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg, 2-3x daily

### Short Arc Quads
1. Lie down with rolled towel under knee
2. Straighten knee, lifting heel off bed
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 15-20 reps, 3x daily

Excellent for quad strengthening with minimal knee stress.

### Walking Program
- Walk with walker or cane as prescribed
- Short, frequent walks (5-10 minutes, 4-6x daily)
- Focus on heel-toe pattern
- Use ice after walking if swollen

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 2-6)

### Terminal Knee Extension
1. Place rolled towel under knee
2. Push back of knee into towel
3. Lift foot, straightening knee
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 15-20 reps, 3 sets

### Standing Hip Abduction
1. Hold onto support
2. Lift operated leg out to side
3. Keep toes forward
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Standing Hip Extension
1. Hold onto support
2. Extend operated leg backward
3. Keep trunk upright (don't lean forward)
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Heel Raises
1. Hold onto support
2. Rise up onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Mini Squats
1. Hold onto support
2. Feet shoulder-width apart
3. Bend knees 30-45 degrees
4. Return to standing
5. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Step-Ups
1. Use a 4-6 inch step
2. Step up with operated leg
3. Step down with non-operated leg
4. 10 reps, 2-3 sets

Progress step height gradually.

### Stationary Bike
Usually starts week 2-3:
- Seat high, low resistance
- May need to rock back and forth initially
- Work toward full rotations
- 10-15 minutes, 1-2x daily

Excellent for regaining flexion and building endurance.

## Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 6-12)

### Wall Squats
1. Back against wall, feet 12 inches from wall
2. Slide down until knees bent 45-60 degrees
3. Hold 10-30 seconds
4. 5-10 reps

### Lunges (Modified)
1. Hold onto support
2. Step forward with non-operated leg
3. Lower gently, keeping operated knee over ankle
4. Return to standing
5. 10 reps each leg

### Balance Exercises
1. Stand on operated leg only
2. Hold onto support as needed
3. Progress to fingertip support, then no support
4. 30 seconds, 3-5 reps

### Step-Downs
1. Stand on step with operated leg
2. Lower opposite foot toward floor
3. Touch lightly, return up
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

### Lateral Step-Ups
1. Stand beside a step
2. Step up sideways with operated leg
3. Step down
4. 10-15 reps each side

### Resistance Exercises
Add resistance band or light weights to:
- Knee extension
- Hamstring curls
- Hip exercises

## Regaining Full Range of Motion

### For Extension (Getting Leg Straight)
**Prone Hang:**
1. Lie face down with knees at edge of bed
2. Let gravity straighten the knee
3. Hold 5-10 minutes, 2-3x daily

**Towel Extension Stretch:**
1. Sit with leg extended, towel around foot
2. Pull toes toward you while pushing knee down
3. Hold 30-60 seconds

### For Flexion (Getting Knee to Bend)
**Wall Slide:**
1. Lie on back, feet on wall
2. Let foot slide down, bending knee
3. Use other foot to push gently
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

**Heel Prop:**
1. Sit on floor, back against wall
2. Slide heel toward buttocks as far as possible
3. Hold 30-60 seconds

**Important:** Some surgeons recommend aggressive flexion work; others prefer gradual progression. Follow your surgeon's specific guidelines.

## Managing Swelling

Swelling is normal and can persist for months. Control it with:
- **Ice**: 15-20 minutes after exercise
- **Elevation**: Above heart level when resting
- **Compression**: As recommended
- **Movement**: Keep ankle pumping throughout day

## Red Flags: When to Contact Your Doctor

- Increased redness, warmth, or drainage at incision
- Fever over 101°F (38.3°C)
- Calf pain, redness, or swelling (possible blood clot)
- Sudden increase in pain not relieved by medication
- Catching or locking of the knee
- Feeling like the knee might give way

## Sample Daily Schedule (Weeks 2-6)

**Morning:**
- Ankle pumps (while still in bed)
- Heel slides, quad sets, straight leg raises
- Short walk (10-15 minutes)
- Ice

**Midday:**
- Seated exercises (knee extension/flexion)
- Standing exercises (hip abduction, heel raises)
- Walking (10-15 minutes)
- Ice

**Afternoon:**
- Stationary bike (10-15 minutes)
- Range of motion stretches
- Ice

**Evening:**
- Strengthening exercises (mini squats, step-ups)
- Walking (10-15 minutes)
- Range of motion work
- Ice and elevation

## Long-Term Expectations

**What your new knee CAN do:**
- Walk unlimited distances
- Climb stairs
- Golf, swim, bike, hike
- Dance
- Most daily activities without pain

**What to AVOID long-term:**
- High-impact sports (running, jumping)
- Contact sports
- Heavy lifting with deep squats
- Activities with high fall risk

**Implant longevity:** Modern knee replacements typically last 15-25 years with appropriate activity levels.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start exercises immediately** after surgery—same day or next day
2. **Extension is priority one** — achieving a straight leg is crucial
3. **Consistency matters** — multiple short sessions beat one long one
4. **Expect ups and downs** — some days will be better than others
5. **Control swelling** aggressively with ice, elevation, movement
6. **Follow your surgeon's specific protocols** — they know your surgery
7. **Be patient** — maximum improvement takes 6-12 months

Your commitment to rehabilitation directly impacts your outcome. The exercises may be uncomfortable, but they're building the foundation for a pain-free, active life with your new knee.
