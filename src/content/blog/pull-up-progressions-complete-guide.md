# Pull-Up Progressions: From Dead Hang to Weighted Pull-Ups

The pull-up is humbling. It doesn't care how much you can bench or how fast you can run. Either you can lift your body weight over the bar, or you can't.

For many people—especially those new to training—the first pull-up seems impossible. But with the right progression, anyone can get there. And once you do, a world of pulling strength opens up.

This guide takes you from not being able to hang on a bar to doing weighted pull-ups. Find your starting point and follow the path.

## Why Pull-Ups Matter

### The Ultimate Relative Strength Test

Pull-ups measure strength relative to body weight. A 180-pound person doing 10 pull-ups is lifting 1,800 pounds of total work. No machine replicates this.

### Complete Upper Body Development

Pull-ups build:
- **Lats**: Primary movers—the V-taper muscles
- **Biceps**: Significant involvement, especially chin-ups
- **Rear delts**: Shoulder stabilization
- **Rhomboids/traps**: Scapular control
- **Core**: Anti-extension during the movement
- **Grip**: Foundational for all pulling

### Functional Transfer

Climbing, swimming, grappling, and countless real-world tasks require pulling strength. Pull-ups build it better than any machine.

## Pull-Up vs. Chin-Up

**Pull-up**: Overhand grip (palms away), wider grip typical
- More lat emphasis
- Slightly harder for most people

**Chin-up**: Underhand grip (palms toward you), narrower grip
- More bicep involvement
- Often easier due to bicep assistance

**Neutral grip**: Palms facing each other
- Middle ground
- Often easiest on shoulders

All three are valuable. Start with whichever you can do—or progress toward.

## Level 1: Building the Foundation (Week 1-3)

### 1A: Dead Hang

Before pulling, can you hang?

1. Grip bar, arms fully extended
2. Shoulders engaged (not shrugging to ears)
3. Hang for time
4. Goal: 30-60 seconds

If you can't hang for 30 seconds, grip is your limiter. Practice daily.

### 1B: Active Hang

1. Dead hang position
2. Pull shoulder blades down and back (without bending elbows)
3. You'll rise slightly—this is scapular engagement
4. Hold 5-10 seconds, release, repeat
5. 8-10 reps, 3 sets

This teaches the first part of the pull-up: engaging lats before pulling.

### 1C: Flexed Arm Hang

1. Use box or jump to get chin over bar
2. Hold this top position as long as possible
3. Goal: 15-30 seconds
4. 3-5 sets

Builds isometric strength at the top of the movement.

### 1D: Inverted Row (High Angle)

1. Bar at chest height (Smith machine, squat rack, or TRX)
2. Hang underneath, body at 45°+ angle
3. Pull chest to bar
4. 10-12 reps, 3 sets

The horizontal pull builds the same muscles with reduced load.

## Level 2: Assisted Pull-Ups (Week 4-6)

### 2A: Band-Assisted Pull-Up

1. Loop resistance band over bar
2. Place knee or foot in band
3. Perform pull-up with band assistance
4. 6-10 reps, 3-4 sets

Progress by using thinner bands as strength builds.

**Band progression** (thickest to thinnest):
- Heavy (green/blue) → Medium (red/black) → Light (yellow/orange) → No band

### 2B: Machine-Assisted Pull-Up

1. Use assisted pull-up machine
2. Set counterweight to allow 8-10 reps
3. Reduce assistance as strength builds
4. 8-10 reps, 3-4 sets

### 2C: Negative Pull-Up

The most effective progression for building pull-up strength:

1. Use box or jump to get chin over bar
2. Lower yourself as slowly as possible (5-10 seconds)
3. At bottom, step up and repeat
4. 4-6 negatives, 3-4 sets

Negatives build eccentric strength—often the missing link to your first pull-up.

### 2D: Inverted Row (Lower Angle)

1. Lower the bar so body is more horizontal
2. Harder than high-angle rows
3. Pull chest to bar
4. 8-10 reps, 3-4 sets

Progress until body is nearly horizontal.

## Level 3: First Pull-Ups (Week 7-10)

### 3A: Jumping Pull-Up

1. Stand under bar with slight jump reach
2. Jump to initiate, pull the rest of the way
3. Lower under control
4. 5-8 reps, 3 sets

Uses legs to assist through the sticking point.

### 3B: Kipping Pull-Up (For Momentum Learning)

1. Swing slightly to generate momentum
2. Use momentum to help pull
3. Not a strength exercise—learning to complete reps
4. Use sparingly; strict is the goal

### 3C: First Strict Pull-Up

When you can do:
- 15-second flexed arm hang
- 5-second negative pull-ups
- Band pull-ups with lightest band

...you're ready to try unassisted.

1. Dead hang
2. Engage scapulae (active hang position)
3. Pull—lead with chest toward bar
4. Chin over bar
5. Lower with control

**Got one? Congratulations.** Now build from there.

### 3D: Singles Practice

1. Do a single pull-up
2. Rest 30-60 seconds
3. Do another single
4. Repeat for 10-15 total singles

This "greasing the groove" approach builds reps without exhaustion.

## Level 4: Building Volume (Week 11-14)

### 4A: Multiple Rep Sets

Week 11: 5 sets of 2 reps
Week 12: 5 sets of 3 reps
Week 13: 4 sets of 4 reps
Week 14: 4 sets of 5 reps

Rest 2-3 minutes between sets for strength.

### 4B: Pyramid Sets

Set 1: 1 rep
Set 2: 2 reps
Set 3: 3 reps
Set 4: 2 reps
Set 5: 1 rep

Total: 9 reps with built-in progression and fatigue management.

### 4C: Pull-Up Ladder

Partner workout or timed:
- You do 1, rest
- You do 2, rest
- You do 3, rest
- Continue until failure
- Start over from 1

Accumulates volume with built-in rest.

### 4D: Chin-Up Sets

Switch to chin-ups for variety:
- Often allows more reps
- Different muscle emphasis
- Break through pull-up plateaus
- 4 sets of max reps

## Level 5: Variations and Intensity (Week 15+)

### 5A: Wide-Grip Pull-Up

1. Hands 1.5x shoulder width
2. More lat isolation
3. Generally harder
4. 6-10 reps, 3-4 sets

### 5B: Close-Grip Chin-Up

1. Hands 6-12 inches apart
2. Bicep emphasis
3. 8-12 reps, 3-4 sets

### 5C: Commando Pull-Up

1. Grip bar with hands in line (like gripping a baseball bat)
2. Pull head to one side of bar
3. Alternate sides each rep
4. 6-8 per side, 3 sets

### 5D: L-Sit Pull-Up

1. Hold legs straight out in front (L-position)
2. Perform pull-up while maintaining L
3. Extreme core demand
4. 5-8 reps, 3 sets

### 5E: Archer Pull-Up

1. Wide grip
2. Pull toward one hand (that arm does most work)
3. Other arm stays relatively straight
4. 4-6 per side, 3 sets

Progression toward one-arm pull-ups.

## Level 6: Weighted and Advanced (Week 20+)

### 6A: Weighted Pull-Up

1. Use dip belt, weighted vest, or dumbbell between feet
2. Start with 5-10 lbs
3. Perform standard pull-up
4. 5-8 reps, 4-5 sets

Progress load over time for strength.

### 6B: Weighted Chin-Up

Same as above with chin-up grip. Often can handle more weight due to bicep assistance.

### 6C: Muscle-Up Progression

1. High pull-up (chest to bar)
2. Explosive pull (bar to waist)
3. Transition practice on low bar
4. Full muscle-up

Requires significant pulling strength plus the transition skill.

### 6D: One-Arm Pull-Up Progression

1. Assisted one-arm (band or towel over bar)
2. One-arm negatives (very slow)
3. Partial one-arm
4. Full one-arm pull-up

Multi-year goal for most people. Requires bodyweight + 50% or more equivalent strength.

## Programming Pull-Ups

### For Your First Pull-Up

- 3-4 days per week
- Negatives: 4 sets of 5-6 (slow)
- Band-assisted: 3 sets of 6-8
- Inverted rows: 3 sets of 10-12
- Dead hangs: Daily practice

### For Building Reps (5→15)

- 3 days per week
- Multiple sets of sub-max reps
- Total volume: 25-50 reps per session
- Grease the groove: Singles throughout day

### For Strength (Weighted)

- 2 days per week
- 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps
- Progressive overload on weight
- Bodyweight volume on other days

### For Muscle Building

- 2-3 days per week
- 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
- Use assistance if needed for rep range
- Include chin-up variations

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

### Mistake: Not Full Range

**Why**: Weakness at bottom, ego, habit

**Fix**: Dead hang between every rep. Full extension to chin over bar.

### Mistake: Kipping When Not Intended

**Why**: Strength gap, trying to complete reps

**Fix**: Reduce reps, do strict or don't count it. Pause at dead hang.

### Mistake: Neck Craning

**Why**: Trying to get "chin over bar" by cheating

**Fix**: Chest leads the pull. Chin naturally clears when chest is high enough.

### Mistake: Shoulders Shrugged at Bottom

**Why**: Missing scapular engagement

**Fix**: Practice active hangs. Pull shoulders down before bending elbows.

### Mistake: Ignoring Negatives

**Why**: Only counting "up" as training

**Fix**: Slow negatives are half the exercise. 3-second minimum descent.

### Mistake: Grip Failure Before Back Failure

**Why**: Grip is the weak link

**Fix**: Dead hangs for grip. Use chalk. Train grip separately.

## Testing Your Pull-Up Strength

### Basic Standards

- **1 pull-up**: You can do it
- **5 pull-ups**: Baseline fitness
- **10 pull-ups**: Good relative strength
- **15-20 pull-ups**: Strong
- **20+ pull-ups**: Very strong upper body

### Strength Standards (Weighted)

- **Beginner**: Bodyweight for 5-8 reps
- **Intermediate**: BW + 25% for 5 reps
- **Advanced**: BW + 50% for 5 reps
- **Elite**: BW + 100% for 1 rep

### Quality Check

Can you:
- Do 5 pull-ups with 3-second descent?
- Pause at dead hang between each rep?
- Perform 5 reps without ANY kipping?

Quality beats quantity.

## Pull-Ups and Body Weight

Here's the reality: pull-ups are easier when you're lighter. A 150-pound person doing pull-ups lifts 150 pounds. A 220-pound person lifts 220 pounds.

**This doesn't mean heavy people can't do pull-ups.** It means:
- Progress may take longer
- Every rep is more impressive
- Assistance is not shame—it's smart training
- Fat loss will make pull-ups easier

## Complementary Exercises

### For Pull-Up Strength

- **Lat pulldowns**: Same motion with adjustable load
- **Barbell rows**: Horizontal pulling strength
- **Face pulls**: Rear delt and scapular health
- **Bicep curls**: Assistance muscle strength
- **Dead hangs**: Grip endurance

### For Balance

Pull-ups are pulling. Balance with pushing:
- Push-ups or bench press
- Overhead press
- Dips

Maintain pulling-to-pushing balance for shoulder health.

## The Bottom Line

The pull-up is earned. There are no shortcuts—only progressions.

Start where you are. Dead hangs if needed. Band assistance when appropriate. Negatives are your best friend. And when you get your first rep, you'll know why it matters.

Then keep going. The pull-up is a lifetime exercise with unlimited progression potential.

## Quick Reference

**Level 1**: Dead hang → Active hang → Flexed arm hang → Inverted rows
**Level 2**: Band-assisted → Machine-assisted → Negatives
**Level 3**: Jumping pull-ups → First strict pull-up → Singles practice
**Level 4**: Volume building → Pyramids → Ladders
**Level 5**: Variations (wide, close, L-sit, archer)
**Level 6**: Weighted → Muscle-up → One-arm

**Form cues**: Full hang at bottom, scapulae engage first, chest to bar, controlled negative

**Progression rule**: Own each level before advancing
