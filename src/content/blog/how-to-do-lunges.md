Lunges are one of the most functional exercises you can do. They build single-leg strength, improve balance, and work your entire lower body—quads, glutes, and hamstrings all get hit.

But lunges also trip up a lot of people (sometimes literally). The movement pattern can feel awkward, and knee pain is a common complaint. Here's how to do lunges correctly and get all the benefits without the problems.

## Why Lunges?

### Single-Leg Training

Most daily movements—walking, climbing stairs, running—happen one leg at a time. Lunges train this pattern, building functional strength that transfers to real life.

### Addresses Imbalances

When you squat with both legs, your stronger leg can compensate for the weaker one. Lunges force each leg to do its own work, revealing and correcting imbalances.

### Lower Body Complete

Lunges work:
- **Quads:** Primary movers during the push back up
- **Glutes:** Hip extension and stabilization
- **Hamstrings:** Assist during the movement
- **Calves:** Stabilization
- **Core:** Balance and stability

### Versatility

Lunges can be done anywhere, with bodyweight or added resistance, in multiple variations to target different areas.

## The Basic Forward Lunge

### Starting Position

1. Stand tall, feet hip-width apart
2. Core engaged, shoulders back
3. Hands on hips, at sides, or holding weights

### The Step

1. Take a large step forward with one leg
2. Step far enough that when you lower, both knees can reach 90°
3. Keep torso upright as you step

### The Descent

1. Lower your body by bending both knees
2. Front knee bends to approximately 90°
3. Back knee drops toward the floor (don't slam it)
4. Stop when back knee is 1-2 inches from the ground
5. Keep front knee tracking over (not past) the toes

### The Return

1. Push through the front heel to drive back up
2. Step back to starting position
3. Reset and repeat with the same or opposite leg

### Breathing

- Inhale during the descent
- Exhale during the push back up

## Common Lunge Mistakes

### Knee Caving Inward

**The problem:** Front knee collapses toward midline, stressing the joint.

**The fix:** Actively push knee outward, tracking over your toes. Strengthen your glutes—weak hips cause knee cave.

### Knee Shooting Too Far Forward

**The problem:** Front knee travels way past toes, increasing patella stress.

**The fix:** Take a longer step. At the bottom, your shin should be roughly vertical or only slightly angled forward.

### Short Steps

**The problem:** Step isn't long enough, creating an awkward position where you can't properly load both legs.

**The fix:** Step far enough that both knees can reach 90° at the bottom.

### Leaning Forward

**The problem:** Torso tilts forward, shifting load to lower back.

**The fix:** Stay upright. Think "chest proud" throughout the movement. Engage your core.

### Rising on Front Toes

**The problem:** Weight shifts to front toes instead of heel.

**The fix:** Drive through the heel of the front foot. You should be able to wiggle your front toes.

### Wobbly Balance

**The problem:** Losing balance and wobbling side to side.

**The fix:** Step in a straight line, not on a tightrope. Keep feet hip-width apart, just stepping forward in parallel lines.

### Back Knee Slamming Down

**The problem:** Dropping the back knee hard into the floor.

**The fix:** Control the descent. The back knee should kiss the ground gently, not crash into it.

## Lunge Variations

### Reverse Lunge

Step backward instead of forward.

**Why:** Often easier on the knees—better control, less forward momentum. Many people find these more comfortable than forward lunges.

**How:** Step back, lower both knees, push through front heel to return to standing.

### Walking Lunges

Step forward into each lunge, traveling across the floor.

**Why:** Adds a dynamic element, great for conditioning and leg development.

**How:** Lunge forward, bring back foot to meet front foot, immediately step into next lunge with opposite leg.

### Static/Split Squat

Feet stay in split position throughout.

**Why:** Eliminates the step, letting you focus purely on the lowering and pushing. Good for learning the pattern.

**How:** Start in a staggered stance, lower straight down, push back up. Complete all reps on one side before switching.

### Bulgarian Split Squat

Rear foot elevated on a bench.

**Why:** Increases range of motion and single-leg emphasis. Excellent for building leg strength and size.

**How:** Rear foot on bench behind you, front foot forward. Lower until front thigh is parallel, push back up.

### Lateral Lunge (Side Lunge)

Step out to the side.

**Why:** Works the adductors and hits the legs from a different angle. Addresses lateral movement patterns.

**How:** Step wide to one side, sit hips back, bend stepping leg while keeping other leg straight. Push back to start.

### Curtsy Lunge

Step back and across behind the front leg.

**Why:** Emphasizes the glutes, particularly the gluteus medius.

**How:** Step one foot back and behind the other leg (like a curtsy). Lower both knees, push back to start.

### Deficit Lunge

Front foot elevated on a small platform.

**Why:** Increases range of motion for greater muscle stretch and development.

**How:** Stand on a platform or step, step back into a reverse lunge, going deeper than normal.

### Jumping Lunges

Explosive lunge with a jump between reps.

**Why:** Builds power, conditioning, and athleticism. Advanced plyometric movement.

**How:** From bottom of lunge, explode up and switch legs in the air, landing in opposite lunge position.

## Programming Lunges

### For Beginners

**Goal:** Build coordination and base strength

- Start with static split squats (no stepping)
- Bodyweight only
- 3 sets of 8-10 reps per leg
- 2-3 times per week
- Progress to reverse lunges, then forward lunges

### For Strength

**Volume:** 3-4 sets of 6-8 reps per leg
**Load:** Dumbbells at sides or barbell on back
**Frequency:** 1-2x per week
**Focus:** Progressive overload—add weight over time

### For Hypertrophy (Muscle Building)

**Volume:** 3-4 sets of 10-12 reps per leg
**Load:** Moderate weight
**Frequency:** 2x per week
**Focus:** Control, full range of motion, muscle tension

### For Conditioning

**Method:** Walking lunges for distance or time
**Example:** 50-100 total lunges, or 3-5 minutes continuous
**Frequency:** 1-2x per week
**Focus:** Maintain form even as fatigue sets in

## Adding Weight to Lunges

### Dumbbells at Sides

Easiest progression. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides.

**Pros:** Simple grip, balanced load
**Cons:** Grip may limit weight before legs are challenged

### Goblet Position

Hold a single dumbbell or kettlebell at chest height.

**Pros:** Encourages upright posture, good for learning
**Cons:** Limited by how much you can hold at chest

### Barbell on Back

Barbell across upper traps (like a back squat).

**Pros:** Allows heavy loading
**Cons:** Requires balance, setup takes more time

### Barbell in Front Rack

Barbell across front of shoulders.

**Pros:** Enforces upright posture
**Cons:** Requires mobility, technically demanding

### Weight Vest

Adds resistance without occupying hands.

**Pros:** Leaves hands free for balance
**Cons:** Limited to vest capacity

## Troubleshooting

### "My Knees Hurt"

- Try reverse lunges instead of forward
- Don't let knee track inward
- Don't step too short
- Strengthen your glutes
- Check that you're pushing through the heel

### "I Keep Losing Balance"

- Widen your stance (feet in parallel lines, not on a tightrope)
- Start with static split squats before moving lunges
- Use a wall or chair for support while learning
- Progress slowly—balance improves with practice

### "I Feel It More in My Quads Than Glutes"

- Take a longer step (longer stride = more glute)
- Focus on pushing through the heel
- Lean torso slightly forward (while keeping back straight)
- Try Bulgarian split squats for more glute emphasis

### "Reverse Lunges Feel Weird"

- They feel different than forward lunges—that's normal
- Start with smaller steps and build up
- Use a mirror or video to check form
- Practice static split squats first

## Lunge Workouts

### Beginner Workout

- Static split squats: 3 x 10 each leg
- Bodyweight reverse lunges: 3 x 8 each leg
- Glute bridges: 3 x 15

### Intermediate Workout

- Goblet reverse lunges: 3 x 10 each leg
- Bulgarian split squats: 3 x 8 each leg
- Walking lunges: 2 x 20 steps total
- Romanian deadlifts: 3 x 10

### Advanced Workout

- Barbell reverse lunges: 4 x 8 each leg
- Deficit reverse lunges: 3 x 10 each leg
- Walking lunges with dumbbells: 3 x 24 steps
- Jumping lunges: 3 x 10 each leg

## The Bottom Line

Lunges are essential for building functional lower body strength:

1. **Take an adequate step** so both knees can reach 90°
2. **Keep torso upright** and core engaged
3. **Push through the front heel** to return
4. **Track knee over toes**—don't let it cave
5. **Control the movement**—no slamming the back knee

Start with the variation that feels most comfortable (often reverse lunges), master the form, then progress to more challenging versions and add weight.

Your legs, glutes, and athletic performance will all benefit from regular lunging.

Now step to it.
