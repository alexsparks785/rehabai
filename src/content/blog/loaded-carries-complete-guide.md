# Loaded Carries: The Most Underrated Exercise for Total-Body Strength

Pick up something heavy. Walk with it. That's the loaded carry—and it might be the most functional exercise in existence.

Legendary strength coach Dan John calls the loaded carry one of the most important movement patterns humans can train. Why? Because it's what we actually do in life. Carrying groceries, moving furniture, hauling equipment—these real-world tasks require exactly what loaded carries develop.

Yet most gym programs completely ignore them.

## Why Loaded Carries Work

### Total-Body Integration

Unlike isolation exercises that train one muscle at a time, loaded carries demand everything working together:

- **Grip**: Hands must hold the load
- **Forearms**: Maintain grip under fatigue
- **Shoulders**: Stabilize the load position
- **Core**: Resist flexion, extension, and rotation
- **Hips**: Stabilize with each step
- **Legs**: Propel you forward under load
- **Feet**: Ground contact and balance

No other exercise integrates this many systems simultaneously.

### Real-World Transfer

The strength you build carrying heavy objects in the gym transfers directly to:
- Carrying children, groceries, luggage
- Moving furniture and equipment
- Manual labor tasks
- Sports requiring grip and core strength
- Injury resilience in daily life

### Benefits

- Builds crushing grip strength
- Develops anti-lateral flexion core stability
- Improves posture and shoulder position
- Increases work capacity and conditioning
- Strengthens the entire posterior chain
- Enhances hip stability and gait mechanics
- Requires minimal technique to start

## The Basic Farmer Walk

### Equipment

- **Dumbbells**: Most accessible option
- **Kettlebells**: Great for most variations
- **Trap bar**: Allows heaviest loads
- **Farmer walk handles**: Specialty equipment for serious training
- **Anything heavy**: Buckets, sandbags, groceries

### Technique

1. **Set up**: Stand between weights, feet hip-width
2. **Lift**: Hip hinge to grab handles, stand up with neutral spine
3. **Position**: Shoulders back and down, chest proud, core braced
4. **Walk**: Take short, controlled steps
5. **Breathe**: Steady breathing, maintain brace
6. **Set down**: Hip hinge to lower with control

### Key Cues

- **"Proud posture"**: Stand tall, don't lean forward
- **"Pack shoulders"**: Down and back, not shrugged
- **"Quick feet"**: Short steps maintain stability
- **"Crush the handles"**: Maximum grip tension
- **"Quiet feet"**: Don't stomp—controlled steps

## Loaded Carry Variations

### Bilateral Carries (Both Hands Loaded)

**Farmer Walk**
- Weights at sides
- Most load potential
- Grip and traps emphasis

**Goblet Carry**
- Single weight held at chest
- Core anti-flexion focus
- Good for learning

**Front Rack Carry**
- Kettlebells or dumbbells at shoulders
- Intense core demand
- Posture challenge

**Overhead Carry**
- Weight(s) locked out overhead
- Shoulder stability focus
- Core anti-extension

**Zercher Carry**
- Barbell or sandbag in elbow crooks
- Brutal core and bicep work
- Awkward object training

### Unilateral Carries (One Hand Loaded)

**Suitcase Carry**
- Single weight at one side
- Anti-lateral flexion challenge
- Obliques and hip stability

**Single-Arm Rack Carry**
- One kettlebell at shoulder
- Rotational stability demand
- Offset loading

**Single-Arm Overhead Carry**
- One weight locked overhead
- Extreme stability challenge
- Shoulder and core integration

### Specialty Carries

**Yoke Walk**
- Bar across upper back
- Maximum loading potential
- Strongman training staple

**Sandbag Carry**
- Bear hug or shoulder position
- Awkward, shifting load
- Real-world transfer

**Keg Carry**
- Similar to sandbag
- Grip challenge with smooth surface

**Backwards Drag**
- Pulling load behind you
- Different muscle emphasis
- Conditioning focus

## Level 1: Foundation (Week 1-2)

### 1A: Goblet Carry

Start with the most stable variation:
1. Hold dumbbell or kettlebell at chest
2. Walk 30-40 yards
3. Focus on upright posture
4. 3-4 sets

### 1B: Light Farmer Walk

1. Moderate dumbbells (start with 25-40% bodyweight total)
2. Walk 30-40 yards
3. Perfect posture throughout
4. 3-4 sets

### 1C: Suitcase Carry (Light)

1. Single dumbbell, one side
2. Walk 20-30 yards
3. Switch hands
4. Keep shoulders level—don't lean
5. 2-3 sets per side

## Level 2: Building Capacity (Week 3-4)

### 2A: Heavy Farmer Walk

1. Increase load (50-75% bodyweight total)
2. Walk 40-60 yards
3. Maintain posture under heavier load
4. 3-4 sets

### 2B: Front Rack Carry

1. Two kettlebells cleaned to shoulders
2. Elbows high, weights resting on shoulders
3. Walk 30-40 yards
4. 3-4 sets

### 2C: Mixed Carry (Farmer + Rack)

1. One weight at side (farmer position)
2. One weight at shoulder (rack position)
3. Walk 30 yards
4. Switch positions
5. 2-3 sets per configuration

## Level 3: Unilateral Focus (Week 5-6)

### 3A: Heavy Suitcase Carry

1. Single heavy dumbbell or kettlebell
2. Walk 30-40 yards
3. Absolutely no lateral lean
4. Switch sides
5. 3-4 sets per side

### 3B: Single-Arm Overhead Carry

1. One kettlebell locked out overhead
2. Bicep by ear, arm vertical
3. Walk 20-30 yards
4. Switch arms
5. 2-3 sets per side

### 3C: Waiter Walk

1. Light weight overhead, balanced like a tray
2. More stability challenge than strength
3. Walk 30-40 yards per arm
4. 2-3 sets per side

## Level 4: Combination Challenges (Week 7-8)

### 4A: Overhead + Farmer

1. One weight overhead, one at side
2. Asymmetric loading challenge
3. Walk 20-30 yards
4. Switch positions
5. 2-3 sets per side

### 4B: Rack + Suitcase

1. One weight at shoulder, one at side
2. Walk 30 yards
3. Switch positions
4. 2-3 sets per configuration

### 4C: Cross-Body Carry

1. One overhead, one at opposite hip
2. Maximum anti-rotation demand
3. Walk 20-30 yards
4. Switch
5. 2 sets per side

## Level 5: Heavy and Complex (Week 9+)

### 5A: Max Farmer Walk

1. Near-maximum load (bodyweight+ in each hand is advanced)
2. Walk as far as possible with perfect form
3. Test grip limits
4. 2-3 max-effort sets

### 5B: Sandbag Bear Hug Carry

1. Heavy sandbag hugged to chest
2. Walk 30-50 yards
3. Awkward load, real-world transfer
4. 3-4 sets

### 5C: Carry Complex

1. Farmer walk 30 yards
2. Rack the weights (clean to shoulders)
3. Front rack carry 30 yards
4. Press overhead
5. Overhead carry 30 yards
6. Return to start
7. 2-3 rounds

## Programming Loaded Carries

### As a Finisher

After your main workout:
- 3-4 sets of farmer walks
- 40-60 yards per set
- Moderate to heavy load
- 60-90 seconds rest

### As Main Core Work

Replace or supplement traditional core:
- Suitcase carries: 3x30 yards per side
- Goblet carries: 3x40 yards
- More functional than planks and crunches

### For Grip Training

- Heavy farmer walks: 3-4 sets to grip failure
- Short distance, maximum load
- No straps allowed

### For Conditioning

- Lighter loads, longer distances
- Farmer walk 100+ yards
- Or timed carries: 60-90 seconds continuous
- Minimal rest between sets

### Full Carry Workout

1. Goblet carry: 3x40 yards (warm-up)
2. Heavy farmer walk: 4x50 yards
3. Suitcase carry: 3x30 yards each side
4. Front rack carry: 3x40 yards
5. Sandbag carry: 2x50 yards (finisher)

## Loading Guidelines

### Farmer Walk

- **Beginner**: 25-50% bodyweight per hand
- **Intermediate**: 50-75% bodyweight per hand
- **Advanced**: 75-100%+ bodyweight per hand

Example: 180 lb person
- Beginner: 45-90 lbs per hand
- Intermediate: 90-135 lbs per hand
- Advanced: 135+ lbs per hand

### Suitcase Carry

- Generally 50-75% of your farmer walk weight
- The asymmetric challenge compensates for lighter load

### Front Rack/Overhead

- Lighter than farmer position
- Focus on position quality over load

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

### Mistake: Leaning Forward

**Why**: Load too heavy, or poor habit

**Fix**: Lighten load. Cue "chest up." Shorten distance.

### Mistake: Leaning to One Side (Suitcase)

**Why**: Not resisting the lateral pull

**Fix**: Engage opposite obliques actively. Lighten load.

### Mistake: Shrugged Shoulders

**Why**: Compensation pattern, or fatigue

**Fix**: Cue "shoulders down and back." Strengthen traps separately.

### Mistake: Looking Down

**Why**: Concentration or heavy load

**Fix**: Eyes forward. Pick a target ahead.

### Mistake: Taking Long Steps

**Why**: Trying to finish faster

**Fix**: Short, quick, controlled steps. "Quick feet."

### Mistake: Holding Breath

**Why**: Over-bracing or unfamiliarity

**Fix**: Rhythmic breathing with steps. Inhale 2-3 steps, exhale 2-3 steps.

### Mistake: Going Too Light

**Why**: Underestimating the exercise

**Fix**: Loaded carries should be challenging. If posture is easy, add weight.

## Carry Progressions Over Time

### Month 1: Learn the Patterns
- Master farmer walk and goblet carry
- Introduce suitcase carry
- Focus on position, not load

### Month 2: Build Load
- Increase farmer walk weight progressively
- Add front rack carry
- Start single-arm overhead work

### Month 3: Add Complexity
- Mixed carries (different positions)
- Longer distances
- Carry complexes

### Month 4+: Push Limits
- Near-max loading on farmer walks
- Awkward implements (sandbags, kegs)
- Sport-specific applications

## Carries for Specific Goals

### For Grip Strength

- Heavy farmer walks to grip failure
- Thick grip implements if available
- No straps—ever
- 3-4 times per week

### For Core Strength

- Prioritize suitcase and single-arm variations
- Overhead carries for anti-extension
- Mix unilateral and bilateral work

### For Conditioning

- Lighter loads, longer distances
- Timed carries (60-90 seconds)
- Minimal rest
- Include in circuits

### For Sport Performance

- Match carry position to sport demands
- Overhead for throwing sports
- Farmer/rack for contact sports
- Include direction changes

### For Rehab/Back Pain

- Start light with goblet carry
- Progress slowly to farmer walk
- Suitcase carries for anti-lateral flexion
- Avoid overhead until cleared

## Why Everyone Should Carry

The loaded carry is the great equalizer. It doesn't require technique coaching or expensive equipment. It builds the kind of strength that matters in real life.

Whether you're a competitive athlete or just want to carry groceries without struggle, loaded carries deliver.

Dan John's challenge: Can you carry your bodyweight (total) for a farmer walk? If not, you have work to do.

## Quick Reference

**Farmer Walk**: Weights at sides, walk with proud posture
**Suitcase Carry**: Single weight, one side, resist leaning
**Front Rack**: Weights at shoulders, elbows high
**Overhead**: Weight(s) locked out, bicep by ear

**Loading**: Start 25-50% BW per hand, progress to 75%+ per hand

**Distance**: 30-60 yards per set typically

**Cues**: Proud posture, quick feet, crush the handles, quiet feet

**Frequency**: 2-4 times per week, as finisher or core work
