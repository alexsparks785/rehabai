# Bursitis Exercises: Treatment and Recovery for Inflamed Bursae

Bursitis—inflammation of the fluid-filled sacs that cushion your joints—can turn simple movements into painful ordeals. Whether it's your hip, shoulder, knee, or elbow, the good news is that most bursitis responds well to conservative treatment, including targeted exercises.

## Understanding Bursitis

Bursae are small, fluid-filled sacs located at points of friction in your body—between bones and soft tissues like muscles, tendons, and skin. When these sacs become inflamed, you get bursitis.

**Common causes:**
- Repetitive movements or overuse
- Prolonged pressure (kneeling, leaning on elbows)
- Acute injury or trauma
- Poor biomechanics
- Tight or weak surrounding muscles
- Underlying conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, gout)

**Common locations:**
- Hip (trochanteric bursitis) — most common
- Shoulder (subacromial bursitis)
- Knee (prepatellar or pes anserine bursitis)
- Elbow (olecranon bursitis)

## When to See a Doctor

Seek medical evaluation if you have:
- Severe pain or inability to move the joint
- Significant swelling, redness, or warmth (possible infection)
- Fever accompanying joint pain
- Pain that doesn't improve with rest after 1-2 weeks
- Recurrent bursitis episodes

Septic (infected) bursitis requires antibiotics—don't delay if infection is possible.

## General Treatment Principles

**Phase 1 (Acute - Days 1-7):**
- Relative rest (avoid aggravating activities)
- Ice for 15-20 minutes several times daily
- Avoid direct pressure on the affected area
- Gentle range of motion only

**Phase 2 (Subacute - Weeks 1-4):**
- Gradual return to movement
- Address underlying causes (weakness, tightness)
- Begin strengthening exercises
- Continue avoiding aggravating activities

**Phase 3 (Recovery - Weeks 4+):**
- Progressive strengthening
- Return to normal activities
- Prevention strategies

## Hip Bursitis (Trochanteric Bursitis)

The most common type, causing pain on the outside of the hip that may radiate down the thigh.

### What to Avoid Initially
- Sleeping on the affected side
- Crossing your legs
- Standing on one leg for prolonged periods
- Deep squats or lunges

### Stretches

**IT Band Stretch:**
1. Stand with affected leg behind you
2. Lean your body away from the affected side
3. You should feel a stretch along the outer hip/thigh
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3x

**Piriformis Stretch:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
3. Pull the uncrossed leg toward your chest
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3x

**Hip Flexor Stretch:**
1. Kneel on affected knee (use padding)
2. Other foot forward in a lunge position
3. Shift weight forward, keeping torso upright
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3x

### Strengthening

**Clamshells:**
1. Lie on unaffected side, knees bent 90 degrees
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Don't let hips roll backward
4. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction:**
1. Lie on unaffected side, legs straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling (keep it straight)
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

**Bridges:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 5 seconds at top
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

**Standing Hip Abduction:**
1. Hold onto support
2. Lift affected leg out to the side
3. Keep toes pointing forward
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

## Shoulder Bursitis (Subacromial Bursitis)

Often accompanies rotator cuff issues, causing pain with overhead movements.

### What to Avoid Initially
- Reaching overhead
- Sleeping on the affected side
- Pushing or pulling heavy objects
- Repetitive arm movements

### Range of Motion

**Pendulum Exercises:**
1. Lean forward, supporting yourself with unaffected arm
2. Let affected arm hang
3. Make small circles (clockwise and counterclockwise)
4. 1-2 minutes, several times daily

**Passive External Rotation:**
1. Hold a stick or towel with both hands
2. Use unaffected arm to push affected arm into external rotation
3. Elbow stays at your side
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3-5x

### Strengthening

**Isometric External Rotation:**
1. Stand with elbow bent 90 degrees, at your side
2. Press the back of your hand against a wall or doorframe
3. Hold 10 seconds (no movement)
4. 10 reps, 2-3 sets

**Scapular Squeezes:**
1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15 reps, 2-3 sets

**Rows with Resistance Band:**
1. Attach band at chest height
2. Pull elbows back, squeezing shoulder blades
3. Return slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

**External Rotation with Band:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Hold band, rotate forearm outward
3. Return slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

## Knee Bursitis

**Prepatellar bursitis** (front of kneecap) often from kneeling.
**Pes anserine bursitis** (inner knee) common in runners and those with knee arthritis.

### What to Avoid
- Kneeling (use knee pads if unavoidable)
- Deep squatting
- High-impact activities
- Stairs (minimize if painful)

### Stretches

**Hamstring Stretch:**
1. Sit with affected leg extended
2. Reach toward toes, keeping back straight
3. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3x

**Quad Stretch:**
1. Stand holding support
2. Bend knee, grab ankle behind you
3. Keep knees together
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3x

### Strengthening

**Quad Sets:**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Tighten thigh, pressing back of knee down
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

**Straight Leg Raises:**
1. Lie on back, one knee bent, affected leg straight
2. Tighten thigh, lift leg 12 inches
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

**Mini Squats:**
1. Stand with support nearby
2. Bend knees 30-45 degrees
3. Return to standing
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

## Elbow Bursitis (Olecranon Bursitis)

Causes swelling at the point of the elbow, often from leaning on hard surfaces.

### What to Avoid
- Leaning on your elbow
- Direct pressure on the elbow
- Repetitive elbow bending under load

### Exercises

**Wrist Curls:**
1. Forearm supported, palm up
2. Hold light weight
3. Curl wrist up and down
4. 10-15 reps, 2 sets

**Reverse Wrist Curls:**
1. Forearm supported, palm down
2. Hold light weight
3. Extend wrist up and down
4. 10-15 reps, 2 sets

**Elbow Flexion/Extension:**
1. Gentle range of motion
2. Bend and straighten elbow slowly
3. 10-15 reps, several times daily

For elbow bursitis, protection from pressure is often more important than exercises.

## Prevention Strategies

**For all types:**
- Warm up before activity
- Progress exercise gradually
- Maintain flexibility and strength
- Use proper technique in sports and work

**Hip bursitis:**
- Strengthen hip abductors
- Stretch hip flexors and IT band
- Avoid sleeping on hard surfaces without cushioning

**Shoulder bursitis:**
- Maintain rotator cuff strength
- Avoid repetitive overhead work
- Use proper posture

**Knee bursitis:**
- Use knee pads when kneeling
- Strengthen quadriceps
- Maintain healthy weight

**Elbow bursitis:**
- Don't lean on elbows
- Use elbow pads if necessary
- Pad hard work surfaces

## Recovery Timeline

- **Acute phase**: 1-2 weeks
- **Rehabilitation**: 2-6 weeks
- **Full recovery**: 4-8 weeks (longer for chronic cases)

Chronic bursitis may take 3-6 months and sometimes requires medical intervention (corticosteroid injection, aspiration).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Rest from aggravating activities** but maintain gentle movement
2. **Address the underlying cause** — weakness, tightness, or biomechanics
3. **Strengthen surrounding muscles** to reduce stress on the bursa
4. **Avoid direct pressure** on the affected area
5. **Be patient** — bursitis can take weeks to fully resolve
6. **Prevent recurrence** by maintaining strength and avoiding known triggers

Most bursitis resolves with conservative treatment. If yours isn't improving after 4-6 weeks of consistent effort, consult a healthcare provider for evaluation.
