# Workout for Arthritis: Exercises That Reduce Pain and Stiffness

If you have arthritis, the idea of exercise might seem counterintuitive. Moving hurts, so why move more? But research is clear: the right kind of exercise is one of the most effective treatments for arthritis—reducing pain, improving function, and slowing disease progression.

## Why Exercise Helps Arthritis

Movement does several things for arthritic joints:

- **Nourishes cartilage** by pumping synovial fluid through the joint
- **Strengthens muscles** that support and protect joints
- **Maintains range of motion** that otherwise declines
- **Reduces inflammation** through systemic effects
- **Helps maintain healthy weight**, reducing joint stress
- **Improves mood and energy** often impacted by chronic pain

The key is finding the balance—enough movement to help, not so much that you aggravate symptoms.

## Types of Exercise You Need

A complete arthritis exercise program includes four components:

### 1. Flexibility and Range of Motion

Keeps joints moving through their full range. Do daily.

### 2. Strengthening

Builds muscles that support and protect joints. 2-3 times per week.

### 3. Low-Impact Cardio

Improves endurance and overall health. 3-5 times per week.

### 4. Balance and Stability

Prevents falls and supports joint function. 2-3 times per week.

## Best Exercises for Arthritis

### Water Exercise

The gold standard for arthritis. Water supports your body weight, reduces joint stress by up to 90%, and provides resistance for strengthening.

**Options:**
- Water aerobics classes
- Pool walking
- Swimming (any stroke that's comfortable)
- Aqua jogging

**Aim for:** 20-30 minutes, 3 times per week

### Walking

Simple, free, and effective. Start where you are and progress gradually.

**Tips for Joint-Friendly Walking:**
- Wear supportive, cushioned shoes
- Choose flat, even surfaces
- Start with 10 minutes and add 2-3 minutes weekly
- Walk when joints feel best (often mid-morning)

**Aim for:** 30 minutes most days, broken up if needed

### Cycling

Takes weight off lower body joints while building leg strength.

**Options:**
- Stationary bike (safest for balance)
- Recumbent bike (easier on back and hips)
- Outdoor cycling (when balance is good)

**Setup tips:**
- Seat high enough that knee is slightly bent at bottom
- Low resistance to start
- Keep cadence moderate (60-80 RPM)

**Aim for:** 15-30 minutes, 3-5 times per week

### Tai Chi

Combines gentle movement, balance, and breathing. Research shows it significantly reduces arthritis pain and improves function.

**Benefits:**
- Improves balance and reduces fall risk
- Low-impact and joint-friendly
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Can be done seated if needed

**Aim for:** 20-30 minutes, 2-3 times per week

### Yoga

Builds flexibility, strength, and body awareness. Choose gentle or restorative styles.

**Best types for arthritis:**
- Gentle yoga
- Chair yoga
- Restorative yoga
- Yin yoga (with modifications)

**Avoid:** Hot yoga, power yoga, or any class that pushes into pain

## Sample Daily Flexibility Routine

Do these every morning to combat stiffness. Hold each stretch 15-30 seconds.

**Neck:**
- Gentle head tilts side to side
- Chin tucks
- Slow head turns

**Shoulders:**
- Arm circles (small to large)
- Shoulder shrugs and rolls
- Cross-body arm stretch

**Hands and Wrists:**
- Make fists, then spread fingers wide
- Wrist circles
- Finger touches (thumb to each fingertip)

**Hips:**
- Seated hip circles
- Figure-4 stretch
- Hip flexor stretch (supported lunge)

**Knees:**
- Seated knee extensions
- Gentle hamstring stretch
- Quad stretch (standing with support)

**Ankles:**
- Ankle circles
- Toe points and flexes
- Alphabet tracing with feet

## Sample Strength Workout

Do this 2-3 times per week. Use light weights or resistance bands. If a joint is flaring, skip exercises for that area.

**Warm-up (5 minutes)**
- March in place or stationary bike
- Arm circles and leg swings

**Lower Body**
1. **Chair squats**: Sit down and stand up from a chair. 2 sets × 10 reps
2. **Standing hip abduction**: Lift leg out to side with support. 2 sets × 10 each side
3. **Calf raises**: Rise onto toes, lower slowly. 2 sets × 12 reps
4. **Bridges**: Lie on back, lift hips. 2 sets × 10 reps

**Upper Body**
1. **Wall push-ups**: 2 sets × 10 reps
2. **Seated rows with band**: 2 sets × 12 reps
3. **Bicep curls**: Light weight. 2 sets × 12 reps
4. **Overhead press**: Light weight. 2 sets × 10 reps

**Core**
1. **Seated marches**: Lift knees alternately. 2 sets × 20 total
2. **Bird dogs**: On hands and knees. 2 sets × 8 each side

**Cool-down (5 minutes)**
- Gentle stretching
- Deep breathing

## Exercising During Flares

When symptoms are worse, don't stop completely—modify.

**During a Flare:**
- Focus on gentle range-of-motion exercises
- Reduce intensity by 50%
- Shorten workout duration
- Avoid the affected joint if necessary
- Water exercise may feel better than land-based

**Signs to Rest:**
- Sharp, sudden pain
- Significant swelling
- Joint that's hot to touch
- Pain that lasts more than 2 hours after exercise

## Managing Exercise Pain

Some discomfort is normal. Here's how to tell the difference:

**Acceptable:**
- Mild muscle fatigue
- Slight joint aching during exercise
- Discomfort that fades within 1-2 hours after

**Not Acceptable:**
- Sharp or stabbing pain
- Pain that worsens during exercise
- Symptoms lasting more than 2 hours after
- Increased swelling, warmth, or redness

### The 2-Hour Rule

If pain or stiffness is worse 2 hours after exercise than before you started, you did too much. Scale back next time.

## Tips for Success

**Time It Right**
Exercise when you feel best—often mid-morning after stiffness eases but before fatigue sets in.

**Warm Up First**
Warm muscles and joints are more pliable. Take a warm shower or use a heating pad before exercise.

**Start Low, Go Slow**
Begin with less than you think you can do. Increase by 10% per week maximum.

**Ice After If Needed**
Applying ice for 15-20 minutes after exercise can reduce inflammation.

**Listen to Your Body**
Some days you'll do more, some less. That's okay.

**Move Throughout the Day**
Short movement breaks every 30-60 minutes prevent stiffness better than one long workout.

## Sample Weekly Schedule

**Monday:** Water aerobics (30 min) + flexibility routine

**Tuesday:** Strength workout (20 min)

**Wednesday:** Walking (20-30 min) + flexibility routine

**Thursday:** Tai Chi or yoga (30 min)

**Friday:** Strength workout (20 min) + flexibility routine

**Saturday:** Cycling or swimming (20-30 min)

**Sunday:** Gentle stretching and rest

## Joint-Specific Modifications

### Hand and Wrist Arthritis
- Use larger grip handles on equipment
- Try grip-strengthening exercises with therapy putty
- Avoid gripping weights tightly—use wrist weights instead

### Hip Arthritis
- Avoid deep squats and lunges
- Use a recumbent bike instead of upright
- Water exercise is especially helpful

### Knee Arthritis
- Avoid high-impact activities
- Strengthen quadriceps (straight leg raises)
- Use a bike with low resistance
- Avoid deep knee bends

### Shoulder Arthritis
- Avoid overhead pressing if painful
- Use resistance bands instead of weights
- Focus on rotator cuff strengthening
- Avoid behind-the-neck movements

### Spine Arthritis
- Avoid twisting under load
- Use machines with back support
- Focus on core stability
- Swimming is often well-tolerated

## Working With Your Healthcare Team

Before starting a new exercise program:

- Get clearance from your doctor
- Consider a physical therapy evaluation
- Ask about specific movements to avoid
- Discuss timing exercise with medications
- Learn how to modify during flares

## The Bottom Line

Exercise is one of the best things you can do for arthritis. It's not about pushing through pain—it's about finding movement that helps your joints feel better and function better.

Start gentle, stay consistent, and adjust based on how your body responds. Over time, you'll learn what works for you and build a routine that supports joint health for years to come.
