# What Muscles Do Upright Rows Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The upright row is a compound exercise targeting the upper traps and lateral deltoids. While effective for building these muscles, it's also controversial due to shoulder impingement concerns. Here's the complete breakdown—including important safety considerations.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Upper Trapezius

**Activation Level: Very High**

The upper traps are heavily targeted:
- Scapular elevation
- Significant activation
- Creates the "yoke" appearance
- Works throughout movement

### Lateral Deltoid (Side Delt)

**Activation Level: Very High**

The side of the shoulder:
- Shoulder abduction
- Works as arms raise
- Creates shoulder width
- Major contributor

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Anterior Deltoid

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The front shoulder:
- Shoulder flexion component
- Assists the lift
- Some contribution

### Biceps Brachii

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The biceps:
- Elbow flexion
- Assist the pull
- Work throughout

### Brachialis

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Under the biceps:
- Elbow flexion
- Assists the movement

### Infraspinatus

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Rotator cuff:
- Stabilization
- Some external rotation

### Forearms

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Grip muscles:
- Hold the weight
- Isometric work

## The Shoulder Impingement Concern

### What Happens

At the top of an upright row:
- Arm is abducted AND internally rotated
- Under load
- This compresses structures in the shoulder joint
- Can irritate rotator cuff tendons
- Risk of impingement syndrome

### The Controversial Position

Arm abduction + internal rotation + load = potential impingement

This is why many experts recommend **avoiding** upright rows or modifying them significantly.

### Risk Factors

Higher risk if you:
- Have existing shoulder issues
- Use narrow grip (more internal rotation)
- Pull too high (above chest)
- Go too heavy
- Have limited shoulder mobility

## Modifications to Reduce Risk

### Wider Grip

**Safest modification:**
- Hands wider than shoulder width
- Reduces internal rotation
- More lateral delt, less trap
- Significantly safer

### Don't Pull Too High

**Stop at chest level:**
- Not chin or face level
- Reduces impingement angle
- Still effective
- Much safer

### Cable Upright Row

**Constant tension, adjustable:**
- Can control angle better
- Smoother resistance
- Still requires caution

### Snatch-Grip High Pull

**Olympic lifting variation:**
- Very wide grip
- Explosive movement
- Different muscle emphasis
- Minimal impingement risk

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Weight hanging at thighs
- Grip (wide recommended)
- Arms extended
- Shoulders down

### Pull Phase (Concentric)
- Pull weight up
- Lead with elbows
- Elbows go out and up
- Stop at chest level (not higher)

### Top Position
- Elbows at or below shoulder level
- Weight at chest height
- Brief pause
- Don't go higher

### Lowering Phase (Eccentric)
- Control the descent
- Return to start
- Maintain tension
- Don't drop weight

## Common Mistakes

**Pulling too high**: Increases impingement. Stop at chest level.

**Narrow grip**: More internal rotation. Use wider grip.

**Going too heavy**: Form breaks down. Moderate weight.

**Elbows below hands**: Inefficient. Elbows should lead.

**Excessive internal rotation**: Shoulder stress. Grip wider.

**Ignoring shoulder pain**: Stop if pain occurs. Don't push through.

**Shrugging too much**: Becomes a shrug. Balanced movement.

## Alternative Exercises

If you want to avoid upright rows entirely:

### For Traps:
- Shrugs
- Face pulls
- Farmer carries

### For Side Delts:
- Lateral raises
- Cable lateral raises
- Leaning lateral raises

### For Both:
- Wide-grip upright rows (much safer)
- Snatch-grip high pulls
- Lu raises

These alternatives provide similar muscle work with less shoulder risk.

## Programming Recommendations

**If Doing Upright Rows:**
- Wide grip only
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Stop at chest level
- Moderate weight
- Stop if any shoulder discomfort

**In a Shoulder/Trap Workout:**
With modifications:
1. Overhead press (main pressing)
2. Lateral raises (side delt isolation)
3. **Wide-grip upright rows** (if chosen)
4. Face pulls (rear delt/health)

**Recommended alternatives instead:**
- Shrugs for traps
- Lateral raises for side delts

## Who Should Avoid Upright Rows

**Consider avoiding if:**
- History of shoulder impingement
- Current shoulder pain
- Rotator cuff issues
- Limited shoulder mobility
- You feel discomfort during the movement

**Safer alternatives exist** that train the same muscles without the risk.

## Who Might Use Them

**May be appropriate for:**
- Those with healthy shoulders
- Using wide grip modification
- Not pulling too high
- Moderate weight
- No pain during movement

Even then, alternatives may be preferable.

## The Honest Assessment

**Upright rows are effective** for traps and lateral delts.

**BUT:**
- Shoulder impingement risk is real
- Safer alternatives exist
- Modifications are essential
- Many experts recommend avoiding them

**Bottom line:** If you do upright rows, use wide grip, stop at chest level, and abandon them if any shoulder issues arise.

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: Upper traps (very high), lateral delts (very high)
- **Secondary**: Anterior delts, biceps, brachialis, forearms
- **Controversial exercise**: Shoulder impingement risk is real
- **If doing them**: Wide grip, stop at chest level, moderate weight
- **Better alternatives**: Shrugs + lateral raises train same muscles safer
- **Listen to your body**: Stop if any shoulder discomfort
- **Not essential**: You can build great traps and delts without this exercise

Upright rows work the upper traps and lateral delts effectively but carry shoulder impingement risk. If you choose to include them, use the modifications outlined. However, safer alternatives like shrugs and lateral raises can achieve similar results without the joint risk. Prioritize shoulder health over any single exercise.
