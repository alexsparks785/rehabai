# Rotator Cuff Surgery Exercises: Post-Operative Rehabilitation

Rotator cuff surgery repairs torn tendons in your shoulder. Success depends heavily on proper rehabilitation—protecting the repair while progressively restoring motion and strength. This guide outlines what to expect.

## Important: Follow Your Surgeon's Protocol

**Every surgery is different.** Your specific protocol depends on:
- Tear size (small, medium, large, massive)
- Repair technique
- Tissue quality
- Surgeon preference

**This guide provides general principles. Always defer to YOUR surgeon's specific instructions.**

## Understanding the Healing Process

**Why rehabilitation takes so long:**
- Tendon-to-bone healing is slow
- Blood supply to tendons is limited
- Repair must be protected from stress
- Strengthening too early can damage repair

**Typical timeline:**
- Sling: 4-6 weeks
- Passive motion: Weeks 0-6
- Active motion: Weeks 6-12
- Strengthening: Weeks 12+
- Full recovery: 6-12 months

## Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0-6)

### Sling Use
- Wear as directed (usually 4-6 weeks)
- Remove only for exercises and hygiene
- Sleep in sling (or as directed)

### Goals
- Protect the repair
- Prevent stiffness (gentle motion)
- Manage pain and swelling
- Maintain elbow, wrist, hand mobility

### Passive Range of Motion

**Passive = someone else moves your arm (or gravity assists)**

**Pendulum Exercises** (Codman's)
1. Lean forward, support yourself
2. Let arm hang relaxed
3. Make small circles (gravity moves arm)
4. 10-15 circles each direction
5. 2-3 times daily

**Passive Forward Flexion** (Supine)
1. Lie on back
2. Use good arm to lift surgical arm
3. Lift to allowed range (often 90-120° initially)
4. Hold briefly, lower slowly
5. 10-15 repetitions

**Passive External Rotation** (Supine)
1. Lie on back, elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Use stick or good arm to rotate forearm outward
3. To allowed range (often 30-45° initially)
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Elbow, Wrist, Hand Exercises

**Keep these joints mobile:**
- Elbow bends and straightens
- Wrist circles and flexion/extension
- Finger movements and fist making
- Grip exercises

### What NOT to Do
- Lift anything with surgical arm
- Reach behind your back
- Active shoulder movement
- Sleep on surgical side
- Drive (until cleared)

## Phase 2: Early Motion (Weeks 6-12)

### Transition Period
- Sling discontinued (usually week 6)
- Begin active-assisted motion
- Progress to active motion
- Still no strengthening

### Active-Assisted Range of Motion

**Pulley Exercises**
1. Over-door pulley system
2. Good arm helps lift surgical arm
3. Progress range as tolerated
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Wand Exercises**
1. Hold stick with both hands
2. Good arm assists surgical arm
3. Forward elevation, external rotation
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Active Range of Motion (When Cleared)

**Active Forward Flexion**
1. Lift arm forward and up
2. Under own power
3. Progress range gradually
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Active External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate forearm outward
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active Abduction** (Side Raise)
1. Lift arm out to side
2. May start in scapular plane (30° forward)
3. Progress range
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Scapular Exercises

**Scapular Squeezes**
1. Squeeze shoulder blades together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Scapular Clocks**
1. Move shoulder blade: up, down, forward, backward
2. Isolate scapular movement
3. 10 each direction

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 12-20)

**Begin strengthening only when cleared by surgeon.**

### Isometric Exercises (First)

**Isometric External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Push back of wrist against wall
3. No movement, just tension
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

**Isometric Internal Rotation**
1. Same position
2. Push palm against wall
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Isometric Forward Flexion**
1. Face wall
2. Push fist into wall
3. Hold 5-10 seconds

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Light weight or no weight
4. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
5. 2-3 sets of 15

**Standing External Rotation with Band**
1. Band anchored at elbow height
2. Elbow at side
3. Rotate outward against band
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Standing Internal Rotation with Band**
1. Same setup
2. Rotate inward
3. 2-3 sets of 15

### Scapular Strengthening

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Lie face down on bench
2. Y: Arms overhead, lift with thumbs up
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze and lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Rows**
1. Light weight or band
2. Pull elbows back
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. 2-3 sets of 15

## Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 20+)

### Progressive Loading

**Increase resistance gradually:**
- Progress weight slowly
- Monitor for pain
- Quality over quantity

### Exercises to Progress

**Shoulder Press** (When Ready)
1. Start very light
2. Progress slowly
3. May begin seated

**Lat Pulldown**
1. Light to moderate weight
2. 2-3 sets of 12-15

**Rows** (Increased Resistance)
1. Cable, band, or dumbbell
2. 2-3 sets of 12

**Push-Ups** (Modified First)
1. Wall → counter → knees → full
2. Progress as strength allows

### Functional Activities
- Reaching overhead
- Lifting light objects
- Daily activity practice
- Work-specific movements

## Phase 5: Return to Activities (Months 6-12)

### Criteria Before Returning
- Full, pain-free ROM
- Strength near normal
- No pain with activities
- Surgeon clearance

### Return to Work
- Desk work: Often 6-8 weeks
- Light manual: 3-4 months
- Heavy manual/overhead: 6-12 months

### Return to Sports
- Varies by sport and demands
- Golf: 4-6 months
- Swimming: 6-9 months
- Throwing/overhead sports: 9-12 months
- Contact sports: 6-12 months

## Sample Weekly Schedule (Week 14)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, ROM
2. Isometrics: All directions
3. Side-lying ER: 2×15
4. Band ER/IR: 2×15 each
5. Rows: 2×15

### Day 2
1. ROM exercises: 10 min
2. Scapular exercises
3. Light stretching

### Day 3
1. Warm-up: 5-10 min
2. Prone Y-T-W: 2×10 each
3. Face pulls: 2×15
4. Band exercises: All directions

### Day 4: Rest or Light ROM

### Day 5
1. Full exercise program
2. Progress weights if tolerated
3. Stretching

## Common Challenges

### Stiffness
- Most common issue
- Maintain ROM exercises
- May need formal PT
- Patience required

### Weakness
- Expected initially
- Progressive strengthening helps
- Full strength takes 6-12 months

### Night Pain
- Common early on
- Sleep positioning important
- Usually improves with time

### Re-Tear
- Risk depends on tear size, tissue quality
- Following restrictions is crucial
- Report sudden pain or weakness

## When to Contact Your Surgeon

- Sudden increase in pain
- New weakness
- Fever, redness, drainage at incision
- Excessive swelling
- Any concerns about progress

## Key Takeaways

1. **Follow YOUR protocol** — every surgery is different
2. **Protect the repair** — don't rush through phases
3. **Passive before active** — let someone else move your arm first
4. **Strengthen when cleared** — usually week 12+
5. **Progress gradually** — slow and steady
6. **Be patient** — full recovery takes 6-12 months
7. **ROM prevents stiffness** — don't skip exercises
8. **Report problems early** — communicate with your surgeon

Rotator cuff surgery rehabilitation is a marathon, not a sprint. Trust the process, follow your protocol, and you'll maximize your chances of a successful outcome.
