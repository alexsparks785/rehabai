      <h1>How to Fix Shoulder Impingement: Exercises That Actually Help</h1>

      <p>Pain when lifting your arm overhead? A pinching sensation in the front or side of your shoulder? Discomfort reaching behind your back? You likely have shoulder impingement—one of the most common shoulder problems. The good news: most cases respond well to the right exercises.</p>

      <h2>What Is Shoulder Impingement?</h2>

      <p>Shoulder impingement occurs when the tendons of the rotator cuff get pinched between the arm bone (humerus) and the roof of the shoulder (acromion) during arm elevation. This compression irritates the tendons and bursa, causing pain and inflammation.</p>

      <p><strong>Common symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain when lifting arm to the side or front</li>
        <li>Pain reaching overhead</li>
        <li>Painful arc (pain between ~60-120 degrees of elevation)</li>
        <li>Discomfort reaching behind your back</li>
        <li>Pain lying on the affected shoulder</li>
        <li>Weakness with overhead activities</li>
        <li>Pain that worsens at night</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Why Does It Happen?</h2>

      <p><strong>Contributing factors:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Poor posture (rounded shoulders, forward head)</li>
        <li>Weak rotator cuff muscles</li>
        <li>Weak scapular stabilizers</li>
        <li>Tight chest and lat muscles</li>
        <li>Poor shoulder blade mechanics</li>
        <li>Repetitive overhead activities</li>
        <li>Sudden increase in overhead work</li>
        <li>Acromion shape (structural factor)</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Most impingement cases stem from muscle imbalances and poor mechanics—things that respond to exercise.</p>

      <h2>The Key to Fixing Impingement</h2>

      <p>The rotator cuff's job is to keep the ball of the humerus centered in the socket as you move. When the rotator cuff is weak or the scapula doesn't move properly, the humeral head rides up and creates impingement.</p>

      <p><strong>The fix:</strong></p>
      <ol>
        <li>Strengthen the rotator cuff (especially external rotators)</li>
        <li>Improve scapular stability and mechanics</li>
        <li>Address posture and mobility issues</li>
        <li>Modify aggravating activities</li>
      </ol>

      <h2>Exercises for Shoulder Impingement</h2>

      <h3>Rotator Cuff Strengthening</h3>

      <h4>1. Side-Lying External Rotation</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side with the affected arm on top. Elbow bent 90 degrees, upper arm resting against your body. Holding a light weight (1-3 lbs to start), rotate your forearm toward the ceiling. Lower slowly. This is the gold-standard rotator cuff exercise.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Standing External Rotation with Band</h4>
      <p>Stand with elbow bent 90 degrees, upper arm at your side. Hold a resistance band attached at elbow height. Rotate your forearm outward against the band. Keep your elbow pinned to your side. Return slowly.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 each arm</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Internal Rotation with Band</h4>
      <p>Same setup but rotate inward against the band. Internal rotators matter too, but external rotators are usually the priority in impingement.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 sets of 15 each arm</strong></p>

      <h4>4. High External Rotation (90/90)</h4>
      <p>Once basic external rotation is strong, progress to this. Arm at 90 degrees abduction, elbow bent 90 degrees. Rotate forearm from pointing down toward pointing up. Use band or light weight. This trains the rotator cuff in a more functional position.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12</strong></p>

      <h3>Scapular Stability</h3>

      <h4>5. Prone Y Raise</h4>
      <p>Lie face down, arms extended overhead at 45 degrees (Y position), thumbs up. Lift arms while squeezing shoulder blades down and back. This strengthens the lower traps—crucial for proper scapular mechanics.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Prone T Raise</h4>
      <p>Same position but arms straight out to the sides (T position). Lift while squeezing shoulder blades together. This hits the mid-traps and rhomboids.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Prone W Raise</h4>
      <p>Arms bent, elbows at about 90 degrees, forming a W shape. Lift while squeezing shoulder blades back and down. Combines external rotation with scapular retraction.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Face Pulls</h4>
      <p>Use a cable or band at face height. Pull toward your face while externally rotating, ending with hands beside your ears and elbows high. Squeeze shoulder blades together. One of the best exercises for shoulder health.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Serratus Wall Slides</h4>
      <p>Face a wall with forearms against it. Slide arms up the wall while pushing into the wall and protracting your shoulder blades (pushing them apart). This strengthens the serratus anterior—key for overhead movement.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12</strong></p>

      <h3>Mobility and Stretching</h3>

      <h4>10. Doorway Chest Stretch</h4>
      <p>Stand in a doorway with forearm on the frame, elbow at shoulder height. Step through gently until you feel a stretch across your chest. Tight pecs contribute to forward shoulder posture and impingement. Don't stretch to pain.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Lat Stretch</h4>
      <p>Kneel facing a bench or chair. Place elbows on the surface, palms together. Sit hips back while letting chest drop through arms. Tight lats can limit overhead mobility.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Thoracic Extension on Foam Roller</h4>
      <p>Lie with a foam roller across your upper back. Support your head. Let your upper back extend over the roller. A stiff thoracic spine makes the shoulder work harder for overhead range.</p>
      <p><strong>2 minutes, moving to different segments</strong></p>

      <h4>13. Sleeper Stretch (Gentle)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your affected side, shoulder at 90 degrees. Use your other hand to gently push your forearm toward the floor, internally rotating the shoulder. Be gentle—don't force this stretch if it causes pain.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 20-30 seconds, only if pain-free</strong></p>

      <h3>Movement Retraining</h3>

      <h4>14. Wall Slides</h4>
      <p>Stand with back against wall, arms in "goal post" position with back of hands, elbows, and shoulders touching the wall. Slowly slide arms up while maintaining wall contact. If you can't keep contact, that shows where mobility or control breaks down.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10</strong></p>

      <h4>15. Scapular Plane Raises</h4>
      <p>Raise your arm in the scapular plane (about 30-45 degrees in front of straight to the side). Thumbs up. This is often less provocative than straight to the side. Start with no weight, progress slowly.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12, only in pain-free range</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Phase 1: Reduce Irritation (Weeks 1-2)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Avoid overhead activities and painful movements</li>
        <li>Side-lying external rotation: 3x15 daily</li>
        <li>Doorway chest stretch: 30 seconds each side</li>
        <li>Prone Y and T raises: 2x12 each</li>
        <li>Ice if needed after activity</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 2: Build Strength (Weeks 3-8)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>All rotator cuff exercises: 3x15</li>
        <li>Prone Y-T-W: 3x12 each</li>
        <li>Face pulls: 3x15</li>
        <li>Serratus wall slides: 3x12</li>
        <li>Thoracic extension mobility: daily</li>
        <li>Wall slides: 3x10</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 3: Return to Activity (Weeks 8+)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Continue strengthening 3x/week</li>
        <li>Gradually reintroduce overhead activities</li>
        <li>Scapular plane raises with light weight</li>
        <li>Monitor for return of symptoms</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Activity Modification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Sleeping:</strong> Don't sleep on the affected shoulder; hug a pillow</li>
        <li><strong>Reaching:</strong> Avoid reaching behind your back; use other arm</li>
        <li><strong>Overhead work:</strong> Reduce or eliminate until symptoms improve</li>
        <li><strong>Posture:</strong> Address rounded shoulders and forward head</li>
        <li><strong>Training:</strong> Avoid upright rows, behind-neck presses, dips that go too deep</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to See Results?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>2-4 weeks:</strong> Reduced daily pain with activity modification</li>
        <li><strong>6-8 weeks:</strong> Significant improvement with consistent exercises</li>
        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Most cases resolve or dramatically improve</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance exercises prevent recurrence</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Red Flags—When to See a Doctor</h2>

      <p>Get evaluated if:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain is severe or started after trauma</li>
        <li>You can't lift your arm at all</li>
        <li>Significant weakness is present</li>
        <li>Pain at rest or waking you at night regularly</li>
        <li>No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling in arm or hand</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises to Avoid</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Upright rows</li>
        <li>Behind-the-neck presses or pulldowns</li>
        <li>Dips (or limit range)</li>
        <li>Overhead pressing through pain</li>
        <li>Bench press with extreme flare (keep elbows at 45-degree angle)</li>
        <li>Any exercise that reproduces the pain</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Shoulder impingement is frustrating but highly treatable with the right approach. Focus on strengthening the rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers, address mobility limitations, and be patient. Most people see significant improvement within 2-3 months of consistent work—often without needing injections or surgery.</p>
