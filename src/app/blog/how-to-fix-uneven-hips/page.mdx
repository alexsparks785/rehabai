      <h1>How to Fix Uneven Hips: Exercises for Pelvic Alignment</h1>

      <p>If one hip sits higher than the other, your pants hem differently on each side, or you always feel "off" when standing—you likely have uneven hips. This common issue can cause back pain, hip discomfort, and movement compensations throughout your body. The good news: most cases respond well to targeted exercise.</p>

      <h2>Types of Hip Unevenness</h2>

      <p><strong>Lateral pelvic tilt:</strong> One hip bone is higher than the other when viewed from behind. This is the most common type people notice.</p>

      <p><strong>Pelvic rotation:</strong> One hip sits forward while the other sits back. Often missed but equally problematic.</p>

      <p><strong>Combined:</strong> Many people have both lateral tilt and rotation occurring together.</p>

      <h2>What Causes Uneven Hips?</h2>

      <p><strong>Structural causes (less common):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>True leg length difference (anatomical)</li>
        <li>Scoliosis</li>
        <li>Previous injury or surgery</li>
        <li>Hip joint abnormalities</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Functional causes (more common and fixable):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Muscle imbalances (tight on one side, weak on the other)</li>
        <li>Habitual postures (always standing on one leg, crossing same leg)</li>
        <li>Repetitive one-sided activities</li>
        <li>Compensation from injury</li>
        <li>Weak core stability</li>
        <li>Tight hip flexors (often asymmetric)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How to Identify Your Pattern</h2>

      <h3>Check for Lateral Tilt</h3>
      <p>Stand in front of a mirror in underwear or tight clothing. Place your hands on your hip bones (the bony points at the front). Are they level? If one hand is higher, that side has the elevated hip.</p>

      <h3>Check for Rotation</h3>
      <p>Same position—but now look at whether one hip bone sits more forward than the other. You can also check by seeing if your belt line is level from the side view.</p>

      <h3>The Functional Test</h3>
      <p>Lie on your back with legs extended. Have someone check if your ankle bones are level, or look in a mirror. Then bend your knees with feet flat—does the unevenness change? If it improves when knees are bent, muscle tightness is likely the culprit.</p>

      <h2>Common Muscle Patterns</h2>

      <p><strong>Hip hiking (elevated hip side):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Tight quadratus lumborum (QL)</li>
        <li>Tight hip adductors</li>
        <li>Weak hip abductors (glute medius)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Hip drop (lower hip side):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Weak quadratus lumborum</li>
        <li>Tight hip abductors</li>
        <li>Weak hip adductors</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Rotational pattern:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>One hip flexor tighter than the other</li>
        <li>Asymmetric oblique strength</li>
        <li>One-sided glute weakness</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises to Balance Your Hips</h2>

      <h3>For the High Hip Side (Lengthen + Relax)</h3>

      <h4>1. Side-Lying QL Stretch</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side (high hip up) with bottom arm extended overhead. Let your top leg drop behind your bottom leg toward the floor. Reach your top arm overhead, creating a long stretch along your high side. Breathe deeply and hold.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 60-90 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Standing Side Bend Stretch</h4>
      <p>Stand with feet hip-width apart. Raise the arm on your high hip side overhead. Lean away from that side, creating a stretch along the elevated hip's side body. Don't twist—pure side bend.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds, repeat 2-3 times</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Adductor Stretch (High Side)</h4>
      <p>In a half-kneeling position with the high hip side leg forward, shift your weight into a groin stretch. Keep your torso upright. The inner thigh of the elevated hip often needs lengthening.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds</strong></p>

      <h3>For the Low Hip Side (Strengthen)</h3>

      <h4>4. Side Plank Hip Dip (Low Side Down)</h4>
      <p>Set up in a side plank on your low hip side (the side that drops). Lower your hip toward the ground, then lift it back up by engaging your side body (QL and obliques). This strengthens the weak stabilizers.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 reps</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Side-Lying Hip Abduction (Low Side)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your high hip side (so your low hip is on top). Lift the top leg toward the ceiling, leading with the heel, keeping the leg straight. This strengthens the glute medius on your weaker side.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>For Both Sides (Restore Symmetry)</h3>

      <h4>6. 90-90 Hip Stretch</h4>
      <p>Sit with one leg in front (hip and knee at 90 degrees) and one leg to the side (also 90-90). Lean your torso toward the front leg. Switch sides and notice the difference—spend extra time on the tighter side.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each side, extra time on tight side</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Dead Bug with Pelvic Control</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, knees bent 90 degrees in the air. Press your lower back flat and keep it there. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg, maintaining a perfectly level pelvis. Use a mirror or hands on hips to monitor.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 8 each side, focusing on keeping pelvis still</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Bird Dog with Hip Focus</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg. The key: keep your hips perfectly level throughout. Don't let the pelvis rotate or one hip hike up. Use a dowel on your back for feedback.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Single-Leg Glute Bridge</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, one foot flat on the floor. Lift the other leg. Bridge up, focusing on keeping your pelvis level (don't let one hip drop). Hold 2 seconds at the top. Compare sides—do extra reps on the weaker side.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Pallof Press with Level Hips</h4>
      <p>Stand sideways to a cable or band anchor. Press the handle straight out, resisting the rotation. Focus intently on keeping your hips level and square—don't let them twist or tilt. This builds symmetric core control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h2>Address Hip Flexor Asymmetry</h2>

      <p>Tight hip flexors on one side can rotate the pelvis forward on that side:</p>

      <h4>11. Half-Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch</h4>
      <p>Kneel on one knee, other foot in front. Tuck your pelvis under (posterior tilt) and shift forward until you feel a stretch in the back leg's hip flexor. Hold longer on the tighter side.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each side, extra on tight side</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Couch Stretch</h4>
      <p>Kneel facing away from a couch or wall, place one foot up against it (toes pointing up). Step the other foot forward into a lunge. Keep your torso upright and pelvis tucked. Intense hip flexor and quad stretch.</p>
      <p><strong>60 seconds each side</strong></p>

        <li>90-90 hip stretch: 60 seconds each, extra on tight</li>

        <li>Single-leg glute bridge: 3x12 each (extra on weak side)</li>
        <li>Pallof press: 3x12 each</li>

      <h2>Lifestyle Changes</h2>

        <li><strong>Switch crossing legs:</strong> Or better, avoid crossing altogether</li>
        <li><strong>Balance your bag:</strong> Alternate shoulders or use a backpack</li>
        <li><strong>Check your workstation:</strong> Make sure you're not always twisted one direction</li>
        <li><strong>Sleep position:</strong> Side sleepers—try a pillow between knees to keep pelvis neutral</li>

        <li>History of scoliosis or hip problems</li>

But for the majority of people with uneven hips, consistent targeted exercise can restore balance and relieve the symptoms that come with asymmetry. The key is identifying your specific pattern and addressing both the tight and weak sides.</p>
