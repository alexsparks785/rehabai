# How to Fix a Sprained Ankle: Recovery Exercises and Timeline

A sprained ankle is one of the most common injuries—and also one of the most commonly poorly managed. Skip the rehab, and you're setting yourself up for chronic instability and repeat sprains. Do it right, and you'll come back stronger than before.

## What Is an Ankle Sprain?

An ankle sprain occurs when ligaments—the bands connecting bones—are stretched or torn. Most sprains (about 85%) are "inversion" sprains, where the ankle rolls outward and the foot turns inward, injuring the outer ligaments.

### Grades of Ankle Sprains

**Grade 1 (Mild):**
- Ligament stretched but not torn
- Mild pain and swelling
- Can usually walk
- Recovery: 1-2 weeks

**Grade 2 (Moderate):**
- Partial tear of ligament
- Moderate pain, swelling, bruising
- Difficulty walking, some instability
- Recovery: 4-6 weeks

**Grade 3 (Severe):**
- Complete ligament tear
- Significant pain, swelling, bruising
- Unable to bear weight, significant instability
- Recovery: 8-12+ weeks

## When to See a Doctor

**Seek medical evaluation if:**
- You can't bear weight at all
- Significant deformity or abnormal position
- Severe pain that doesn't improve
- Numbness in the foot
- Swelling doesn't decrease after a few days
- You heard a "pop" and have significant instability
- Previous ankle injuries that didn't heal properly

**X-rays or imaging** may be needed to rule out fractures.

## Phase 1: Protection and Pain Management (Days 1-3)

### PEACE Protocol (Updated Approach)

**P**rotection — Avoid activities that increase pain
**E**levation — Elevate ankle above heart level when possible
**A**void anti-inflammatories initially — Early inflammation aids healing
**C**ompression — Elastic bandage to control swelling
**E**ducation — Understand that active recovery (not prolonged rest) leads to better outcomes

### What to Do

#### Rest (But Not Complete Immobilization)
- Avoid activities that cause significant pain
- Gentle movement within pain tolerance is okay
- Use crutches if needed for Grade 2-3 sprains

#### Ice
- Apply ice for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours
- Wrap in a thin towel to protect skin
- Helps control pain and swelling in first 48-72 hours

#### Compression
- Elastic bandage wrapped from toes toward calf
- Should be snug but not cutting off circulation
- Rewrap if it becomes too loose or too tight

#### Elevation
- Elevate ankle above heart level when resting
- Helps reduce swelling
- Use pillows under the calf

### Early Movement (Within Pain Tolerance)

#### Ankle Pumps
**How to do it:**
1. Flex foot up (toes toward shin)
2. Point foot down (toes away)
3. Do 20-30 pumps, several times daily
4. Promotes circulation without stressing ligaments

#### Ankle Circles
**How to do it:**
1. Rotate ankle in circles
2. Keep movement gentle and within comfortable range
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Stop if painful

## Phase 2: Early Recovery (Days 4-14)

### Goals
- Restore range of motion
- Begin weight bearing as tolerated
- Reduce swelling
- Prevent stiffness

### Range of Motion Exercises

#### Towel Stretch (Plantar Flexion)
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Loop towel around ball of foot
3. Gently pull foot toward you
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 3-4 times

#### Alphabet Writing
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with foot elevated
2. Use your big toe to "write" the alphabet in the air
3. This moves the ankle through all ranges
4. Do once or twice daily

#### Seated Ankle Range of Motion
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with foot flat on floor
2. Slide foot forward and back (like accelerator pedal)
3. Then side to side (windshield wiper motion)
4. 15-20 reps each direction

### Weight Bearing Progression

**Progress based on pain, not time:**
1. Non-weight bearing with crutches
2. Partial weight bearing (25-50%)
3. Weight bearing as tolerated with crutches
4. Weight bearing without crutches
5. Normal walking

**Rule:** If walking increases pain or swelling significantly, you're doing too much.

### Gentle Strengthening

#### Isometric Exercises
**How to do it:**
1. Push foot against an immovable object (wall, table leg)
2. Push in all four directions: up, down, in, out
3. Hold each for 5-10 seconds
4. Do 10 reps each direction
5. Should be painless or minimal discomfort

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 2-6)

### Goals
- Rebuild strength in all directions
- Improve proprioception (balance)
- Progress toward normal activities

### Resistance Band Exercises

#### 4-Way Ankle Strengthening
**How to do it:**

**Dorsiflexion (pulling up):**
1. Loop band around foot, anchor in front
2. Pull toes toward shin against resistance
3. 15-20 reps

**Plantarflexion (pointing):**
1. Loop band around foot, hold ends in hands
2. Point toes away against resistance
3. 15-20 reps

**Inversion (turning in):**
1. Loop band around foot, anchor to the outside
2. Turn sole of foot inward against resistance
3. 15-20 reps

**Eversion (turning out):**
1. Loop band around foot, anchor to the inside
2. Turn sole of foot outward against resistance
3. 15-20 reps—ESPECIALLY IMPORTANT after inversion sprain

#### Calf Raises
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet hip-width apart
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. Progress: two legs → single leg
5. Do 15-20 reps

### Balance Training (Critical!)

Poor proprioception is the main reason people re-sprain their ankles. This training is essential.

#### Single-Leg Balance
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on injured leg
2. Hold for as long as possible
3. Use wall for support initially
4. Progress: eyes open → eyes closed
5. Work up to 60 seconds

#### Tandem Stance
**How to do it:**
1. Stand heel-to-toe (like on a balance beam)
2. Hold for 30 seconds
3. Switch which foot is in front
4. Progress to eyes closed

#### Balance on Unstable Surface
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on a pillow, foam pad, or wobble board
2. Two legs first, then single leg
3. Build up time gradually

## Phase 4: Functional Return (Weeks 4-8+)

### Goals
- Return to normal activities
- Build confidence in the ankle
- Prevent re-injury

### Agility Exercises

#### Heel Walking and Toe Walking
**How to do it:**
1. Walk on heels for 20-30 steps
2. Walk on toes for 20-30 steps
3. Strengthens different ankle muscles

#### Lateral Stepping
**How to do it:**
1. Step sideways over a line
2. Stay on the balls of your feet
3. Progress speed as tolerated
4. 20 steps each direction

#### Figure-8 Walking
**How to do it:**
1. Walk in a figure-8 pattern
2. Start with wide curves
3. Progress to tighter turns
4. Focus on stability through the turns

### Sport-Specific Progression

**For Runners:**
1. Walk → Fast walk → Jog (straight lines)
2. Add curves and turns
3. Progress to running
4. Build distance and speed gradually

**For Cutting Sports:**
1. Linear running
2. Wide turns and curves
3. Controlled direction changes
4. Reactive agility drills
5. Practice situations
6. Return to full play

### Jump and Land Training

**Two-Leg Jumps:**
1. Small jumps in place
2. Progress height gradually
3. Focus on soft, controlled landing

**Single-Leg Hops:**
1. Small hops on injured leg
2. Progress height and distance
3. Practice landing stability

**Multi-Directional Hops:**
1. Hop forward, backward, side to side
2. Create a cross pattern on the floor
3. Hop to each point and stick the landing

## Preventing Re-Injury

Ankle sprains have high re-injury rates—up to 70% without proper rehab. Key prevention strategies:

### Continue Balance Training
- Even after recovery, do balance exercises 2-3 times per week
- This is the single most effective prevention measure

### Ankle Strengthening
- Maintain strength in all directions
- Eversion strength is particularly important

### Proper Footwear
- Appropriate shoes for your activity
- Consider high-top shoes for support in certain sports
- Replace worn-out shoes

### Bracing (When Appropriate)
- Ankle braces or taping can help during high-risk activities
- Especially useful when returning to sport
- Doesn't replace strength and balance training

### Warm-Up
- Always warm up before activity
- Include ankle mobility and activation exercises

## Sample Weekly Program (Weeks 3-4)

**Day 1:**
- Range of motion exercises
- 4-way resistance band: 2 sets of 15
- Single-leg balance: 3 x 30 seconds
- Calf raises: 2 x 15

**Day 2:**
- Light walking/cycling
- Balance on unstable surface: 3 x 20 seconds

**Day 3:**
- 4-way resistance band: 2 sets of 15
- Heel/toe walking: 30 steps each
- Single-leg balance with eyes closed: 3 x 15 seconds
- Lateral stepping

**Day 4:**
- Active rest—gentle walking

**Day 5:**
- 4-way resistance band: 2 sets of 20
- Figure-8 walking
- Single-leg hops (if ready): 2 x 10
- Balance training

**Day 6-7:**
- Light activity
- Balance practice
- Stretching

## When to Return to Sport

Criteria for safe return:
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength equal to uninjured side
- Can jog, cut, and jump without pain
- Can complete sport-specific drills
- Confidence in the ankle
- No swelling with activity

## The Bottom Line

Ankle sprains heal, but ligaments are forever changed after a sprain. The difference between chronic instability and a fully functional ankle comes down to rehabilitation—especially balance training.

Don't skip the boring exercises. Don't return to sport too quickly. Don't assume it's fine just because the pain is gone.

Phases matter: protect early, restore motion, build strength, train balance, progress functionally. Rush any phase, and you'll likely be dealing with this ankle again.

Invest 6-8 weeks in proper rehab now, or deal with a recurring problem for years. The choice is clear.
