# Stress Fracture Recovery Exercises: Return to Activity Safely

A stress fracture means your bone couldn't keep up with the demands placed on it. Recovery requires patience—but not complete inactivity. The right exercises maintain fitness, address contributing factors, and prepare you for a safe return to activity.

## Understanding Stress Fractures

Stress fractures occur when repetitive loading exceeds the bone's ability to repair itself. Unlike acute fractures from trauma, stress fractures develop gradually from accumulated microtrauma.

**Common locations:**
- Metatarsals (foot) — most common in runners
- Tibia (shin) — second most common
- Fibula (lower leg)
- Femoral neck (hip) — high risk, requires careful management
- Navicular (foot) — notorious for slow healing
- Sacrum and pelvis

**Risk factors:**
- Sudden increase in training volume or intensity
- Poor bone health (low vitamin D, calcium, or hormonal issues)
- Biomechanical factors (foot type, muscle weakness, flexibility deficits)
- Inadequate recovery between sessions
- Low energy availability (undereating relative to activity)
- Female athlete triad / RED-S

## Healing Timeline

**General timeline (varies by location and severity):**
- Initial rest phase: 2-6 weeks (weight-bearing status varies)
- Progressive loading: Weeks 4-8
- Return to impact: Weeks 6-12
- Full return to sport: 8-16 weeks

**High-risk stress fractures (longer healing, stricter management):**
- Femoral neck (hip)
- Anterior tibial cortex ("dreaded black line")
- Navicular
- Fifth metatarsal base (Jones fracture)
- Sesamoids

Always follow your physician's specific guidance—these timelines are general.

## Phase 1: Rest and Cross-Training (Weeks 0-4)

### Goals:
- Protect healing bone
- Maintain cardiovascular fitness
- Address contributing factors
- Prevent deconditioning

### Non-Impact Cardio Options

**Pool Running (Aqua Jogging)**
Excellent for maintaining running-specific fitness without impact.

**How to do it:**
1. Use flotation belt in deep water
2. Mimic running motion
3. Keep body upright
4. Focus on form: high knees, arm drive
5. Can do intervals or steady-state

**Workouts:**
- Easy: 30-45 min steady
- Tempo: 10 min easy, 20 min moderate-hard, 10 min easy
- Intervals: 5 min easy, 6×2 min hard/1 min easy, 5 min easy

**Cycling**
Low-impact option if tolerated (some stress fracture locations may not allow).

**Guidelines:**
- Stationary bike preferred initially
- Keep resistance moderate
- Watch for pain at fracture site
- Great for maintaining leg strength

**Swimming**
Zero impact, full-body cardio.

**Note:** Kicking may stress some stress fracture locations (tibia, foot).

**Elliptical/Anti-Gravity Treadmill**
- Elliptical: Low-impact option if pain-free
- AlterG (anti-gravity treadmill): Allows walking/running at reduced body weight

### Upper Body and Core Training

Maintain overall fitness while protecting the injury.

**Safe exercises:**
- Seated upper body weights
- Core work (planks, dead bugs, bird dogs—if position is tolerated)
- Resistance band exercises
- Machine-based training

### What to Avoid
- Impact activities (running, jumping, hopping)
- Activities that cause pain at fracture site
- Excessive walking if weight-bearing restricted
- High-risk falls (unstable surfaces)

## Phase 2: Progressive Loading (Weeks 4-8)

As bone healing progresses, gradually reintroduce loading.

### Walking Progression

**Criteria to begin:**
- Pain-free with daily activities
- No tenderness at fracture site (or significantly reduced)
- Cleared by physician

**Sample progression:**
- Week 1: 10-15 min flat walking daily
- Week 2: 20-25 min walking
- Week 3: 30 min walking, introduce gentle hills
- Week 4: 30-40 min walking, varied terrain

**Pain rule:** Stop if pain returns at fracture site.

### Lower Body Strengthening

Address muscle weaknesses that contributed to the stress fracture.

**Calf Raises**
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on flat ground (progress to step later)
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. Both legs together initially

**Progression:**
- Bilateral → single-leg
- Flat → raised (heel drop below step)
- Bodyweight → added weight

**Hip Strengthening**
Hip weakness is a major contributor to lower extremity stress fractures.

**Key exercises:**
- Clamshells with band
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Single-leg glute bridge
- Standing hip abduction with band
- Monster walks

**Reps:** 2-3 sets of 15-20

**Quad and Hamstring Strengthening**
- Leg press (when cleared for loading)
- Hamstring curls
- Romanian deadlifts (bodyweight → weighted)
- Step-ups (low step initially)

### Balance and Proprioception

**Single-leg balance progression:**
1. Two legs → single leg
2. Eyes open → eyes closed
3. Stable surface → foam pad
4. Add movement challenges (reaching, catching)

**Goal:** 30+ seconds single-leg balance with eyes closed

### Core Stability

Strong core reduces stress on lower extremities.

**Exercises:**
- Dead bug variations
- Bird dog
- Pallof press
- Side plank progressions
- Plank with limb lifts

## Phase 3: Return to Running (Weeks 8-12+)

### Criteria to Begin Running
- Pain-free walking 45-60 min
- No tenderness at fracture site
- Single-leg hop test pain-free (if cleared)
- Physician clearance
- Adequate strength (single-leg calf raise, balance tests)

### Walk-Run Progression

**Week 1:**
- Day 1: Walk 5 min, jog 1 min, repeat 4 times
- Day 2: Rest
- Day 3: Walk 5 min, jog 1 min, repeat 5 times
- Day 4-5: Rest or cross-train

**Week 2:**
- Walk 4 min, jog 2 min, repeat 4 times
- Every other day, rest days between

**Week 3:**
- Walk 3 min, jog 3 min, repeat 4 times

**Week 4:**
- Walk 2 min, jog 4 min, repeat 4 times

**Week 5:**
- Walk 1 min, jog 5 min, repeat 4 times

**Week 6:**
- Continuous jog 20-25 min at easy pace

**Continue progressing:** Add 5-10% volume per week after establishing pain-free base.

### Running Surface
- Start on forgiving surfaces (track, grass, treadmill)
- Progress to firmer surfaces gradually
- Avoid cambered roads initially

### Pain Monitoring
- Any pain at fracture site: Stop and rest
- Soreness elsewhere (muscles): Generally OK
- Next-day symptoms: Rest until resolved

## Addressing Contributing Factors

### Biomechanical Assessment

Consider evaluation for:
- Foot mechanics (overpronation, high arch, flat feet)
- Running gait analysis
- Hip and ankle mobility
- Leg length discrepancy

**May need:**
- Orthotics or supportive footwear
- Gait retraining (increased cadence often helpful)
- Flexibility work

### Training Errors

Most stress fractures involve training errors. Review:
- Recent volume increases (>10% per week is risky)
- Intensity spikes (new speed work, hills)
- Recovery adequacy (sleep, easy days, rest days)
- Surface changes (soft → hard)
- Footwear (old shoes, new shoes)

### Nutritional Factors

**Bone health checklist:**
- Adequate calorie intake for activity level
- Calcium: 1000-1300 mg/day
- Vitamin D: 600-2000 IU/day (check levels)
- Protein: 1.2-2.0 g/kg body weight

**Red flags for evaluation:**
- Menstrual irregularities
- History of disordered eating
- Multiple stress fractures
- Stress fracture at young age

### Sleep and Recovery

- 7-9 hours quality sleep
- Adequate rest days (bone remodels during rest)
- Stress management

## Site-Specific Considerations

### Metatarsal Stress Fractures
- Usually heal well with 4-6 weeks modified weight-bearing
- Stiff-soled shoe or walking boot often used
- 5th metatarsal base (Jones fracture) is higher risk

### Tibial Stress Fractures
- Posterior/medial: Better prognosis
- Anterior cortex: Higher risk, may need longer rest
- Address shin splint prevention strategies

### Femoral Neck Stress Fractures
- HIGH RISK—can progress to complete fracture
- Often non-weight-bearing initially
- May require surgery if displaced
- Longer return timeline (3-6 months)

### Navicular Stress Fractures
- Notoriously slow healers
- Often non-weight-bearing for 6-8 weeks
- High re-injury rate
- May require prolonged cross-training

## Prevention of Recurrence

### Training Principles
- Progress volume by ≤10% per week
- Include recovery weeks (reduce volume every 3-4 weeks)
- Adequate rest days between hard sessions
- Cross-train to reduce impact load

### Strength Training
- Continue lower body strength work 2-3x/week
- Focus on hip, calf, and foot intrinsic muscles
- Single-leg exercises for symmetry

### Monitoring
- Track training load (volume, intensity)
- Note early warning signs (bone pain during activity)
- Regular footwear assessment
- Annual bone health check if high risk

### Footwear
- Replace running shoes every 300-500 miles
- Match shoe to foot type and running style
- Consider rotating between multiple pairs

## Sample Weekly Schedule: Phase 2 (Week 6)

**Monday:**
- Pool running: 30 min (intervals)
- Hip strengthening: 3 exercises, 2×15

**Tuesday:**
- Walking: 30 min
- Core work: 15 min
- Upper body: 20 min

**Wednesday:**
- Cycling: 30 min easy
- Calf raises: 3×15
- Balance work: 10 min

**Thursday:**
- Rest

**Friday:**
- Pool running: 35 min (steady)
- Hip and glute work: 20 min

**Saturday:**
- Walking: 35-40 min
- Stretching: 15 min

**Sunday:**
- Rest or gentle movement

## When to Seek Help

**Return to physician if:**
- Pain returns during walking progression
- Pain at fracture site that doesn't resolve with rest
- New pain at different location
- Swelling or warmth at fracture site
- Unable to progress through return-to-run program

**Consider physical therapy for:**
- Biomechanical assessment
- Gait analysis
- Individualized strengthening program
- Return-to-sport guidance

## Key Takeaways

1. **Patience is essential** — stress fractures heal on bone's timeline, not yours
2. **Cross-train to maintain fitness** — pool running, cycling, swimming
3. **Address root causes** — training errors, weakness, nutrition, footwear
4. **Strengthen while healing** — hips, calves, core, balance
5. **Progress return to running gradually** — walk-run progressions over 6+ weeks
6. **Monitor closely** — any pain at fracture site means stop
7. **Prevent recurrence** — 10% rule, rest days, continued strength work
8. **High-risk sites need extra care** — femoral neck, navicular, anterior tibia

A stress fracture is a signal that something in your training, body, or recovery wasn't balanced. Use the recovery period to identify and fix those factors—you'll return stronger and more resilient than before.
