# Distal Radius Fracture Exercises: Wrist Recovery After Broken Arm

A distal radius fracture—the most common type of broken wrist—requires dedicated rehabilitation to restore full function. Whether treated with a cast or surgery, the right exercises help you regain mobility and strength.

## Understanding Distal Radius Fractures

The distal radius is the larger forearm bone at the wrist. Fractures here typically occur from falling on an outstretched hand.

**Common types:**
- **Colles fracture:** Distal fragment angles backward (most common)
- **Smith fracture:** Distal fragment angles forward
- **Barton fracture:** Involves the wrist joint surface
- **Intra-articular:** Extends into the joint

**Treatment approaches:**
- **Cast:** For stable, non-displaced fractures (6 weeks typically)
- **Surgery:** For displaced, unstable, or intra-articular fractures
  - Plate and screws (ORIF)
  - External fixator
  - Pins

## Recovery Timeline

### Cast Treatment
- Immobilization: 4-6 weeks
- Therapy begins after cast removal
- Full recovery: 3-6 months

### Surgical Treatment
- Immobilization: 2-6 weeks (varies by fixation)
- Early therapy may begin sooner with stable fixation
- Full recovery: 4-6 months

## Exercises While in Cast

### Finger Exercises (Start Immediately)

**Full Fist Making**
1. Make tight fist
2. Open fully, spread fingers
3. 10-15 repetitions
4. Several times daily

**Tendon Gliding Exercises**
1. Fingers straight (tabletop)
2. Hook position (bend tips only)
3. Full fist
4. Flat fist (bend at knuckles)
5. 10 repetitions through sequence

**Thumb Movements**
1. Touch thumb to each fingertip
2. Make "O" with thumb and each finger
3. Thumb circles
4. 10 repetitions each

### Shoulder and Elbow Exercises

Prevent stiffness in unaffected joints:

**Shoulder Pendulums**
1. Lean forward slightly
2. Let arm hang
3. Make small circles
4. 10 circles each direction

**Shoulder Shrugs and Rolls**
1. Shrug shoulders up and down
2. Roll forward, then backward
3. 10 repetitions each

**Elbow Bends** (if not casted)
1. Bend and straighten elbow fully
2. 15-20 repetitions

**Forearm Rotation** (if allowed)
1. Elbow at side
2. Turn palm up, then down
3. Only if cast allows this motion

## Phase 1: Early Mobility (After Cast Removal)

### Wrist Range of Motion

**Wrist Flexion/Extension**
1. Support forearm on table
2. Hand off edge
3. Bend wrist down (flexion)
4. Bend wrist up (extension)
5. 15-20 repetitions
6. Expect limitation initially

**Radial/Ulnar Deviation**
1. Forearm supported, thumb up
2. Move wrist toward thumb (radial)
3. Move toward pinky (ulnar)
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Wrist Circles**
1. Small, controlled circles
2. Both directions
3. 10-15 each direction

### Forearm Rotation

**Pronation/Supination**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate palm up (supination)
3. Rotate palm down (pronation)
4. 15-20 repetitions

This motion is critical for daily function.

### Self-Mobilization

**Joint Glides**
1. Support wrist with other hand
2. Gently move wrist bones up and down
3. Small oscillations
4. Helps restore motion

### Edema Control

Swelling is common after cast removal:
- Elevate hand above heart
- Make fists frequently
- Ice 15-20 minutes after exercise
- Compression glove if recommended

## Phase 2: Progressive Mobility (Weeks 2-4 Post-Cast)

### Stretching

**Wrist Flexion Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm up
2. Use other hand to pull fingers back
3. Feel stretch in forearm
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 3-5 times

**Wrist Extension Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm down
2. Push wrist down with other hand
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3-5 times

**Prayer Stretch**
1. Palms together, fingers up
2. Lower hands toward waist
3. Keep palms together
4. Hold 30 seconds

**Reverse Prayer**
1. Backs of hands together
2. Raise toward chin
3. Hold 30 seconds

**Forearm Stretch**
1. Arm extended, palm down
2. Twist forearm to stretch pronators
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Reverse for supinators

### Passive Range of Motion

Use uninvolved hand to assist movement into stretch.

**Passive Wrist Flexion**
1. Good hand holds injured wrist
2. Gently assist into flexion
3. Hold at end range 10-15 seconds

**Passive Wrist Extension**
Same technique into extension.

### Scar Massage (Surgical Patients)

After incision fully healed (2-3 weeks):
1. Massage scar in circles
2. Lift and roll the tissue
3. Work perpendicular to scar
4. 2-3 minutes daily

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 4-8 Post-Cast)

### Isometric Exercises

**Isometric Wrist Flexion**
1. Palm against underside of table
2. Push up without movement
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Wrist Extension**
1. Back of hand against table
2. Push up
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Pronation/Supination**
1. Elbow at side, forearm neutral
2. Partner or other hand provides resistance
3. Try to rotate against resistance
4. Hold 5-10 seconds each direction

### Light Resistance Exercises

**Wrist Curls**
1. Light weight (1-2 lbs)
2. Forearm on table, wrist at edge
3. Curl wrist up (palm up position)
4. Lower slowly
5. 2-3 sets of 15

**Reverse Wrist Curls**
1. Palm down
2. Extend wrist up
3. Lower slowly
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Deviation with Weight**
1. Hold weight like hammer (thumb up)
2. Lift toward thumb (radial deviation)
3. 2 sets of 15
4. Progress to ulnar deviation

**Forearm Rotation with Weight**
1. Hold hammer or weighted rod
2. Rotate fully both directions
3. 2 sets of 15

### Grip Strengthening

**Soft Ball Squeeze**
1. Squeeze stress ball or putty
2. Hold 3-5 seconds
3. 2-3 minutes

**Finger Pinches**
1. Pinch putty between thumb and fingers
2. 10 pinches per finger

**Hand Gripper**
1. Light resistance gripper
2. 2 sets of 10-15

## Phase 4: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-12+)

### Increased Resistance

**Progress Weights**
- Increase by 0.5-1 lb increments
- 2-3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Never push through pain

**Resistance Bands**
- Flexion/extension against band
- Pronation/supination against band
- Multiple directions

### Functional Exercises

**Towel Wringing**
1. Twist wet towel both directions
2. 10-15 twists each way

**Jar Opening/Closing**
1. Practice opening various lids
2. Start loose, progress tighter

**Tool Use Simulation**
1. Practice using screwdriver motion
2. Hammer grip activities
3. Squeezing activities

### Weight-Bearing Progression

**Level 1:** Partial weight on hands (tabletop lean)
**Level 2:** Quadruped (hands and knees)
**Level 3:** Push-ups on knees
**Level 4:** Modified planks
**Level 5:** Full push-ups

**Progress only when pain-free at current level.**

## Phase 5: Return to Activities (Months 3-6)

### Criteria for Full Activities
- 80%+ range of motion
- Grip strength near normal
- No pain with daily activities
- Doctor clearance

### Sport-Specific Return

**Contact sports:**
- Protective brace may be needed
- Gradual return to practice
- Full participation when cleared

**Racquet sports:**
- Grip strength adequate
- Practice swings first
- Light play, then full

**Weightlifting:**
- Start light
- Wrist wraps for support
- Gradual progression
- Full load at 6+ months

### Work Return
- Desk work: Usually 2-4 weeks post-cast
- Light manual: 6-8 weeks
- Heavy manual: 3-6 months
- Varies by job demands

## Sample Daily Program

### While Casted
**3-4 times daily:**
1. Finger exercises: 15 reps each
2. Thumb movements: 10 reps
3. Shoulder/elbow mobility: 10 reps
4. Elevation: 15-20 minutes

### Week 1-2 Post-Cast
**3 times daily:**
1. Wrist ROM: 15 reps each direction
2. Forearm rotation: 15 reps
3. Stretches: 30 sec each (2-3 times)
4. Finger exercises: 15 reps
5. Ice if swollen: 15 min

### Week 4-6 Post-Cast
**2 times daily:**
1. Stretches: 5 minutes
2. Active ROM: 15 reps
3. Isometrics: 10 reps each
4. Light weights: 2x15
5. Grip work: 2-3 minutes

### Week 8+ Post-Cast
**Once daily:**
1. Stretches: 5 minutes
2. Strengthening: 2-3 exercises, 2x15
3. Grip progression
4. Functional exercises
5. Weight-bearing practice

## Common Complications

### Stiffness
- Very common after immobilization
- Consistent stretching essential
- May take months to maximize

### Weakness
- Expected after disuse
- Progressive strengthening
- Grip often slowest to recover

### CRPS (Complex Regional Pain Syndrome)
- Burning pain, swelling, color changes
- Requires immediate attention
- Early treatment crucial

### Malunion
- Bone heals in poor position
- May affect function
- Sometimes needs surgery

### Hardware Issues (Surgical)
- Tendon irritation
- Prominent plate
- May need removal

## Working with Therapists

### Occupational/Hand Therapist
Especially helpful for:
- Custom splints
- Joint mobilization
- Edema management
- Scar treatment
- Functional training

### When to Seek Therapy
- Limited progress with home program
- Significant stiffness or weakness
- Pain not improving
- Return to demanding activities

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start finger exercises immediately** — even while casted
2. **Expect stiffness** — it's normal and improves with time
3. **Stretch consistently** — multiple times daily early on
4. **Progress strength gradually** — isometrics, then resistance
5. **Address swelling** — elevation, ice, compression
6. **Grip takes longest** — be patient with this
7. **Weight-bearing builds function** — progress through levels
8. **Full recovery takes months** — typically 3-6 months

Distal radius fractures are common and usually heal well with proper rehabilitation. Stay consistent with your exercises, follow your medical team's guidance, and you'll regain excellent function.
