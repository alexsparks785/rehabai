      <p className="lead">
        Wrist pain can make pressing, pulling, and even gripping weights uncomfortable or impossible. The good news: most lifting-related wrist pain comes from technique issues that can be fixed. Here's how to identify and resolve the problem.
      </p>

      <h2>Common Causes of Wrist Pain When Lifting</h2>

      <h3>1. Poor Wrist Position During Pressing</h3>
      <p>
        The most common cause. When the wrist bends back excessively during bench press or overhead press, it creates stress on the joint.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Signs:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain on top or back of the wrist</li>
        <li>Pain that worsens with heavier pressing</li>
        <li>Wrist feels "cocked back" during the lift</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Position the bar lower in your palm, closer to the heel of your hand</li>
        <li>Keep wrists stacked over forearms—neutral, not bent back</li>
        <li>Grip the bar firmly to create wrist stability</li>
        <li>Consider wrist wraps for heavy pressing</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>2. Front Rack Position Issues</h3>
      <p>
        Front squats and cleans require significant wrist extension. Limited mobility makes this painful.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Signs:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain during front squats or cleans</li>
        <li>Inability to get a proper front rack</li>
        <li>Pain in the back of the wrist</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Work on wrist extension mobility</li>
        <li>Use a cross-arm grip for front squats if wrist mobility is limited</li>
        <li>Try wrist straps looped around the bar</li>
        <li>Stretch lats and triceps, which affect rack position</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>3. Excessive Gripping</h3>
      <p>
        Repetitive gripping during deadlifts, rows, pull-ups, and carries can strain wrist structures.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Signs:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain related to pulling exercises</li>
        <li>Aching after high-volume grip work</li>
        <li>Pain on the thumb-side or pinky-side of the wrist</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Use lifting straps to reduce grip demands</li>
        <li>Vary grip positions (overhand, neutral, underhand)</li>
        <li>Progress grip volume gradually</li>
        <li>Include grip and forearm strengthening</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>4. Curl Grip Problems</h3>
      <p>
        Straight barbell curls force the wrist into a fixed position that doesn't suit everyone's anatomy.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Signs:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain during or after barbell curls</li>
        <li>Pain on the thumb-side of the wrist</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Switch to EZ curl bar (angled grip)</li>
        <li>Use dumbbells, which allow natural wrist rotation</li>
        <li>Try hammer curls (neutral grip)</li>
        <li>Reduce curl weight and increase reps</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>5. Push-Up/Plank Position</h3>
      <p>
        Weight-bearing on flat hands requires significant wrist extension. High volume can cause pain.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Use push-up handles or parallettes to keep wrists neutral</li>
        <li>Make fists and do push-ups on knuckles</li>
        <li>Grip dumbbells as handles during push-ups</li>
        <li>Work on wrist extension mobility</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Wrist-Friendly Technique Adjustments</h2>

      <h3>Bench Press</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Bar should sit on the heel of your palm, not the fingers</li>
        <li>Wrists should be stacked straight over forearms</li>
        <li>Avoid excessive wrist extension (bent back)</li>
        <li>Squeeze the bar hard to create stability</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Overhead Press</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Same principles: bar on heel of hand, wrists neutral</li>
        <li>Forearms should be vertical when viewed from the side</li>
        <li>Avoid letting wrists cock back under the weight</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Deadlift</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Maintain a straight wrist—don't let it flex</li>
        <li>Grip firmly but avoid over-gripping</li>
        <li>Use straps if grip is limiting and causing wrist strain</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to Use Wrist Wraps</h2>

      <p>
        Wrist wraps provide external support and can help with pain during pressing. Good use cases:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Heavy bench press and overhead press</li>
        <li>When wrist pain is limiting training</li>
        <li>As a temporary measure while fixing technique</li>
        <li>For lifters with naturally loose joints</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Caution:</strong> Wraps shouldn't replace proper technique. Fix positioning first; use wraps as support, not a crutch.
      </p>

      <h2>Exercises to Strengthen and Mobilize Wrists</h2>

      <h3>Wrist Strengthening</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Wrist curls:</strong> Flexion and extension with light dumbbells</li>
        <li><strong>Radial/ulnar deviation:</strong> Side-to-side wrist movements with weight</li>
        <li><strong>Plate pinches:</strong> Grip strength that supports wrist stability</li>
        <li><strong>Farmer's carries:</strong> Overall grip and wrist strengthening</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Wrist Mobility</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Wrist circles:</strong> Full range of motion in both directions</li>
        <li><strong>Prayer stretch:</strong> Palms together, press down for extension stretch</li>
        <li><strong>Reverse prayer:</strong> Backs of hands together for flexion stretch</li>
        <li><strong>Tabletop rocks:</strong> On hands and knees, rock forward over wrists</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to Seek Help</h2>

      <p>
        See a professional if:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain persists despite technique changes</li>
        <li>You have numbness or tingling (possible nerve involvement)</li>
        <li>There's swelling or visible deformity</li>
        <li>Pain affects daily activities</li>
        <li>There was a specific injury (fall on outstretched hand, etc.)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Conditions to Be Aware Of</h2>

      <h3>Wrist Tendinitis</h3>
      <p>
        Inflammation of wrist tendons from overuse. Usually responds to rest, technique changes, and gradual loading.
      </p>

      <h3>TFCC Injury</h3>
      <p>
        Damage to the cartilage complex on the pinky side of the wrist. Often from twisting or impact. May need professional evaluation.
      </p>

      <h3>Carpal Tunnel Syndrome</h3>
      <p>
        Nerve compression causing numbness/tingling in thumb, index, and middle fingers. Lifting can aggravate it but usually isn't the primary cause.
      </p>

      <h3>Ganglion Cyst</h3>
      <p>
        Fluid-filled lump, often on the back of the wrist. Common in lifters. Usually benign but can cause discomfort.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Wrist pain during lifting is usually caused by poor positioning or excessive repetitive stress. Fixing technique—especially keeping wrists neutral during pressing—resolves most issues.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Keep wrists neutral (stacked) during pressing—avoid bending back</li>
        <li>Position the bar on the heel of your palm, not the fingers</li>
        <li>Use EZ bars or dumbbells if straight bars cause pain</li>
        <li>Wrist wraps can help but shouldn't replace good technique</li>
        <li>Strengthen and mobilize wrists proactively</li>
        <li>Use lifting straps to reduce grip demands when needed</li>
        <li>Seek help if pain persists or includes numbness/tingling</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        Your wrists don't have to limit your lifting. Address positioning, build strength, and most wrist pain will resolve.
      </p>
