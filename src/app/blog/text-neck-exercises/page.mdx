Text neck is the modern epidemic of neck pain caused by looking down at phones, tablets, and laptops for hours each day. The average person spends 2-4 hours daily looking at their phone—that's up to 1,400 hours per year of increased stress on the neck.

The good news: targeted exercises can reverse the effects of text neck and restore proper head position.

## Understanding Text Neck

### What's Happening
When you look down at your phone, your head moves forward and down. For every inch your head moves forward, the effective weight on your neck increases by about 10 pounds.

- **Neutral position:** Head weighs ~10-12 lbs
- **15° forward tilt:** ~27 lbs of force
- **30° forward tilt:** ~40 lbs of force
- **45° forward tilt:** ~49 lbs of force
- **60° forward tilt:** ~60 lbs of force

This constant load causes muscle strain, joint stress, and postural changes over time.

### Symptoms of Text Neck
- Neck pain and stiffness
- Upper back pain (between shoulder blades)
- Headaches (often starting at base of skull)
- Shoulder pain
- Rounded shoulders
- Reduced neck mobility
- Muscle tightness in chest and front of neck
- Weakness in neck and upper back muscles

### The Postural Pattern
Text neck typically involves:
- Forward head posture
- Rounded upper back (thoracic kyphosis)
- Rounded shoulders
- Tight chest and front neck muscles
- Weak deep neck flexors and upper back muscles

## Exercises to Fix Text Neck

### Chin Tucks
The single most important exercise for text neck.

1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Look straight ahead
3. Pull chin straight back (making a "double chin")
4. Keep eyes level—don't look up or down
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. Repeat 10-15 times
7. Perform multiple times daily

**Progression:** Do chin tucks lying down, against a wall, or with resistance band for added challenge.

### Chin Tuck with Extension
1. Perform a chin tuck
2. While holding the tuck, look up at the ceiling
3. Return to neutral, maintaining tuck
4. Repeat 10 times

### Wall Angels
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Feet 6 inches from wall
3. Press lower back, upper back, and head against wall
4. Raise arms to "goalpost" position (elbows at shoulder height, bent 90°)
5. Slowly slide arms up and down the wall
6. Keep all contact points on wall
7. 10-15 repetitions

### Doorway Chest Stretch
Opens tight chest muscles that pull shoulders forward.

1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearms on door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step one foot through doorway
4. Lean forward until stretch in chest
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Repeat 2-3 times

### Upper Trapezius Stretch
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Tilt right ear toward right shoulder
3. Place right hand gently on left side of head
4. Apply gentle pressure—don't force
5. Hold 30 seconds each side
6. Repeat 2-3 times

### Levator Scapulae Stretch
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Turn head 45° to the right
3. Look down toward your armpit
4. Place right hand on back of head
5. Apply gentle pressure down and forward
6. Feel stretch in back/side of neck
7. Hold 30 seconds each side

### SCM Stretch (Sternocleidomastoid)
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Tilt head back slightly, looking up
3. Rotate head to one side
4. Feel stretch in front/side of neck
5. Hold 20-30 seconds each side

### Thoracic Extension on Foam Roller
1. Lie on foam roller positioned across upper back
2. Support head with hands
3. Let upper back extend over the roller
4. Roll up and down between shoulder blades and mid-back
5. Pause at tight spots for 30 seconds
6. 2-3 minutes total

### Cat-Cow
1. Hands and knees position
2. Cow: Drop belly, lift head and tailbone
3. Cat: Round back, tuck chin
4. Move slowly, emphasizing upper back movement
5. 10-15 cycles

### Prone Y-T-W Raises
Strengthens upper back muscles.

1. Lie face down on floor or bench
2. Y: Arms overhead at 45°, thumbs up, lift arms
3. T: Arms straight out to sides, thumbs up, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades, lift
5. Hold each position 3-5 seconds
6. 10 repetitions of each

### Resistance Band Pull-Aparts
1. Hold band at shoulder height, arms extended
2. Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades
3. Control return
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. 2-3 sets

### Face Pulls
1. Anchor band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. Externally rotate at end (thumbs back)
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 15-20 repetitions

### Deep Neck Flexor Exercise
Strengthens the muscles that hold your head in proper alignment.

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Tuck chin (don't lift head)
3. Gently press back of head into floor
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Repeat 10-15 times

**Progression:** Hold longer or add slight head lift while maintaining chin tuck.

## Daily Exercise Routine

### Quick Routine (5 minutes) - Do 3-4x Daily

1. Chin tucks: 10 reps
2. Upper trap stretch: 20 seconds each side
3. Chest stretch: 20 seconds
4. Shoulder rolls: 10 forward, 10 backward

### Complete Routine (15 minutes) - Do 1-2x Daily

**Stretching (5 minutes):**
1. Upper trapezius stretch: 30 sec each side
2. Levator scapulae stretch: 30 sec each side
3. Doorway chest stretch: 30 sec × 2
4. SCM stretch: 20 sec each side

**Mobility (3 minutes):**
1. Cat-cow: 10 cycles
2. Thoracic extension on roller: 2 minutes

**Strengthening (5 minutes):**
1. Chin tucks: 15 reps
2. Deep neck flexor holds: 10 × 5 seconds
3. Wall angels: 10 reps
4. Prone Y-T-W: 10 each position

**Band Work (2 minutes) - if available:**
1. Pull-aparts: 15 reps
2. Face pulls: 15 reps

## Postural Habits to Change

### Phone Use
- Raise phone to eye level
- Use voice commands when possible
- Take breaks every 20-30 minutes
- Be aware of head position

### Computer Work
- Monitor at eye level
- Maintain neutral head position
- Use a document holder if typing from papers
- Take micro-breaks to move neck

### Reading
- Hold books/tablets higher
- Use a book stand or pillow for support
- Don't read in bed with head propped awkwardly

### Sleeping
- Use a supportive pillow that maintains neck alignment
- Avoid sleeping on stomach
- Consider a cervical pillow

### Driving
- Adjust headrest to support middle of head
- Keep head back against headrest
- Check mirrors require proper head position

## Quick Fixes Throughout the Day

### The Reset (30 seconds)
Do this every hour:
1. Chin tuck × 5
2. Shoulder rolls × 5
3. Look up at ceiling, hold 5 seconds

### The Phone Check
Before checking your phone:
1. Bring phone to eye level
2. Stand or sit tall
3. Make it a habit

### The Posture Check
Set hourly reminders to:
1. Notice your head position
2. Perform a chin tuck
3. Roll shoulders back
4. Continue with proper posture

## How Long Until Improvement?

### Typical Timeline
- **1-2 weeks:** Awareness improves, exercises become habit
- **2-4 weeks:** Pain begins decreasing
- **4-8 weeks:** Noticeable posture improvement
- **8-12 weeks:** Significant change in resting posture

### Keys to Success
- **Consistency:** Brief, frequent sessions beat occasional long ones
- **Awareness:** Notice your posture throughout the day
- **Habit change:** Modify how you use devices
- **Patience:** Postural changes take time

## When to Seek Help

### See a Professional If:
- Pain is severe or worsening
- Numbness or tingling in arms/hands
- Headaches are frequent or severe
- Symptoms don't improve with 4-6 weeks of exercise
- Pain disrupts sleep or daily activities

### Treatment Options:
- Physical therapy
- Massage therapy
- Chiropractic care
- Ergonomic assessment

## Prevention for Kids and Teens

Young people are especially vulnerable to text neck during growth:
- Limit screen time
- Encourage breaks during device use
- Model good posture yourself
- Make exercises fun and brief
- Ensure proper desk setup for homework

## The Bottom Line

Text neck is reversible with consistent effort. The combination of strengthening weak muscles, stretching tight ones, and changing daily habits can restore proper alignment and eliminate pain.

**Key takeaways:**
- Chin tucks are the foundation—do them often
- Strengthen the upper back and deep neck muscles
- Stretch chest and front neck muscles
- Raise your phone instead of lowering your head
- Take breaks during device use
- Be patient—postural changes take weeks to months

Your head weighs about 10 pounds. Keep it balanced over your spine, and your neck will thank you.
