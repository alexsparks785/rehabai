# Knee Osteoarthritis Exercises: Manage Arthritis Pain and Stay Active

Knee osteoarthritis (OA) affects millions of people, but exercise is one of the most effective treatments. Research consistently shows that strengthening and movement reduce pain and improve function—often as effectively as medication.

## Understanding Knee Osteoarthritis

Knee OA involves breakdown of cartilage, the smooth covering that cushions joint surfaces. This leads to:
- Pain with activity
- Stiffness, especially in the morning
- Swelling
- Decreased range of motion
- Weakness
- Difficulty with stairs, walking, standing

**Key insight:** While cartilage can't regenerate, exercise helps by:
- Strengthening muscles that support the joint
- Improving joint lubrication
- Reducing inflammation
- Maintaining mobility
- Managing weight
- Decreasing pain sensitivity

## Exercise Principles for Knee OA

### The 2-Hour Rule
If pain increases and lasts more than 2 hours after exercise, you did too much. Reduce intensity or duration next time.

### Motion is Lotion
Movement pumps fluid through cartilage and keeps joints lubricated. Some discomfort during exercise is OK; significant pain is not.

### Consistency Over Intensity
Regular, moderate exercise is better than occasional intense sessions.

### Start Low, Progress Slow
Begin with easier exercises and gradually increase as tolerated.

## Range of Motion Exercises

### Heel Slides

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back
2. Slide heel toward buttock
3. Slide back out
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. Several times daily

### Seated Knee Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in chair
2. Straighten one knee
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 15-20 repetitions each

### Knee Circles

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with foot off floor
2. Make small circles with lower leg
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Each knee

### Passive Knee Flexion

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in chair
2. Slide foot under chair to increase bend
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. Repeat 5-10 times

## Strengthening Exercises

### Quad-Focused Exercises

**Quad Sets**
1. Sit or lie with leg straight
2. Push knee into floor
3. Tighten thigh
4. Hold 10 seconds
5. 20-30 repetitions

**Straight Leg Raise**
1. Lie on back, one knee bent
2. Tighten quad of straight leg
3. Lift 6-12 inches
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 3 sets of 15

**Terminal Knee Extension**
1. Roll towel under knee
2. Push knee down into towel
3. Lift heel off floor
4. 3 sets of 15

**Short Arc Quads**
1. Roll under knee
2. Straighten knee over roll
3. 3 sets of 15

### Functional Quad Exercises

**Mini Squats**
1. Partial squat (pain-free range)
2. Don't force through pain
3. 3 sets of 10-15

**Wall Sits**
1. Slide down wall to comfortable angle
2. Hold 15-30 seconds
3. Progress duration

**Step-Ups** (Low Step)
1. 4-6 inch step
2. Step up and down
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Hamstring Strengthening

**Hamstring Curls** (Standing)
1. Hold support
2. Bend knee, bringing heel toward buttock
3. 3 sets of 15 each

**Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift hips, squeeze glutes
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

### Hip Strengthening

Hip weakness affects knee loading and pain.

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Lift top knee, feet together
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Bottom knee bent, top leg straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Standing Hip Abduction**
1. Hold support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. 3 sets of 15 each

### Calf Strengthening

**Heel Raises**
1. Hold support
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. 3 sets of 15

**Single-Leg Heel Raise** (When Ready)
1. Progress to one leg
2. 2-3 sets of 10-12 each

## Low-Impact Cardiovascular Exercise

### Walking

**Guidelines:**
- Start with short distances
- Flat, even surfaces
- Supportive shoes
- Progress gradually
- Some discomfort OK, significant pain not

### Cycling

**Why it helps:**
- Non-weight-bearing
- Maintains motion
- Builds leg strength

**Tips:**
- Stationary bike is easiest to control
- Adjust seat height properly
- Start with low resistance
- 10-30 minutes

### Water Exercise

**Benefits:**
- Buoyancy reduces joint stress
- Resistance builds strength
- Warmth reduces pain

**Options:**
- Water walking
- Swimming
- Aqua aerobics

### Elliptical

- Lower impact than walking
- Maintains knee motion
- Good cardiovascular exercise

## Stretching and Flexibility

### Quad Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Stand, hold support
2. Grab ankle, pull toward buttock
3. Keep knees together
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Hamstring Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit on edge of chair
2. Extend one leg straight
3. Lean forward from hips
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Calf Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Face wall, hands on wall
2. Step back with one leg
3. Keep heel down
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### IT Band/Outer Thigh

**How to do it:**
1. Cross one leg behind other
2. Lean toward side of back leg
3. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Balance Exercises

Balance training reduces fall risk and improves joint stability.

### Single-Leg Stance

**Progression:**
1. Hold support → no support
2. Eyes open → eyes closed
3. Firm surface → foam pad
4. Goal: 30 seconds each leg

### Heel-Toe Walking

**How to do it:**
1. Walk in straight line
2. Heel of front foot touches toe of back
3. 10-20 steps

### Weight Shifts

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width
2. Shift weight side to side
3. Then forward and backward
4. 1-2 minutes

## Managing Flare-Ups

### When Pain Increases
- Reduce exercise intensity
- Apply ice after activity
- Focus on range of motion
- Don't stop completely

### What's Normal
- Some discomfort during exercise
- Mild soreness after
- Pain that resolves within 2 hours

### Warning Signs
- Sharp, intense pain
- Significant swelling
- Pain lasting more than 2 hours
- Progressive worsening

## Sample Weekly Program

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Bike or walking 5-10 min
2. Quad sets: 2×20
3. Straight leg raise: 2×15
4. Bridge: 2×15
5. Clamshells: 2×15 each
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 2
1. Walking or bike: 15-20 min
2. Balance exercises: 5 min
3. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 3
1. Warm-up: 5-10 min
2. Mini squats: 2×12
3. Step-ups: 2×10 each
4. Standing hip abduction: 2×15
5. Heel raises: 2×15
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 4
1. Water exercise or bike: 20-30 min
2. Light stretching

### Day 5
1. Warm-up: 5-10 min
2. Terminal knee extension: 2×15
3. Hamstring curl: 2×15 each
4. Side-lying abduction: 2×15 each
5. Wall sit: 3×20 sec
6. Balance: 5 min
7. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 6
1. Walking or low-impact cardio: 20-30 min

### Day 7
1. Rest or gentle stretching

## Lifestyle Considerations

### Weight Management
Every pound of weight loss removes 4 pounds of pressure from knees. Weight management is crucial for OA.

### Footwear
- Supportive, cushioned shoes
- Avoid worn-out shoes
- Consider orthotics if needed

### Activity Modification
- Break up prolonged standing
- Use handrails on stairs
- Avoid high-impact activities
- Pace activities throughout day

### Assistive Devices
- Use cane if helpful (opposite hand from affected knee)
- Consider knee brace for support
- Use chair risers if getting up is difficult

## When to Seek Help

**Consult a healthcare provider if:**
- Pain significantly limits daily activities
- Exercise consistently worsens symptoms
- Joint locks or gives way
- Significant swelling that doesn't resolve
- Considering joint replacement

**Additional treatments:**
- Physical therapy
- Injections (corticosteroid, hyaluronic acid)
- Medications
- Joint replacement (when conservative fails)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Exercise is medicine** — as effective as drugs for many
2. **Motion is lotion** — movement lubricates joints
3. **2-hour rule** — pain lasting longer means do less next time
4. **Strengthen the whole leg** — quads, hamstrings, hips, calves
5. **Low-impact cardio** — cycling, swimming, walking
6. **Consistency matters** — regular moderate exercise
7. **Weight management helps** — reduces joint stress
8. **Don't stop moving** — modify but don't avoid

Knee osteoarthritis doesn't have to mean giving up an active lifestyle. With the right exercise program, most people can significantly reduce pain and maintain function—often delaying or avoiding joint replacement for years.
