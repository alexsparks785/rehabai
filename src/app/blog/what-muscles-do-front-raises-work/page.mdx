# What Muscles Do Front Raises Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The front raise is an isolation exercise targeting the anterior (front) deltoid. While simple in execution, this movement directly targets the front of the shoulder. Here's the complete breakdown—including why you might not need as much of this exercise as you think.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Anterior Deltoid (Front Deltoid)

**Activation Level: Maximum**

The front delt is isolated:
- Front portion of the shoulder
- Shoulder flexion (raising arm forward)
- Works through full range
- Direct isolation

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Lateral Deltoid (Side Deltoid)

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The side delt assists:
- Some contribution
- Minimal compared to lateral raises
- Assists the lift

### Clavicular Pectoralis (Upper Chest)

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

Upper chest:
- Assists shoulder flexion
- Minor contribution
- Similar motion pattern

### Serratus Anterior

**Activation Level: Low**

Along the ribcage:
- Scapular upward rotation
- Stabilization

### Trapezius (Upper)

**Activation Level: Low**

Upper traps:
- Should be minimized
- Can take over if shrugging
- Keep shoulders down

### Core

**Activation Level: Low**

Stabilization:
- Prevents swaying
- Isometric work

## The Front Raise Controversy

### Front Delts Get Plenty of Work

Here's the reality: **anterior deltoids are heavily trained by pressing movements**:

- Bench press
- Overhead press
- Incline press
- Push-ups

For most people, front delts are **overdeveloped** relative to side and rear delts.

### When Front Raises Make Sense

**May benefit:**
- Bodybuilders wanting maximum front delt development
- Those who don't press much
- Specific aesthetic goals
- Rehab/prehab situations

**Probably unnecessary for:**
- Most recreational lifters
- Those doing plenty of pressing
- People with shoulder imbalances (front > side/rear)

### Priority Should Be

1. **Lateral raises** (side delts—need isolation)
2. **Face pulls/rear delt work** (rear delts—need isolation)
3. **Front raises** (front delts—already get worked)

## Front Raise Variations

| Variation | Front Delt | Difficulty | Equipment |
|-----------|------------|------------|-----------|
| Dumbbell front raise | Maximum | Easy | Dumbbells |
| Barbell front raise | High | Easy | Barbell |
| Cable front raise | High | Easy | Cable |
| Plate front raise | High | Easy | Weight plate |

### Dumbbell Front Raise

Most common:
- Alternating or simultaneous
- Full range of motion
- Easy to perform
- Standard choice

### Barbell Front Raise

Using a barbell:
- Both arms together
- Fixed grip
- Different feel
- Less common

### Cable Front Raise

Using low pulley:
- Constant tension
- Different resistance curve
- Good variation
- Smooth movement

### Plate Front Raise

Holding a weight plate:
- Neutral grip
- Both arms together
- Simple option
- Functional feel

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Standing, weights at thighs
- Slight elbow bend
- Palms down or neutral
- Core engaged

### Raising Phase (Concentric)
- Lift arms forward and up
- Stop at shoulder level
- Front delts contract
- Control the movement

### Top Position
- Arms parallel to floor (shoulder height)
- Don't go higher (traps take over)
- Brief pause
- Maximum contraction

### Lowering Phase (Eccentric)
- Control the descent
- Return to thighs
- Maintain tension
- Don't swing

## Common Mistakes

**Going above shoulder level**: Traps take over. Stop at parallel.

**Swinging/momentum**: Using body. Control the weight.

**Going too heavy**: Form breaks down. Light to moderate weight.

**Shrugging shoulders**: Traps working. Keep shoulders down.

**Leaning back**: Body english. Stay upright.

**Locking elbows**: Stress on joint. Keep slight bend.

**Rushing**: No tension. Slow and controlled.

**Overtraining front delts**: Already worked by pressing. Consider need.

## How to Maximize Front Delt Activation

1. **Stop at shoulder level**: Don't go higher
2. **Control the weight**: No swinging
3. **Keep shoulders down**: Don't shrug
4. **Slight elbow bend**: Fixed throughout
5. **Squeeze at top**: Feel front delt contract
6. **Slow negative**: 2-3 seconds down
7. **Light to moderate weight**: Isolation weight
8. **Mind-muscle connection**: Feel front delts

## Programming Recommendations

**If Including Front Raises:**
- 2-3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Light to moderate weight
- After pressing movements
- 1-2 times per week maximum

**In a Shoulder Workout:**
Lower priority than side/rear:
1. Overhead press (compound)
2. Lateral raises (side delts—priority)
3. Face pulls/rear delt work (rear delts—priority)
4. **Front raises** (optional/if needed)

**Most people:** Skip front raises and add more lateral/rear delt work instead.

## Front Raises vs Pressing for Front Delts

| Exercise | Front Delt Activation |
|----------|----------------------|
| Overhead press | Very High |
| Incline bench | High |
| Flat bench | Moderate-High |
| Front raises | Maximum (isolated) |

Pressing movements provide substantial front delt work already.

## Grip Variations

| Grip | Effect |
|------|--------|
| Palms down (pronated) | Standard |
| Thumbs up (neutral) | Often more comfortable |
| Palms up (supinated) | Different feel |

**Most common:** Palms down or thumbs up (neutral).

## Alternating vs Simultaneous

| Style | Benefit |
|-------|---------|
| Alternating | Better control, one at a time |
| Simultaneous | Faster, both together |

Both work. Alternating allows more focus per arm.

## Should You Do Front Raises?

**Ask yourself:**
- Do I do regular pressing (bench, overhead)? → Probably don't need front raises
- Are my front delts underdeveloped? → Rarely the case
- Are my side/rear delts underdeveloped? → More common—prioritize those

**The honest answer for most lifters:** Front raises are the least necessary shoulder isolation exercise. Side and rear delts need more direct work.

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: Anterior deltoid (maximum isolation)
- **Secondary**: Lateral delt, upper chest, serratus, traps
- **Isolation exercise**: Direct front delt work
- **Often unnecessary**: Front delts get heavy work from pressing
- **Lower priority**: Side and rear delts usually need more attention
- **If doing them**: Light weight, shoulder height max, controlled
- **Don't shrug**: Keep shoulders down
- **Consider your program**: More pressing = less need for front raises

Front raises isolate the anterior deltoid effectively. However, for most lifters, front delts receive ample stimulation from pressing movements. Prioritize lateral raises and rear delt work first—these muscles actually need the isolation. Include front raises only if you have specific reasons or genuinely underdeveloped front delts (rare).
