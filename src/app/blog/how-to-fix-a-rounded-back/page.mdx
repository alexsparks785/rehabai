# How to Fix a Rounded Back: Exercises for Upper Back Posture

A rounded upper back—that hunched, stooped posture—is epidemic in our screen-obsessed world. Beyond appearances, it causes real problems: neck pain, shoulder issues, breathing restrictions, and accelerated spinal wear. The good news? Postural rounding is largely reversible with consistent work.

## Understanding a Rounded Back

Your upper back (thoracic spine) naturally curves slightly forward—this is normal kyphosis. Problems arise when this curve becomes excessive (hyperkyphosis) or when it's combined with forward head and rounded shoulders.

### The Rounded Back Pattern
- Increased curve in upper/mid back
- Shoulders rolled forward
- Head jutting forward
- Chest collapsed
- Shoulder blades winging out
- Often combined with anterior pelvic tilt

### Who Gets It?
- Desk workers and students
- Heavy phone users
- Drivers
- Elderly (age-related changes)
- People who do lots of forward-reaching work
- Anyone who sits for extended periods

## Signs of a Rounded Back

- Visible hump or curve when viewed from the side
- Difficulty standing fully straight
- Shoulders that default to a forward position
- Neck and upper back pain
- Reduced ability to reach overhead
- Feeling like your head is "heavy"
- Upper back stiffness
- Shallow breathing

## Self-Assessment

### Wall Test
1. Stand with back against a wall
2. Try to get your head, upper back, and butt all touching the wall
3. Note: How hard is this? Does your head not reach? Does your lower back arch excessively?

### Side Photo
Have someone take a photo from the side. Look for:
- Ear forward of shoulder
- Shoulder forward of hip
- Exaggerated curve in upper back

### Overhead Reach
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Try to raise arms overhead to touch the wall
3. Don't let your lower back arch off the wall
4. If you can't touch the wall without arching, you likely have restrictions.

## What Causes a Rounded Back?

### Muscle Imbalances

**Tight/shortened:**
- Chest muscles (pectoralis major and minor)
- Front shoulders
- Upper abdominals
- Latissimus dorsi

**Weak/lengthened:**
- Thoracic extensors
- Rhomboids and middle trapezius
- Lower trapezius
- Deep neck flexors

### Lifestyle Factors
- Hours of sitting and screen use
- Looking down at phones
- Driving
- Working with arms in front
- Carrying heavy bags

### Habit and Awareness
- Poor postural awareness
- Learned patterns from childhood
- Fatigue leading to slumping

## Exercises to Fix a Rounded Back

### Mobility: Unlock the Thoracic Spine

#### 1. Foam Roller Thoracic Extension
**How to do it:**
1. Lie with foam roller across your mid-back
2. Support head with hands
3. Extend backward over the roller
4. Move roller up and down to target different segments
5. Spend 2-3 minutes total

**Key:** This is the single most effective exercise for thoracic mobility.

#### 2. Cat-Cow (Emphasis on Cow)
**How to do it:**
1. On all fours
2. Cow: Drop belly, lift head and tailbone—focus on extending the UPPER back
3. Cat: Round back, tuck chin and tailbone
4. Spend more time in cow, feeling the upper back extend
5. 15-20 reps

#### 3. Thread the Needle
**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Reach right arm under body to the left
3. Let right shoulder and ear come to floor
4. Feel stretch and rotation in upper back
5. Hold 20-30 seconds, then reach up and rotate open
6. 8-10 reps per side

#### 4. Thoracic Extension on Chair
**How to do it:**
1. Sit backward on a chair, facing the backrest
2. Hold the backrest
3. Let chest sink forward and down
4. Then lift chest and extend upper back
5. 10-15 reps

#### 5. Open Book
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your side, knees bent and stacked
2. Arms extended in front, palms together
3. Rotate top arm up and over, opening chest to ceiling
4. Follow hand with eyes
5. Hold 20-30 seconds per side
6. 8-10 reps per side

### Stretching: Open the Chest

#### 6. Doorway Pec Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Stand in doorway
2. Place forearms on frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step forward and lean through
4. Hold 45-60 seconds
5. Repeat with arms higher and lower

#### 7. Floor Pec Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down
2. Extend one arm out to the side at 90 degrees
3. Roll body away from that arm
4. Feel deep stretch in chest
5. Hold 60-90 seconds per side

#### 8. Foam Roller Chest Opener
**How to do it:**
1. Lie lengthwise on foam roller (along spine)
2. Head and tailbone supported
3. Arms out to sides, palms up
4. Let gravity open chest
5. Hold 3-5 minutes

#### 9. Lat Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Kneel beside a bench or chair
2. Place elbow on surface, palm up
3. Sit back toward heels
4. Feel stretch along side and armpit
5. Hold 45-60 seconds per side

### Strengthening: Build the Back

#### 10. Prone Y-T-W Raises
**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down on floor or incline bench
2. Y: Arms at 45 degrees overhead, thumbs up, lift
3. T: Arms straight out to sides, thumbs up, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades as you lift
5. Hold each for 3 seconds at top
6. 10 reps of each

#### 11. Wall Angels
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Feet a few inches from wall
3. Press lower back, upper back, and head into wall
4. Arms in "goal post" position against wall
5. Slide arms up and down, maintaining contact
6. 10-15 reps

**If you can't keep contact, focus more on chest stretching.**

#### 12. Band Pull-Aparts
**How to do it:**
1. Hold resistance band at shoulder height
2. Arms straight in front
3. Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades
4. Control return
5. 15-20 reps

#### 13. Face Pulls
**How to do it:**
1. Cable or band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high and wide
3. Rotate hands outward at the end
4. Squeeze shoulder blades
5. 12-15 reps

#### 14. Rows with Scapular Retraction
**How to do it:**
1. Any row variation (cable, band, dumbbell)
2. Initiate by squeezing shoulder blade back first
3. Then complete the row
4. Focus on the scapular movement
5. 10-15 reps per arm

### Postural Activation

#### 15. Chin Tucks
**How to do it:**
1. Pull chin straight back (double chin)
2. Keep eyes level
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 15-20 reps, multiple times daily

#### 16. Brugger's Relief Position
**How to do it:**
1. Sit on edge of chair
2. Legs spread slightly
3. Turn palms forward, spread fingers
4. Pull chin back
5. Lift chest
6. Hold 20-30 seconds
7. Do every 30-60 minutes at desk

## Your Daily Routine

### Morning (7 minutes)
- Foam roller thoracic extension: 2 minutes
- Cat-cow: 15 reps
- Doorway chest stretch: 45 seconds
- Wall angels: 10 reps
- Chin tucks: 15 reps

### Throughout the Day
- Brugger's relief position every 30-60 minutes
- Chin tucks hourly
- Brief chest stretches during breaks

### Evening (15 minutes)
- Foam roller lying chest opener: 3 minutes
- Thread the needle: 8 per side
- Open book: 8 per side
- Floor pec stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Prone Y-T-W: 10 of each
- Band pull-aparts: 15 reps
- Lat stretch: 45 seconds per side

### Strengthening (3x/Week)
- Rows: 3 sets of 12
- Face pulls: 3 sets of 15
- Prone Y-T-W: 2 sets of 10 each

## Posture Cues

**"Stack your spine":** Ears over shoulders, shoulders over hips.

**"String from your head":** Imagine a string gently pulling you up from the crown of your head.

**"Ribs down":** Don't flare ribs to compensate for rounded upper back.

**"Show your chest":** Think of letting your chest be visible, not hidden.

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Week 1-2:** Increased awareness, some mobility gains
- **Week 3-4:** Stretches feel easier, posture feels more accessible
- **Week 6-8:** Visible changes in standing posture
- **3-6 months:** Significant, lasting improvement

**Key:** Daily consistency beats occasional intense sessions.

## Common Mistakes

**Overarching lower back:** When trying to stand tall, people often arch the lower back instead of extending the upper back.

**Only stretching the back:** The back is often already overstretched. You need to open the CHEST.

**Forcing "perfect" posture:** You can't hold military posture all day. Focus on mobility and strength; good posture becomes easier.

**Skipping strengthening:** Stretching alone won't fix posture. You need strength to maintain new positions.

## The Bottom Line

A rounded upper back develops over years of forward-facing posture and won't fix itself. The solution requires opening what's tight (chest, lats), strengthening what's weak (mid/lower traps, thoracic extensors), and building new postural awareness.

Foam roller thoracic extension and chest stretches give you immediate access to better posture. Strengthening exercises (Y-T-W, face pulls, rows) build the muscles to maintain it. Postural cues throughout the day reinforce the pattern.

Most people see meaningful improvement within 6-8 weeks of daily work. Your spine wasn't meant to be stuck in flexion—help it remember how to extend.

Stand tall. Your future self will thank you.
