Sciatica—that shooting, burning, or tingling pain that radiates from your lower back down your leg—affects millions of people. While it can be debilitating, most cases improve significantly with the right exercises and conservative treatment.

This guide covers exercises that help relieve sciatic pain, what causes it, and how to prevent future episodes.

## What Is Sciatica?

Sciatica refers to pain that radiates along the sciatic nerve, which runs from your lower back through your hips and buttocks and down each leg. It's a symptom, not a diagnosis—something is irritating or compressing the nerve.

### Common Causes
- **Herniated disc:** Most common cause—disc material pressing on nerve root
- **Spinal stenosis:** Narrowing of the spinal canal
- **Piriformis syndrome:** Piriformis muscle compressing the sciatic nerve
- **Degenerative disc disease:** Age-related changes
- **Spondylolisthesis:** Vertebra slipping forward

### Typical Symptoms
- Pain radiating from lower back to buttock and down leg
- Usually affects one side
- Can be sharp, burning, or like an electric shock
- Numbness or tingling in leg or foot
- Weakness in affected leg
- Symptoms often worse with sitting, coughing, or sneezing

## Important Considerations

### When to Seek Immediate Medical Attention
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Progressive weakness in legs
- Numbness in groin/saddle area
- Severe symptoms after trauma
- Symptoms accompanied by fever

These could indicate a medical emergency (cauda equina syndrome).

### Finding What Works for You
Different causes of sciatica respond to different exercises. What helps one person may worsen another's symptoms.

**General rule:** If an exercise reduces your leg symptoms (even if back pain stays the same), it's probably helpful. If it increases leg symptoms, stop.

## Exercises for Acute Sciatica

### Prone Lying
Sometimes just reducing load on the spine helps.

1. Lie face down on firm surface
2. Arms at sides or folded under forehead
3. Breathe and relax for 5-10 minutes
4. Let spine decompress

### Prone on Elbows
1. Lie face down
2. Prop up on elbows, forearms flat
3. Keep hips on floor
4. Hold 30 seconds to 2 minutes
5. Return to flat, repeat

If this reduces leg symptoms, it's a good sign—try the next progression.

### Press-Up (McKenzie Extension)
For disc-related sciatica:

1. Lie face down, hands under shoulders
2. Press up, straightening arms
3. Keep hips on floor
4. Hold briefly at top
5. Lower and repeat
6. 10 repetitions, several times daily

**Note:** This may initially increase back pain while decreasing leg pain—that's actually a good response (called "centralization").

### Knee-to-Chest Stretch
1. Lie on back
2. Bring one knee toward chest
3. Hold with both hands
4. Keep other leg flat or bent
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. Switch legs
7. Then try both knees together

### Figure-4 Stretch (Piriformis)
For piriformis-related sciatica:

1. Lie on back
2. Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
3. Pull bottom leg toward chest
4. Feel stretch in buttock of crossed leg
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Repeat 2-3 times each side

## Nerve Gliding Exercises

These help the sciatic nerve move more freely.

### Sciatic Nerve Glide (Seated)
1. Sit at edge of chair
2. Straighten affected leg
3. Flex foot (toes toward you)
4. Simultaneously look down
5. Then point toes and look up
6. Alternate in smooth motion
7. 10-15 repetitions

### Sciatic Nerve Glide (Lying)
1. Lie on back
2. Lift affected leg, knee bent
3. Hold behind thigh
4. Straighten knee while flexing foot
5. Bend knee and point toes
6. Alternate smoothly
7. 10-15 repetitions

**Important:** Nerve glides should create gentle tension, not reproduce sharp pain.

## Stretching Exercises

### Piriformis Stretch (Seated)
1. Sit in chair
2. Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
3. Lean forward, keeping back straight
4. Feel stretch in buttock
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Repeat 2-3 times

### Lower Back Rotation Stretch
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Keep shoulders flat on floor
3. Let both knees fall to one side
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Return to center, repeat other side
6. 3 times each side

### Cat-Cow Stretch
1. Hands and knees position
2. Cat: Round back toward ceiling, tuck chin
3. Cow: Drop belly, lift head and tailbone
4. Move slowly between positions
5. 10-15 cycles

### Child's Pose
1. Kneel on floor
2. Sit back toward heels
3. Reach arms forward, lower chest toward floor
4. Hold 30-60 seconds
5. Focus on relaxing lower back

### Hip Flexor Stretch
Tight hip flexors can contribute to sciatica.

1. Half-kneeling position
2. Tuck pelvis under (flatten lower back)
3. Lean forward slightly
4. Feel stretch in front of hip/thigh
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

## Core Strengthening

A strong core supports the spine and reduces nerve compression.

### Dead Bug
1. Lie on back, arms toward ceiling
2. Knees bent 90 degrees, shins parallel to floor
3. Press lower back into floor
4. Slowly extend opposite arm and leg
5. Return, repeat other side
6. 10 each side, 2-3 sets

### Bird Dog
1. Hands and knees position
2. Keep spine neutral (flat back)
3. Extend opposite arm and leg
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. Return, repeat other side
6. 10 each side, 2-3 sets

### Partial Curl-Up
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Hands supporting neck or crossed on chest
3. Lift head and shoulders slightly off floor
4. Hold 2-3 seconds
5. Lower
6. 10-15 repetitions, 2 sets

**Note:** Don't do full sit-ups—they can worsen disc problems.

### Plank
When ready for more challenge:

1. Forearms and toes on floor
2. Body straight from head to heels
3. Keep core engaged, don't let hips sag
4. Hold 20-60 seconds
5. 3 repetitions

### Side Plank
1. Lie on side, forearm under shoulder
2. Stack feet or stagger them
3. Lift hips off floor
4. Keep body straight
5. Hold 15-30 seconds each side
6. 2-3 repetitions

## Walking

Walking is often the best exercise for sciatica:
- Promotes circulation to spine
- Maintains mobility without excessive loading
- Generally well-tolerated
- Start with short walks, increase gradually
- Avoid walking through significant pain

## Exercises to Avoid (Initially)

Certain exercises may worsen sciatica:
- **Heavy lifting:** Increases disc pressure
- **Forward bending:** May aggravate disc herniation
- **Hamstring stretches with back flexion:** Puts tension on nerve
- **High-impact activities:** Running, jumping
- **Long periods of sitting:** Increases disc pressure

As you recover, these activities can gradually be reintroduced.

## Sample Routine

### Acute Phase (First 1-2 weeks)
**3-4 times daily:**
1. Prone lying: 5 minutes
2. Press-ups: 10 reps (if helpful)
3. Knee-to-chest: 30 seconds each leg
4. Figure-4 stretch: 30 seconds each side
5. Walking: 5-10 minutes

### Recovery Phase (Weeks 2-6)
**2 times daily:**
1. Press-ups: 10 reps
2. Sciatic nerve glide: 10 reps
3. Figure-4 stretch: 30 seconds each
4. Cat-cow: 10 cycles
5. Dead bug: 10 each side
6. Walking: 15-20 minutes

**Add 3 times per week:**
- Bird dog: 2 × 10 each side
- Partial curl-up: 2 × 15
- Side plank: 2 × 15 seconds each

### Maintenance Phase (Ongoing)
**3-4 times per week:**
1. Core exercises (dead bug, bird dog, plank): 2 sets each
2. Stretching routine: 5-10 minutes
3. Walking or other activity: 20-30 minutes

## Tips for Managing Sciatica

### Positions That Help
- Lying down with knees bent or supported
- Standing and walking (often better than sitting)
- Avoiding prolonged sitting

### Hot vs. Cold
- **Ice:** Better for acute inflammation (first 48-72 hours)
- **Heat:** Better for muscle tension and chronic symptoms
- Try both and use what feels better

### Sleep Position
- Side sleeping with pillow between knees
- Back sleeping with pillow under knees
- Avoid stomach sleeping

### Sitting
- Use lumbar support
- Get up and move every 20-30 minutes
- Consider standing desk for work

## Recovery Expectations

- **Most cases:** Significant improvement in 4-6 weeks
- **Some cases:** 12 weeks or longer
- **Chronic sciatica:** May need additional treatment

**Good prognostic signs:**
- Symptoms centralizing (moving from leg toward back)
- Decreasing leg symptoms
- Increasing tolerance for activity

**Consider professional help if:**
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks
- Symptoms worsening
- Progressive weakness
- Significant functional limitation

## Prevention

After recovery, prevent recurrence with:

### Core Strength
Maintain core exercises 3-4 times weekly.

### Good Posture
- Sitting: Lumbar support, neutral spine
- Standing: Avoid prolonged static positions
- Lifting: Bend at hips and knees, keep load close

### Stay Active
Regular movement keeps discs healthy and muscles strong.

### Maintain Flexibility
Continue stretching routine, especially hips and hamstrings.

### Healthy Weight
Excess weight increases spinal load.

## The Bottom Line

Sciatica is painful but usually manageable with the right approach. Exercises help reduce nerve compression, improve mobility, and build the core strength that prevents recurrence.

**Key takeaways:**
- Find exercises that decrease leg symptoms (centralization)
- If an exercise increases leg pain, stop
- Walking is usually safe and helpful
- Build core strength as symptoms allow
- Most cases improve significantly in 4-6 weeks
- Seek help if symptoms are severe, progressive, or not improving

Listen to your body, be consistent with exercises, and know that improvement is expected. The majority of sciatica cases resolve without surgery.
