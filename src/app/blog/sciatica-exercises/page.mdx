# Sciatica Exercises: Relieve Nerve Pain and Restore Function

Sciatica—that shooting, burning pain that travels from your lower back down your leg—can be debilitating. But for most people, the right exercises provide significant relief without surgery.

**Key insight:** Sciatica usually responds to specific directional exercises. Finding YOUR direction preference is the key to relief.

---

## Understanding Sciatica

### What Is Sciatica?

Sciatica is pain that radiates along the sciatic nerve path:
- Lower back → buttock → back of thigh → calf → foot

It's a symptom, not a diagnosis. Common causes include:
- Herniated or bulging disc (most common)
- Spinal stenosis
- Piriformis syndrome
- Degenerative disc disease

### Symptoms

- Sharp, shooting pain down leg
- Burning or tingling sensation
- Numbness in leg or foot
- Weakness in leg muscles
- Pain worse with sitting, coughing, sneezing

### True Sciatica vs. Referred Pain

| True Sciatica | Referred Pain |
|---------------|---------------|
| Travels below knee | Usually stops at thigh |
| Follows specific nerve path | More diffuse, vague |
| May have numbness/tingling | Typically just aches |
| Specific movements trigger | Less pattern-dependent |

## Finding Your Direction Preference

Most sciatica from disc problems responds to extension (arching backward). But some respond to flexion (bending forward).

### Extension Test

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with hands on lower back
2. Gently arch backward
3. Notice: Does leg pain decrease, stay same, or worsen?

**If pain decreases or centralizes (moves toward back):** Extension exercises likely help.

### Flexion Test

**How to do it:**
1. Sit and round forward toward toes
2. Notice: Does leg pain decrease, stay same, or worsen?

**If pain decreases with flexion:** May have stenosis or other flexion-preferring cause.

### The Centralization Principle

- **Good sign:** Pain moves from leg toward back (centralizing)
- **Caution:** Pain moves from back toward foot (peripheralizing)

If exercises cause pain to travel further down the leg, stop and try the opposite direction.

## Extension-Based Exercises

For those whose pain improves with backward bending (most disc-related sciatica).

### Prone Lying

Start simple.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down on firm surface
2. Arms at sides or under forehead
3. Let back relax completely
4. Stay 5-10 minutes
5. Repeat several times daily

### Prone on Elbows

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down
2. Prop up on elbows, forearms flat
3. Let hips sag toward floor
4. Relax lower back—don't tense
5. Hold 2-3 minutes
6. Repeat multiple times daily

### Prone Press-Up (McKenzie Extension)

The gold standard for disc-related sciatica.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down, hands under shoulders
2. Press upper body up, keeping hips on floor
3. Let lower back sag (don't tense muscles)
4. Hold at top 2-3 seconds
5. Lower and repeat
6. Perform 10-15 reps, every 2 hours

**Key:** Watch for centralization. If leg pain decreases or moves toward back, this is working.

### Standing Extension

Good for throughout the day.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with hands on lower back
2. Gently lean backward
3. Hold 2-3 seconds
4. Return to standing
5. Repeat 10 times, multiple times daily

## Flexion-Based Exercises

For those whose pain improves with forward bending (often spinal stenosis).

### Knee-to-Chest

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Pull one knee toward chest
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. Repeat other side
5. Then pull both knees together

### Child's Pose

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel, sit back on heels
2. Reach arms forward, lower chest toward floor
3. Rest forehead on ground
4. Hold 30-60 seconds
5. Breathe deeply

### Posterior Pelvic Tilt

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Flatten lower back into floor
3. Tuck tailbone under
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Repeat 10-15 times

## Nerve Mobilization (Flossing)

Helps the nerve glide smoothly through surrounding tissues.

### Sciatic Nerve Floss

**How to do it:**
1. Sit on edge of chair
2. Slump upper back, tuck chin
3. Straighten knee while keeping slumped
4. Then look up while bending knee
5. Create alternating tension: head up/knee bent, head down/knee straight
6. Perform 10-15 gentle repetitions

**Key:** This should NOT reproduce pain. If it does, reduce range or skip.

### Supine Nerve Floss

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, one knee bent
2. Hold behind thigh of other leg
3. Straighten knee while pointing toes away
4. Then bend knee while pulling toes toward you
5. Alternate smoothly
6. Perform 10-15 repetitions

### Seated Slump Floss

**How to do it:**
1. Sit tall, then slump forward
2. Tuck chin to chest
3. Slowly straighten one knee
4. Point and flex ankle
5. Return leg, sit up
6. Perform 10 each leg

## Core Stability Exercises

Build protection for your spine.

### Pelvic Tilts

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Alternate between arching and flattening lower back
3. Find the neutral position between them
4. Hold neutral 10 seconds
5. Repeat 10-15 times

### Dead Bug (Modified)

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent 90°, arms toward ceiling
2. Press lower back flat
3. Slowly lower ONE leg (foot toward floor)
4. Keep back flat—stop if it arches
5. Return and repeat other side
6. Perform 8-10 each side

### Bird Dog (When Ready)

**How to do it:**
1. On hands and knees
2. Brace core gently
3. Extend opposite arm and leg
4. Keep back flat—no rotation
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. Repeat other side
7. Perform 8-10 each side

### Side Plank (Modified)

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, prop on forearm
2. Knees bent for support
3. Lift hips off ground
4. Hold 15-30 seconds
5. Repeat other side

## Piriformis Stretches

If piriformis syndrome is contributing to your sciatica.

### Supine Piriformis Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
3. Pull bottom leg toward chest
4. Feel stretch deep in buttock
5. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Seated Piriformis Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in chair
2. Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
3. Keep back straight, lean forward from hips
4. Feel stretch in buttock
5. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Pigeon Pose

**How to do it:**
1. From hands and knees, bring one knee forward
2. Place shin on ground, angled toward opposite hand
3. Extend other leg straight back
4. Lower hips toward ground
5. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

## What to Avoid

### Exercises That Often Worsen Sciatica

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Sit-ups/crunches | Repeated spinal flexion loads disc |
| Toe touches (standing) | Loaded flexion stretches nerve |
| Both legs straight leg raise | Maximum nerve tension |
| Heavy deadlifts (acute phase) | Spinal loading |
| Running (during flare) | Impact and repeated motion |

### Positions to Minimize

- Prolonged sitting (especially slumped)
- Bending and twisting simultaneously
- Lifting with rounded back
- Long car rides without breaks

## Daily Routine

### Acute Phase (Pain Severe)

**Every 2 hours:**
1. Prone lying (5-10 minutes)
2. Prone press-ups (10 reps) OR knee-to-chest (if flexion helps)
3. Gentle walking (5-10 minutes)

### Subacute Phase (Pain Improving)

**Morning:**
1. Prone press-ups or preferred direction (10 reps)
2. Nerve floss (10 reps, gentle)
3. Pelvic tilts (10 reps)

**Throughout day:**
1. Direction-specific exercises every 2-3 hours
2. Walking 10-20 minutes, 2-3 times

**Evening:**
1. Piriformis stretch (if helpful) (30 sec each side)
2. Core exercise (dead bug or bird dog) (10 reps)
3. Preferred direction stretches

### Recovery Phase

**3-4 times per week:**
1. Direction-specific exercises (maintenance)
2. Core stability progression
3. Walking or swimming 20-30 minutes
4. Gradual return to normal activities

---

## Red Flags (Seek Immediate Help)

- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Progressive weakness in leg
- Numbness in groin/saddle area
- Severe, unremitting pain
- Pain following trauma

---

## Progress Indicators

### Good Signs
- Pain centralizing (moving toward back)
- Pain-free range increasing
- Less frequent symptoms
- Better tolerance for sitting/standing
- Reduced medication need

### Concerning Signs
- Pain peripheralizing (moving down leg)
- Increasing numbness or weakness
- No improvement after 2-3 weeks of consistent exercise
- Symptoms worsening

---

## When Exercises Aren't Enough

Consider professional help if:
- No improvement after 4-6 weeks
- Symptoms worsening despite exercise
- Significant weakness developing
- Unable to find direction preference
- Pain preventing basic function

Most sciatica resolves without surgery, but some cases need:
- Guided physical therapy
- Epidural injections
- Rarely, surgical intervention

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Find your direction** — Extension or flexion based on what helps
2. **Centralization is key** — Pain moving toward back is good
3. **Frequency matters** — Multiple short sessions beat one long one
4. **Nerve flossing helps** — Gentle mobilization, not aggressive stretching
5. **Core stability protects** — Build it as pain allows
6. **Most cases resolve** — Surgery is rarely needed

**The formula:**
- Direction-specific exercises every 2-3 hours
- Gentle nerve mobilization daily
- Core stability as tolerated
- Walking as comfortable
- Patience—improvement typically takes 4-12 weeks

Sciatica is painful and frustrating, but it's highly treatable with the right approach. Find what works for your body, stay consistent, and recovery will follow.
