# How to Fix Anterior Pelvic Tilt: Exercises and Stretches That Work

Anterior pelvic tilt (APT) is one of the most common postural issues—and one of the most fixable. If your lower back arches excessively and your belly pushes forward, you likely have it. Here's how to correct it.

## What Is Anterior Pelvic Tilt?

Anterior pelvic tilt is when the front of your pelvis drops forward and the back rises up. Imagine your pelvis as a bowl of water—APT means the water would spill out the front.

**Signs you have it:**
- Excessive lower back curve
- Belly that protrudes (even if lean)
- Tight hip flexors
- Lower back pain, especially when standing
- Glutes that appear flat

## What Causes APT?

It's a muscle imbalance:

**Tight muscles (pulling pelvis forward):**
- Hip flexors (iliopsoas)
- Lower back extensors (erector spinae)
- Rectus femoris (quad that crosses hip)

**Weak muscles (can't hold pelvis back):**
- Abdominals (especially lower abs)
- Glutes
- Hamstrings

**The culprit:** Usually prolonged sitting. Sitting shortens hip flexors and weakens glutes and abs.

## The Fix: Stretch Tight + Strengthen Weak

Correcting APT requires both:
1. Stretching hip flexors and lower back
2. Strengthening glutes and core

Plus: practicing neutral pelvis position.

## Stretches for Anterior Pelvic Tilt

### 1. Half-Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch

**The most important stretch.**

**How to do it:**
1. Half-kneeling position, pad under back knee
2. Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt)
3. Squeeze your back glute
4. Lean forward slightly while maintaining tuck
5. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

**Key:** The pelvic tuck is essential. Without it, you're just stretching the front of your thigh, not the hip flexor.

### 2. Couch Stretch

**Intense hip flexor and quad stretch.**

**How to do it:**
1. Back foot on couch or wall behind you
2. Front foot forward in lunge position
3. Keep torso upright
4. Tuck tailbone under
5. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

**Why it works:** Stretches both hip flexor and rectus femoris (quad that contributes to APT).

### 3. Lying Hip Flexor Stretch

**Gentle option.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back on edge of bed or bench
2. Pull one knee to chest
3. Let other leg hang off edge
4. Feel stretch in hanging leg's hip flexor
5. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

### 4. Child's Pose with Pelvic Tuck

**Stretches lower back.**

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel, sit back on heels
2. Fold forward, arms extended
3. Tuck tailbone under, rounding lower back
4. Breathe and relax
5. Hold 1-2 minutes

### 5. Cat Stretch (Hold)

**Lower back release.**

**How to do it:**
1. On hands and knees
2. Round your back toward ceiling
3. Tuck chin and tailbone
4. Hold the rounded position 20-30 seconds
5. Repeat several times

## Strengthening Exercises for APT

### 1. Glute Bridge with Posterior Pelvic Tilt

**Foundational exercise.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Flatten lower back into floor (posterior tilt)
3. Drive through heels, lift hips
4. Squeeze glutes hard at top
5. Maintain the pelvic tuck throughout
6. Lower with control

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 15 reps

**Key:** Keep lower back flat against floor at the start. Don't arch at the top.

### 2. Dead Bug

**Core control without hip flexor dominance.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees bent 90°
2. Press lower back firmly into floor
3. Lower opposite arm and leg slowly
4. Don't let lower back arch off floor
5. Return and switch sides

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 10 each side

**Key:** If your back arches, you've gone too far. Reduce range of motion.

### 3. Reverse Plank

**Strengthens glutes and hamstrings.**

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with legs extended, hands behind you
2. Lift hips toward ceiling
3. Squeeze glutes
4. Body forms straight line
5. Hold 20-30 seconds

**Sets/reps:** 3 holds

### 4. Plank with Posterior Pelvic Tilt

**Core strengthening in correct position.**

**How to do it:**
1. Forearm plank position
2. Tuck tailbone under (round lower back slightly)
3. Squeeze glutes
4. This reduces lower back arch
5. Hold with this position

**Sets/reps:** 3 × 30-45 seconds

**Key:** Most people plank with APT. Actively tuck to correct it.

### 5. Lying Leg Raise (Controlled)

**Lower ab strength.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, hands under lower back
2. Press back into hands (maintain contact)
3. Raise legs to 90°
4. Lower slowly, only as far as back stays flat
5. If back arches, stop and raise legs

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 10 reps

**Key:** Range of motion is limited by ability to keep back flat.

### 6. Hip Thrust

**Glute strength in hip extension.**

**How to do it:**
1. Upper back on bench, feet flat
2. Drive through heels, lift hips
3. Squeeze glutes hard at top
4. Don't hyperextend (common mistake)
5. Lower with control

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 12 reps

## Complete APT Correction Routine

### Daily Quick Fix (10 minutes)

**Stretches (5 minutes):**
- Hip flexor stretch: 45 seconds each side
- Couch stretch: 30 seconds each side
- Cat stretch hold: 3 × 15 seconds

**Strengthening (5 minutes):**
- Glute bridges with pelvic tilt: 15 reps
- Dead bugs: 10 each side
- Plank with posterior tilt: 30 seconds

### Full Correction Session (20 minutes)

**Stretching (8 minutes):**
- Hip flexor stretch: 1 minute each side
- Couch stretch: 45 seconds each side
- Lying hip flexor stretch: 45 seconds each side
- Child's pose with tuck: 1 minute
- Cat stretch holds: 5 × 20 seconds

**Strengthening (10 minutes):**
- Glute bridges: 3 × 15
- Dead bugs: 3 × 10 each side
- Plank with posterior tilt: 3 × 30 seconds
- Hip thrusts: 2 × 12
- Lying leg raises: 2 × 10

**Posture Practice (2 minutes):**
- Stand and practice posterior pelvic tilt
- Hold neutral pelvis position
- Check in mirror or use wall

## Posture Awareness Throughout the Day

### Standing
- Think "belt buckle up"
- Slight tuck of tailbone
- Engage glutes lightly
- Don't clench—just activate

### Sitting
- Avoid sitting in APT
- Use lumbar support
- Don't perch on front of chair
- Stand up every 30 minutes

### Walking
- Engage glutes with each step
- Maintain slight posterior tilt
- Don't walk with belly leading

## How Long Does Correction Take?

**Week 1-2:** Increased awareness, temporary improvements

**Week 3-4:** Stretches feel easier, beginning to notice changes

**Month 2-3:** Significant improvement in resting posture

**Month 3-6:** New position becomes more automatic

**Reality:** Habits developed over years take months to change. Consistency is everything.

## Common Mistakes

### Stretching Without Strengthening
- Loose hip flexors don't fix weak glutes
- Must address both sides of imbalance

### Forgetting the Pelvic Tuck
- Hip flexor stretches without tuck miss the point
- Core exercises in APT reinforce the problem

### All-or-Nothing Approach
- Occasional long sessions don't work
- Daily short practice is essential

### Hyperextending During Glute Work
- Bridges and thrusts with arched back
- Maintain neutral or slight posterior tilt

## When to Seek Help

See a professional if:
- Significant lower back pain
- Numbness or tingling in legs
- No improvement after consistent work
- Pain during exercises

Physical therapists can identify specific issues and provide targeted treatment.

## The Bottom Line

Anterior pelvic tilt is fixable with consistent work. Stretch your hip flexors daily—with a proper pelvic tuck. Strengthen your glutes and core with exercises that maintain neutral pelvis.

Practice awareness throughout the day. It took years to develop APT; give yourself months to correct it.

Stand taller, reduce back pain, and move better. The fix works—you just have to do it.
