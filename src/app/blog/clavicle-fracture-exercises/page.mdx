# Clavicle Fracture Exercises: Recovery After Broken Collarbone

A clavicle (collarbone) fracture is one of the most common fractures, especially in young, active people. Proper rehabilitation restores shoulder function and prepares you for return to activities.

## Understanding Clavicle Fractures

The clavicle connects the shoulder to the chest and is vulnerable to fracture from:
- Direct impact (contact sports, falls)
- Falling on outstretched hand
- Falling on the shoulder

**Location of fracture:**
- **Midshaft:** Most common (~80%)
- **Distal (outer):** Near shoulder (~15%)
- **Medial (inner):** Near sternum (~5%)

## Treatment Options

### Non-Surgical (Sling)
- For most midshaft fractures
- Non-displaced or minimally displaced fractures
- Healing time: 6-12 weeks

### Surgical (Plate/Screws or Pins)
- For displaced fractures
- Shortened fractures
- Fractures near shoulder joint
- Some athletic considerations

**Your rehabilitation depends on treatment type—follow your specific protocol.**

## Phase 1: Protection Phase (Weeks 0-3)

### Sling Use
- Wear as directed (typically most of the time)
- Remove for exercises and hygiene
- May have figure-8 brace or just sling

### Keep Distal Joints Moving

**Elbow Exercises**
1. Remove arm from sling
2. Support upper arm against body
3. Bend and straighten elbow fully
4. 15-20 repetitions
5. Several times daily

**Wrist Circles**
1. Rotate wrist in circles
2. 10 circles each direction
3. Keep elbow supported

**Fist Making**
1. Open and close fist
2. Spread fingers wide
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Finger Exercises**
1. Touch thumb to each fingertip
2. Spread and squeeze fingers
3. Maintains circulation and mobility

### Gentle Shoulder Motion (When Cleared)

**Pendulum Exercises**
1. Lean forward, supporting uninvolved side
2. Let arm hang (may start while in sling)
3. Small circles, gravity-assisted
4. 10 circles each direction
5. May begin at week 1-2 depending on stability

**Passive Forward Flexion**
1. Lie on back
2. Use good arm to lift affected arm
3. Only to tolerance (often limited to 90° initially)
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Scapular Exercises (Important!)

**Scapular Squeezes**
1. While seated or standing
2. Squeeze shoulder blades together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Scapular Clock**
1. Move shoulder blade in all directions
2. Don't move the arm—just the blade
3. 10 movements each direction

## Phase 2: Early Motion (Weeks 3-6)

### Progressive Range of Motion

**Active-Assisted Forward Flexion**
1. Use pulley, wand, or good arm
2. Lift arm forward and up
3. Progress range as tolerated
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Active-Assisted External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Use wand or good hand to assist
3. Rotate forearm outward
4. Progress to 45-60 degrees
5. 15-20 repetitions

**Active-Assisted Abduction**
1. Lift arm out to side
2. Assistance as needed
3. Progress range gradually
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Active Range of Motion

**Active Forward Flexion**
1. Lift arm forward without assistance
2. As high as comfortable
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate outward actively
3. 15-20 repetitions

### Continue Scapular Work

**Add:** Scapular shrugs with movement
1. Shrug shoulders up
2. Roll back and down
3. 15 repetitions

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 6-12)

Begin when fracture shows adequate healing (confirmed by doctor).

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. Light weight or no weight initially
5. 3 sets of 15

**Standing External Rotation with Band**
1. Band anchored at waist height
2. Elbow at side
3. Rotate outward
4. 3 sets of 15

**Standing Internal Rotation with Band**
1. Same setup
2. Rotate inward
3. 3 sets of 15

### Scapular Strengthening

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Lie face down on bench
2. Y: Arms overhead, thumbs up, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze and lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Rows**
1. Band or light dumbbell
2. Pull elbows back
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 3 sets of 15

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. 3 sets of 15

**Serratus Punches**
1. Lying on back, arm straight up
2. Push toward ceiling
3. 3 sets of 15

### Deltoid Strengthening

**Lateral Raises**
1. Very light weight to start
2. Lift arms out to sides
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Front Raises**
1. Light weight
2. Lift arms forward
3. 2-3 sets of 15

## Phase 4: Progressive Loading (Weeks 10-16)

### Pushing Exercises

**Wall Push-Ups**
1. Start vertical
2. Progress angle gradually
3. 3 sets of 15

**Counter Push-Ups**
1. Hands on counter
2. Full range
3. 3 sets of 15

**Knee Push-Ups**
1. On knees
2. Full range
3. Progress to full push-ups

### Pressing Progression

**Chest Press (Machine)**
1. Light resistance
2. Full range
3. 3 sets of 12

**Dumbbell Press**
1. Light weight
2. Control throughout
3. Progress gradually

### Overhead Work (Last to Return)

**Overhead Press**
1. Very light weight
2. Progress slowly
3. Often the most cautious movement

### Pulling Exercises

**Lat Pulldown**
1. Light to moderate
2. 3 sets of 12-15

**Cable/Band Rows**
1. Full range
2. 3 sets of 15

## Phase 5: Return to Activity (Weeks 12-16+)

### Sport-Specific Preparation

**Contact sports:**
1. Non-contact training first
2. Protective padding may be used
3. Gradual return to contact
4. Full contact when cleared (often 3-4 months)

**Throwing:**
1. Begin interval throwing program
2. Start with easy tosses
3. Progress distance and velocity

**Weightlifting:**
1. Progress all lifts gradually
2. Watch for pain at fracture site
3. Full load may take 4-6 months

### Return Criteria

- Fracture healed on imaging
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength near pre-injury level
- Can perform sport-specific tasks
- Doctor clearance

## Surgical vs. Non-Surgical Considerations

### Non-Surgical Recovery
- May have visible bump at fracture site
- Full healing: 6-12 weeks
- Full return: 3-4 months typically

### Surgical Recovery
- Plate and screws stabilize fracture
- May begin motion earlier
- Hardware may need eventual removal
- Return timeline similar or slightly faster

### Both Require
- Progressive rehabilitation
- Patience
- Protection from re-injury

## Managing Common Issues

### Stiffness
- Very common after immobilization
- Consistent ROM exercises essential
- May take weeks to resolve

### Weakness
- Expected after disuse
- Progressive strengthening
- Full recovery takes months

### Visible Bump
- Common with non-surgical treatment
- Usually cosmetic only
- Doesn't indicate problem

### Numbness
- May occur near incision (surgical)
- Usually improves over time
- Report significant numbness to doctor

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 8)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, circles, 3 min
2. Active ROM: All directions, 15 reps
3. Side-lying ER: 3×15
4. Standing IR band: 3×15
5. Rows: 3×15
6. Prone T raises: 2×12

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Face pulls: 3×15
3. Prone Y-W raises: 2×10 each
4. Serratus punches: 3×15
5. Wall push-ups: 3×15
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. All strengthening exercises
3. Lateral raises: 2×15
4. Progress push-up angle
5. Cool-down stretches

## Key Takeaways

1. **Keep moving below the fracture** — elbow, wrist, hand exercises throughout
2. **Scapular exercises are crucial** — start immediately, continue throughout
3. **Follow your specific protocol** — surgical and non-surgical differ
4. **Progress ROM before strength** — mobility first
5. **Pushing/pressing comes last** — most stress on clavicle
6. **Expect a bump** — normal with non-surgical treatment
7. **Protect during return** — padding for contact sports
8. **Full recovery takes 3-6 months** — be patient

Clavicle fractures heal well with proper rehabilitation. Stay consistent with your exercises, protect the healing bone, and progress systematically back to full activity.
