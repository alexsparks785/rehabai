# Pain Between Shoulder Blades: Exercises for Relief and Prevention

Pain between the shoulder blades—medically called interscapular pain—is one of the most common upper body complaints. It can range from a dull ache to sharp, burning pain that makes it hard to focus on anything else. The good news: most interscapular pain responds extremely well to targeted exercises.

## Understanding Interscapular Pain

### Common Causes

**Postural (Most Common):**
- Prolonged computer/desk work
- Forward head posture
- Rounded shoulders
- Slouching while sitting or standing

**Muscular:**
- Weak rhomboids and middle/lower trapezius
- Tight pectorals pulling shoulders forward
- Trigger points in trapezius, rhomboids, or levator scapulae
- Overstretched and weak upper back muscles

**Thoracic Spine:**
- Stiff thoracic vertebrae
- Poor thoracic mobility
- Rib joint dysfunction

**Other Causes:**
- Cervical disc issues (referred pain)
- Nerve compression
- Stress and tension

### The Postural Problem

Here's the irony: the muscles between your shoulder blades often hurt because they're **overstretched and weak**, not tight.

When you slouch forward:
1. Your pecs shorten and tighten
2. Your shoulders round forward
3. Your upper back muscles get pulled long
4. These overstretched muscles become strained and painful

**The solution isn't to stretch the painful area**—it's to strengthen it while stretching the opposite side.

## Phase 1: Pain Relief (Immediate)

### Tennis Ball Release (Rhomboids/Trapezius)

1. Place a tennis ball between your upper back and a wall
2. Position it on the painful area (between spine and shoulder blade)
3. Apply pressure by leaning into the ball
4. Hold on tender spots for 20-30 seconds
5. Roll small circles to find multiple tender areas

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes per side

### Thoracic Extension Over Roller

1. Place a foam roller horizontally under your upper back
2. Support your head with your hands
3. Let your upper back extend over the roller
4. Move the roller to different levels of your upper back
5. Breathe deeply and let gravity do the work

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds at each level (3-4 positions)

### Prone Press-Up

1. Lie face down with hands under your shoulders
2. Press your upper body up while keeping hips down
3. Let your lower back arch
4. This helps extend the thoracic spine indirectly

**Reps:** 10 reps, hold each for 3 seconds

### Heat Application

Apply heat to the area between your shoulder blades for 15-20 minutes. Heat relaxes muscles and increases blood flow.

## Phase 2: Stretch What's Actually Tight

### Doorway Pec Stretch

**This is critical—tight pecs pull your shoulders forward:**

1. Stand in a doorway with your forearm against the frame
2. Elbow at 90° for lower pec, elbow above shoulder for pec minor
3. Step through the doorway until you feel a stretch
4. Keep your ribs down—don't arch your lower back

**Hold:** 30-45 seconds per side, both angles

### Corner Pec Stretch

1. Stand facing a corner
2. Place forearms on each wall, elbows at shoulder height
3. Lean into the corner until you feel a stretch across your chest
4. Keep your core engaged

**Hold:** 30-45 seconds, 2-3 sets

### Lat Stretch

1. Kneel facing a bench or chair
2. Place your elbows on the surface
3. Push your hips back and let your chest drop toward the floor
4. Feel the stretch along your sides

**Hold:** 30-45 seconds

### Levator Scapulae Stretch

1. Sit or stand tall
2. Turn your head 45° to one side
3. Look down toward your armpit
4. Gently assist the stretch with your hand

**Hold:** 30 seconds per side

### Upper Trapezius Stretch

1. Tip your ear toward your shoulder
2. Reach the opposite hand behind your back or sit on it
3. Gently assist with your other hand on top of your head

**Hold:** 30 seconds per side

## Phase 3: Strengthen the Upper Back

### Prone Y-T-W Raises

**The gold standard for interscapular strengthening:**

1. Lie face down on a bench or the floor
2. **Y:** Raise arms at 45° angles, thumbs up, squeeze shoulder blades
3. **T:** Raise arms straight out to sides, thumbs up, squeeze shoulder blades
4. **W:** Arms at 45° with elbows bent, externally rotate, squeeze shoulder blades

**Reps:** 10 of each position, 2-3 sets

**Focus:** The squeeze between your shoulder blades is more important than how high you lift

### Face Pulls

1. Set a cable or band at face height
2. Pull toward your face with elbows high
3. At the end, externally rotate (thumbs back)
4. Squeeze your shoulder blades hard
5. Control the return

**Reps:** 15-20 reps, 3 sets

### Band Pull-Aparts

1. Hold a resistance band in front of you at shoulder height
2. Pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades
3. Don't let your shoulders shrug up
4. Control the return

**Reps:** 15-20 reps, 3 sets

### Prone Rows (Chest Supported)

1. Lie face down on an incline bench
2. Hold dumbbells hanging straight down
3. Row both weights up, squeezing shoulder blades
4. Lower with control

**Reps:** 12-15 reps, 3 sets

**Key:** Focus on the squeeze between shoulder blades, not pulling with arms

### Wall Angels

1. Stand with your back against a wall
2. Press your lower back, upper back, and head into the wall
3. Place arms in a "W" position against the wall
4. Slide arms up toward a "Y" while maintaining wall contact
5. Return to "W"

**Reps:** 10-12 reps, 3 sets

**If you can't maintain wall contact, work within available range**

### Scapular Retraction Hold

1. Stand or sit tall
2. Pull your shoulder blades together and down
3. Hold this position
4. Don't let shoulders shrug up

**Hold:** 10-15 seconds, 10 reps

### Seated Row with Pause

1. Use a cable machine or resistance band
2. Sit tall and row the handles toward your torso
3. At the end, squeeze your shoulder blades together and hold for 2 seconds
4. Control the return

**Reps:** 12-15 reps, 3 sets

## Phase 4: Improve Thoracic Mobility

### Thread the Needle

1. Start on all fours
2. Reach one arm under your body, rotating your torso
3. Let your shoulder and head rest on the ground
4. Feel the rotation through your mid-back

**Hold:** 30 seconds per side, 2-3 sets

### Open Book

1. Lie on your side with knees bent to 90°
2. Extend both arms in front of you
3. Open your top arm, rotating it toward the ceiling/floor behind you
4. Follow your hand with your eyes
5. Let your torso rotate while keeping hips stacked

**Reps:** 10 per side, hold each for 5 seconds

### Cat-Cow with Mid-Back Focus

1. Start on all fours
2. **Cow:** Drop belly, arch back, but focus on your mid-back moving
3. **Cat:** Round back, focus on mid-back rounding
4. Exaggerate the movement in your upper/mid back

**Reps:** 15-20 cycles

### Thoracic Rotation with Reach

1. Start in a half-kneeling position
2. Place one hand on the floor for support
3. Rotate your torso, reaching the other arm toward the ceiling
4. Follow your hand with your eyes
5. Return and repeat

**Reps:** 10 per side

## Daily Routine for Interscapular Pain

### Morning (5 minutes)

1. Cat-cow with mid-back focus: 10 reps
2. Thread the needle: 20 seconds per side
3. Band pull-aparts: 15 reps
4. Scapular retraction hold: 5 x 10 seconds

### At Work (Every Hour)

1. Scapular retraction: Hold 10 seconds, 5 times
2. Shoulder rolls: 10 forward, 10 backward
3. Chin tucks: 10 reps
4. Brief standing and walking

### Desk Stretch Break (Every 2-3 Hours)

1. Doorway pec stretch: 20 seconds per side
2. Upper trap stretch: 20 seconds per side
3. Thread the needle: 15 seconds per side
4. 10 band pull-aparts (keep a band at your desk)

### Evening Routine (10-15 minutes)

1. Tennis ball release: 2 minutes per side
2. Foam roller thoracic extension: 2 minutes
3. Doorway pec stretch: 30 seconds per side
4. Lat stretch: 30 seconds
5. Prone Y-T-W: 10 each position
6. Open book: 10 per side
7. Wall angels: 10 reps

## Posture Corrections

### Sitting

- **Monitor height:** Top of screen at eye level
- **Keyboard position:** Elbows at 90°, shoulders relaxed
- **Chair:** Support for lower back, feet flat
- **Frequent breaks:** Stand every 30-45 minutes

### Standing

- **Ears over shoulders** over hips over ankles
- **Shoulder blades gently back and down**
- **Avoid locking knees**
- **Weight distributed evenly**

### Phone and Tablet Use

- **Bring the device up** rather than looking down
- **Use a stand** for tablets
- **Take frequent breaks** when scrolling

### Sleeping

- **Back sleepers:** Use a pillow that keeps head neutral, not pushed forward
- **Side sleepers:** Hug a pillow to prevent top shoulder from rolling forward
- **Avoid stomach sleeping:** Forces neck rotation

## Strengthening Program for Long-Term Prevention

### 3x Per Week

**Exercise 1:** Prone Y-T-W raises - 3 sets x 10 each position
**Exercise 2:** Face pulls - 3 sets x 15-20 reps
**Exercise 3:** Seated rows with pause - 3 sets x 12 reps
**Exercise 4:** Wall angels - 3 sets x 10 reps
**Exercise 5:** Push-ups - 3 sets x 10-15 reps (yes, push-ups done correctly strengthen the serratus anterior)

### Daily

**Stretching:** Pec stretches and upper trap stretches
**Mobility:** Thread the needle and thoracic extension
**Posture:** Conscious corrections throughout the day

## Red Flags: When to Seek Medical Attention

See a doctor immediately if your interscapular pain is accompanied by:

- Chest pain or pressure
- Difficulty breathing
- Pain radiating down your arm
- Sudden severe pain (especially with nausea)
- Numbness or weakness in arms or hands
- Pain that doesn't change with position or movement
- Fever or unexplained weight loss
- History of cancer

These could indicate cardiac issues, cervical disc problems, or other conditions requiring medical evaluation.

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Week 1-2:** Pain reduction with release techniques and stretching
- **Week 2-4:** Improved posture awareness and beginning strength gains
- **Week 4-8:** Significant pain reduction and postural improvement
- **Week 8+:** Long-term maintenance with occasional flare-ups during stress/overwork

## Key Takeaways

1. **The pain is often from overstretched, weak muscles**—not tight ones
2. **Strengthen the upper back** (rhomboids, middle/lower traps) with Y-T-W, face pulls, rows
3. **Stretch the chest and front of shoulders**—these are actually tight
4. **Improve thoracic mobility** with rotation and extension exercises
5. **Fix your posture**—this is the root cause for most people
6. **Frequency matters**—daily mobility and posture work beats occasional long sessions
7. **Release techniques provide temporary relief**—strength provides long-term solution

Pain between the shoulder blades usually has a clear cause (posture and muscle imbalance) and a clear solution (stretching what's tight, strengthening what's weak). Commit to the routine for 4-6 weeks and you should see significant improvement.
