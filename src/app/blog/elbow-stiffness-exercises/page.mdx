# Elbow Stiffness Exercises: Restore Range of Motion

Elbow stiffness is one of the most common complications after injury or surgery. The elbow is particularly prone to losing motion, but dedicated exercises can help restore flexibility and function.

## Understanding Elbow Stiffness

The elbow is a "temperamental" joint that doesn't tolerate immobility well. Stiffness can develop from:

**Causes:**
- Fractures (even well-healed)
- Dislocations
- Burns
- Prolonged immobilization
- Surgery
- Inflammatory arthritis
- Infection (rare)

**Types of limitation:**
- **Flexion contracture:** Can't fully straighten (most common)
- **Extension limitation:** Can't fully bend
- **Rotation limitation:** Can't fully turn palm up/down

**Contributing factors:**
- Capsular tightness
- Scar tissue
- Muscle shortening
- Heterotopic ossification (bone in soft tissue)
- Joint surface damage

## Normal vs. Functional Range

**Normal elbow motion:**
- Flexion: 0-145 degrees
- Extension: 0 degrees (fully straight)
- Pronation: 80 degrees (palm down)
- Supination: 80 degrees (palm up)

**Functional range (for most daily activities):**
- Flexion-extension arc: 30-130 degrees
- Rotation: 50 degrees each direction

**Goal:** Achieve at least functional range, ideally more.

## When to Exercise vs. Seek Medical Help

**Exercise is appropriate when:**
- Stiffness from disuse or minor injury
- Post-surgical rehabilitation (when cleared)
- Gradual motion loss
- No underlying mechanical block

**Seek medical evaluation for:**
- Sudden motion loss
- Severe stiffness not improving with exercise
- Pain out of proportion to stiffness
- History of significant trauma
- Suspected heterotopic ossification

## Principles of Treating Stiffness

### Low-Load, Prolonged Stretch
- Gentle force over extended time
- More effective than aggressive short stretching
- Less risk of injury

### Heat Before, Ice After
- Heat improves tissue pliability
- Ice reduces post-exercise inflammation

### Multiple Daily Sessions
- Short, frequent sessions work better
- Total stretch time matters

### End-Range Holds
- Hold positions at end of range
- Longer holds (30+ seconds) are effective

### Avoid Aggressive Manipulation
- Can cause more stiffness
- Risk of injury
- Gentle progression is safer

## Warm-Up Techniques

### Heat Application

**Options:**
- Warm shower (10-15 minutes)
- Heating pad (15-20 minutes)
- Warm water soak
- Exercise in warm pool

**Why it helps:**
- Increases tissue extensibility
- Reduces muscle guarding
- Decreases pain

### Active Movement Warm-Up

**Gentle pumping motions:**
1. Bend and straighten elbow repeatedly
2. 30-50 repetitions
3. Gets joint fluid moving
4. Prepares tissues for stretching

## Exercises for Flexion Contracture (Can't Straighten)

### Gravity-Assisted Extension

**Lying position:**
1. Lie on back
2. Hold upper arm straight up toward ceiling
3. Let forearm hang backward toward shoulder
4. Gravity stretches elbow toward extension
5. Hold 2-5 minutes

**Prone hang:**
1. Lie face down on bed
2. Upper arm on bed, forearm hanging off edge
3. Let gravity straighten elbow
4. Light weight in hand increases stretch
5. Hold 2-5 minutes

### Passive Extension Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Support upper arm on table
2. Use other hand to gently press forearm down
3. Apply steady, gentle pressure
4. Hold 30-60 seconds
5. Rest and repeat 5-10 times

**Progression:** Add light weight on wrist to increase stretch.

### Towel Prop Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Roll towel and place under wrist
2. Let elbow rest on flat surface
3. Gravity stretches elbow straight
4. Hold 10-20 minutes
5. Repeat several times daily

### Static Progressive Stretch

**Using splint or turnbuckle orthosis:**
- Gradual, sustained stretch
- Often used for significant contractures
- Worn for extended periods
- Prescribed by therapist or physician

## Exercises for Limited Flexion (Can't Bend)

### Self-Assisted Flexion

**How to do it:**
1. Sit or stand
2. Use other hand to help bend elbow
3. Push gently to end range
4. Hold 30-60 seconds
5. Repeat 5-10 times

### Wall Slide Flexion

**How to do it:**
1. Stand facing wall
2. Place hand on wall at shoulder height
3. Slide hand up wall (elbow bends)
4. Lean body toward wall to increase flexion
5. Hold at end range 30 seconds

### Weighted Elbow Flexion

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with forearm supported
2. Hold light weight
3. Let weight help bend elbow
4. Hold 2-5 minutes

## Exercises for Limited Rotation

### Forearm Rotation Active

**How to do it:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate palm fully up (supination)
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Rotate palm fully down (pronation)
5. Hold 5 seconds
6. 15-20 repetitions

### Assisted Rotation

**Supination assist:**
1. Hold wrist with other hand
2. Gently turn palm upward
3. Hold at end range 30 seconds

**Pronation assist:**
1. Same technique, turn palm downward
2. Hold 30 seconds

### Rotation with Weight

**How to do it:**
1. Hold hammer or rod
2. Rotate to end range
3. Weight provides stretch
4. Hold 30-60 seconds each direction

### Door Frame Rotation

**Supination stretch:**
1. Stand in doorway
2. Forearm at side, palm against frame
3. Turn body away to stretch supination
4. Hold 30 seconds

**Pronation stretch:**
1. Back of hand against frame
2. Turn body to stretch pronation
3. Hold 30 seconds

## Strengthening Exercises

Strengthen after achieving functional motion.

### Isometric Exercises

**Flexion:**
1. Elbow bent, press into other hand
2. No movement
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Extension:**
1. Elbow bent, try to straighten against resistance
2. Hold 5-10 seconds
3. 10 repetitions

### Dynamic Strengthening

**Biceps Curls:**
2-3 sets of 15, light to moderate weight

**Triceps Extensions:**
2-3 sets of 15

**Forearm Rotation:**
With weight or band, 2-3 sets of 15

**Wrist Curls:**
Both directions, 2-3 sets of 15

## Advanced Techniques

### Static Progressive Splinting

**How it works:**
- Custom or commercial splint
- Positions elbow at end range
- Worn for extended periods (30+ min to hours)
- Adjustable as motion improves

**When used:**
- Moderate to severe contractures
- When exercises alone insufficient
- Prescribed by therapist/physician

### Dynamic Splinting

**How it works:**
- Spring-loaded splint
- Provides constant low-load stretch
- Worn for hours at a time
- Common for elbow contractures

### Serial Casting

**When used:**
- Severe contractures
- Done by medical professionals
- Cast changed as motion improves

## Sample Daily Program

### Morning (20-30 minutes)
1. Heat: 15 min (shower or pad)
2. Pumping warm-up: 50 reps
3. Extension stretches: 5 min
4. Flexion stretches: 5 min
5. Rotation stretches: 3 min each direction

### Midday (10-15 minutes)
1. Brief warm-up: 30 reps
2. Extension focus: 5 min
3. Rotation: 3 min each direction

### Evening (20-30 minutes)
1. Heat: 15 min
2. Full stretching routine: 15 min
3. Strengthening (if appropriate): 10 min
4. Ice: 15 min

### Splint/Stretch Time (If Using)
- Additional 30 min to several hours in static progressive or dynamic splint
- As directed by healthcare provider

## Tracking Progress

### Measure Your Motion
- Use goniometer or compare to other arm
- Track weekly
- Note flexion, extension, and rotation

### Progress Expectations
- Gains may be slow (1-2 degrees per week)
- Most improvement in first 3-6 months
- Some motion loss may be permanent

### Signs of Good Progress
- Gradual improvement in motion
- Decreasing stiffness
- Better function with daily activities

### When to Be Concerned
- No progress after 4-6 weeks of consistent exercise
- Increasing pain
- Loss of previously gained motion

## When Exercise Isn't Enough

**Consider medical intervention for:**
- Severe contracture (>30 degrees flexion contracture)
- No progress with conservative treatment
- Significant functional limitation
- Known mechanical block (bone, hardware)

**Options may include:**
- Formal physical/occupational therapy
- Custom splinting program
- Injection therapy
- Manipulation under anesthesia
- Arthroscopic or open surgical release

## Key Takeaways

1. **Heat before stretching** — improves tissue extensibility
2. **Low-load, long duration** — more effective than aggressive stretching
3. **Multiple daily sessions** — better than one long session
4. **End-range holds** — 30+ seconds for best results
5. **Be patient** — motion recovery takes weeks to months
6. **Track your progress** — measure weekly
7. **Strengthen once mobile** — supports maintained motion
8. **Seek help if not improving** — therapy or medical intervention may be needed

Elbow stiffness is challenging but often responds to dedicated, consistent exercise. Stay patient, follow the principles of low-load prolonged stretch, and work with your healthcare team if progress stalls.
