# What Muscles Do Cable Pull-Throughs Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Cable pull-throughs are a hip hinge exercise where you pull a cable between your legs from behind, driving your hips forward to standing. This movement pattern directly targets the posterior chain—glutes, hamstrings, and lower back—making it excellent for learning the hip hinge and building these muscles.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Gluteus Maximus

**Activation Level: Very High**

The glutes are the star of this movement:
- Primary hip extensors
- Drive the hips forward against cable resistance
- Peak contraction at lockout
- Work through full range of hip motion

Cable pull-throughs keep constant tension on the glutes throughout the entire rep—there's no "easy" portion like with free weights.

### Hamstrings

**Activation Level: High**

All three hamstring muscles contribute:
- **Biceps femoris** (lateral)
- **Semimembranosus** (medial)
- **Semitendinosus** (medial)

The hamstrings:
- Assist hip extension
- Stretch significantly during the forward lean
- Work synergistically with glutes
- Control the eccentric (lowering) phase

### Erector Spinae

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The spinal erectors along your back:
- Maintain neutral spine during the hinge
- Resist spinal flexion
- Work isometrically throughout
- More active than in hip thrusts

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Adductor Magnus

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The largest inner thigh muscle:
- Assists hip extension (often overlooked role)
- Works with glutes and hamstrings
- Significant posterior chain contributor

### Core Stabilizers

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Your core maintains spinal position:
- **Transverse abdominis**: Creates stability
- **Obliques**: Prevent rotation
- **Rectus abdominis**: Prevents hyperextension at lockout

### Gluteus Medius/Minimus

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The lateral glute muscles:
- Stabilize the hips
- Prevent lateral movement
- Support the stance

### Forearms/Grip

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Holding the rope attachment:
- Forearm flexors engaged
- Grip typically not limiting
- Use rope or handle attachment

## Why Cable Pull-Throughs Excel

### Constant Tension

Unlike barbell hip hinges:
- Cable provides resistance throughout
- No "dead spots" in the movement
- Maximum time under tension
- Great for hypertrophy

### Glute Emphasis

Compared to deadlifts:
- More direct glute activation
- Less lower back fatigue
- Easier to feel the target muscles
- Perfect for glute mind-muscle connection

### Learning the Hip Hinge

For beginners:
- Cable guides the movement pattern
- Easier to master than Romanian deadlifts
- Lower injury risk
- Teaches the "hips back" motion

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Standing facing away from cable
- Cable between legs, rope in both hands
- Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider
- Slight knee bend

### Hinge Phase (Eccentric)
- Push hips back (not down)
- Cable pulls between legs
- Hamstrings and glutes stretch
- Erectors maintain spine position
- Torso hinges forward

### Bottom Position
- Maximum hip flexion (hips back)
- Hamstrings stretched
- Glutes loaded
- Brief pause

### Drive Phase (Concentric)
- Glutes and hamstrings contract forcefully
- Drive hips forward
- Stand tall
- Squeeze glutes at lockout

### Lockout
- Full hip extension
- Glutes maximally contracted
- Stand tall (don't hyperextend)
- Brief squeeze

## Common Mistakes

**Squatting instead of hinging**: Bending knees too much, moving up and down instead of back and forward. Push hips BACK, not down.

**Rounding the lower back**: Losing neutral spine. Keep chest proud, back flat throughout.

**Pulling with arms**: Using arms to move the weight. Arms are just hooks—the hips do the work.

**Hyperextending at the top**: Leaning back excessively. Stand tall, don't overarch.

**Not hinging deep enough**: Partial range of motion. Hips should go well behind heels.

**Stance too narrow**: Limits hip hinge depth. Shoulder-width or slightly wider.

## How to Maximize Glute Activation

1. **Think "hips back"**: Not "bend over"
2. **Squeeze at lockout**: 1-2 second glute squeeze at top
3. **Control the eccentric**: 2-3 seconds on the hinge back
4. **Feel the stretch**: Loaded hamstring stretch at bottom
5. **Drive through heels**: Shifts emphasis to posterior chain
6. **Keep shins vertical**: Sign of proper hip hinge
7. **Initiate with glutes**: Think "glutes drive hips forward"

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Standard pull-through | Baseline |
| Band pull-through | Bands instead of cable, different resistance curve |
| Single-leg pull-through | Unilateral, added stability demand |
| Paused pull-through | Pause at bottom, increases difficulty |
| Slow eccentric pull-through | 4-5 second hinge, more muscle damage |
| Wide stance pull-through | More adductor involvement |

### Band Pull-Through

Using resistance bands:
- Can do anywhere (no cable machine)
- Band resistance increases as you extend
- Slightly different feel
- Great alternative

## Cable Pull-Through vs Other Hip Hinges

| Exercise | Glute Activation | Lower Back Load | Skill Required | Loading Capacity |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|----------------|------------------|
| Cable pull-through | Very High | Moderate | Low | Moderate |
| Romanian deadlift | High | High | Moderate | Very High |
| Barbell hip thrust | Very High | Low | Low | Very High |
| Kettlebell swing | High | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate |
| Good morning | Moderate-High | High | Moderate | High |

Cable pull-throughs offer high glute activation with moderate back stress and low skill requirement.

## Programming Recommendations

**For Glute Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 12-20 reps
- Moderate to heavy weight
- Focus on squeeze at top
- 2-3 times per week

**For Learning Hip Hinge:**
- 3 sets of 10-15 reps
- Light to moderate weight
- Perfect the pattern before loading
- Can do daily while learning

**As Warm-Up/Activation:**
- 2 sets of 15-20 reps
- Light weight
- Before deadlifts or squats
- Focus on glute engagement

**As Finisher:**
- 2-3 sets to near failure
- After main compound lifts
- High reps, constant tension
- Maximum pump

**Integration Ideas:**
- Superset with hip thrusts
- Before Romanian deadlifts (activation)
- Part of glute circuit
- Alternative to RDL when back is fatigued

## Who Should Use Cable Pull-Throughs

**Beginners:**
- Learning the hip hinge pattern
- Building posterior chain strength
- Before progressing to deadlifts

**Intermediate/Advanced:**
- Accessory work for glutes
- Lower back-friendly hip hinge option
- Variety in training

**Those with Back Issues:**
- Lower spinal load than deadlifts
- Still trains posterior chain
- Can substitute for barbell hinging

**Anyone wanting better glute activation:**
- Excellent mind-muscle connection
- Direct glute work
- Constant tension

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: Glutes (very high), hamstrings (high), erector spinae (moderate-high)
- **Secondary**: Adductor magnus, core stabilizers, glute med/min
- **Key benefit**: Constant cable tension = maximum time under tension
- **Great for**: Learning hip hinge, glute activation, back-friendly posterior chain work
- **Movement pattern**: Hip hinge—hips go BACK, not down
- **Common error**: Squatting instead of hinging
- **Key cue**: "Hips back, squeeze at top"

Cable pull-throughs are an underrated posterior chain builder. They teach the hip hinge, provide constant glute tension, and spare the lower back compared to barbell hinging. Add them to your routine for better glutes and a stronger posterior chain.
