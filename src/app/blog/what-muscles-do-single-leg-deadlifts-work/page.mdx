# What Muscles Do Single Leg Deadlifts Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The single leg deadlift (single leg RDL) is a unilateral hip hinge where you balance on one leg while hinging forward, the other leg extending behind you. This exercise builds posterior chain strength, corrects imbalances, and challenges your balance and stability like few other movements can.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Gluteus Maximus (Standing Leg)

**Activation Level: Very High**

The glute of your standing leg works intensely:
- Primary hip extensor
- Drives you back to standing
- Works through full hip hinge range
- Must work without help from the other leg

Single leg work increases per-leg glute activation compared to bilateral exercises.

### Hamstrings (Standing Leg)

**Activation Level: Very High**

All three hamstring muscles work hard:
- **Biceps femoris**
- **Semimembranosus**
- **Semitendinosus**

The hamstrings:
- Assist hip extension
- Control the eccentric (lowering) phase
- Stretch significantly during the hinge
- Work in conjunction with glutes

### Gluteus Medius and Minimus (Standing Leg)

**Activation Level: Very High**

The lateral glutes are crucial for balance:
- Prevent hip drop
- Stabilize the pelvis
- Control frontal plane movement
- Work much harder than in bilateral deadlifts

This is a key difference from regular deadlifts—the single leg version heavily taxes hip stabilizers.

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Erector Spinae

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The spinal erectors:
- Maintain neutral spine during hinge
- Work isometrically throughout
- Resist spinal flexion
- Support the movement

### Core Stabilizers

**Activation Level: Very High**

Your entire core works overtime:
- **Obliques**: Anti-rotation (huge demand)
- **Transverse abdominis**: Creates stability
- **Quadratus lumborum**: Anti-lateral flexion
- **Rectus abdominis**: Prevents hyperextension

The anti-rotation demand in single leg deadlifts is exceptional.

### Adductors (Standing Leg)

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The inner thigh:
- Assist hip extension (adductor magnus)
- Stabilize the stance
- Control lateral movement

### Hip Flexors (Floating Leg)

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The hip flexors of the back leg:
- Hold the leg in position
- Maintain hip extension
- Work isometrically

### Foot and Ankle Stabilizers

**Activation Level: High**

The intrinsic foot muscles and ankle stabilizers:
- Maintain balance
- Make constant micro-adjustments
- Often the limiting factor for beginners

## Standing Leg vs Floating Leg

| Leg | Role | Muscle Work |
|-----|------|-------------|
| Standing leg | Does all the work | Glutes, hamstrings, stabilizers (very high) |
| Floating leg | Counterbalance | Hip extensors (isometric), minimal work |

The floating leg acts as a counterweight—it's not working significantly.

## What Makes Single Leg Deadlifts Special

### Balance Challenge
- Single leg stance requires constant stabilization
- Trains proprioception
- Develops ankle and foot stability
- Transfers to real-world activities

### Identifies Imbalances
- Can't hide a weak side
- Exposes stability deficits
- Shows which leg needs work
- Objective feedback

### Anti-Rotation Demand
- Body wants to rotate during the hinge
- Core must fight to stay square
- Excellent rotational stability training
- Functional for sports

### Reduced Spinal Load
- Less total weight needed
- Same muscular challenge per leg
- Easier on the lower back
- Good for those with back sensitivity

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Standing on one leg
- Slight knee bend
- Core braced
- Weight in hand(s)

### Hinge Phase (Eccentric)
- Hinge at hip, sending floating leg back
- Hamstrings and glutes lengthen under load
- Core fights rotation
- Hip stabilizers work to maintain balance
- Torso moves toward parallel with floor

### Bottom Position
- Maximum hip flexion (standing leg)
- Maximum hamstring stretch
- Floating leg extended behind
- Brief pause

### Return Phase (Concentric)
- Glutes and hamstrings drive hip extension
- Core maintains stability
- Return to standing
- Control throughout

## Common Mistakes

**Rotating the hips**: Opening up toward the floating leg side. Keep hips square to the floor.

**Rounding the back**: Losing neutral spine. Maintain flat back throughout.

**Knee caving**: Standing leg knee collapsing inward. Push knee out over toes.

**Looking up**: Craning neck. Keep head neutral, gaze follows natural spine position.

**Going too fast**: Losing balance and control. Slow, controlled movement.

**Not hinging deep enough**: Partial range of motion. Hinge until torso is near parallel.

**Floating leg going off to the side**: Should track straight back. Keep it in line with your body.

## How to Maximize Muscle Activation

1. **Stay slow**: 3-4 seconds down, controlled up
2. **Hips square**: Fight the rotation
3. **Full range**: Torso toward parallel
4. **Soft knee**: Standing leg slightly bent
5. **Reach the weight down**: Toward or past standing foot
6. **Drive through heel**: To engage posterior chain
7. **Squeeze at top**: Full hip extension, glute squeeze

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Bodyweight SLDL | Learning balance, no load |
| Dumbbell SLDL | One or two dumbbells |
| Kettlebell SLDL | Single KB, contralateral or ipsilateral |
| Barbell SLDL | Higher loading, advanced |
| Landmine SLDL | Guided path, balance assist |
| B-stance RDL | Staggered stance, partial support |
| Kickstand RDL | Toe touch for balance |

### B-Stance and Kickstand Variations

If balance is limiting your loading:

**B-Stance RDL:**
- Back foot staggers behind, ball of foot on ground
- 80/20 weight distribution
- Builds toward true single leg

**Kickstand RDL:**
- Back toe touches floor lightly
- Provides balance reference
- Still primarily single leg work

These progressions help you build up to full single leg deadlifts.

### Contralateral vs Ipsilateral Loading

| Loading | Description | Effect |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Contralateral | Weight in opposite hand from standing leg | More anti-rotation, more natural |
| Ipsilateral | Weight in same hand as standing leg | Less anti-rotation, targets hip more |
| Bilateral | Weights in both hands | Balanced load, different feel |

Most people start with contralateral (cross-body) loading.

## Programming Recommendations

**For Balance/Stability:**
- 3 sets of 8-12 reps per leg
- Light weight or bodyweight
- Focus on control and position
- 2-3 times per week

**For Strength:**
- 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps per leg
- Moderate to heavy loading
- Progress load over time
- 2 times per week

**For Hypertrophy:**
- 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps per leg
- Moderate weight
- Slow tempo, full ROM
- 2-3 times per week

**Integration Ideas:**
- After bilateral deadlifts for extra volume
- On a unilateral-focused day
- Superset with single leg hip thrusts
- As accessory work for runners/athletes

## Single Leg Deadlift vs Bilateral Deadlifts

| Aspect | Single Leg DL | Romanian DL |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Balance demand | Very High | Low |
| Anti-rotation | Very High | Low |
| Load capacity | Moderate | Very High |
| Hip stabilizer work | Very High | Moderate |
| Skill requirement | High | Moderate |
| Identifies imbalances | Yes | No |

Both have value—single leg DL excels at stability and balance, bilateral excels at loading.

## Who Benefits Most

**Athletes:**
- Running, cutting, jumping are single-leg activities
- Builds functional stability
- Transfers to sport performance

**Runners:**
- Addresses common imbalances
- Builds eccentric hamstring strength
- Injury prevention

**Those with imbalances:**
- Forces equal work per side
- Can't compensate with strong side
- Diagnostic tool

**Anyone wanting functional strength:**
- Real-world activities are often single-leg
- Builds complete posterior chain
- Improves balance and proprioception

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles (standing leg)**: Glutes (very high), hamstrings (very high), hip stabilizers (very high)
- **Secondary**: Erector spinae, core (anti-rotation), adductors, ankle stabilizers
- **Unique benefits**: Balance challenge, identifies imbalances, huge anti-rotation demand
- **Common error**: Hip rotation—keep hips square
- **Progression**: Bodyweight → B-stance → kickstand → full single leg
- **Loading**: Contralateral (opposite hand) is most common
- **Balance limits load**: Accept lighter weight for the stability benefits

Single leg deadlifts are one of the best exercises for building functional posterior chain strength. The balance and stability demands make them challenging even with light weight, and the unilateral nature ensures balanced development.
