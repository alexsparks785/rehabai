# Cervical Radiculopathy Exercises: Relieve Pinched Nerve Pain

Cervical radiculopathy—a pinched nerve in your neck—causes pain, numbness, tingling, or weakness that radiates down your arm. The right exercises can reduce nerve compression, improve mobility, and promote healing without surgery.

## Understanding Cervical Radiculopathy

A cervical nerve root becomes compressed or irritated where it exits the spine. This can happen from:
- Herniated disc pressing on the nerve
- Bone spurs (cervical spondylosis)
- Degenerative changes narrowing the nerve passage
- Acute injury

**Common symptoms:**
- Neck pain that radiates into shoulder, arm, hand, or fingers
- Numbness or tingling in specific patterns
- Weakness in arm or hand
- Pain with certain neck positions
- Symptoms often follow a dermatomal pattern (specific nerve distribution)

**Which nerves are commonly affected:**
- C5: Shoulder, upper arm (deltoid weakness)
- C6: Thumb and index finger (biceps weakness)
- C7: Middle finger (triceps weakness) — most common
- C8: Ring and pinky finger (grip weakness)

## When to Seek Immediate Care

**Red flags requiring urgent evaluation:**
- Progressive weakness in arms or legs
- Loss of coordination or balance
- Bladder or bowel dysfunction
- Symptoms in both arms or legs
- Severe, unrelenting pain

These may indicate spinal cord compression requiring immediate treatment.

## Positions That Help vs. Hurt

Understanding position effects helps manage symptoms.

**Often helpful:**
- Placing arm overhead (hand on top of head)
- Lying flat with proper neck support
- Slight extension (looking slightly up)

**Often aggravating:**
- Looking down (cervical flexion)
- Turning head toward painful side
- Extending neck to the affected side
- Carrying heavy items

**Individual variation exists**—pay attention to what helps your specific situation.

## Phase 1: Reduce Nerve Irritation

### Cervical Retraction (Chin Tucks)

Opens the space where nerves exit, reducing compression.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Draw chin straight back (making a "double chin")
3. Keep eyes level—don't look up or down
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Release
6. 10-15 repetitions, several times daily

**Key:** This should not increase arm symptoms. If it does, reduce the force.

### Cervical Retraction with Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Perform chin tuck
2. While maintaining tuck, gently look up
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Return to neutral
5. 10 repetitions

**Note:** Only add extension if it reduces or centralizes symptoms.

### Supported Cervical Retraction

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back with small towel roll under neck
2. Perform gentle chin tuck into the towel
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Positional Relief

**Overhead Arm Position**
If placing your hand on your head relieves symptoms:
1. Rest affected arm on top of head
2. Maintain for symptom relief
3. Use during flares

## Nerve Gliding Exercises

Nerve glides (neural mobilization) help the nerve move freely through surrounding tissues.

### Upper Limb Tension Test Position / Nerve Glide

**Median Nerve Glide (C6-C7)**
1. Stand sideways to wall, place hand on wall at shoulder height
2. Fingers pointing back, arm straight
3. Gently turn head away from the wall
4. Feel stretch along arm/nerve
5. Hold 3-5 seconds
6. Release by turning head toward arm
7. 10-15 repetitions, 2-3 sets

**Gentler version:**
1. Sit with arm at side
2. Bend wrist back (extension)
3. Straighten elbow
4. Tilt head away
5. Hold 3 seconds, release
6. 10-15 repetitions

### Radial Nerve Glide (C5-C7)

**How to do it:**
1. Arm at side
2. Make a fist
3. Bend wrist down (flexion)
4. Rotate arm inward (pronation)
5. Extend elbow
6. Depress shoulder
7. Tilt head away
8. Hold 3 seconds, release
9. 10-15 repetitions

### Ulnar Nerve Glide (C8-T1)

**How to do it:**
1. Arm at side
2. Bend elbow, bring hand toward face
3. Make "OK" sign over eye (like glasses)
4. Extend wrist back
5. Tilt head away
6. Hold 3 seconds, release
7. 10-15 repetitions

### Important Nerve Glide Principles
- Gentle movement—no pain
- Stop if symptoms increase
- If symptoms worsen, reduce range or stop
- "Tensioners" (all components at once) are more aggressive
- "Sliders" (moving one end while releasing the other) are gentler

## Stretching Exercises

### Upper Trapezius Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit, hold chair with hand on affected side
2. Tilt head away from that side
3. Add gentle pressure with opposite hand
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 2-3 repetitions each side

### Scalene Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit, hand under thigh on side to stretch
2. Tilt head away
3. Rotate chin slightly upward
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 2-3 repetitions

**Caution:** Scalene stretches can sometimes aggravate nerve symptoms. Stop if arm symptoms increase.

### Levator Scapulae Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Turn head 45 degrees to one side
2. Look down toward armpit
3. Add gentle pressure with hand
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 2-3 repetitions each side

### Pectoral Stretch

Tight pecs contribute to forward shoulder posture, which can affect nerve tension.

**Doorway stretch:**
1. Place forearm on doorframe
2. Step through with one foot
3. Feel stretch in chest
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat each side

## Strengthening Exercises

### Deep Neck Flexor Strengthening

Weak deep neck flexors are common with cervical issues.

**Chin Tuck with Lift**
1. Lie on back
2. Perform gentle chin tuck
3. Lift head 1 inch off surface
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Lower slowly
6. 10 repetitions

**Progression:** Increase hold time to 30 seconds

### Cervical Isometrics

**Flexion Isometric**
1. Place palm on forehead
2. Push head forward into hand
3. Hand prevents movement
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

**Extension Isometric**
1. Hands behind head
2. Push head backward into hands
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Side Bend Isometric**
1. Hand on side of head
2. Push head sideways into hand
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions each side

**Rotation Isometric**
1. Hand on forehead (side)
2. Try to turn head into hand
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions each side

### Scapular Stabilization

Proper scapular position reduces nerve tension.

**Scapular Retractions**
1. Squeeze shoulder blades together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. Release
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Lie face down on bench/bed
2. Y: Arms overhead, thumbs up, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades, lift
5. Hold each 5 seconds
6. 10 repetitions each position

**Rows**
1. Band anchored at chest height
2. Pull toward chest, squeezing shoulder blades
3. 3 sets of 15 repetitions

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. External rotation at end
4. 3 sets of 15 repetitions

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening

**External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Band in hand
3. Rotate forearm outward
4. Control return
5. 3 sets of 15 repetitions

**Internal Rotation**
1. Same setup
2. Rotate forearm inward against band
3. 3 sets of 15 repetitions

## Postural Exercises

### Wall Angels

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Arms in "goalpost" position
3. Press back, arms against wall
4. Slide arms up and down
5. Maintain wall contact
6. 10-15 repetitions

### Chin Tuck with Shoulder Retraction

**How to do it:**
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Chin tuck
3. Squeeze shoulder blades back and down
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

### Thoracic Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in chair with mid-back against top
2. Place hands behind head
3. Gently extend upper back over chair
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

## Cervical Traction

Traction can create space for the nerve. Options include:

### Self-Traction

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back
2. Place hands behind head/neck
3. Gently lift head, creating traction
4. Hold 30-60 seconds
5. Release slowly

### Over-the-Door Traction

**Commercial devices available:**
- Start with low weight (5-10 lbs)
- Use for 10-15 minutes
- Stop if symptoms worsen

**Consult your healthcare provider before using traction devices.**

## Activity Modifications

### Sleeping
- Neutral spine position
- Supportive pillow (not too high or too low)
- Avoid sleeping with arm overhead if it aggravates
- Consider cervical roll in pillowcase

### Working at Computer
- Monitor at eye level
- Keyboard and mouse at proper height
- Avoid forward head posture
- Take regular breaks

### Driving
- Proper seat position
- Head rest at correct height
- Breaks on long drives

### Lifting
- Avoid carrying heavy loads on affected side
- Use proper body mechanics
- Keep loads close to body

## Exercise Progression

### Week 1-2: Pain Relief
- Chin tucks
- Positional relief
- Gentle stretching
- Heat/ice for comfort

### Week 3-4: Add Nerve Glides
- Begin gentle nerve glides
- Progress stretching
- Add isometric strengthening

### Week 5-8: Strengthen
- Progress isometrics
- Add scapular strengthening
- Include postural exercises

### Week 8+: Maintenance
- Continue strengthening
- Maintain flexibility
- Progress to normal activities

## When Conservative Treatment Isn't Enough

**Seek medical consultation if:**
- Symptoms not improving after 4-6 weeks
- Progressive weakness
- Significant functional limitation
- Severe pain despite treatment

**Additional treatments may include:**
- Physical therapy
- Medications (NSAIDs, nerve pain medications)
- Epidural steroid injections
- Surgery (if conservative treatment fails)

**Good news:** Most cervical radiculopathy improves without surgery. Studies show 75-90% of people improve with conservative care.

## Sample Daily Program

### Morning (10-15 minutes)
1. Chin tucks: 15 repetitions
2. Cervical retraction with extension: 10 reps
3. Upper trap stretch: 30 sec each side
4. Scalene stretch (if tolerated): 30 sec each side
5. Gentle nerve glides: 10 reps each nerve

### Midday (5-10 minutes)
1. Posture reset: chin tucks, shoulder blade squeeze
2. Thoracic extension: 10 reps
3. Doorway pec stretch: 30 sec each side

### Evening (15 minutes)
1. All stretches: 30 sec each
2. Nerve glides: 10-15 reps each
3. Isometric strengthening: 10 reps each direction
4. Scapular exercises (rows, Y-T-W): 2 sets of 10

### Throughout Day
- Posture awareness
- Regular breaks from static positions
- Avoid aggravating positions

## Key Takeaways

1. **Chin tucks are foundational** — create space for the nerve
2. **Nerve glides help mobility** — gentle, pain-free movement
3. **Position matters** — identify what helps and hurts
4. **Strengthen neck and scapula** — supports cervical spine
5. **Address posture** — forward head increases nerve tension
6. **Be patient** — improvement takes weeks to months
7. **Progress gradually** — don't rush strengthening
8. **Seek help if not improving** — most cases respond to conservative care

Cervical radiculopathy can be painful and frustrating, but most people recover without surgery. Consistent exercise, posture modification, and patience usually lead to significant improvement.
