# What Muscles Do Close-Grip Bench Press Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The close-grip bench press is a compound exercise that shifts emphasis from the chest to the triceps. By narrowing your grip, you increase triceps involvement while still getting significant chest and shoulder work. Here's the complete breakdown.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Triceps Brachii

**Activation Level: Maximum**

The triceps become primary movers:

**Long Head:**
- Largest tricep head
- Significant involvement
- Shoulder position allows good work

**Lateral Head:**
- Outer "horseshoe"
- Very active in pressing
- Creates visible tricep shape

**Medial Head:**
- Deep head
- Works throughout
- Stabilizes the movement

### Pectoralis Major

**Activation Level: High**

The chest still works significantly:
- Less emphasis than wide grip
- Still a major contributor
- Sternal head primarily
- Reduced stretch compared to wide grip

### Anterior Deltoids

**Activation Level: High**

The front shoulders:
- Shoulder flexion
- Significant contribution
- Work throughout press

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Serratus Anterior

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Along the ribcage:
- Scapular protraction
- Shoulder stability
- Works at lockout

### Core

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

Stabilization:
- Maintains body position
- Assists leg drive transfer
- Isometric work

## Close-Grip vs Regular Bench Press

| Factor | Close-Grip | Regular Grip |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| Triceps | Maximum | High |
| Chest | High | Very High |
| Chest stretch | Reduced | Full |
| Weight possible | Lower | Higher |
| ROM (elbow) | Greater | Moderate |
| Shoulder stress | Often less | Moderate |

**Close-grip:** More triceps, less chest stretch
**Regular:** Balanced chest and triceps

## Grip Width

### How Close?

**Recommended:** Shoulder width or slightly narrower

**Too narrow (hands touching):**
- Wrist stress
- Elbow stress
- Not more tricep activation
- Avoid this

**Optimal:** Shoulder width gives maximum tricep emphasis while staying safe.

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Setup
- Grip bar at shoulder width (or slightly inside)
- Retract scapulae
- Slight arch, feet planted
- Unrack bar

### Descent (Eccentric)
- Lower bar with control
- Elbows closer to body than wide grip
- Bar path to lower chest
- Feel triceps stretch

### Bottom Position
- Bar touches lower chest
- Elbows at ~45° or less
- Controlled touch
- Brief pause optional

### Press (Concentric)
- Drive bar up
- Triceps and chest contract
- Elbows extend
- Full lockout

## Common Mistakes

**Grip too narrow**: Wrist/elbow stress. Shoulder width is sufficient.

**Elbows flaring wide**: Becomes regular bench. Keep elbows tucked.

**Bouncing off chest**: Cheating. Controlled touch.

**Losing scapular retraction**: Shoulders round forward. Stay tight.

**Partial lockout**: Missing tricep contraction. Full extension.

**Going too heavy**: Grip weakens, form breaks. Moderate weight.

**Bar path issues**: Should go to lower chest. Not neck or stomach.

## How to Maximize Tricep Activation

1. **Shoulder-width grip**: Not narrower
2. **Elbows tucked**: Close to body
3. **Full lockout**: Squeeze triceps at top
4. **Lower to lower chest**: Natural bar path
5. **Control the negative**: 2-3 seconds down
6. **Squeeze at lockout**: Brief hold
7. **Mind-muscle connection**: Feel triceps working
8. **Don't bounce**: Controlled touch

## Programming Recommendations

**For Tricep Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
- Moderate to heavy weight
- Full ROM
- 2 times per week

**For Strength:**
- 4-5 sets of 4-6 reps
- Heavy weight
- Full rest between sets
- Progressive overload

**In a Push/Tricep Workout:**
Primary or secondary pressing:
1. Regular bench press (if doing both)
2. **Close-grip bench press**
3. Tricep isolation (pushdowns, skull crushers)
4. Shoulder/chest accessories

**As Primary Pressing:**
1. **Close-grip bench press** (main lift)
2. Incline pressing
3. Isolation work

## Close-Grip Bench vs Tricep Isolation

| Factor | Close-Grip Bench | Tricep Pushdowns |
|--------|------------------|------------------|
| Movement type | Compound | Isolation |
| Loading | Heavy | Moderate |
| Muscles worked | Tri, chest, delts | Triceps only |
| Strength building | Excellent | Moderate |
| Mass building | Excellent | Good |

**Use both:** Close-grip for heavy compound work; isolation for finishing.

## Benefits Over Isolation Exercises

### Heavy Loading

Close-grip bench allows:
- Heavier weights than pushdowns
- Progressive overload
- Compound stimulus
- Greater total muscle work

### Functional Pressing

Builds:
- Lockout strength for bench press
- Real-world pushing power
- Athletic application

### Time Efficiency

One exercise trains:
- Triceps (primary)
- Chest (secondary)
- Shoulders (secondary)

## Variations

| Variation | Equipment | Note |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| Barbell close-grip | Standard | Most common |
| Smith machine close-grip | Smith machine | More stability |
| Dumbbell close-grip | Dumbbells | Greater ROM |
| Floor press (close-grip) | Floor | Limited ROM, lockout focus |

### Floor Press Variation

Lying on floor, close grip:
- Reduced ROM
- Focuses on lockout
- Less shoulder stress
- Tricep emphasis maximized

## Who Benefits Most

**Great for:**
- Bench press lockout weakness
- Tricep development
- Those who want compound tricep work
- Powerlifters
- Anyone wanting big arms

**Also useful for:**
- Shoulder-friendly pressing option (often)
- Bench press accessory
- Strength athletes

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: Triceps (maximum), pectoralis major (high), anterior delts (high)
- **Secondary**: Serratus anterior, core
- **Compound tricep builder**: Heavy loading possible
- **Grip width**: Shoulder width—not narrower
- **Elbows tucked**: Closer to body than regular bench
- **Full lockout**: Essential for tricep work
- **Complements bench press**: Builds lockout strength
- **Heavy + isolation**: Use with pushdowns/extensions for complete triceps

The close-grip bench press is the best compound exercise for tricep development. The narrow grip shifts emphasis to the triceps while still training chest and shoulders. Use shoulder-width grip (not narrower), tuck your elbows, and lock out completely for maximum tricep gains.
