# What Muscles Do Hanging Knee Raises Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Hanging knee raises are a classic ab exercise performed while hanging from a pull-up bar. You raise your knees toward your chest, working both the hip flexors and abdominals. Understanding which muscles work—and how to emphasize each—helps you get the most from this movement.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Hip Flexors

**Activation Level: Very High**

The hip flexors are the primary movers—this is important to understand:

**Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus):**
- The strongest hip flexors
- Initiate and drive the knee raise
- Work through full range
- Active from the first degree of movement

**Rectus Femoris:**
- Part of the quadriceps
- Assists hip flexion
- Also works since knee is flexed

Many people feel hip flexors more than abs during hanging knee raises. This isn't wrong—it's how the exercise works.

### Rectus Abdominis

**Activation Level: Moderate to High (Technique-Dependent)**

The six-pack muscle works, but HOW you perform the movement matters:

**If you just raise knees:**
- Abs work isometrically (stabilizing)
- Hip flexors do most of the work
- Moderate ab activation

**If you add posterior pelvic tilt (curling pelvis up):**
- Abs work concentrically
- Spine actually flexes
- Much higher ab activation
- This is the key to targeting abs

### Obliques

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The side abs contribute:
- Stabilize the trunk
- Prevent rotation
- More active in twisting variations

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Forearms (Grip)

**Activation Level: High**

Your grip works hard:
- Supporting entire body weight
- Often becomes the limiting factor
- Forearm flexors engaged throughout
- Consider straps if grip fails before abs

### Latissimus Dorsi

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The lats help stabilize:
- Prevent excessive swinging
- More active in active hang position
- Engage to control the body

### Transverse Abdominis

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The deep core:
- Creates intra-abdominal pressure
- Stabilizes the spine
- Works isometrically throughout

### Brachialis and Biceps

**Activation Level: Low**

Elbow flexors maintain arm position:
- Slight isometric engagement
- Not a significant contributor

## The Hip Flexor vs Ab Question

This is the most important concept for hanging knee raises:

**The movement itself (raising knees) is hip flexion—performed by hip flexors.**

**To engage abs significantly, you must add spinal flexion (pelvic tilt/curl).**

| Technique | Primary Muscles | Ab Activation |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Raise knees to 90° only | Hip flexors | Low-Moderate |
| Raise knees + curl pelvis up | Hip flexors + Abs | High |
| Raise knees + round lower back | Hip flexors + Abs | Very High |

**The cue:** "Bring your pelvis toward your ribcage" not just "raise your knees."

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Hanging Position (Start)
- Grip engaged, supporting body weight
- Core slightly braced
- Legs hanging straight or slight knee bend
- Controlled position (no swinging)

### Knee Raise (Hip Flexion)
- Hip flexors contract concentrically
- Knees drive toward chest
- Core stabilizes
- Minimal swinging

### Pelvic Curl (If Included)
- Rectus abdominis contracts concentrically
- Pelvis tilts posteriorly (tailbone tucks)
- Lower back rounds slightly
- This is where abs really work

### Lowering Phase
- Hip flexors work eccentrically
- Controlled descent
- Core maintains stability
- Full extension at bottom

## Common Mistakes

**Swinging/using momentum**: Kipping up with body swing. Use strict, controlled movement.

**Not curling the pelvis**: Just raising knees without pelvic tilt. Add the curl for abs.

**Going too fast**: Speed reduces tension. Slow and controlled wins.

**Incomplete range of motion**: Not bringing knees high enough. Full range for full activation.

**Grip failing early**: Letting grip limit the exercise. Use straps if needed.

**Hyperextending at bottom**: Arching back excessively. Maintain slight core tension throughout.

## How to Maximize Ab Activation

1. **Curl your pelvis**: The single most important cue
2. **Think "belly button to chin"**: Focus on spinal flexion
3. **Pause at top**: 1-2 second squeeze
4. **Control the descent**: Don't just drop
5. **Minimize swing**: Strict form over high reps
6. **Exhale as you raise**: Helps engage abs
7. **Bring knees past 90°**: Higher = more pelvic curl opportunity

## Variations and Their Emphasis

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Hanging knee raise (standard) | Balanced hip flexor + ab work |
| Hanging leg raise (straight leg) | Harder, more hip flexor demand |
| Hanging knee raise with twist | Adds oblique emphasis |
| Toes-to-bar | Maximum range, advanced |
| Captain's chair knee raise | Back supported, easier |
| Lying knee raise | Floor version, beginner-friendly |
| Weighted knee raise | Ankle weights or dumbbell between feet |

### Straight Leg vs Bent Knee

| Aspect | Knee Raise | Leg Raise |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| Difficulty | Easier | Harder |
| Hip flexor demand | High | Very High |
| Lever arm | Shorter | Longer |
| Ab activation | Depends on curl | Depends on curl |
| Recommended for | Most people | Those with strong hip flexors |

Both can effectively target abs if you add the pelvic curl.

### Captain's Chair Version

Using a captain's chair (vertical knee raise station):
- Back is supported
- Reduces grip demands
- Allows focus on core
- Good for beginners
- Same technique principles apply

## Hanging Knee Raises vs Other Ab Exercises

| Exercise | Ab Activation | Hip Flexor Role | Equipment |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Hanging knee raise | Moderate-High | High (primary mover) | Pull-up bar |
| Plank | Moderate | Low | Floor |
| Cable crunch | High | Low | Cable machine |
| Ab wheel rollout | Very High | Low | Ab wheel |
| Lying leg raise | Moderate-High | Very High | Floor |
| Bicycle crunch | Very High | Moderate | Floor |

Hanging knee raises have higher hip flexor involvement than most ab exercises—this isn't bad, just something to understand.

## Programming Recommendations

**For Core Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps
- Focus on pelvic curl
- Controlled tempo
- 2-3 times per week

**For Ab Hypertrophy:**
- 3 sets of 12-20 reps
- Add weight (ankle weights or dumbbell)
- Slow eccentrics (3-4 seconds down)
- Maximum contraction at top

**For Beginners:**
- Start with captain's chair version
- Or lying knee raises
- Build to hanging version
- Focus on pelvic tilt technique

**Integration:**
- After compound lifts
- Part of core circuit
- Can superset with planks

## Addressing Hip Flexor Fatigue

If your hip flexors tire before abs:

1. **Pre-exhaust abs**: Do crunches first, then hanging raises
2. **Use captain's chair**: Reduces some hip flexor demand
3. **Focus on shorter range**: Partial reps with emphasis on curl
4. **Train hip flexors**: They may just be weak
5. **Accept the reality**: Hip flexors work—strengthen them too

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary movers**: Hip flexors (very high), rectus abdominis (moderate-high with proper technique)
- **Secondary**: Grip/forearms, obliques, transverse abdominis
- **The key to ab activation**: Posterior pelvic tilt (curling pelvis up)
- **Common issue**: Hip flexor dominance—not a mistake, just the nature of the exercise
- **Add the curl**: "Pelvis to ribcage" is the money cue
- **Grip can limit**: Use straps if needed
- **Control beats speed**: Slow, strict reps with curl outperform fast swinging

Hanging knee raises are effective for abs when done correctly. The secret is adding spinal flexion through pelvic curl—without this, your hip flexors do most of the work.
