# How to Fix Deadlift Form: Common Mistakes and Corrections

The deadlift is beautifully simple—pick up the bar, put it down. But simple doesn't mean easy. Poor deadlift form limits your strength, destroys your back, and leaves pounds on the platform. Here's how to identify and fix the most common deadlift mistakes.

## The Ideal Deadlift

### Setup
- Bar over mid-foot (1-1.5 inches from shins)
- Feet hip-width apart
- Grip just outside legs
- Hips between knees and shoulders
- Back flat (neutral spine)
- Chest up, shoulders over or slightly in front of bar
- Arms straight, lats engaged

### Execution
- Push floor away (leg drive)
- Bar stays close to body
- Hips and shoulders rise together
- Lock out by squeezing glutes
- Controlled descent along same path

## Common Problems and Fixes

### Problem 1: Rounded Lower Back

**What it looks like:** Lower back curves into flexion during the lift. The most dangerous deadlift mistake.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak core/erectors
- Starting with hips too low (squat-lifting)
- Pulling before getting tight
- Weight too heavy
- Not knowing what neutral feels like

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Proud chest" or "Show your logo"

**Setup fix:**
1. Hinge at hips to grab bar (don't squat down)
2. Pull slack out of bar before lifting
3. Take a breath, brace HARD
4. Lift only when you feel tight

**Exercises:**
- Romanian deadlifts: 3x10 (teaches flat back hip hinge)
- Paused deadlifts: 2-3 sec pause 1 inch off floor
- Good mornings: 3x10
- Planks and dead bugs for core

**If it still rounds:** The weight is too heavy. Reduce and build back up.

### Problem 2: Rounded Upper Back

**What it looks like:** Thoracic spine curves, shoulders round forward.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak upper back
- Not engaging lats
- Bar drifting away from body
- Poor scapular position at setup

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Protect your armpits" or "Bend the bar around your legs"

**Setup:** Before pulling, engage lats by thinking about putting your shoulder blades in your back pockets.

**Exercises:**
- Barbell rows: 3x8-10
- Face pulls: 3x15
- Lat pulldowns: 3x10
- Paused deadlifts (forces back engagement)

### Problem 3: Bar Drifts Away from Body

**What it looks like:** Bar swings forward during the lift instead of traveling straight up.

**Why it happens:**
- Not engaging lats
- Starting with bar too far from shins
- Thinking "up" instead of "back"

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Drag the bar up your legs" or "Paint your shins with the bar"

**Setup:** Bar should start over mid-foot, touching or nearly touching your shins.

**Lat engagement:** Before you pull, think about bending the bar around your legs. This engages the lats and keeps the bar close.

**Exercises:**
- Snatch-grip deadlifts (forces lat engagement)
- Block pulls focusing on bar path
- Barbell rows

### Problem 4: Hips Rising Too Fast (Stiff-Leg Start)

**What it looks like:** Hips shoot up first, turning the deadlift into a stiff-leg deadlift. Back becomes nearly horizontal.

**Why it happens:**
- Starting with hips too low
- Weak quads (can't push)
- Not thinking about leg drive

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Push the floor away" or "Leg press the earth"

**Setup fix:** Your hips need to be in the right position—high enough that your shoulders are over the bar, low enough that you can use leg drive.

**Think of the deadlift as:**
- Phase 1: Leg press (until bar passes knees)
- Phase 2: Hip hinge (bar to lockout)

**Exercises:**
- Pause deadlifts (1" off floor)
- Deficit deadlifts (increases leg drive demands)
- Leg press

### Problem 5: Hips Too Low (Squatting the Deadlift)

**What it looks like:** Hips start very low, shoulders behind the bar, almost looks like a squat.

**Why it happens:**
- Trying to use more legs
- Copying Olympic lifters (different movement)
- Not understanding the hip hinge

**How to fix it:**

The deadlift is a hip hinge, not a squat. Hips should be higher than in a squat.

**Setup method:**
1. Stand with bar over mid-foot
2. Bend over and grab bar (don't squat)
3. Bend knees until shins touch bar
4. Lift chest—this is your position

**Your hips will be between your knees and shoulders.**

### Problem 6: Hitching/Ramping

**What it looks like:** Bar stops mid-thigh and you "hump" it up by thrusting and resting the bar on your thighs.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak lockout strength
- Lost lat tightness
- Upper back gave out
- Tried to lift too heavy

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Finish with your hips, not your back"

**At lockout:** Squeeze glutes to drive hips through. Don't hyperextend your back.

**Exercises:**
- Block pulls/rack pulls from knee height
- Romanian deadlifts
- Hip thrusts
- Barbell glute bridges

### Problem 7: Hyperextending at Lockout

**What it looks like:** Leaning back excessively at the top, overarching the lower back.

**Why it happens:**
- Thinking "get the shoulders back"
- Weak glutes (using back to finish)
- Competition habit (exaggerated lockout)

**How to fix it:**

**Correct lockout:** Stand tall, squeeze glutes, lock knees. That's it. No need to lean back.

**Cue:** "Stand tall" or "Squeeze glutes, not back"

### Problem 8: Mixed Grip Issues

**What it looks like:** Bar spins, bicep strain, or rotating torso with mixed grip.

**Why it happens:**
- Bicep of underhand arm is flexed (injury risk)
- Body rotates toward supinated (underhand) side

**How to fix it:**

**For bicep safety:** Keep both arms completely straight. Think "long arms."

**For rotation:** Focus on keeping shoulders square. Alternate which hand is supinated.

**Alternatives:**
- Hook grip (painful but effective)
- Straps for training (mixed grip for competition)
- Double overhand as long as possible

## The Setup Checklist

Use this every rep:

1. **Bar position:** Over mid-foot
2. **Stance:** Hip-width, toes slightly out
3. **Grip:** Just outside legs, crush the bar
4. **Hip height:** Hinge to bar, bend knees to shins touch
5. **Chest:** Lift it to flatten back
6. **Lats:** Engage by "bending the bar"
7. **Breath:** Big breath, brace core
8. **Slack:** Pull slack out of bar (until it clicks against the plates)
9. **Pull:** Push floor away, keep bar close

## Mobility for Better Deadlifts

### Hip Hinge Mobility
- Romanian deadlift stretch: 3x30 sec holds
- 90-90 hip stretch: 60 sec per side
- Hip flexor stretch: 60 sec per side

### Thoracic Mobility
- Foam roller extension: 2 minutes
- Cat-cow: 15 reps

### Hamstring Flexibility
- Standing toe touch: 30 sec
- Lying hamstring stretch: 30 sec per side

## Warm-Up Progression

1. **Hip hinges (no weight):** 10 reps
2. **Romanian deadlifts (light):** 10 reps
3. **Deadlifts (empty bar or light):** 5 reps
4. **Gradual loading:** Work up in 20-25% jumps

## When Form Breaks Down

**Off the floor:** Weak quads or poor positioning
**At the knees:** Bar too far from body or weak back
**At lockout:** Weak glutes or upper back fatigue
**Throughout:** Too heavy or fundamental technique issue

**Rule:** If form breaks, reduce weight. Never sacrifice form for load.

## The Bottom Line

Most deadlift problems come from:
1. Poor setup (wrong hip/shoulder position)
2. Not getting tight before pulling
3. Bar drifting away from body
4. Weak links (back, glutes, or legs)

The fix is almost always: slow down the setup, get tight, keep the bar close, and lift weights you can actually handle with good form.

Film yourself. Compare to good lifters. Be honest about your form.

The deadlift rewards patience and technique. Build the pattern first, then add weight. Your back will thank you, and your numbers will grow.
