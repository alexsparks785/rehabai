# Marathon Training Exercises: Strength Work for Distance Runners

Running 26.2 miles requires more than just running miles. Strength training improves running economy, delays fatigue, and dramatically reduces injury risk. Yet most recreational marathon runners skip it entirely.

**Key insight:** Research shows strength training improves running economy by 2-8%—meaning the same effort produces faster times. It's not about bulk; it's about resilience.

---

## Why Marathoners Need Strength Training

### The Problem with Running-Only Training

- **Muscle imbalances develop:** Running uses the same muscles in the same pattern
- **Supporting muscles weaken:** Glutes, hips, and core get neglected
- **Injury rates climb:** 50-70% of runners get injured each year
- **Form breaks down:** Late-race fatigue causes compensation patterns
- **Performance plateaus:** Speed can't improve without strength

### What Strength Training Does for Runners

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Improved economy | Better force production per stride |
| Injury prevention | Stronger tissues handle repetitive loading |
| Delayed fatigue | Muscles work at lower % of max capacity |
| Better form | Core and hip strength maintains posture |
| Faster finishing | Kick power when others fade |

---

## Key Areas for Marathon Runners

### Glutes

The engine of running. Weak glutes cause knee pain, IT band issues, and lower back problems.

### Hips (Abductors/Adductors)

Stabilize the pelvis. Weakness causes hip drop and knee cave.

### Core

Maintains posture when fatigued. Without it, form collapses.

### Single-Leg Strength

Running is a series of single-leg hops. Training should reflect this.

### Calves and Feet

Absorb impact and provide push-off. Often undertrained.

## Essential Exercises

### Glute Bridges

**Why it matters:** Activates glutes that are often dormant from sitting.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Drive through heels, lift hips
3. Squeeze glutes at top for 2 seconds
4. Lower with control
5. 3 sets of 15

**Progression:** Single-leg glute bridge

### Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

**Why it matters:** Builds posterior chain strength in the exact single-leg stance of running.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Hinge at hip, reaching opposite hand toward ground
3. Keep slight bend in standing leg
4. Return to standing
5. 3 sets of 10 each leg

**Key:** Keep back flat—don't round.

### Bulgarian Split Squat

**Why it matters:** Single-leg quad and glute strength for push-off power.

**How to do it:**
1. Rear foot elevated on bench or step
2. Lower until front thigh is parallel
3. Drive up through front heel
4. 3 sets of 8-10 each leg

### Clamshells

**Why it matters:** Targets glute medius for hip stability.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, knees bent 45°
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Don't let hips roll back
4. 3 sets of 15-20 each side

### Side-Lying Hip Abduction

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, legs straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. Keep toes pointing forward (not up)
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

### Step-Ups

**How to do it:**
1. Step up onto box or bench
2. Drive through top leg (don't push off bottom)
3. Control descent
4. 3 sets of 10 each leg

### Calf Raises (Single-Leg)

**Why it matters:** Calf strength for push-off and injury prevention.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on one leg on step
2. Lower heel below step level
3. Rise as high as possible
4. 3 sets of 15 each leg

**Key:** Full range of motion—both lowering and raising.

### Heel Walks

**Why it matters:** Strengthens tibialis anterior to prevent shin splints.

**How to do it:**
1. Walk on heels, toes pointed up
2. 30-40 yards
3. 2-3 sets

## Core Exercises for Runners

### Plank

**How to do it:**
1. Forearms on ground, body straight
2. Don't let hips sag or pike up
3. Hold 30-60 seconds
4. 3 sets

### Side Plank

**How to do it:**
1. Stack feet or stagger them
2. Lift hips, body in straight line
3. Hold 30 seconds each side
4. 3 sets

### Dead Bug

**Why it matters:** Trains core to stabilize while limbs move—like running.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees at 90°
2. Press lower back into floor
3. Lower opposite arm and leg
4. Return, switch sides
5. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Bird Dog

**How to do it:**
1. On all fours
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep back flat, hips level
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Pallof Press

**How to do it:**
1. Stand perpendicular to band/cable
2. Press arms straight out
3. Resist rotation
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Runner's Crunch

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back
2. Bring one knee toward chest
3. Simultaneously crunch opposite elbow toward knee
4. Alternate like running motion
5. 3 sets of 20 total

## Hip Mobility for Runners

### Hip Flexor Stretch (Half-Kneeling)

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on one knee
2. Tuck pelvis under
3. Shift hips forward
4. Hold 30-45 seconds each side

### 90/90 Hip Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with front leg at 90°, back leg at 90°
2. Lean forward over front shin
3. Hold 30-45 seconds each position

### Pigeon Pose

**How to do it:**
1. Front shin across body (as perpendicular as comfortable)
2. Back leg extended straight
3. Lean forward
4. Hold 45-60 seconds each side

### Leg Swings

**How to do it:**
1. Hold wall for balance
2. Swing leg forward and back (10 each)
3. Swing side to side (10 each)
4. Dynamic warm-up, not held stretches

## Sample Strength Programs

### Beginner (2x per week, 20-25 minutes)

**Do on non-running or easy running days**

**Workout A:**
- Glute bridges: 3x15
- Clamshells: 3x15 each
- Step-ups: 3x10 each
- Plank: 3x30 sec
- Hip flexor stretch: 30 sec each

**Workout B:**
- Single-leg glute bridge: 3x10 each
- Side-lying hip abduction: 3x15 each
- Bodyweight squats: 3x15
- Dead bug: 3x10 each
- Side plank: 3x20 sec each

### Intermediate (2-3x per week, 30-35 minutes)

**Workout A - Lower Body Focus:**
- Single-leg RDL: 3x10 each
- Bulgarian split squat: 3x8 each
- Calf raises (single-leg): 3x12 each
- Clamshells: 3x15 each
- Plank: 3x45 sec

**Workout B - Core and Stability:**
- Glute bridge (weighted): 3x12
- Step-ups: 3x10 each
- Side-lying hip abduction: 3x15 each
- Dead bug: 3x10 each
- Side plank: 3x30 sec each
- Pallof press: 3x10 each

**Workout C (optional) - Maintenance:**
- Single-leg squat to box: 3x8 each
- Single-leg calf raise: 3x12 each
- Bird dog: 3x10 each
- Heel walks: 2x30 yards

### Race Week Adjustments

**7 days out:** Normal strength workout
**5-6 days out:** Light session (50% volume, no heavy loads)
**3-4 days out:** Just activation (glute bridges, clamshells, planks)
**1-2 days out:** Rest or very light jog only

## Periodizing with Your Training

### Base Building Phase

- 2-3 strength sessions per week
- Focus on building strength foundation
- Can include heavier loads

### Build Phase (Race-Specific)

- 2 sessions per week
- Maintain strength, don't build new
- Reduce volume as running volume increases

### Peak Phase

- 1-2 lighter sessions per week
- Focus on activation and maintenance
- No heavy lifting 10-14 days before race

### Recovery Phase (Post-Race)

- Return to strength focus
- Rebuild areas that weakened during taper
- Address any imbalances that emerged

## Injury Prevention Specific Work

### For IT Band Issues

- Clamshells: 3x20 daily
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Foam roll (but don't overdo it)
- Single-leg work to address weakness

### For Shin Splints

- Heel walks: 2x30 yards daily
- Calf raises (full range): 3x15
- Toe taps: 30 seconds
- Address footwear and running surface

### For Runner's Knee

- Terminal knee extensions
- Step-downs (emphasize control)
- Quad/hip flexor stretching
- Single-leg strength to correct imbalances

### For Plantar Fasciitis

- Calf stretches (both straight and bent knee)
- Single-leg calf raises
- Arch strengthening (towel scrunches)
- Rolling bottom of foot

---

## Common Mistakes

### Training Errors

- **Skipping strength on hard running days:** Do it on easy days
- **Going too heavy:** Running performance is the priority
- **Neglecting single-leg work:** Running is single-leg; train that way
- **Doing strength right before quality runs:** Allow 6-8 hours minimum
- **Stopping during taper:** Maintain with reduced volume, don't eliminate

### Form Issues

- **Knees caving in on squats/lunges:** Use mirror or video, strengthen glute medius
- **Rounding back on deadlifts:** Reduce weight, focus on hip hinge
- **Breath holding:** Exhale on effort
- **Rushing through reps:** Control matters more than speed

---

## The Bottom Line

Strength training isn't optional for marathon success—it's essential for durability, economy, and finishing strong. Two 20-30 minute sessions per week is enough to see significant benefits.

**Non-negotiables:**
1. Glute work (bridges, clamshells) 2-3x per week
2. Single-leg exercises (RDL, split squat)
3. Core stability (plank, dead bug)
4. Hip mobility (stretching daily)
5. Calf strength (single-leg raises)

The runners who strength train get faster. The runners who don't get injured.
