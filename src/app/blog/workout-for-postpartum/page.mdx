# Workout for Postpartum: Safe Return to Exercise After Baby

Your body just did something incredible. Now it needs time to heal—and the right kind of movement to rebuild. Rushing back to intense workouts can set you back; a smart, gradual approach gets you stronger faster.

## When Can You Start Exercising?

The old "6-week clearance" rule is oversimplified. Here's a more realistic timeline:

**Days 1-14: Healing Focus**
- Gentle walking as tolerated
- Pelvic floor activation
- Deep breathing
- Rest and recovery

**Weeks 2-6: Foundation Building**
- Gradually increase walking
- Core reconnection exercises
- Light stretching
- Check for diastasis recti

**6+ Weeks: Gradual Progression**
- After provider clearance
- Start structured workouts
- Still prioritize pelvic floor and core
- Slowly add intensity

**C-Section Recovery**
If you had a C-section, add 2-4 weeks to this timeline. Your abdominal muscles and incision need extra healing time.

## First Priority: Reconnect Your Core

Pregnancy stretches your abdominal muscles and changes how they function. Before doing any "ab exercises," you need to re-establish the connection.

### Diaphragmatic Breathing

The foundation of postpartum core work:

1. Lie on your back with knees bent
2. Place hands on your ribcage
3. Inhale through nose, feeling ribs expand sideways
4. Exhale slowly, feeling ribs draw in
5. Practice 5 minutes, 2-3 times daily

### Core Connection Breath

Combines breathing with pelvic floor and deep core:

1. Inhale, relax pelvic floor and belly
2. Exhale, gently lift pelvic floor and draw belly in
3. Don't clench or strain—think 30% effort
4. Hold 5 seconds, then release
5. Repeat 10 times

### Heel Slides

Gentle core activation:

1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Exhale, engage core, slide one heel out
3. Keep lower back neutral (don't arch or flatten)
4. Inhale, slide heel back
5. Alternate sides, 10 each

## Check for Diastasis Recti

Many women have abdominal separation after pregnancy. Check before starting core exercises:

### How to Check

1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Place fingers at belly button
3. Lift head slightly (like a mini crunch)
4. Feel for a gap between your muscles
5. Check above and below belly button

**What You're Looking For**
- More than 2 finger-widths of separation
- Soft, weak feeling at the gap
- "Doming" when you lift your head

If you have significant separation, modify your workouts and consider pelvic floor physical therapy.

## Pelvic Floor Recovery

Pregnancy and birth affect your pelvic floor whether you delivered vaginally or by C-section.

### Kegels Done Right

1. Imagine picking up a blueberry with your vagina
2. Lift and squeeze gently (don't bear down)
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Fully release and relax
5. Repeat 10 times, 3 sets daily

### Signs Your Pelvic Floor Needs Help

- Leaking urine when you cough, sneeze, or jump
- Feeling of heaviness or bulging
- Pain during intercourse
- Difficulty controlling gas
- Needing to urinate urgently or frequently

If you have these symptoms, see a pelvic floor physical therapist.

## Early Postpartum Routine (Weeks 2-6)

Focus on healing, not fitness.

**Daily Walking**
- Start with 10-15 minutes
- Increase by 5 minutes each week
- Listen to your body—fatigue is normal

**Core Reconnection (Daily)**
- Diaphragmatic breathing: 5 minutes
- Core connection breath: 10 reps
- Heel slides: 10 each side
- Pelvic floor exercises: 3 sets × 10

**Gentle Stretching**
- Neck circles
- Cat-cow on hands and knees
- Hip flexor stretch (kneeling)
- Chest stretch at doorway

## 6-Week Workout Phase (Weeks 6-12)

After clearance from your provider, begin structured workouts.

### Sample Beginner Routine

**Warm-up (5 minutes)**
- Walk in place
- Hip circles
- Arm circles

**Main Workout (20 minutes)**

1. **Glute bridges**: 2 sets × 12 reps
   - Exhale and engage core as you lift

2. **Wall push-ups**: 2 sets × 10 reps
   - Keep core engaged throughout

3. **Bird dogs**: 2 sets × 8 each side
   - Move slowly, no rushing

4. **Squats to chair**: 2 sets × 10 reps
   - Sit back onto a chair or bench

5. **Side-lying leg lifts**: 2 sets × 12 each side
   - Rebuild hip strength

6. **Standing rows with band**: 2 sets × 12 reps
   - Great for posture

**Cool-down (5 minutes)**
- Gentle stretching
- Deep breathing

### Exercises to Avoid (For Now)

- Crunches and sit-ups
- Planks (unless core is reconnected)
- Running and jumping
- Heavy lifting
- Anything that causes doming or leaking

## Building Back Phase (Months 3-6)

Your core foundation is established. Time to progress.

### Sample Intermediate Routine

**Warm-up (5 minutes)**
- March in place
- Bodyweight squats
- Arm swings

**Main Workout (25-30 minutes)**

1. **Modified plank on knees**: 3 sets × 20 seconds
   - Only if no doming occurs

2. **Goblet squats**: 3 sets × 12 reps
   - Hold a light weight at chest

3. **Romanian deadlifts**: 3 sets × 10 reps
   - Light dumbbells to start

4. **Incline push-ups**: 2 sets × 10 reps
   - Hands on bench or stairs

5. **Single-leg glute bridges**: 2 sets × 10 each side
   - Progress from double-leg

6. **Pallof press with band**: 2 sets × 10 each side
   - Anti-rotation core work

7. **Bent-over rows**: 3 sets × 12 reps
   - Keep spine neutral

8. **Dead bugs**: 2 sets × 8 each side
   - Core control exercise

**Cool-down (5 minutes)**
- Full body stretching

## Returning to Running and High-Impact

Running puts significant stress on your pelvic floor. Don't rush it.

### Before You Run, Can You:

- Walk briskly for 30 minutes without fatigue?
- Do single-leg squats without pain or wobbling?
- Jump and land without leaking?
- Hold a plank for 30 seconds without doming?
- Run in place for 1 minute without symptoms?

### Return-to-Running Progression

**Week 1:** Walk 4 min, jog 1 min, repeat × 5

**Week 2:** Walk 3 min, jog 2 min, repeat × 5

**Week 3:** Walk 2 min, jog 3 min, repeat × 5

**Week 4:** Walk 1 min, jog 4 min, repeat × 5

**Week 5-6:** Continuous jogging, increasing distance

If you leak or feel pelvic pressure, step back and work on your foundation more.

## Working Out With Baby

Real talk: finding time and energy is the hardest part.

### Practical Tips

**Lower the bar.** 10 minutes counts. 15 minutes is great. Perfect is the enemy of done.

**Include baby.** Some exercises work with baby on your chest or in a carrier.

**Use nap time wisely.** Sometimes that's for rest, sometimes for workouts. Both are valid.

**Accept interruptions.** Your workout might get cut short. That's okay.

### Baby-Friendly Exercises

- Squats while holding baby
- Walking with stroller or carrier
- Floor exercises while baby has tummy time
- Dancing with baby in your arms

## Sample Weekly Schedule

**Monday:** Core + lower body (20 min)

**Tuesday:** Walk with stroller (30 min)

**Wednesday:** Rest or gentle stretching

**Thursday:** Full body strength (25 min)

**Friday:** Walk with stroller (30 min)

**Saturday:** Core focus + mobility (15 min)

**Sunday:** Active rest or family activity

## Red Flags to Watch For

Stop exercising and consult your provider if you experience:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking a pad in an hour)
- Increased lochia (postpartum bleeding)
- Pain that worsens with exercise
- Feeling of organs "falling out"
- Abdominal bulging or doming that won't improve
- Persistent leaking despite modifications

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Doing too much too soon.** Your body needs time. Progress takes months, not weeks.

**Ignoring your pelvic floor.** It's not glamorous, but it's essential.

**Comparing to others.** Everyone's recovery is different.

**Skipping the foundation.** Core reconnection prevents problems later.

**"Bouncing back."** Reject this language. Your body changed. That's okay.

## When to See a Pelvic Floor PT

Consider seeing a specialist if you have:

- Any incontinence (even just with sneezing)
- Pelvic pain or pressure
- Painful intercourse
- Diastasis recti that's not improving
- Difficulty feeling your pelvic floor muscles
- Just want to make sure you're healing well

Many pelvic floor PTs can see you at 6 weeks postpartum—you don't have to wait for a problem.

## The Long View

Full postpartum recovery takes 12-18 months, not 6 weeks. Be patient with yourself.

Focus on building a strong foundation now. It will serve you better than any quick-fix program. Your body did something amazing—give it the time and care it deserves to come back strong.
