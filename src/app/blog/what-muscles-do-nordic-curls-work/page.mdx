# What Muscles Do Nordic Curls Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Nordic curls—also called Nordic hamstring curls or NHCs—are widely considered the single most effective exercise for building hamstring strength and preventing hamstring injuries. They've become a staple in professional sports, physical therapy, and strength training programs worldwide.

But what makes them so special? Understanding the anatomy will show you exactly why this exercise deserves its reputation.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Hamstrings (Maximum Activation)

The hamstrings are THE target of Nordic curls, and no other exercise loads them quite like this:

**Biceps femoris (long head):** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Maximum)
- Takes the highest demand during the eccentric lowering phase
- This is the most commonly injured hamstring muscle
- Nordic curls specifically target this injury-prone muscle

**Semitendinosus:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Maximum)
- Works maximally throughout the movement
- Important for knee flexion and hip extension together

**Semimembranosus:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Maximum)
- Deep hamstring muscle that's often undertrained
- Gets full activation during Nordic curls

**Why the hamstring activation is unique:**
Nordic curls work your hamstrings at **long muscle lengths** under **high loads** during the **eccentric (lowering) phase**. This combination creates a training stimulus that other exercises simply cannot replicate.

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Gastrocnemius (Calves) - High Activation

The calf muscles cross the knee joint and assist with knee flexion:
- Help control the lowering phase
- Activation increases as hamstrings fatigue
- Your heels being anchored loads the calves isometrically

### Glutes - Moderate Activation

The gluteus maximus assists with hip extension:
- Helps maintain hip position throughout
- Prevents hip flexion during the movement
- Works to keep your body in a straight line

### Core Muscles - High Activation

Your entire core works isometrically:
- **Rectus abdominis:** Prevents excessive hip flexion
- **Erector spinae:** Maintains spinal position
- **Obliques:** Prevents rotation

### Gracilis - Moderate Activation

This inner thigh muscle also crosses the knee:
- Assists with knee flexion
- Provides additional stability

## The Science: Why Nordic Curls Are Special

### Eccentric Overload

During Nordic curls, the lowering phase puts your hamstrings under extreme eccentric load. Research shows this eccentric emphasis:
- Increases fascicle length (longer muscle fibers)
- Builds strength at longer muscle lengths
- Reduces injury risk by up to 51% (systematic review data)

### The Hamstring Injury Problem

Hamstring strains typically occur when:
- The muscle is lengthening (eccentric contraction)
- During high-speed movements (sprinting)
- At long muscle lengths (hip flexed, knee extended)

Nordic curls train exactly these conditions in a controlled environment.

Nordic curls win on all three factors that matter most for hamstring health.

## How to Perform Nordic Curls

### Setup
1. Kneel on a pad with ankles secured (partner, bar, or strap)
2. Keep your body in a straight line from knees to head
3. Arms ready to catch yourself

### Execution (Beginner Approach)
1. **Lower slowly:** Take 3-5 seconds to lower your body forward
2. **Maintain straight line:** Don't bend at the hips
3. **Catch yourself:** Push off the ground to return up
4. **Use assistance:** Band, partner, or push-up to return

### Key Technique Points
- **Control the eccentric:** The lowering phase IS the exercise
- **No hip hinge:** Body stays straight, only knee angle changes
- **Full range when possible:** Work toward going all the way down
- **Quality over quantity:** 3-5 good reps beats 10 sloppy ones

## Progressive Approach

Nordic curls are extremely challenging. Here's how to build up:

### Level 1: Eccentric Only (Weeks 1-4)
- Lower as slowly as possible
- Push off floor to return
- 3 sets of 3-5 reps

### Level 2: Assisted Concentrics (Weeks 4-8)
- Use a band attached overhead
- Or partner pushing on your back
- 3 sets of 5-6 reps

### Level 3: Partial Range (Weeks 8-12)
- Lower to halfway point
- Pull yourself back up
- 3 sets of 5-8 reps

### Level 4: Full Nordic Curls (Weeks 12+)
- Complete the full movement
- Control down and up
- 3 sets of 3-6 reps

### Level 5: Loaded Nordics (Advanced)
- Hold weight at chest
- Very advanced—most people never need this

## Common Mistakes

### Hip Hinge ("Cheating")
- **Problem:** Bending at hips instead of controlling with hamstrings
- **Fix:** Squeeze glutes and maintain straight line

### Going Too Fast
- **Problem:** Dropping quickly misses the training stimulus
- **Fix:** Minimum 3-second lowering phase

### Too Much Volume Too Soon
- **Problem:** Extreme DOMS or hamstring cramping
- **Fix:** Start with just 2 sets of 3-4 reps

### Skipping Progression Steps
- **Problem:** Attempting full Nordics before ready
- **Fix:** Earn each progression level over weeks

## Who Should Do Nordic Curls?

### Highly Recommended For:
- Athletes in running sports (soccer, football, track)
- Anyone with history of hamstring strains
- Sprinters and field sport athletes
- General strength training enthusiasts

### Use Caution If:
- Currently recovering from hamstring injury (progress carefully)
- Severe hamstring tightness (address mobility first)
- Knee pain when kneeling

### May Need Modifications If:
- Can't kneel comfortably (use padding)
- No way to anchor feet (get a Nordic curl strap or use gym equipment)

## Programming Recommendations

### For Injury Prevention
- 2x per week, 3-4 sets of 5-6 reps
- Start during preseason
- Maintain year-round

### For Strength Building
- 2x per week
- Progress load/difficulty over time
- Complement with hip-dominant work (RDLs)

### For Rehabilitation
- Start only when cleared by PT
- Begin with isometric hamstring work first
- Progress very gradually through eccentric phases

## Equipment Options

### Partner-Assisted
- Partner holds ankles firmly
- Classic and effective

### Nordic Curl Strap
- Attaches to doorframe or rack
- ~$30-50, worth the investment

### Loaded Barbell
- Kneel behind loaded bar
- Heels under bar
- Needs enough weight to hold you

### Nordic Curl Bench (GHD)
- Purpose-built equipment
- Found in many gyms

### DIY: Couch or Bed
- Feet under heavy furniture
- Pad your knees well

## The Research

Nordic curls have more research supporting their effectiveness than almost any other hamstring exercise:

- **51% reduction** in hamstring injuries (large meta-analysis)
- Used by **professional soccer teams worldwide** as standard protocol
- **FIFA 11+** program includes Nordic curls
- Effective even with **just 1x per week** frequency

## Key Takeaways

1. **Nordic curls target all three hamstring muscles** at maximum intensity
2. **Eccentric emphasis and long-length loading** create unique training stimulus
3. **Proven to reduce hamstring injuries** significantly
4. **Progress gradually**—this exercise is very demanding
5. **2x per week** is sufficient for most people
6. **Quality over quantity**—control matters more than reps

## The Bottom Line

Nordic curls are the closest thing to a "must-do" exercise for anyone who runs, sprints, or plays sports involving high-speed movement. The combination of eccentric overload at long muscle lengths creates a training stimulus that other hamstring exercises simply cannot match.

If you're not doing Nordic curls, your hamstring training is incomplete—period.

Start with assisted variations, progress gradually, and your hamstrings will thank you with fewer injuries and more strength.
