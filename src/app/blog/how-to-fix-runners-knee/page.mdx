      <h1>How to Fix Runner's Knee: Exercises for Patellofemoral Pain</h1>

      <p>That nagging ache around your kneecap that flares up during running, squatting, or climbing stairs? That's runner's knee—one of the most common knee complaints. Despite the name, it affects more than just runners. Here's how to fix it.</p>

      <h2>What Is Runner's Knee?</h2>

      <p>Runner's knee (patellofemoral pain syndrome or PFPS) refers to pain around or behind the kneecap. It's caused by irritation where the kneecap meets the thigh bone, often due to the kneecap not tracking properly in its groove.</p>

      <p><strong>Symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Dull, aching pain around or behind the kneecap</li>
        <li>Pain worsens with stairs (especially going down)</li>
        <li>Pain with squatting, kneeling, or prolonged sitting</li>
        <li>Pain during or after running</li>
        <li>Grinding or popping sensation</li>
        <li>Stiffness after sitting ("movie theater sign")</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What Causes Runner's Knee?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Weak quadriceps:</strong> Especially the VMO (vastus medialis oblique)</li>
        <li><strong>Weak hips:</strong> Poor control lets the knee collapse inward</li>
        <li><strong>Tight muscles:</strong> IT band, hamstrings, calves, quads</li>
        <li><strong>Overuse:</strong> Too much running or activity too soon</li>
        <li><strong>Poor running form:</strong> Overstriding, excessive heel striking</li>
        <li><strong>Flat feet or overpronation:</strong> Alters knee mechanics</li>
        <li><strong>Muscle imbalances:</strong> Quad dominance over hamstrings/glutes</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Real Fix: Strengthen Hips AND Knees</h2>

      <p>Research consistently shows that hip strengthening is as important—maybe more important—than quad strengthening for runner's knee. Weak hips allow the knee to collapse inward, putting stress on the kneecap.</p>

      <h2>Hip Strengthening Exercises</h2>

      <h4>1. Clamshells</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side, knees bent at 45 degrees. Keep feet together and lift your top knee toward the ceiling. Don't let hips roll back. Add a resistance band for more challenge.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Side-Lying Hip Abduction</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side, lift your top leg toward the ceiling with toe pointed slightly down (not up). Keep hips stacked. This targets glute medius.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Lateral Band Walks</h4>
      <p>Band around ankles or above knees. Take side steps while maintaining band tension and keeping feet parallel. Don't let steps get small as you fatigue.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 steps each direction</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Single-Leg Glute Bridge</h4>
      <p>Lie on back, one foot flat, other leg extended. Bridge up keeping hips level. This builds glute strength crucial for knee stability.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift</h4>
      <p>Stand on one leg, hinge forward with control. Focus on keeping your standing knee tracking straight—not caving inward. This trains hip stability through the entire chain.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h2>Quad Strengthening (With Proper Form)</h2>

      <h4>6. Terminal Knee Extension (TKE)</h4>
      <p>Loop a band around a post at knee height and around the back of your knee. Step back to create tension. From a slightly bent position, straighten your knee against the band resistance. This specifically targets the VMO.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 each leg</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Wall Sits</h4>
      <p>Slide down a wall until thighs are parallel to floor (or as low as pain-free). Keep knees tracking over toes. Hold. This builds quad endurance isometrically.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 30-60 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Step-Downs (Controlled)</h4>
      <p>Stand on a step. Slowly lower your other foot toward the ground by bending the standing knee. Control the descent without letting your knee cave inward. This is the key exercise for runner's knee—go slow and focus on control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each leg</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Single-Leg Squat to Box</h4>
      <p>Stand in front of a box or chair. Slowly lower on one leg until you touch the box, then stand back up. Focus on keeping knee over second toe—no inward collapse. Lower the box height as you get stronger.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 8-10 each leg</strong></p>

      <h2>Stretching and Foam Rolling</h2>

      <h4>10. Quad Stretch</h4>
      <p>Standing or lying down, pull your heel toward your glute. Keep knees together. Tight quads can increase kneecap pressure.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>11. IT Band Foam Roll</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side with a roller under your outer thigh. Roll from hip to just above the knee. Focus on the glutes and TFL rather than the IT band itself.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Quad Foam Roll</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with roller under your thighs. Roll from hip to just above the knee. Hit different angles by rotating your legs.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>13. Calf Stretch</h4>
      <p>Wall calf stretch with both straight leg (gastrocnemius) and bent leg (soleus). Tight calves affect knee mechanics.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each position, each leg</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Daily (10 minutes)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Quad stretch: 30 seconds each side</li>
        <li>Calf stretches: 30 seconds each position</li>
        <li>Foam rolling (quads, IT band): 2-3 minutes</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Strength Sessions (3-4x/week)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Clamshells: 3x15 each side</li>
        <li>Side-lying abduction: 3x15 each side</li>
        <li>Lateral band walks: 3x10 each direction</li>
        <li>Single-leg glute bridge: 3x12 each</li>
        <li>Terminal knee extension: 3x15 each</li>
        <li>Step-downs: 3x10 each (focus on control)</li>
        <li>Wall sits: 3x30-60 seconds</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Activity Modification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Reduce running volume:</strong> Temporarily, while building strength</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid deep squats and lunges:</strong> If painful, reduce depth</li>
        <li><strong>Limit stairs:</strong> Take elevator when possible during acute phase</li>
        <li><strong>Don't sit too long:</strong> Get up and move every 30-60 minutes</li>
        <li><strong>Check shoes:</strong> Worn or wrong shoes worsen mechanics</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Running Form Tips</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Increase cadence by 5-10% (reduces overstriding)</li>
        <li>Land with knee slightly bent, foot under body</li>
        <li>Avoid letting knees collapse inward</li>
        <li>Consider gait analysis from a professional</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to Heal?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>2-4 weeks:</strong> Noticeable improvement with consistent work</li>
        <li><strong>6-8 weeks:</strong> Significant reduction in symptoms</li>
        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Most cases resolve</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance exercises prevent recurrence</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain is severe or sudden onset</li>
        <li>Knee locks, catches, or gives way</li>
        <li>Significant swelling</li>
        <li>No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise</li>
        <li>Pain at rest</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Runner's knee is frustrating but very treatable. The key insight: strengthen your hips as much as your knees. When your glutes control your leg position from above, your kneecap tracks properly and the pain resolves. Be patient, stay consistent with the exercises, and you'll get back to running pain-free.</p>
    </BlogLayout>
  )
