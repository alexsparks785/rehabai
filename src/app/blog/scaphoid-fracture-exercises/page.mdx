# Scaphoid Fracture Exercises: Recovery After Wrist Bone Injury

The scaphoid is the most commonly fractured carpal bone, and rehabilitation is crucial for restoring wrist function. This guide covers exercises after your fracture has healed—whether treated in a cast or with surgery.

## Understanding the Scaphoid

The scaphoid is a small, boat-shaped bone in your wrist, critical for:
- Wrist stability
- Smooth motion between forearm and hand
- Load transmission through the wrist

**Location:** On the thumb side of the wrist, connecting the radius to the hand bones.

**Why scaphoid fractures are tricky:**
- Often missed initially (may look like sprain)
- Poor blood supply (risk of avascular necrosis)
- Long immobilization time (8-12 weeks)
- Significant stiffness after healing

## Healing Timelines

### Cast Treatment (Non-Displaced Fractures)
- Cast immobilization: 8-12+ weeks
- Exercises begin after cast removal
- Full recovery: 3-6 months

### Surgical Treatment
- For displaced fractures, non-unions, or by preference
- May begin exercises earlier (surgeon dependent)
- Protected early motion possible with secure fixation
- Full recovery: 4-6 months

**Critical:** Do NOT begin exercises until cleared by your doctor. Starting too early can displace the fracture or hardware.

## Phase 1: Early Mobility (After Cast Removal or Clearance)

### Finger Exercises (May Start While Casted)

**Finger Tendon Glides**
1. Start with fingers straight
2. Hook: bend fingertips (claw position)
3. Fist: make full fist
4. Flat fist: bend at middle joints, fingers straight
5. Straight: extend fully
6. 10 repetitions through sequence

**Finger Spreads**
1. Spread fingers wide
2. Bring together
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Thumb Opposition**
1. Touch thumb to each fingertip
2. Then to base of each finger
3. 2-3 rounds

### Wrist Range of Motion

**Wrist Flexion/Extension**
1. Support forearm on table
2. Let hand hang off edge
3. Gently bend wrist down (flexion)
4. Then up (extension)
5. 10-15 repetitions
6. Expect limited motion initially

**Radial/Ulnar Deviation**
1. Forearm on table, thumb up
2. Move wrist toward thumb (radial)
3. Move toward pinky (ulnar)
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Wrist Circles**
1. Small, controlled circles
2. Both directions
3. 10 each direction

### Forearm Rotation

**Supination/Pronation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate palm up (supination)
3. Rotate palm down (pronation)
4. 10-15 repetitions

**This motion is usually well-preserved** even after immobilization.

### What to Expect
- Stiffness is normal
- Motion may be 50% or less initially
- Don't force through pain
- Progress gradually over weeks

## Phase 2: Progressive Mobility (Weeks 2-6 Post-Cast)

### Stretching

**Wrist Flexion Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm up
2. Use other hand to gently pull fingers back
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3-5 times

**Wrist Extension Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm down
2. Gently press wrist down
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3-5 times

**Prayer Stretch**
1. Palms together in front of chest
2. Lower hands toward waist
3. Keep palms together
4. Hold 30 seconds

**Reverse Prayer Stretch**
1. Back of hands together
2. Raise hands toward chin
3. Hold 30 seconds

### Passive Range of Motion

Use your other hand to assist movement.

**Passive Wrist Flexion**
1. Use good hand to gently bend injured wrist
2. Gentle pressure at end range
3. Hold 10-15 seconds
4. Release slowly

**Passive Wrist Extension**
Same concept, assist into extension.

### Scar Mobilization (If Surgical)

If you had surgery:
1. Massage scar after incision fully healed (usually 2-3 weeks)
2. Circular motions with finger
3. Lift and roll the scar
4. 2-3 minutes daily
5. Prevents adhesions

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 4-8 Post-Cast)

### Isometric Exercises

No joint movement—builds strength safely.

**Isometric Wrist Flexion**
1. Place palm under edge of table
2. Push up gently
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Wrist Extension**
1. Place back of hand under table
2. Push up
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Deviation**
1. Side of hand against wall/table
2. Push sideways
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Both directions

### Grip Strengthening

**Soft Ball/Putty Squeeze**
1. Squeeze therapy putty or stress ball
2. Hold 3-5 seconds
3. Release
4. 2-3 minutes
5. Start with soft resistance

**Finger Pinches**
1. Pinch putty between thumb and each finger
2. 10 pinches per finger

### Light Resistance Exercises

**Wrist Curls**
1. Very light weight (1 lb or soup can)
2. Forearm on table, wrist at edge
3. Curl wrist up (flexion), lower slowly
4. 2 sets of 15

**Reverse Wrist Curls**
1. Palm down
2. Lift wrist up (extension)
3. 2 sets of 15

**Radial Deviation with Weight**
1. Hold weight, thumb up (like hammer)
2. Lift by deviating toward thumb
3. 2 sets of 15

## Phase 4: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 8-12+)

### Increased Resistance

**Progress weights gradually:**
- Increase by 0.5-1 lb as comfortable
- Never work through pain
- Maintain good form

**Resistance Band Exercises**
1. Band around hand or wrist
2. Flexion/extension against resistance
3. Forearm rotation against resistance
4. 2-3 sets of 15

### Functional Grip

**Hand Gripper**
1. Standard hand gripper
2. Full squeeze, controlled release
3. 2-3 sets of 10-15

**Towel Wringing**
1. Wring out wet towel
2. Both directions
3. Great for grip and forearm

**Jar Opening Practice**
1. Practice opening and closing jars
2. Start with loose lids
3. Progress to tighter

## Phase 5: Return to Function (Months 3-6)

### Weight-Bearing Progression

**Level 1:** Partial weight through wrist (tabletop position)
**Level 2:** Quadruped position (hands and knees)
**Level 3:** Push-ups on knees
**Level 4:** Full push-ups
**Level 5:** Dynamic weight-bearing

### Sport-Specific Return

**For contact sports:**
- May need protective splint
- Gradual return to practice
- Full contact when cleared

**For racquet sports:**
- Grip strengthening first
- Practice swings
- Light rallies
- Full return gradually

**For weightlifting:**
- Start with machines/cables
- Progress to free weights
- May need wrist wraps initially
- Avoid heavy loads until 6+ months

## Sample Daily Program

### Week 1 Post-Cast
**3 times daily:**
1. Finger tendon glides: 10 reps
2. Wrist ROM: 10 reps each direction
3. Forearm rotation: 10 reps
4. Ice if swollen: 15 min

### Week 3-4 Post-Cast
**2 times daily:**
1. Stretches: 30 sec each
2. Active ROM: 15 reps each
3. Isometric exercises: 10 reps each
4. Light grip work: 2 min

### Week 6-8 Post-Cast
**Once daily:**
1. Stretches: 5 min
2. Wrist curls: 2x15
3. Reverse curls: 2x15
4. Grip exercises: 5 min
5. Band exercises: 2x15

### Week 10+ Post-Cast
1. Continued strengthening 3-4x/week
2. Weight-bearing progression
3. Sport-specific training
4. Gradual return to activities

## Monitoring Your Progress

### Track Your Range of Motion
- Wrist flexion: Normal ~80 degrees
- Wrist extension: Normal ~70 degrees
- Ulnar deviation: Normal ~30 degrees
- Radial deviation: Normal ~20 degrees

**Expect 80-90% of normal range** after healing.

### Signs of Good Progress
- Increasing motion each week
- Decreasing stiffness
- Better grip strength
- Less pain with activity

### Warning Signs
- Increasing pain
- New clicking or catching
- Persistent swelling
- Loss of motion

## Common Challenges

### Persistent Stiffness
- Continue stretching and ROM
- May need physical therapy
- Can take 6+ months to maximize

### Weakness
- Be patient with strengthening
- Grip may take longest to recover
- Continue exercises for months

### Pain with Activity
- Modify activities
- May need longer recovery
- Discuss with surgeon

### Non-Union or Delayed Union
- Diagnosed by imaging
- May need additional treatment
- Surgery if bone not healing

## Working with Healthcare Providers

### Physical/Occupational Therapist
Consider referral for:
- Joint mobilization
- Individualized progression
- Modalities (heat, ultrasound)
- Functional training

### Follow-Up with Surgeon
- Typically at 6 weeks, 3 months, 6 months
- X-rays to confirm healing
- Clearance for activities

## Key Takeaways

1. **Wait for clearance** — don't start exercises until bone has healed
2. **Expect stiffness** — motion recovery takes weeks to months
3. **Progress gradually** — isometrics before resistance
4. **Grip takes time** — may be the slowest to recover
5. **Stretch consistently** — multiple times daily initially
6. **Monitor for problems** — pain, clicking, or loss of motion
7. **Be patient** — full recovery takes 4-6 months
8. **Protect during return** — especially for contact sports

Scaphoid fractures require patience, but with consistent rehabilitation, most people regain excellent wrist function. Follow your surgeon's guidance, stay consistent with exercises, and progress systematically.
