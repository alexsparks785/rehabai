# Jumper's Knee Exercises: Complete Patellar Tendinopathy Rehab

Jumper's knee (patellar tendinopathy) is one of the most stubborn sports injuries, affecting athletes in jumping sports like basketball, volleyball, and track and field. The good news: with the right approach, most cases respond to progressive loading exercises without surgery.

## Understanding Jumper's Knee

Patellar tendinopathy occurs when the patellar tendon—connecting your kneecap to your shinbone—becomes overloaded and degenerates. Unlike acute inflammation, this is a failed healing response in the tendon tissue itself.

**Key insight:** This isn't primarily an inflammation problem (despite the old name "patellar tendinitis"). Anti-inflammatories and rest alone won't fix it. The tendon needs progressive loading to remodel and strengthen.

**Where it hurts:**
- Bottom of the kneecap (most common)
- Less commonly, top of the shinbone
- Pain is localized—you can point to it with one finger

**What makes it worse:**
- Jumping and landing
- Squatting (especially deep squats)
- Going downstairs
- Prolonged sitting with bent knees (movie sign)
- Running, especially hills

## Self-Assessment: Staging Your Injury

Understanding your current stage helps guide treatment intensity.

### Reactive Stage (Acute overload)
- Sudden onset after spike in activity
- Significant pain and stiffness
- May have some swelling
- **Focus:** Load reduction, pain management, isometrics

### Dysrepair Stage (Failing to heal)
- Symptoms present for several weeks
- Pain with activity, eases with warm-up
- Tendon may appear thickened
- **Focus:** Progressive loading, eccentric exercises

### Degenerative Stage (Chronic)
- Months to years of symptoms
- Tendon structurally changed
- May still respond to loading
- **Focus:** Heavy slow resistance, patience

## Phase 1: Pain Management and Isometrics

Start here if you're in significant pain (>5/10 during daily activities).

### Isometric Leg Extensions
Research shows isometrics provide immediate pain relief and begin tendon loading.

**The Gold Standard Protocol:**
1. Sit on a table or use a leg extension machine
2. Extend knee to about 60 degrees (not fully straight)
3. Hold against resistance for 45 seconds
4. Rest 2 minutes between sets
5. Perform 5 sets total

**Load:** As heavy as you can while maintaining the hold. Pain during should be minimal (<3/10).

**Frequency:** 2-3 times daily during acute phase

**Why 60 degrees?** This angle places optimal load on the patellar tendon.

### Wall Sit Isometrics
Alternative if you don't have equipment.

**How to do it:**
1. Slide down wall until knees are at 90 degrees
2. Hold position for 45 seconds
3. Rest 2 minutes
4. Repeat 5 times

**Progression:** Add weight (hold dumbbells or weighted vest)

### Ice vs. Heat
- **After activity:** Ice for 15-20 minutes
- **Before exercise:** Brief warm-up preferred over ice
- **Avoid:** Ice before jumping sports (numbs pain signals)

## Phase 2: Eccentric Loading (Weeks 2-6)

Eccentric exercises (lowering against resistance) are the cornerstone of patellar tendinopathy rehab.

### Decline Single-Leg Squats
This is the most studied and effective exercise for jumper's knee.

**Equipment needed:** 25-degree decline board (can improvise with wedge or incline)

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on decline board, injured leg only
2. Slowly squat down to 90 degrees (3-4 seconds lowering)
3. Use other leg to help push back up (minimize load on the way up)
4. Keep trunk upright—don't lean forward

**The Alfredson Protocol:**
- 3 sets of 15 repetitions
- Twice daily
- 7 days per week
- For 12 weeks

**Pain guidelines:**
- Pain up to 5/10 during exercise is acceptable
- Pain should resolve within 24 hours
- If next-day pain is worse, reduce load or volume

### Decline Board Alternatives
If you don't have a decline board:
- Stack books or use a weight plate under heels
- Smith machine with heels elevated
- Hack squat machine with feet forward on platform

### Spanish Squat
Targets the patellar tendon with sustained loading.

**Equipment:** Resistance band anchored at knee height

**How to do it:**
1. Loop band behind knees, facing anchor point
2. Lean back into the band
3. Squat down until thighs parallel
4. Hold for 45 seconds
5. Rest, repeat 3-4 times

**Key point:** The band pulls your shins forward, increasing patellar tendon load.

## Phase 3: Heavy Slow Resistance (Weeks 6-12)

Research shows heavy slow resistance (HSR) is as effective as eccentrics and may be more practical for athletes.

### Leg Press (Heavy and Slow)
**How to do it:**
1. Position feet shoulder-width on platform
2. Lower weight for 3 full seconds
3. Pause 2 seconds at bottom (90 degrees knee bend)
4. Push up for 3 seconds
5. Pause at top

**Loading progression:**
- Week 1-2: 15 RM (can do 15 reps, but just barely)
- Week 3-4: 12 RM
- Week 5-6: 10 RM
- Week 7-8: 8 RM
- Week 9-12: 6 RM

**Frequency:** 3 times per week (not daily like eccentrics)

### Leg Extension (Heavy and Slow)
Same tempo as leg press: 3 seconds down, 2 second pause, 3 seconds up.

**Range of motion:** 90 to full extension. Pain typically occurs at specific angles—work around these initially.

### Hack Squat
Allows heavy loading with reduced balance demands.

**Same protocol as leg press:** Progressive loading from 15 RM to 6 RM over 12 weeks.

## Phase 4: Energy Storage Exercises (Weeks 8-12+)

Before returning to jumping, the tendon must learn to handle rapid stretch-shortening cycles.

### Jump Preparation
Only begin when:
- Pain during daily activities <2/10
- Single-leg decline squat pain <3/10
- Completed at least 6 weeks of progressive loading

### Forward Step-Downs
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on a step (4-6 inches)
2. Step down slowly with opposite leg
3. Barely touch heel to ground
4. Return to start
5. Progress height gradually

### Double-Leg Jump and Stick
**How to do it:**
1. Small two-footed jump (few inches)
2. Land softly and freeze ("stick the landing")
3. Hold 3 seconds
4. Reset and repeat
5. Progress height gradually

### Pogo Jumps
Low amplitude, quick jumps focusing on stiffness.

**How to do it:**
1. Jump using ankles only (minimal knee bend)
2. Quick ground contact
3. Land and immediately rebound
4. Start 10-15 reps, progress to 30+

### Box Jumps (Start Low)
**Progression:**
1. Start with very low box (6-8 inches)
2. Step down after landing (don't jump down)
3. Gradually increase height
4. Eventually add jump down (increased demand)

## Sport-Specific Progression

### Running
**Criteria to start:**
- Pain-free decline squats
- Completed energy storage phase
- Pain during step-downs <2/10

**Progression:**
- Walk-jog intervals initially
- Flat surfaces first, hills later
- Increase speed before distance

### Jumping Sports (Basketball, Volleyball)
**Progression:**
1. Bilateral jumping, low intensity
2. Bilateral jumping, moderate intensity
3. Unilateral hops and bounds
4. Sport-specific movements at low intensity
5. Full practice (with management)
6. Return to competition

**Key principle:** Increase one variable at a time (height, intensity, volume, sport specificity).

## Managing Training Load

### The "In-Season" Athlete Problem
Complete rest isn't always possible for competitive athletes.

**Load management strategies:**
- Reduce jumping volume (quality over quantity)
- Isometrics before practice for pain relief
- Modified practice (limited jumping drills)
- Strategic rest days around competition
- Supplement training with heavy slow resistance

### Monitoring Pain
Use the 24-hour rule: if pain is worse the day after activity, you did too much.

**Acceptable pain during exercise:** 0-5/10
**Pain after exercise:** Should settle to baseline within 24 hours
**Pain trend:** Should gradually decrease over weeks

### Training Load Spikes
Most patellar tendinopathy starts with sudden increases in load:
- Tournament weekend
- Preseason intensity spike
- Return from vacation
- New training program

**Prevention:** Progress jumping volume by no more than 10-15% per week.

## Adjunct Treatments

### Massage and Soft Tissue Work
Beneficial for:
- Quad tightness (can increase patellar tendon load)
- ITB tightness
- Calf tightness

**Avoid:** Aggressive massage directly on the tendon (can irritate).

### Patellar Tendon Straps
**The evidence:** Mixed, but many athletes report reduced pain during activity.

**How they work:** May distribute load across a wider area or alter pain signaling.

**Use for:** Sports activities, not 24/7.

### Extracorporeal Shockwave Therapy (ESWT)
Some evidence for chronic cases not responding to exercise.

### PRP Injections
Research is mixed. May help chronic cases, but loading exercises are still required.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Complete Rest
Rest weakens tendons. Relative rest (avoiding aggravating activities while maintaining controlled loading) is the approach.

### 2. Stretching the Tendon
Aggressive quad stretching can compress the tendon against the kneecap. Gentle stretching is fine; avoid extreme stretches.

### 3. Returning Too Soon
Feeling better doesn't mean the tendon has remodeled. Full rehabilitation takes 3-6 months minimum.

### 4. Inconsistent Loading
Tendons respond to consistent stimulus. Missing sessions or varying load randomly impairs adaptation.

### 5. Ignoring the Kinetic Chain
Hip weakness, ankle stiffness, and core deficits all affect knee loading. Address the whole chain.

## Additional Exercises for the Kinetic Chain

### Hip Strengthening
Weak hips increase knee valgus (inward collapse) during landing.

**Key exercises:**
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Clamshells with band
- Single-leg Romanian deadlift
- Hip thrusts

### Calf Strengthening
The calf contributes significantly to jump absorption.

**Key exercises:**
- Single-leg calf raises
- Bent-knee (soleus) calf raises
- Pogos and hops (in later phases)

### Core Stability
Trunk control affects landing mechanics.

**Key exercises:**
- Dead bug
- Pallof press
- Single-leg balance activities

## Timeline Expectations

**Weeks 1-2:** Pain management, isometrics, possible pain increase as you begin loading
**Weeks 2-6:** Eccentric loading, gradual pain reduction during daily activities
**Weeks 6-12:** Heavy slow resistance, able to do more activities
**Weeks 12-16:** Energy storage introduction, sport-specific progression
**Weeks 16-24:** Return to full sport (varies based on sport demands)

**Key point:** Even after symptoms resolve, continue maintenance loading to prevent recurrence.

## Maintenance Program

After full return to sport:
- Heavy slow resistance 1-2x per week
- Monitor symptoms closely during high-load periods
- Back off at first sign of flare-up
- Annual progression management (especially preseason)

## When to Seek Help

- Pain persisting beyond 3-4 months of quality rehab
- Unable to make any progress with loading
- Significant swelling or warmth (may indicate different diagnosis)
- Complete tendon rupture (sudden pop, inability to extend knee—emergency)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Load the tendon progressively** — rest doesn't heal tendons
2. **Decline squats are gold standard** — eccentric loading on a decline board
3. **Heavy slow resistance works** — alternative to eccentrics with good evidence
4. **Manage pain with isometrics** — 45-second holds provide immediate relief
5. **Progress to jumping gradually** — energy storage before sport-specific
6. **Address the whole chain** — hips, core, and calves matter
7. **Be patient** — 3-6 months minimum for full rehabilitation
8. **Maintain forever** — tendons need ongoing stimulus to stay healthy

Jumper's knee is frustrating but treatable. With consistent loading, patience, and gradual progression back to sport, most athletes return to full activity—often with a stronger, more resilient tendon than before.
