# Wrist Sprain Exercises: Recover from Ligament Injury

A wrist sprain—stretching or tearing of the ligaments—is a common injury from falls, sports, or accidents. The right exercises restore mobility, rebuild strength, and help you return to normal activities safely.

## Understanding Wrist Sprains

Wrist sprains occur when ligaments connecting the wrist bones are stretched beyond their normal range, often from:
- Falling on an outstretched hand (FOOSH injury)
- Sports impacts
- Twisting injuries
- Repetitive stress

**Sprain grades:**
- **Grade I (Mild):** Ligament stretched but intact. Pain and mild swelling.
- **Grade II (Moderate):** Partial tear. Moderate pain, swelling, some instability.
- **Grade III (Severe):** Complete tear. Significant pain, swelling, instability.

**Common ligaments injured:**
- Scapholunate ligament (most common significant injury)
- Lunotriquetral ligament
- TFCC (triangular fibrocartilage complex)
- Various smaller wrist ligaments

## When to Seek Medical Attention

**See a doctor if:**
- Severe pain or inability to move wrist
- Significant swelling
- Visible deformity
- Numbness or tingling in hand
- Pain persists beyond 2 weeks
- Suspected fracture (fall on wrist)
- Clicking, catching, or instability

**Important:** Grade III sprains and certain ligament injuries may require surgical repair.

## Phase 1: Acute Phase (Days 1-7)

### RICE Protocol
- **Rest:** Avoid activities that cause pain
- **Ice:** 15-20 minutes, several times daily
- **Compression:** Elastic wrap or brace
- **Elevation:** Above heart level when possible

### Gentle Range of Motion

**Start early** with gentle movement to prevent stiffness.

**Wrist Circles**
1. Support forearm on table
2. Let hand hang off edge
3. Make small circles with wrist
4. 10 circles each direction
5. Pain-free movement only

**Finger Movements**
1. Open and close fist gently
2. Spread fingers wide
3. Touch thumb to each fingertip
4. Keeps tendons gliding

**Forearm Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Turn palm up, then palm down
3. 10-15 repetitions
4. If this hurts wrist, skip temporarily

### Splinting
May need splint or brace initially:
- Protects healing ligament
- Don't wear 24/7 long-term—remove for exercises
- Follow provider recommendations

## Phase 2: Early Mobility (Weeks 1-3)

### Range of Motion Exercises

**Wrist Flexion and Extension**
1. Rest forearm on table, wrist at edge
2. Bend wrist down (flexion)
3. Return to neutral
4. Bend wrist up (extension)
5. 15-20 repetitions

**Radial and Ulnar Deviation**
1. Forearm supported, thumb up
2. Move wrist toward thumb (radial deviation)
3. Move wrist toward pinky (ulnar deviation)
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Active Pronation/Supination**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate forearm palm up (supination)
3. Rotate palm down (pronation)
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Stretching

**Wrist Flexor Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm up
2. Use other hand to gently pull fingers back
3. Feel stretch in forearm and wrist
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 3 times

**Wrist Extensor Stretch**
1. Extend arm, palm down
2. Use other hand to gently push wrist down
3. Feel stretch on top of forearm
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Repeat 3 times

**Prayer Stretch**
1. Press palms together in front of chest
2. Lower hands toward waist
3. Keep palms together
4. Feel stretch in wrists
5. Hold 30 seconds

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 3-6)

### Isometric Exercises

No joint movement—builds strength safely.

**Isometric Flexion**
1. Place palm under table
2. Push up gently (as if flexing wrist)
3. Table prevents movement
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Extension**
1. Place back of hand under table
2. Push up (as if extending wrist)
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Deviation**
1. Side of hand against wall
2. Push toward wall (thumb side for radial, pinky side for ulnar)
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions each direction

### Progressive Strengthening

**Wrist Curls (Flexion)**
1. Forearm on table, palm up, wrist at edge
2. Hold light weight (1-2 lbs or can of soup)
3. Lower weight by extending wrist
4. Curl weight up by flexing wrist
5. 2-3 sets of 15 repetitions

**Reverse Wrist Curls (Extension)**
1. Forearm on table, palm down
2. Hold light weight
3. Raise weight by extending wrist
4. Lower slowly
5. 2-3 sets of 15 repetitions

**Radial Deviation with Weight**
1. Hold weight, thumb up (like holding hammer)
2. Lift weight by deviating wrist toward thumb
3. Lower slowly
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Ulnar Deviation with Weight**
Same position, but rotate so pinky is up, lift by deviating toward pinky.

### Forearm Strengthening

**Pronation/Supination with Weight**
1. Hold weight (or hammer) with elbow at side
2. Rotate forearm palm up, then palm down
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Towel Wringing**
1. Hold towel with both hands
2. Wring as if squeezing out water
3. Twist in both directions
4. 2-3 sets of 10 twists each direction

## Phase 4: Grip and Function (Weeks 4-8)

### Grip Strengthening

**Tennis Ball Squeeze**
1. Hold tennis ball in hand
2. Squeeze firmly
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. Release
5. 15-20 repetitions

**Stress Ball/Putty**
1. Squeeze therapy putty or stress ball
2. Work for 2-3 minutes
3. Progress to firmer putty

**Hand Gripper**
1. Squeeze hand gripper
2. Hold 3-5 seconds
3. 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps

### Finger Strengthening

**Finger Spreads**
1. Place rubber band around all fingers
2. Spread fingers apart against resistance
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Finger Pinches**
1. Pinch therapy putty between thumb and each finger
2. 10 pinches per finger

### Functional Exercises

**Coin Flips**
1. Hold coin between fingers
2. Flip to different fingers
3. Works fine motor control

**Weight Bearing**
1. Start with partial weight on hands (tabletop position)
2. Progress to push-up position holds
3. Then wall push-ups, elevated push-ups
4. Only when pain-free

## Phase 5: Return to Activities (Weeks 6-12)

### Sport-Specific Training

**For racquet sports:**
1. Practice grip and swing motions without ball
2. Progress to light hitting
3. Gradually increase intensity

**For weightlifting:**
1. Start with bodyweight or very light weights
2. Increase gradually
3. Use wrist wraps if helpful

**For contact sports:**
1. Consider protective taping or bracing
2. Gradual return to practice
3. Full participation when strength restored

### Criteria for Full Return
- Full range of motion
- 80-90% strength compared to other side
- No pain with activity
- Able to complete sport-specific tasks

## Taping and Bracing

### When to Use
- During activity return
- For instability
- Added confidence
- Protection during healing

### Types
- **Athletic tape:** For support during sports
- **Wrist brace:** For daily support
- **Wrist wrap:** For weightlifting

### How Long
- May need for weeks to months during activities
- Gradually wean as strength improves
- Some athletes use preventively long-term

## Exercises to Avoid Initially

**During acute phase:**
- Weight-bearing on hand
- Push-ups
- Heavy gripping
- Impact activities
- Twisting motions

**Reintroduce gradually** as healing progresses.

## Sample Daily Program

### Week 1-2 (Acute)
- Ice: 15-20 min, 3-4 times daily
- Gentle ROM: Circles, finger motion
- Wear splint as directed

### Week 3-4 (Mobility)
**Morning:**
1. ROM exercises: 15 reps each direction
2. Stretches: 30 sec each, 3 times
3. Isometric exercises: 10 reps each

**Evening:**
1. ROM exercises: 15 reps
2. Stretches: 30 sec each
3. Begin light strengthening if cleared

### Week 5-6 (Strengthening)
**Morning:**
1. Stretches: 5 min
2. Wrist curls: 2x15
3. Reverse curls: 2x15
4. Grip exercises: 2 min

**Evening:**
1. Stretches: 5 min
2. Deviation exercises: 2x15
3. Forearm rotation: 2x15
4. Functional exercises: 5-10 min

### Week 7+ (Return to Function)
- Continue strengthening 3-4x per week
- Sport-specific training
- Gradual return to activities

## Preventing Re-Injury

- Continue strengthening exercises
- Warm up before activity
- Use wrist support if needed
- Avoid overuse
- Address technique issues (sports, work)
- Strengthen whole upper extremity

## When Recovery Isn't Progressing

**Seek evaluation if:**
- Pain persists beyond 4-6 weeks
- Clicking, popping, or instability
- Weakness not improving
- Unable to perform daily activities

**May indicate:**
- More severe ligament injury
- Missed fracture
- TFCC injury
- Cartilage damage

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start ROM early** — gentle motion prevents stiffness
2. **Progress through phases** — don't rush strengthening
3. **Isometrics first** — safe strength building
4. **Grip strength matters** — functional recovery
5. **Protect during return** — taping or bracing
6. **Gradual return to activities** — don't rush
7. **Persistent symptoms need evaluation** — may be more serious
8. **Prevention is key** — continue exercises long-term

Most wrist sprains heal well with appropriate rehabilitation. Be patient, progress systematically, and you'll return to full function—often stronger than before the injury.
