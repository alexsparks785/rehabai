      <p className="lead">
        What you eat before training affects your energy, performance, and how you feel during the workout. But pre-workout nutrition doesn't need to be complicated. Here's what actually matters—and what doesn't.
      </p>

      <h2>The Goals of Pre-Workout Nutrition</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Energy:</strong> Fuel for the work ahead</li>
        <li><strong>Performance:</strong> Avoid feeling sluggish or weak</li>
        <li><strong>Comfort:</strong> No nausea, cramping, or digestive distress</li>
        <li><strong>Muscle preservation:</strong> Amino acids available to reduce breakdown</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Timing: When to Eat</h2>

      <h3>2-3 Hours Before</h3>
      <p>
        A full meal can be eaten 2-3 hours before training. This gives time for digestion while ensuring nutrients are available.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Include protein, carbs, and moderate fat</li>
        <li>Regular meal-sized portion</li>
        <li>Example: Chicken with rice and vegetables</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>1-2 Hours Before</h3>
      <p>
        A smaller meal or large snack works well here. Reduce fat and fiber to speed digestion.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Focus on carbs and protein</li>
        <li>Lower fat and fiber</li>
        <li>Example: Greek yogurt with banana, or sandwich with lean protein</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>30-60 Minutes Before</h3>
      <p>
        Quick-digesting snack only. Keep it small and simple.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Simple carbs, maybe a small amount of protein</li>
        <li>Minimal fat and fiber</li>
        <li>Example: Banana, rice cakes, toast with honey</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Less Than 30 Minutes</h3>
      <p>
        If you're eating this close to training, keep it very light or skip it. Some people do fine; others feel sick.
      </p>

      <h2>What to Eat: Macronutrients</h2>

      <h3>Carbohydrates (Primary Fuel)</h3>
      <p>
        Carbs are your muscles' preferred fuel for intense exercise. For strength training and high-intensity work, carbs before training can improve performance.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Complex carbs (oats, rice, potatoes) for meals 2-3 hours out</li>
        <li>Simple carbs (fruit, bread, rice cakes) closer to training</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Protein (Muscle Support)</h3>
      <p>
        Pre-workout protein ensures amino acids are available during and after training. It's less critical if you ate protein a few hours earlier.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>20-40g protein in your pre-workout meal</li>
        <li>Can be part of a meal or a shake closer to training</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Fat (Limit Pre-Workout)</h3>
      <p>
        Fat slows digestion. While not harmful, high-fat meals close to training can cause discomfort. Save fatty foods for other times.
      </p>

      <h3>Fiber (Limit Pre-Workout)</h3>
      <p>
        High-fiber foods can cause bloating and GI distress during training. Keep fiber moderate in pre-workout meals.
      </p>

      <h2>Pre-Workout Meal Examples</h2>

      <h3>2-3 Hours Before</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Grilled chicken, rice, and steamed vegetables</li>
        <li>Lean beef with sweet potato</li>
        <li>Pasta with marinara sauce and ground turkey</li>
        <li>Eggs, toast, and fruit</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>1-2 Hours Before</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Greek yogurt with berries and granola</li>
        <li>Turkey sandwich on white bread</li>
        <li>Protein smoothie with banana and oats</li>
        <li>Cottage cheese with fruit</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>30-60 Minutes Before</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Banana</li>
        <li>Rice cakes with honey</li>
        <li>Handful of dried fruit</li>
        <li>White toast with jam</li>
        <li>Small protein shake</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Should You Train Fasted?</h2>

      <p>
        Training on an empty stomach (fasted training) is popular, especially for morning exercisers. Here's the reality:
      </p>

      <h3>Potential Benefits</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Convenience (no meal prep or digestion time)</li>
        <li>Some people feel lighter and more focused</li>
        <li>May enhance fat oxidation during exercise (though overall fat loss depends on total calories)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Potential Drawbacks</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Reduced performance, especially for intense or long sessions</li>
        <li>May increase muscle protein breakdown</li>
        <li>Low energy or lightheadedness</li>
        <li>Some people feel weak or nauseous</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>The Bottom Line on Fasted Training</h3>
      <p>
        For light to moderate training or short sessions, fasted training is fine if you tolerate it. For intense strength training or longer sessions, eating beforehand usually improves performance.
      </p>

      <p>
        Experiment and see how you feel. There's no universal answer.
      </p>

      <h2>Hydration</h2>

      <p>
        Don't forget fluids. Dehydration impairs performance more than lack of food.
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Drink water consistently throughout the day</li>
        <li>16-20 oz of water 2-3 hours before training</li>
        <li>8 oz about 30 minutes before</li>
        <li>Sip during training as needed</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Pre-Workout Supplements</h2>

      <p>
        Common pre-workout supplements and their value:
      </p>

      <h3>Caffeine</h3>
      <p>
        The most effective performance enhancer. 3-6 mg/kg body weight, 30-60 minutes before training. Can also come from coffee.
      </p>

      <h3>Creatine</h3>
      <p>
        Works, but timing doesn't matter much. Taking it pre-workout is fine, but post-workout or any time works equally well.
      </p>

      <h3>Pre-Workout Blends</h3>
      <p>
        Most benefits come from caffeine. Other ingredients may help marginally or not at all. Not necessary but not harmful if you enjoy them.
      </p>

      <h3>BCAAs</h3>
      <p>
        Unnecessary if you're eating adequate protein. Whole food protein contains BCAAs plus other amino acids.
      </p>

      <h2>Individual Variation</h2>

      <p>
        Pre-workout nutrition is highly individual. Some people perform great fasted; others need food. Some have iron stomachs; others get nauseous easily.
      </p>

      <p>
        Experiment to find what works for you:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Try different timing windows</li>
        <li>Test different foods</li>
        <li>Note how you feel and perform</li>
        <li>Adjust based on results</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Mistakes</h2>

      <h3>Eating Too Much Too Close</h3>
      <p>
        A large meal 30 minutes before training leads to discomfort, nausea, and sluggishness.
      </p>

      <h3>Eating Nothing for Long Workouts</h3>
      <p>
        Training for 90+ minutes on an empty stomach depletes glycogen and impairs performance.
      </p>

      <h3>Overthinking It</h3>
      <p>
        Pre-workout nutrition matters, but not as much as total daily nutrition. Don't stress over perfect timing.
      </p>

      <h3>High Fat/Fiber Close to Training</h3>
      <p>
        These slow digestion and can cause GI issues during exercise.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Eat a balanced meal 2-3 hours before training, or a lighter snack 1-2 hours before. Include carbs for energy and protein for muscle support. Stay hydrated. Experiment to find what works for your body.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>2-3 hours out: full meal with protein, carbs, moderate fat</li>
        <li>1-2 hours out: smaller meal, focus on carbs and protein</li>
        <li>30-60 minutes out: small, simple carbs only</li>
        <li>Limit fat and fiber close to training</li>
        <li>Fasted training is fine for some but may hurt performance</li>
        <li>Stay hydrated—this matters as much as food</li>
        <li>Individual response varies—experiment</li>
      </ul>
