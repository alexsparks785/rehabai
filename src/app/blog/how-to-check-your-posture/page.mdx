# How to Check Your Posture: Simple Self-Assessment Tests

You've been told to "stand up straight" your whole life, but what does good posture actually look like? Here's how to assess your own alignment and identify areas that need work.

## What Good Posture Actually Looks Like

Before checking for problems, understand the goal.

### Standing Posture (Side View)

Imagine a vertical line dropped from above. In ideal posture, this line passes through:

1. **Earlobe**
2. **Shoulder (middle)**
3. **Hip joint**
4. **Just behind the kneecap**
5. **Just in front of the ankle bone**

Your spine should have three natural curves:
- **Cervical (neck):** Slight inward curve
- **Thoracic (upper back):** Slight outward curve
- **Lumbar (lower back):** Slight inward curve

These curves are normal and necessary—the goal isn't a perfectly straight spine.

### Standing Posture (Front View)

- Head centered, not tilted
- Shoulders level (not one higher)
- Hips level
- Knees facing forward
- Feet parallel or slightly turned out

## The Wall Test

The simplest posture check you can do at home.

### How to Do It

1. Stand with your back against a wall
2. Heels about 6 inches from the wall
3. Let your buttocks and shoulder blades touch the wall
4. Look straight ahead (don't tilt your head back to touch the wall)

### What to Check

**Head position:**
- Can you touch the wall with the back of your head without tilting it back?
- If yes: Good
- If no (head is forward): Forward head posture

**Lower back:**
- Slide your hand between your lower back and wall
- You should fit your flat hand (about 1 inch gap)
- Too much space: Excessive lordosis (sway back)
- No space/back flat against wall: Reduced lordosis

**Shoulders:**
- Both shoulder blades should touch the wall equally
- If they don't touch easily: Rounded shoulders

### Common Findings

| Finding | What It Means |
|---------|---------------|
| Head doesn't touch wall | Forward head posture |
| Large gap at lower back | Excessive lumbar curve |
| Shoulders don't touch | Rounded shoulder posture |
| One side different | Asymmetry to address |

## The Mirror Test

Use a full-length mirror for front and side views.

### Front View Check

Stand naturally facing the mirror:

**Head:**
- Is it tilted to one side?
- Is it rotated (one ear more visible)?

**Shoulders:**
- Are they level?
- Is one more forward than the other?
- Are they rounded forward?

**Hips:**
- Are they level?
- Is one side higher?

**Knees:**
- Do they point straight ahead?
- Do they collapse inward?
- Are they hyperextended (locked back)?

**Feet:**
- Are arches present?
- Do feet turn out excessively?
- Is weight even on both feet?

### Side View Check

Turn sideways to the mirror:

**Head:**
- Is your ear in front of your shoulder?
- Is your chin poking forward?

**Upper back:**
- Excessive rounding (kyphosis)?
- Shoulders rolled forward?

**Lower back:**
- Excessive arch?
- Flat back?
- Pelvis tilted forward or back?

**Knees:**
- Hyperextended (bowing back)?
- Bent?

## The Photo Test

More objective than mirrors because you can't unconsciously adjust.

### How to Do It

1. Have someone take photos of you standing naturally
2. Get front, back, and both side views
3. Wear fitted clothing so alignment is visible
4. Stand how you normally would (don't pose)

### Analyzing Your Photos

Draw or imagine vertical and horizontal lines:
- Vertical: Should pass through ear, shoulder, hip, knee, ankle
- Horizontal: Shoulders and hips should be level

Compare left and right sides—asymmetries are common but worth noting.

## Specific Posture Tests

### Forward Head Posture Test

**The measurement:**
1. Stand sideways next to a wall
2. Back touching wall (shoulders and buttocks)
3. Measure distance from wall to back of head

**Results:**
- 0-1 inch: Normal
- 1-2 inches: Mild forward head
- 2+ inches: Significant forward head posture

### Shoulder Rounding Test

**Pencil test:**
1. Stand relaxed with arms at sides
2. Hold a pencil in each hand
3. Note where pencils point

**Results:**
- Pencils point forward: Rounded shoulders (internal rotation)
- Pencils point toward each other: Neutral (good)
- Pencils point outward: Externally rotated (less common)

### Pelvic Tilt Test

**Assessment:**
1. Stand sideways to mirror
2. Place one hand on front of hip bone
3. Place other hand on pubic bone
4. Note the angle between these points

**Results:**
- Hands roughly vertical: Neutral pelvis
- Top hand forward of bottom: Anterior pelvic tilt
- Top hand behind bottom: Posterior pelvic tilt

### Flat Back vs. Sway Back

**Lumbar curve check:**
1. Lie on your back on a hard floor
2. Try to slide your hand under your lower back

**Results:**
- Hand slides through with slight space: Normal
- Lots of space: Excessive lordosis
- No space (back flat to floor): Flat back/posterior tilt

## Dynamic Posture Checks

Static posture is only part of the picture. How you move matters too.

### Single Leg Balance

**Test:**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Observe in mirror or have someone watch

**What to look for:**
- Hip drop on non-standing side (Trendelenburg sign)
- Excessive trunk lean
- Knee collapsing inward
- Inability to maintain balance

**What it indicates:** Hip weakness, core stability issues

### Overhead Reach Test

**Test:**
1. Stand against wall (heels, buttocks, shoulders touching)
2. Raise both arms overhead trying to touch wall

**What to look for:**
- Can arms touch wall without arching back?
- Do ribs flare out?
- Does lower back come off wall?

**What it indicates:** Shoulder mobility, lat tightness, core control

### Squat Assessment

**Test:**
1. Perform a bodyweight squat
2. Observe or video from front and side

**What to look for:**
- Knees caving inward
- Heels rising
- Excessive forward lean
- Lower back rounding at bottom
- Asymmetrical depth

**What it indicates:** Ankle mobility, hip mobility, core stability, movement patterns

## Common Posture Patterns

### Upper Crossed Syndrome

**What it looks like:**
- Forward head
- Rounded shoulders
- Increased upper back curve

**What's tight:**
- Chest muscles
- Upper trapezius
- Levator scapulae
- Suboccipitals

**What's weak:**
- Deep neck flexors
- Lower trapezius
- Rhomboids
- Serratus anterior

### Lower Crossed Syndrome

**What it looks like:**
- Excessive lower back arch
- Protruding belly
- Anterior pelvic tilt

**What's tight:**
- Hip flexors
- Lower back extensors

**What's weak:**
- Abdominals
- Glutes

### Sway Back Posture

**What it looks like:**
- Hips pushed forward
- Upper body leaning back
- Flat lower back
- Forward head

**What's happening:**
- Passive hanging on ligaments
- Minimal muscle engagement
- Common in people who stand a lot

## What Your Posture Assessment Tells You

### Prioritize Based on Findings

Not all posture issues need immediate attention:

**High priority:**
- Issues causing pain
- Significant asymmetries
- Patterns affecting function

**Lower priority:**
- Minor deviations
- Asymptomatic variations
- Normal anatomical differences

### Normal Variation Exists

Perfect posture is a myth. Bodies vary:
- Spinal curves differ between individuals
- Some asymmetry is normal
- "Ideal" posture is a range, not a single position

The goal is functional, comfortable posture—not textbook perfection.

## Taking Action

### Based on Your Findings

**Forward head posture:**
- Chin tucks
- Neck flexor strengthening
- Upper back mobility

**Rounded shoulders:**
- Chest stretches
- Rows and reverse flies
- Scapular strengthening

**Anterior pelvic tilt:**
- Hip flexor stretches
- Glute strengthening
- Core work (especially anti-extension)

**Posterior pelvic tilt:**
- Hip flexor strengthening
- Lower back mobility
- Hamstring flexibility work

### General Recommendations

1. **Move more:** Static positions (even "good" ones) aren't ideal
2. **Strengthen what's weak:** Usually posterior chain and deep stabilizers
3. **Stretch what's tight:** Usually chest, hip flexors, and neck
4. **Set up your environment:** Ergonomic workspace matters
5. **Build awareness:** Check in with your posture regularly

## When to Seek Professional Help

See a physical therapist or qualified professional if:

- Posture issues cause pain
- Significant asymmetry or scoliosis concerns
- Posture is rapidly changing
- Self-correction isn't helping
- You're unsure how to address findings
- Pain accompanies movement assessments

## The Bottom Line

Checking your posture is simple:
1. **Wall test:** Basic alignment check
2. **Mirror/photo:** Visual assessment from multiple angles
3. **Specific tests:** Forward head, shoulder rotation, pelvic tilt
4. **Movement tests:** How you move under load

Most people have some deviation from "ideal"—that's normal. Focus on:
- What causes symptoms
- What limits function
- What's significantly asymmetrical

Posture isn't about perfection. It's about having the mobility and strength to be comfortable in many positions and to move well.

Check your posture, note your patterns, address what matters, and keep moving.
