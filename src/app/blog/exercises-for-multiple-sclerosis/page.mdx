# Exercises for Multiple Sclerosis: Stay Active Safely

Exercise was once thought to be harmful for people with multiple sclerosis. We now know the opposite is true—regular physical activity improves strength, balance, mood, and quality of life for most people with MS. The key is exercising smartly, managing fatigue, and avoiding overheating.

**Key insight:** Exercise doesn't worsen MS. While symptoms may temporarily increase with exertion or heat, this returns to baseline with rest and cooling. The long-term benefits outweigh short-term fluctuations.

*Work with your healthcare team to develop an exercise plan appropriate for your symptoms and abilities.*

---

## Understanding Exercise and MS

### Benefits of Exercise

- **Improved strength:** Counters weakness and muscle loss
- **Better balance:** Reduces fall risk
- **Increased endurance:** Helps manage fatigue over time
- **Enhanced mood:** Reduces depression and anxiety
- **Better cognition:** May help with MS-related cognitive issues
- **Improved function:** Daily activities become easier

### Challenges to Address

- **Fatigue:** The most common MS symptom
- **Heat sensitivity:** Symptoms worsen with elevated body temperature
- **Balance issues:** May affect exercise choices
- **Weakness:** Varies by individual and time
- **Spasticity:** Muscle tightness and spasms

---

## Managing Heat Sensitivity

Many people with MS experience symptom worsening when body temperature rises (Uhthoff's phenomenon). This is temporary but uncomfortable.

### Cooling Strategies

**Before Exercise:**
- Exercise in air conditioning
- Pre-cool with cold drink
- Wear cooling vest

**During Exercise:**
- Keep cool water nearby
- Use fans
- Take breaks in cool environment
- Avoid hot, humid conditions

**After Exercise:**
- Cool down completely
- Cold drink or cold towel
- Air conditioning

### Best Times to Exercise

- Early morning (cooler)
- Evening (after day's heat)
- In climate-controlled environments
- Avoid midday heat

### Water Exercise

Pool exercise is often ideal:
- Water cools the body
- Buoyancy supports weight
- Reduces impact on joints
- Temperature should be 80-84°F (not too warm)

---

## Managing Fatigue

### Types of Fatigue in MS

- **Primary fatigue:** Related to MS itself
- **Secondary fatigue:** From poor sleep, medication, deconditioning
- **Exercise-related fatigue:** Normal muscle tiredness

### Strategies for Exercise with Fatigue

**Timing:**
- Exercise when energy is highest (often morning)
- Avoid exercising when already fatigued
- Schedule rest before and after

**Pacing:**
- Shorter sessions may work better than long ones
- Take breaks as needed
- It's okay to stop early

**Energy Conservation:**
- Sit for exercises when possible
- Use support/assistive devices
- Focus on efficiency

**Building Endurance:**
- Start very gently
- Gradual progression
- Consistency helps reduce fatigue over time

## Stretching Exercises

Stretching helps manage spasticity and maintain flexibility.

### Lower Body

**Hamstring Stretch:**
1. Sit on bed or floor, one leg extended
2. Lean forward gently
3. Hold 30-60 seconds
4. Repeat other side

**Hip Flexor Stretch:**
1. Step into lunge position (hold support)
2. Shift weight forward gently
3. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

**Calf Stretch:**
1. Face wall, hands on wall
2. Step one foot back
3. Keep heel on floor, lean forward
4. Hold 30-60 seconds each side

**Inner Thigh Stretch:**
1. Sit, soles of feet together
2. Gently press knees toward floor
3. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Upper Body

**Chest Stretch:**
1. Arm on doorframe or wall
2. Turn body away
3. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Shoulder Stretch:**
1. Pull arm across body
2. Hold with other hand
3. Hold 30 seconds each side

**Neck Stretches:**
1. Tilt ear toward shoulder
2. Hold 20-30 seconds each side
3. Rotate gently each direction

## Strengthening Exercises

### Seated Exercises

**Seated Leg Extensions:**
1. Sit in chair
2. Straighten one knee
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps each leg

**Seated Marching:**
1. Sit tall
2. Lift one knee toward chest
3. Lower, switch legs
4. 2-3 minutes

**Seated Arm Raises:**
1. Hold light weights or none
2. Raise arms forward, then overhead
3. Lower with control
4. 10-15 reps

**Seated Rows:**
1. Resistance band anchored in front
2. Pull elbows back
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 10-15 reps

### Standing Exercises (With Support)

**Mini Squats:**
1. Hold support (chair, counter)
2. Bend knees slightly
3. Straighten
4. 10-15 reps

**Hip Abduction:**
1. Hold support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg

**Heel Raises:**
1. Hold support
2. Rise onto balls of feet
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 reps

**Standing Hip Flexion:**
1. Hold support
2. Lift knee toward chest
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each leg

### Floor Exercises

**Glute Bridges:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift hips toward ceiling
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10-15 reps

**Bird Dog:**
1. On hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 8-10 reps each side

**Side-Lying Leg Lifts:**
1. Lie on side
2. Lift top leg
3. Lower slowly
4. 10-15 reps each side

## Balance Exercises

Balance issues are common in MS. Practice with support nearby.

### Basic Balance

**Weight Shifts:**
1. Stand with support available
2. Shift weight side to side
3. Then forward and back
4. 10 shifts each direction

**Narrow Stance:**
1. Feet close together
2. Hold 30 seconds
3. Support nearby

**Tandem Stance:**
1. One foot in front of other
2. Hold 10-30 seconds
3. Switch feet

### Progressive Balance

**Single Leg (With Support):**
1. Hold support
2. Lift one foot slightly
3. Hold 10-30 seconds
4. Switch legs

**Reaching:**
1. Stand near support
2. Reach in various directions
3. Maintain balance

**Heel-to-Toe Walking:**
1. Walk in straight line
2. Heel touches toe
3. Support available nearby

## Cardiovascular Exercise

### Options Based on Ability

**Walking:**
- Indoors or outdoors (consider temperature)
- With assistive device if needed
- Start with 5-10 minutes

**Recumbent Bike:**
- Supports back
- Seated position
- Good for balance concerns

**Arm Ergometer:**
- Upper body cardio
- Seated
- Good if legs are more affected

**Swimming/Water Exercise:**
- Cooling effect
- Low impact
- Excellent for MS

**Seated Cardio:**
- Arm movements
- Leg movements
- Chair aerobics

### Guidelines

- Start with 5-10 minutes
- Stay cool
- Moderate intensity (can talk but not sing)
- Rest as needed
- Build gradually

## Sample Exercise Programs

### Beginner (Seated Focus)

**3 times per week, 15-20 minutes:**

**Warm-up (3 min):**
- Seated marching
- Arm circles
- Ankle pumps

**Strength (8-10 min):**
- Seated leg extensions: 10 each
- Seated arm raises: 10
- Seated rows: 10
- Seated marching: 2 min

**Stretching (5 min):**
- Hamstring stretch
- Shoulder stretch
- Neck stretches
- Chest stretch

### Intermediate (Combined)

**3-4 times per week, 25-30 minutes:**

**Cardio (10 min):**
- Walking or recumbent bike
- Stay cool

**Strength (10-12 min):**
- Mini squats: 12
- Hip abduction: 10 each
- Heel raises: 15
- Glute bridges: 12
- Seated rows: 12
- Arm raises: 12

**Balance (3-5 min):**
- Weight shifts
- Narrow stance
- Tandem stance

**Stretching (5 min):**
- Full body stretch routine

## During Relapses

### Adjust Your Approach

- Reduce or pause exercise during active relapse
- Focus on rest and recovery
- Gentle stretching may be okay if not worsening symptoms
- Resume gradually as relapse resolves

### Returning to Exercise

- Start below your previous level
- Progress slowly
- Don't rush back to full routine
- Listen to your body

---

## Working with Your Healthcare Team

### Who Can Help

- **Neurologist:** Medical management, exercise clearance
- **Physical therapist:** Develop appropriate program, address specific deficits
- **Occupational therapist:** Adaptations for daily activities
- **Exercise physiologist:** MS-informed fitness programming

### What to Communicate

- Current symptoms and limitations
- Goals for exercise
- Concerns about specific activities
- How exercise is affecting you

---

## The Bottom Line

Exercise is medicine for MS—it improves symptoms, function, and quality of life. The key is finding what works for your body, managing heat and fatigue, and being consistent while flexible.

**Exercise principles for MS:**
1. Stay cool—heat sensitivity is real but temporary
2. Manage fatigue—exercise when energy is best
3. Start gently—you can always add more
4. Include all components—strength, balance, flexibility, cardio
5. Be flexible—adjust for good days and bad days

Your body may work differently, but it still benefits from movement. Find your way to stay active.
