# Sports Hernia Exercises: Athletic Pubalgia Rehabilitation

A sports hernia (athletic pubalgia) causes chronic groin pain in athletes, particularly those who twist, turn, and kick. Unlike traditional hernias, there's no visible bulge—but the pain can be just as debilitating. Proper rehabilitation focuses on restoring core and hip function.

## Understanding Sports Hernia

Athletic pubalgia involves weakness or injury to the muscles and tendons of the lower abdominal wall and groin area, where they attach to the pubic bone.

**Affected structures:**
- Lower abdominal muscles (obliques, rectus abdominis)
- Adductor muscles (inner thigh)
- Pubic bone attachment sites
- Inguinal canal area

**Common in:**
- Soccer players
- Hockey players
- Football players
- Any sport with cutting, twisting, kicking

**Symptoms:**
- Deep groin or lower abdominal pain
- Pain with twisting, cutting, kicking
- Pain with sit-ups or crunches
- Pain that improves with rest, returns with activity
- May radiate to inner thigh
- Often unilateral (one side)

## Diagnosis Challenge

Sports hernias are often difficult to diagnose because:
- No visible or palpable bulge
- Imaging may be normal or inconclusive
- Symptoms overlap with other conditions
- Often requires specialist evaluation

**Other conditions to rule out:**
- Hip impingement (FAI)
- Adductor strain
- Osteitis pubis
- Inguinal hernia
- Hip labral tear

## Conservative vs. Surgical Treatment

**Conservative treatment (try first):**
- Rest and activity modification
- Physical therapy
- Core and hip strengthening
- Gradual return to sport

**Success rate:** 50-90% return to sport with conservative care

**Surgery considered when:**
- Failed 6-12 weeks of quality rehabilitation
- Unable to return to desired activity level
- Significant functional limitation

## Phase 1: Pain Management and Rest (Weeks 1-3)

### Relative Rest
- Avoid aggravating activities (twisting, cutting, kicking)
- Walking and light activity usually OK
- Complete rest not typically needed

### Gentle Mobility

**Hip Flexor Stretch** (Gentle)
1. Kneeling hip flexor stretch
2. Avoid deep stretch if painful
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3 times each side

**Gentle Adductor Stretch**
1. Seated, soles of feet together
2. Let knees fall outward gently
3. No forced stretching
4. Hold 30 seconds

**Supine Hip Rotation**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Let knees fall to one side
3. Return to center
4. 10 each side

### Early Core Activation

**Abdominal Drawing In**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Gently draw belly button toward spine
3. Don't flatten back completely
4. Hold 10 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

**Pelvic Floor Engagement**
1. Same position
2. Contract pelvic floor (like stopping urine)
3. Combine with abdominal drawing in
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 2: Core Stability (Weeks 3-6)

### Inner Core Strengthening

**Dead Bug** (Modified)
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees bent 90°
2. Maintain neutral spine
3. Lower opposite arm and leg slowly
4. Return and repeat other side
5. 10 reps each side, 2-3 sets

**Bird Dog**
1. Hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep spine neutral
4. 10 reps each side

**Quadruped Rocking**
1. Hands and knees
2. Rock back toward heels
3. Keep spine neutral throughout
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Anti-Movement Core Work

**Pallof Press**
1. Band anchored at side
2. Press band straight out from chest
3. Resist rotation
4. 10-15 reps each side

**Side Plank** (Modified)
1. On elbow and knees
2. Lift hips, create straight line
3. Hold 20-30 seconds
4. Progress to full side plank

**Plank**
1. Forearms and toes
2. Maintain straight line
3. Hold 30-60 seconds
4. Progress duration

### Hip Strengthening

**Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Lift top knee, keep feet together
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Bottom knee bent, top leg straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 6-10)

### Adductor Strengthening (Key for Sports Hernia)

**Isometric Adduction**
1. Lie on back, ball between knees
2. Squeeze ball
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Side-Lying Adduction**
1. Lie on affected side
2. Top leg supported on bench or pillow
3. Lift bottom leg toward ceiling
4. 3 sets of 15

**Copenhagen Adduction** (Progressive)
1. Side plank position
2. Top leg on bench
3. Lift bottom leg to meet top
4. Start with short holds
5. Progress to full exercise

### Abdominal Strengthening

**Curl-Up** (Modified McGill)
1. One knee bent, one straight
2. Hands under low back
3. Lift head and shoulders slightly
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10 repetitions each side

**Reverse Crunch**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift hips slightly off floor
3. Don't use momentum
4. 2-3 sets of 12-15

**Oblique Exercises**
1. Side-lying rotations
2. Pallof press variations
3. Side plank with rotation (later)

### Hip and Pelvis Integration

**Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
1. One leg extended
2. Bridge on single leg
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

**Hip Hikes**
1. Stand on step
2. Let one hip drop
3. Lift using opposite hip muscles
4. 2-3 sets of 15 each side

**Lateral Band Walks**
1. Band around ankles
2. Side-step with tension
3. 20 steps each direction

## Phase 4: Functional Training (Weeks 8-12)

### Movement Integration

**Lunge Variations**
1. Forward lunge
2. Reverse lunge
3. Lateral lunge
4. Progress to walking lunges
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Step-Ups**
1. Various heights
2. Drive through front leg
3. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Single-Leg Deadlift**
1. Hinge on one leg
2. Reach opposite leg back
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Rotational Core Work

**Medicine Ball Rotations**
1. Seated or standing
2. Rotate side to side
3. Start light
4. 2-3 sets of 15 each side

**Cable/Band Rotations**
1. Anchor at side
2. Rotate away from anchor
3. Control the movement
4. 2-3 sets of 12 each side

**Chops and Lifts**
1. High-to-low chops
2. Low-to-high lifts
3. Control rotation
4. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Plyometric Introduction

**Box Jumps** (Low)
1. Step down initially
2. Progress to jumping down
3. 2-3 sets of 8-10

**Lateral Bounds**
1. Small side-to-side jumps
2. Land softly
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

## Phase 5: Return to Sport (Weeks 10-16)

### Running Progression
1. Light jogging
2. Tempo runs
3. Acceleration drills
4. Direction changes
5. Full sprinting

### Cutting and Agility
1. Controlled direction changes
2. Progress speed
3. Sport-specific patterns
4. Full-speed cutting

### Kicking Progression
1. Easy passing
2. Moderate power
3. Full-power kicking
4. Match simulation

### Return Criteria
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Core strength restored
- No pain with sport-specific movements
- Passed progressive testing
- Completed full rehabilitation program

## Sport-Specific Considerations

### Soccer
- Focus on hip adductor strength
- Kicking mechanics
- Cutting and acceleration
- May take 3-4 months

### Hockey
- Skating mechanics
- Shot mechanics
- Direction changes
- May need longer recovery

### Football
- Cutting and pivoting
- Contact preparation
- Position-specific training

## Exercises to Avoid Initially

**Early rehabilitation (avoid):**
- Traditional sit-ups/crunches
- Full twisting movements
- Kicking/shooting
- Cutting and pivoting
- Heavy adductor loading

**Reintroduce gradually** as strength and pain allow.

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 7)

### Day 1: Core Focus
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Dead bug: 3×10 each side
3. Pallof press: 3×12 each side
4. Side plank: 3×30 sec each side
5. Bird dog: 3×10 each side
6. Curl-up: 3×10

### Day 2: Hip/Leg Strength
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Glute bridge: 3×15
3. Clamshells: 3×15 each
4. Isometric adduction: 3×15
5. Lateral band walks: 2×20
6. Step-ups: 3×12 each

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4: Integration
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Lunges: 3×10 each
3. Single-leg RDL: 3×10 each
4. Medicine ball rotations: 2×15 each side
5. Copenhagen progression: 2×10
6. Plank: 3×45 sec

## When Surgery is Needed

**Surgical indications:**
- Failed comprehensive rehabilitation (3-6 months)
- Unable to return to desired activity level
- Confirmed diagnosis by specialist

**Surgical options:**
- Open repair
- Laparoscopic repair
- Various techniques based on pathology

**Post-surgical rehabilitation:**
- Structured protocol
- Often similar progression to conservative care
- May return to sport 6-12 weeks post-surgery

## Key Takeaways

1. **Core stability is fundamental** — rebuild from the inside out
2. **Adductor strength is critical** — often weak or imbalanced
3. **Avoid early twisting and kicking** — reintroduce gradually
4. **Progress through phases** — don't skip steps
5. **Conservative care works for many** — give it 3-6 months
6. **Return to sport progressively** — follow the protocol
7. **Surgery is effective if needed** — when conservative fails
8. **Address contributing factors** — flexibility, mechanics, training errors

Sports hernia rehabilitation requires patience and progressive strengthening. With a comprehensive program, most athletes return to full activity—whether through conservative care or after surgery.
