# Shoulder Arthroscopy Exercises: Post-Operative Recovery Guide

Shoulder arthroscopy is a minimally invasive procedure using a small camera and instruments through tiny incisions. Recovery varies significantly based on what was done during the procedure—from a few weeks for simple procedures to several months for complex repairs.

## Procedures Performed Arthroscopically

**Simple procedures (faster recovery):**
- Diagnostic arthroscopy
- Debridement (cleanup)
- Loose body removal
- Bursectomy
- Minor labral debridement

**Complex procedures (longer recovery):**
- Rotator cuff repair
- Labral repair (Bankart, SLAP)
- Capsular release
- Subacromial decompression
- Distal clavicle excision

**Your protocol depends on what was done. This guide focuses on simpler procedures; see specific guides for major repairs.**

## Follow Your Surgeon's Protocol

**Recovery expectations vary dramatically:**
- Simple debridement: 2-4 weeks
- Subacromial decompression: 4-8 weeks
- Major repairs: 4-6 months

**Always follow YOUR surgeon's specific instructions.**

## Phase 1: Initial Recovery (Days 1-7)

### First Few Days
- Sling for comfort (duration varies by procedure)
- Ice: 15-20 minutes, several times daily
- Elevation when possible
- Pain medication as prescribed

### Early Movement (For Simple Procedures)

**Pendulum Exercises**
1. Lean forward, support yourself
2. Let arm hang and swing gently
3. Small circles and pendulum motions
4. 10-15 circles each direction
5. 3-4 times daily

**Elbow, Wrist, Hand Exercises**
1. Bend and straighten elbow fully
2. Wrist circles
3. Finger movements and grip
4. Keep distal joints mobile

**Passive Forward Elevation** (If Allowed)
1. Use good arm to lift surgical arm
2. Stay within prescribed range
3. 10-15 repetitions

### Wound Care
- Keep incisions clean and dry
- Watch for signs of infection
- Follow dressing change instructions

## Phase 2: Range of Motion (Weeks 1-3)

### Active-Assisted Exercises

**Pulley System**
1. Over-door pulley
2. Good arm helps lift surgical arm
3. 15-20 repetitions
4. Progress range as tolerated

**Wand Exercises**
1. Hold stick with both hands
2. Good arm guides surgical arm
3. Forward flexion, abduction, rotation
4. 15-20 repetitions each

**Wall Climb**
1. Face wall, fingers touching
2. "Walk" fingers up wall
3. Progress height gradually
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Active Range of Motion

**When Cleared (Often Week 1-2 for Simple Procedures):**

**Active Forward Flexion**
1. Lift arm forward under own power
2. Progress to full range
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active Abduction**
1. Lift arm out to side
2. Progress range
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active External/Internal Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Rotate forearm in and out
3. 15-20 repetitions

### Stretching

**Cross-Body Stretch**
1. Pull arm across chest
2. Use other hand to assist
3. Hold 30 seconds

**Sleeper Stretch** (If Allowed)
1. Lie on surgical side
2. Push forearm toward bed
3. Stretches posterior capsule
4. Hold 30 seconds

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 3-6)

**Begin when cleared by surgeon.**

### Rotator Cuff Exercises

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Small weight or no weight
4. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
5. 2-3 sets of 15

**Standing Band External Rotation**
1. Band at elbow height
2. Elbow at side
3. Rotate outward
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Standing Band Internal Rotation**
1. Same setup
2. Rotate inward
3. 2-3 sets of 15

### Scapular Strengthening

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Face down on bench
2. Y: Arms overhead, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze and lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Rows**
1. Band or light weight
2. Pull elbows back
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face
3. 2-3 sets of 15

### Deltoid Strengthening

**Lateral Raises**
1. Light weight
2. Lift arms to sides
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Front Raises**
1. Light weight
2. Lift arms forward
3. 2-3 sets of 15

## Phase 4: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 6-12)

### Increased Resistance

**Progress all exercises:**
- Increase weight gradually
- Maintain good form
- Monitor for pain

### Functional Exercises

**Wall Push-Ups**
1. Start against wall
2. Progress angle
3. Eventually to floor (if cleared)

**Chest Press**
1. Light resistance
2. Seated or standing
3. 2-3 sets of 12

**Lat Pulldown**
1. Light to moderate weight
2. 2-3 sets of 12

**Rows** (Heavier)
1. Progress weight
2. Various angles
3. 2-3 sets of 12

## Phase 5: Return to Activities

### Timeline (Varies by Procedure)

**Simple debridement:**
- Return to daily activities: 1-2 weeks
- Return to sports: 3-6 weeks

**Subacromial decompression:**
- Return to daily activities: 2-4 weeks
- Return to sports: 6-12 weeks

**More complex procedures:**
- See specific rehabilitation guides

### Criteria Before Full Return
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength adequate for demands
- No pain with activities
- Surgeon clearance

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 4, Simple Procedure)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, ROM exercises
2. Band ER/IR: 2×15 each
3. Side-lying ER: 2×15
4. Rows: 2×15
5. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 2
1. ROM exercises: 10 min
2. Prone Y-T-W: 2×10 each
3. Face pulls: 2×15
4. Light stretching

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: 5-10 min
2. All strengthening exercises
3. Progress resistance if tolerated
4. Stretching

### Day 5
1. Full ROM exercises
2. Scapular strengthening
3. Light cardio (walking, bike)
4. Stretching

## Managing Common Issues

### Stiffness
- Maintain ROM exercises daily
- Stretch consistently
- May need physical therapy

### Swelling
- Ice after activity
- Elevation
- Usually resolves within 1-2 weeks

### Portal Site Discomfort
- Small incisions may be tender
- Usually resolves quickly
- Avoid direct pressure

### Persistent Pain
- Some soreness is normal
- Sharp or worsening pain needs attention
- Contact surgeon if concerned

## When to Contact Your Surgeon

- Increasing pain beyond first few days
- Signs of infection (redness, warmth, drainage, fever)
- Significant swelling
- Numbness or weakness
- Any concerning symptoms

## Key Takeaways

1. **Recovery depends on procedure** — simple vs. complex
2. **Follow YOUR protocol** — individualized instructions
3. **Early motion prevents stiffness** — gentle movement
4. **Progress gradually** — don't rush strengthening
5. **Simple procedures recover fast** — weeks, not months
6. **Complex repairs take longer** — follow specific guides
7. **Monitor for problems** — communicate with surgeon
8. **Complete rehabilitation** — don't stop early

Shoulder arthroscopy recovery varies widely based on what was done. Simple procedures may have you back to normal in weeks, while complex repairs require months. Follow your surgeon's guidance and progress systematically through each phase.
