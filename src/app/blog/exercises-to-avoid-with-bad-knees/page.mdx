# Exercises to Avoid with Bad Knees (And What to Do Instead)

Having "bad knees" doesn't mean you can't exercise—but it does mean you need to be smart about which exercises you choose. The right exercises can actually help your knees by strengthening the muscles that support them. The wrong exercises can make things worse.

## Understanding Knee Issues

### Common Knee Conditions

Different conditions have different exercise considerations:

**Osteoarthritis:** Degenerative joint disease; often improves with appropriate exercise
**Patellofemoral pain (runner's knee):** Pain under/around kneecap; avoid deep knee flexion
**Meniscus issues:** Torn cartilage; avoid twisting, deep squats
**Ligament problems (ACL, MCL):** Instability; avoid cutting, pivoting
**Patellar tendinopathy:** Tendon pain; avoid jumping, deep squats initially

### General Principles

1. **Pain is your guide:** If it hurts during or after, modify or skip it
2. **Low-impact beats high-impact:** Less jarring is better for most knee issues
3. **Strength protects:** Muscles absorb force; stronger muscles = less knee stress
4. **Avoid extremes:** Deep flexion and full extension under load can be problematic
5. **Everyone is different:** What bothers one person's knees may be fine for another

## Exercises to Avoid

### 1. Deep Squats (Full Depth)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Maximum pressure on the kneecap at deep flexion
- Increased stress on meniscus
- Problematic for arthritis and patellofemoral pain

**Better alternatives:**
- **Box squat:** Sit back to a box set at comfortable depth
- **Wall sit:** Isometric hold at partial depth
- **Partial squat:** Only go to 45-60° knee bend
- **Leg press (limited range):** Control the depth

### 2. Full Lunges (Knee Past Toes/Deep)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Deep knee flexion at the bottom
- High load on the front knee
- Patella compressed against femur

**Better alternatives:**
- **Reverse lunge (partial):** Step back, don't go as deep
- **Split squat (limited depth):** Static position, control range
- **Step-up:** Less knee flexion, more glute/hip dominant
- **Bulgarian split squat (shallow):** Back foot elevated, partial range

### 3. Jumping and Plyometrics

**Why it's problematic:**
- High-impact landing forces
- Sudden loading of joint
- Risk of awkward landing

**Better alternatives:**
- **Swimming:** Zero impact cardio
- **Cycling:** Low impact, controlled motion
- **Step-ups:** Builds similar muscles without impact
- **Slow eccentric work:** Builds similar strength safely

### 4. Running (Especially on Hard Surfaces)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Repetitive impact loading
- 2-3x bodyweight force with each step
- Hard surfaces worse than soft

**Better alternatives:**
- **Walking:** Much lower impact
- **Elliptical:** Similar motion, no impact
- **Cycling:** Excellent knee-friendly cardio
- **Pool running:** All the motion, no impact
- **Rowing machine:** If it doesn't aggravate symptoms

### 5. Leg Extensions (Full Range)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Maximum shear force on patella at full extension
- Open chain exercise—all stress on the knee
- Controversial for rehabilitation

**Better alternatives:**
- **Terminal knee extension (TKE):** With band, limited range
- **Wall sit:** Isometric, closed chain
- **Step-down:** Eccentric quad work
- **Leg press (partial range):** Closed chain, controlled

### 6. Leg Curl Machine (Heavy)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Isolated hamstring loading can stress the knee
- Often performed with poor form
- May aggravate certain conditions

**Better alternatives:**
- **Glute bridge:** Works hamstrings with hip extension
- **Romanian deadlift (RDL):** Hip-dominant hamstring work
- **Slider hamstring curl:** Bodyweight, controlled
- **Nordic curl (if tolerated):** Eccentric focus

### 7. High-Impact Aerobics/Dance

**Why it's problematic:**
- Repeated jumping and landing
- Quick direction changes
- Cumulative impact stress

**Better alternatives:**
- **Low-impact aerobics:** Keep one foot on ground
- **Swimming or water aerobics:** Supportive, zero impact
- **Cycling:** Indoor or outdoor
- **Elliptical or arc trainer:** Low impact

### 8. Hurdler's Stretch

**Why it's problematic:**
- Twisting stress on bent knee
- Forces knee into awkward position
- Risk of meniscus strain

**Better alternatives:**
- **Supine hamstring stretch:** Leg straight, no knee twist
- **Standing hamstring stretch:** Leg on low surface
- **Seated stretch (both legs forward):** No knee twisting

### 9. Deep Knee Bends/Duck Walks

**Why it's problematic:**
- Extreme knee flexion under load
- Maximum patellofemoral pressure
- Meniscus compression

**Better alternatives:**
- **Partial squats:** Limited range
- **Hip hinge movements:** Load hips, not knees
- **Bridges and hip thrusts:** Build glutes without knee stress

### 10. Stair Running/Climbing (Fast)

**Why it's problematic:**
- Impact plus deep flexion
- High forces on kneecap going down
- Risk of missteps

**Better alternatives:**
- **Walking stairs slowly:** Controlled, careful
- **Stair stepper machine:** Controlled motion
- **Step-ups (low step):** Controlled height
- **Incline treadmill walking:** No impact

## Knee-Friendly Exercise Plan

### Cardio Options (Best to Worst for Knees)

1. **Swimming:** Zero impact, full support
2. **Pool walking/running:** Impact-free movement
3. **Cycling:** Low impact, controlled
4. **Elliptical:** Low impact
5. **Rowing:** If tolerated
6. **Walking:** Low impact, natural
7. **Hiking (flat terrain):** Watch for uneven surfaces

### Strength Training Options

**For Quads:**
- Wall sits (at comfortable depth)
- Terminal knee extension with band
- Step-ups (low step)
- Partial leg press
- Straight leg raises

**For Hamstrings:**
- Glute bridges
- Hip thrusts
- Romanian deadlifts
- Slider hamstring curls

**For Glutes (Critical for Knee Health):**
- Clamshells
- Side-lying leg raises
- Glute bridges
- Hip thrusts
- Lateral band walks

**For Calves:**
- Seated calf raises
- Standing calf raises

### Sample Knee-Friendly Lower Body Workout

1. **Warm-up:** 5 minutes cycling or walking
2. **Glute bridges:** 3x15
3. **Clamshells:** 3x15 per side
4. **Wall sit:** 3x30 seconds
5. **Step-ups (low step):** 3x12 per leg
6. **Terminal knee extension:** 3x15 per leg
7. **Romanian deadlift:** 3x12
8. **Lateral band walks:** 3x15 each direction
9. **Standing calf raises:** 3x15

## Making Exercises Knee-Friendlier

### Range of Motion Modifications

- Limit squat/lunge depth to 45-60°
- Don't lock out leg extension exercises
- Control the depth of all movements

### Loading Modifications

- Start with bodyweight
- Progress slowly
- Use lighter weights with more reps
- Consider blood flow restriction training

### Surface and Impact

- Choose padded/soft surfaces when possible
- Avoid concrete for any impact
- Use cushioned footwear
- Consider water-based exercise

### Speed and Control

- Slow down all movements
- No bouncing or jerky motions
- Control both lifting and lowering phases
- No ballistic movements

## The Importance of Hip Strength

Weak hips are one of the biggest contributors to knee pain. When your hip muscles (especially glute medius) are weak, your knee collapses inward during movement, causing pain and damage.

**Essential hip exercises:**
- Clamshells
- Side-lying leg raises
- Lateral band walks
- Single-leg glute bridge
- Hip hikes

## Exercises That Often Help Bad Knees

**Straight Leg Raises:**
- Strengthens quads without bending knee
- Good for post-surgery rehab

**Terminal Knee Extension:**
- Targets the last few degrees of extension
- Activates VMO (inner quad)

**Cycling:**
- Maintains range of motion
- Strengthens without impact

**Swimming/Pool Exercises:**
- Builds strength in supportive environment
- Maintains fitness during flare-ups

**Clamshells and Hip Work:**
- Addresses root cause of many knee problems

## When to See a Doctor

Seek medical evaluation if:

- Significant swelling
- Knee locks or gives way
- Pain prevents daily activities
- Pain is getting worse despite rest
- Visible deformity
- Unable to bear weight
- Sudden onset after injury

## Key Takeaways

1. **Avoid deep flexion** in squats, lunges, and kneeling
2. **Skip high-impact activities** like running and jumping
3. **Choose low-impact cardio:** swimming, cycling, elliptical
4. **Strengthen your hips**—weak hips cause knee problems
5. **Pain is your guide**—if it hurts, stop or modify
6. **Partial range is fine**—you don't need deep squats
7. **Swimming is your friend**—excellent for knee issues
8. **Strengthen, don't avoid**—strong muscles protect your knees

Bad knees don't mean no exercise. With smart modifications and the right exercise selection, you can stay active, build strength, and often even improve your knee health over time.
