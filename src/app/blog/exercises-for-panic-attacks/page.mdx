# Exercises for Panic Attacks: Physical Techniques to Calm Your Body

During a panic attack, your body's fight-or-flight response goes into overdrive. Physical exercises can help interrupt this response, signal safety to your nervous system, and bring you back to calm. This guide provides techniques to use during panic attacks and exercises to reduce their frequency.

## Understanding What's Happening

**During a panic attack, your body experiences:**
- Rapid heartbeat
- Shallow, fast breathing (or feeling you can't breathe)
- Muscle tension
- Sweating
- Trembling
- Feelings of unreality or detachment

**Important:** These are your body's protective responses gone haywire - uncomfortable but not dangerous. Physical exercises can directly counteract these symptoms.

## During a Panic Attack

### Breathing Exercises (Most Important)

**Why breathing helps:** Panic often involves hyperventilation (too much breathing out of CO2), which causes many panic symptoms. Slowing your breathing restores balance.

**Physiological Sigh (Quickest Relief)**
- Inhale through nose
- Take a second small inhale on top (fills lungs completely)
- Long slow exhale through mouth
- Repeat 3-5 times
- This is the body's natural calming breath

**Extended Exhale Breathing**
- Inhale for 4 counts
- Exhale for 6-8 counts
- The long exhale activates parasympathetic (calm) nervous system
- Continue for 2-3 minutes

**Box Breathing**
- Inhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- Exhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- Repeat for 2-3 minutes

**Pursed Lip Breathing**
- Inhale through nose for 2 counts
- Purse lips like blowing through a straw
- Exhale slowly for 4-6 counts
- Slows breathing naturally

### Grounding Through Your Body

**Cold Water Technique**
- Splash cold water on face
- Hold ice cubes
- Run cold water over wrists
- Activates dive reflex, slows heart rate

**Feet on Ground**
- If sitting, press feet firmly into floor
- Notice the sensation of ground supporting you
- Push down as if trying to push through the floor
- Grounding reminds your body it's safe

**Hand Exercises**
- Press palms together firmly for 10 seconds
- Release and notice the sensation
- Make tight fists, hold 5 seconds, release
- The contrast between tension and release is calming

### Progressive Muscle Relaxation (Quick Version)

**Tense-Release Sequence:**

1. **Hands**: Make tight fists, hold 5 seconds, release
2. **Arms**: Flex biceps, hold 5 seconds, release
3. **Shoulders**: Shrug up to ears, hold 5 seconds, drop
4. **Face**: Scrunch face tightly, hold 5 seconds, release

Notice the warmth and relaxation that follows each release. This teaches your body that you control the tension.

### Movement During Panic

**Walking**
- If you can, walk slowly
- Notice each foot hitting the ground
- Count your steps
- Movement discharges adrenaline

**Gentle Bouncing**
- Stand with soft knees
- Gently bounce up and down
- Shake out arms
- Helps release tension

**Push Against a Wall**
- Place hands on wall
- Push firmly as if trying to move the wall
- Hold 10 seconds
- Release
- Engages muscles and discharges energy

### 5-4-3-2-1 Sensory Grounding

While doing breathing, engage your senses:
- **5 things you can see** (name them)
- **4 things you can touch** (feel them)
- **3 things you can hear** (identify them)
- **2 things you can smell** (notice them)
- **1 thing you can taste** (describe it)

This pulls your attention out of the panic spiral and into present reality.

## After a Panic Attack

**Once the acute phase passes:**

### Gentle Movement (10 minutes)

**Slow Walking**
- Walk slowly, focusing on breath
- 5-10 minutes
- Helps process remaining adrenaline

**Gentle Stretching**
- Neck rolls
- Shoulder rolls
- Side bends
- Forward fold (head below heart is calming)

### Restorative Positions

**Legs Up the Wall**
- Lie on back, legs up wall
- Arms relaxed by sides
- Stay 5-10 minutes
- Very calming for nervous system

**Child's Pose**
- Kneel, sit back on heels
- Fold forward, arms extended or by sides
- Forehead resting on floor
- Stay 2-3 minutes

**Fetal Position**
- Lie on side, knees toward chest
- Arms comfortable
- Natural protective position
- Stay as long as needed

### Self-Compassion
- Place hand on heart
- Speak kindly to yourself
- "That was hard. I'm okay now. I got through it."

## Exercises to Prevent Panic Attacks

**Regular practice reduces panic attack frequency and intensity:**

### Daily Breathing Practice (5 minutes)

**Diaphragmatic Breathing Training**
- Lie on back or sit comfortably
- Place one hand on chest, one on belly
- Breathe so belly hand rises, chest stays still
- Practice 5 minutes daily
- Trains proper breathing pattern

**Box Breathing Practice**
- 4-4-4-4 pattern
- Practice when calm
- Becomes automatic during stress

### Vagal Toning Exercises

**The vagus nerve connects brain to body and controls relaxation response. These exercises strengthen it:**

**Humming/Singing**
- Hum for 30 seconds
- Feel vibration in chest
- Stimulates vagus nerve
- Singing has same effect

**Gargling**
- Gargle water vigorously
- 30 seconds
- Activates vagus nerve in throat

**Cold Exposure**
- End shower with 30-60 seconds cold water
- Or splash cold water on face
- Builds stress tolerance

**Slow Exhale with Sound**
- Make "voooo" sound on exhale
- Long, slow exhale
- Feel vibration in chest
- 10 breaths

### Body Awareness Practice

**Body Scan (5-10 minutes)**
- Lie down comfortably
- Mentally scan from feet to head
- Notice each body part without judging
- Release tension when found
- Practice daily, ideally same time

**Yoga Nidra**
- Guided deep relaxation
- Many free recordings available
- Highly effective for anxiety
- 15-30 minutes

### Physical Exercise

**Regular exercise is one of the best anxiety treatments:**

**Cardio**
- 30 minutes most days
- Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming
- Burns off stress hormones
- Improves mood through endorphins

**Strength Training**
- 2-3x per week
- Increases body confidence
- Provides sense of control
- Reduces baseline anxiety

**Yoga**
- Combines movement, breath, and mindfulness
- Particularly effective for anxiety
- Even 15 minutes helps

### Stress-Reducing Movement Practices

**Morning Routine (10 minutes)**
- Cat-cow stretches: 10 reps
- Neck rolls: 5 each direction
- Shoulder rolls: 10 each direction
- Standing forward fold: 60 seconds
- Deep breathing: 2 minutes

**Before Potentially Stressful Situations**
- Physiological sigh: 3-5 breaths
- Shoulder rolls
- Shake out hands
- Ground through feet

**Evening Wind-Down (10 minutes)**
- Legs up wall: 3 minutes
- Supine twist: 1 minute each side
- Knees to chest: 1 minute
- Body scan: 3 minutes

## Specific Situations

### Panic in Public

**Discreet techniques:**
- Slow breathing (no one notices)
- Press feet firmly into ground
- Squeeze fist under table, release
- Trace your palm with one finger
- Count backward from 100 by 7s

### Panic While Driving

**Pull over if possible, then:**
- Park safely
- Turn off or lower music
- Extended exhale breathing
- Grip steering wheel tight, release
- Cold air on face (open window or AC)
- Do not drive until panic subsides

### Panic at Night

**In bed:**
- 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8)
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Place hand on heart, feel it slow
- If can't sleep: get up, do gentle stretching, return when calm

### Panic Before an Event

**Preparation:**
- Do 5 minutes of breathing exercises beforehand
- Physical movement (walk, jump, shake)
- Cold water on face
- Have an exit strategy (reduces anticipatory anxiety)

## Building a Prevention Practice

### Week 1-2: Foundation
**Daily:**
- 5 minutes diaphragmatic breathing
- Body scan before bed

**Learn:**
- Physiological sigh
- Extended exhale technique
- Basic grounding

### Week 3-4: Build Habits
**Daily:**
- 10-minute morning movement routine
- 5 minutes breathing
- Evening wind-down

**Add:**
- 30 minutes exercise 3x weekly
- Vagal toning exercises

### Month 2+: Maintenance
**Continue:**
- Daily breathing and movement
- Regular exercise
- Evening relaxation

**Track:**
- Panic attack frequency
- Intensity
- What techniques helped

## When to Seek Help

**See a mental health professional if:**
- Panic attacks are frequent (multiple per week)
- You're avoiding activities due to fear of panic
- Anxiety significantly impacts daily life
- Self-help techniques aren't providing relief
- You have thoughts of self-harm

**Effective treatments include:**
- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
- Exposure therapy
- Medication (when appropriate)
- EMDR for trauma-related anxiety

Exercise and breathing techniques work well alongside professional treatment.

## Key Takeaways

**During panic:**
- Focus on breath first (physiological sigh or extended exhale)
- Ground through physical sensation (feet, cold, pressure)
- Move if possible (walk, shake, push against wall)
- This will pass - symptoms peak and decline

**For prevention:**
- Daily breathing practice trains your nervous system
- Regular exercise reduces baseline anxiety
- Vagal toning exercises strengthen your calm response
- Body awareness helps you notice early warning signs

**Most effective techniques:**
- Physiological sigh (fastest relief)
- Extended exhale breathing
- Cold water on face
- Movement/walking
- Grounding through senses

The more you practice these techniques when calm, the more automatically they work during panic. Your nervous system can learn new patterns - it just takes consistent practice.
