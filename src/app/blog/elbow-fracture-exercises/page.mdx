# Elbow Fracture Exercises: Recovery After Broken Elbow

Elbow fractures require careful rehabilitation to restore motion and prevent permanent stiffness. The elbow is particularly prone to stiffness after injury, making proper exercise progression essential.

## Types of Elbow Fractures

### Radial Head Fracture
- Most common elbow fracture
- Occurs from fall on outstretched hand
- Affects forearm rotation
- Often treated without surgery (non-displaced)

### Olecranon Fracture
- Fracture of elbow tip
- Usually from direct impact or fall
- Often requires surgical fixation
- Affects elbow extension

### Distal Humerus Fracture
- Fracture of upper arm bone at elbow
- Usually from high-energy injury
- Often complex, may need surgery
- Affects both flexion and extension

### Coronoid Fracture
- Part of ulna inside elbow joint
- Often associated with dislocation
- Affects elbow stability

## Treatment Approaches

### Non-Surgical
- For stable, non-displaced fractures
- Brief immobilization (sling, splint)
- Early motion when cleared
- Common for many radial head fractures

### Surgical
- For displaced or unstable fractures
- Plates, screws, or pins
- Excision (removal) for some radial head fractures
- Replacement for severe radial head fractures

**Critical:** Follow YOUR surgeon's specific protocol. Timing and restrictions vary.

## General Principles

### Early Motion is Essential
The elbow is notorious for developing stiffness after injury. Early motion (when cleared) is crucial.

### Avoid Passive Stretching Initially
Aggressive stretching can cause heterotopic ossification (abnormal bone formation). Gentle, active motion is safer.

### Functional Range Goals
- Full extension is often difficult to regain
- Losing 10-15 degrees of extension is common
- Functional range: 30-130 degrees flexion
- Focus on achieving functional motion first

## Phase 1: Early Protected Motion

### When to Start
- After doctor clearance
- Usually 1-2 weeks post-injury or surgery
- May be immediate for stable fractures

### Active Elbow Flexion/Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Sit comfortably, arm supported
2. Slowly bend elbow as far as comfortable
3. Slowly straighten (don't force)
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Multiple times daily (5-6 sessions)

### Gravity-Assisted Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back or sit in chair
2. Hold upper arm straight up
3. Let forearm hang back toward shoulder
4. Gravity assists extension
5. Hold 1-2 minutes, repeat frequently

### Forearm Rotation

**For radial head fractures especially important:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Slowly rotate palm up (supination)
3. Slowly rotate palm down (pronation)
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. Multiple times daily

### Wrist and Hand Exercises

**Maintain hand function:**
- Make fists and spread fingers
- Wrist circles
- Grip and release
- 15-20 repetitions each

### Shoulder Exercises

**Prevent shoulder stiffness:**
- Pendulum swings
- Shoulder shrugs
- Assisted forward elevation
- 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 2: Progressive Mobility (Weeks 3-8)

### Active-Assisted Exercises

Use other hand to gently assist motion.

**Assisted Flexion**
1. Use good arm to help bend elbow further
2. Gentle, sustained pressure
3. Hold at end range 15-30 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Assisted Extension**
1. Support forearm on table
2. Let gravity help straighten
3. Or gently assist with other hand
4. Hold 15-30 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

### Stretching (When Cleared)

Usually begin 4-6 weeks post-injury/surgery.

**Elbow Flexion Stretch**
1. Push hand toward shoulder
2. Use other hand to assist
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3-5 times

**Elbow Extension Stretch**
1. Place forearm on table
2. Gently press elbow straight
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3-5 times

**Forearm Rotation Stretch**
1. Elbow at side
2. Use other hand to assist rotation
3. Both directions
4. Hold 30 seconds each

### Heat Application

Use before exercise to improve mobility:
- Warm shower or bath
- Heating pad 10-15 minutes
- Warm water soak

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 6-12)

Begin when fracture is healing well and motion is improving.

### Isometric Exercises

**Isometric Flexion**
1. Elbow bent 90 degrees
2. Press into other hand (trying to bend)
3. No movement occurs
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Extension**
1. Same position
2. Press down (trying to straighten)
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Rotation**
1. Forearm neutral
2. Resist rotation with other hand
3. Both directions
4. Hold 5-10 seconds each

### Progressive Resistance

**Biceps Curls**
1. Light weight (1-2 lbs initially)
2. Full comfortable range
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Triceps Extensions**
1. Light weight
2. Extend elbow overhead or kickback
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Forearm Rotation with Weight**
1. Hold hammer or weighted rod
2. Rotate fully both directions
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Wrist Curls**
1. Palm up, curl wrist
2. Palm down, extend wrist
3. 2-3 sets of 15 each

### Grip Strengthening

**Ball Squeeze**
1. Soft ball initially
2. Progress to firmer
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Putty Exercises**
1. Squeeze, pinch, manipulate
2. 3-5 minutes

## Phase 4: Functional Recovery (Weeks 10-16+)

### Closed Chain Exercises

**Wall Push-Ups**
1. Start with hands on wall
2. Progress angle as tolerated
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Table Weight-Bearing**
1. Hands on table
2. Lean and support weight
3. Progress to more weight

**Push-Up Progression**
1. Wall → counter → knees → full
2. Only progress when pain-free

### Functional Activities

**Door handle practice:**
Opening and closing doors

**Jar opening:**
Twisting motions

**Carrying:**
Gradually increase weight

**Reaching:**
High shelf and behind back

### Sport-Specific

**Throwing:**
1. Light catches first
2. Gradual distance and force
3. Full throwing when cleared

**Weightlifting:**
1. Machines first
2. Light free weights
3. Gradual progression
4. May take 6+ months for full load

## Fracture-Specific Considerations

### Radial Head Fracture

**Key focus:**
- Forearm rotation (pronation/supination)
- This motion often limited
- May take months to maximize

**Exercises emphasized:**
- Rotation exercises daily
- Rotation strengthening
- Grip strength

### Olecranon Fracture

**Key focus:**
- Elbow extension
- Triceps strengthening
- Avoiding stress on fixation initially

**Restrictions:**
- No active extension against resistance initially
- Follow surgeon's specific protocol
- Progress strengthening gradually

### Distal Humerus Fracture

**Key focus:**
- Both flexion and extension
- Often complex recovery
- May have prolonged restrictions

**Considerations:**
- Follow surgeon's protocol strictly
- May need longer before strengthening
- Often benefit from formal therapy

## Preventing Stiffness

### Exercise Frequently
- Multiple short sessions daily
- Better than one long session
- Consistency is key

### Use Heat
- Before exercise
- Improves tissue pliability
- Helps achieve more motion

### Avoid Aggressive Stretching
- Especially in first 6 weeks
- Active motion is safer
- Progress stretching gradually

### Monitor for Problems
- Increasing pain or swelling
- Loss of motion
- May need medical attention

## Sample Daily Program

### Week 2-4
**5-6 times daily:**
1. Active ROM: 15 reps each motion
2. Gravity-assisted extension: 2 min
3. Wrist/hand exercises: 15 reps
4. Ice after: 15 min

### Week 5-8
**3-4 times daily:**
1. ROM exercises: 15 reps
2. Assisted stretching: 30 sec holds
3. Begin isometrics: 10 reps
4. Heat before, ice after

### Week 9-12
**2 times daily:**
1. Stretching: 5 min
2. Strengthening: 2-3 exercises, 2x15
3. Grip exercises: 2-3 min
4. Functional activities

### Week 12+
**Once daily + gym:**
1. Maintenance stretching
2. Progressive strengthening
3. Closed chain progression
4. Sport-specific training

## When to Seek Help

**Contact your doctor if:**
- Increasing pain or swelling
- Loss of previously gained motion
- Numbness or tingling
- Wound problems (surgical)
- Unable to progress with exercises

**Physical/Occupational therapy helpful for:**
- Formal supervised progression
- Joint mobilization
- Custom splinting
- Modalities (heat, ultrasound)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Early motion prevents stiffness** — the biggest complication
2. **Multiple daily sessions** — better than one long session
3. **Active motion before passive** — safer for healing bone
4. **Heat improves mobility** — use before exercise
5. **Progress strengthening gradually** — protect healing fracture
6. **Expect some motion loss** — 10-15 degrees is common
7. **Fracture type matters** — follow specific protocols
8. **Patience is essential** — full recovery takes months

Elbow fractures require dedicated rehabilitation, but most people achieve good functional outcomes. Follow your surgeon's guidance, stay consistent with exercises, and be patient with the recovery process.
