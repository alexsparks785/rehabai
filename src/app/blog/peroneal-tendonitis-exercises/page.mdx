# Peroneal Tendonitis Exercises: Strengthen Your Outer Ankle

Peroneal tendonitis causes pain along the outer ankle and can make walking, running, and standing uncomfortable. The good news: this condition responds well to progressive exercises that strengthen and stabilize the peroneal tendons.

## Understanding Peroneal Tendonitis

The peroneal muscles (peroneus longus and brevis) run down the outer lower leg, behind the ankle bone (lateral malleolus), and attach to the foot. They're responsible for:
- Ankle eversion (turning the foot outward)
- Stabilizing the ankle during walking and running
- Assisting with plantarflexion (pointing the foot)

**Where it hurts:**
- Behind and below the outer ankle bone
- May extend along the outer foot
- Pain increases with activity, especially on uneven surfaces

**Common causes:**
- Overuse (increased running, walking, or hiking)
- Ankle sprains (peroneals work overtime to stabilize)
- High-arched feet (increases peroneal strain)
- Improper footwear
- Running on sloped surfaces (road camber)
- Sudden increase in activity

## Types of Peroneal Problems

### Peroneal Tendinopathy
Degeneration of the tendon from chronic overload. May involve thickening and pain with activity.

### Peroneal Tenosynovitis
Inflammation of the tendon sheath surrounding the tendons. Often acute onset with swelling.

### Peroneal Subluxation
Tendons slip out of the groove behind the ankle bone. Causes snapping sensation.

### Peroneal Tendon Tears
Partial or complete tears. May require imaging and sometimes surgery.

This guide focuses on tendinopathy and tenosynovitis, which respond to conservative management.

## Phase 1: Pain Management (Weeks 1-2)

### Relative Rest
Complete rest is rarely needed. Instead:
- Reduce aggravating activities (running, hiking, uneven surfaces)
- Walk on flat, even surfaces
- Avoid prolonged standing

### Ice
Apply ice to the outer ankle for 15-20 minutes after activity or when painful. Helps reduce inflammation in acute phases.

### Footwear Modifications
- Supportive shoes with good heel counter
- May benefit from lateral wedge or orthotic
- Avoid flat, unsupportive shoes

### Compression
Ankle sleeve or compression wrap can provide support and reduce swelling.

### Gentle Range of Motion

**Ankle Circles**
1. Sit with leg elevated
2. Draw circles with your foot
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Several times daily

**Ankle Alphabet**
1. Trace letters A-Z with your big toe
2. Uses all ankle motions
3. 1-2 times through daily

## Phase 2: Mobility and Early Strengthening (Weeks 2-4)

### Stretching

**Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius)**
1. Stand facing wall, hands on wall
2. Step back with affected leg
3. Keep back knee straight, heel on floor
4. Lean into wall until stretch felt
5. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times

**Soleus Stretch**
1. Same position as above
2. Bend back knee while keeping heel down
3. Shifts stretch to deeper calf muscle
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times

**Peroneal Stretch**
1. Sit with affected leg crossed over opposite knee
2. Grasp foot and gently turn sole inward (inversion)
3. Should feel stretch along outer lower leg
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times

### Early Strengthening

**Isometric Eversion**
1. Sit with feet flat on floor
2. Press outer edge of foot against immovable object (wall, furniture leg)
3. Hold 10 seconds without moving
4. Repeat 10-15 times

**Towel Scrunches**
1. Place towel flat on floor
2. Using only toes, scrunch towel toward you
3. Strengthens foot intrinsics
4. 2-3 sets of 10-15 scrunches

**Marble Pickups**
1. Pick up marbles (or similar small objects) with toes
2. Place in cup
3. 15-20 pickups per session

### Balance Introduction

**Double-Leg Stance**
1. Stand on firm surface
2. Progress to single-leg as tolerated
3. Hold onto support initially
4. Work up to 30 seconds

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 4-8)

### Resistance Band Exercises

**Resisted Eversion (Key Exercise)**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Loop resistance band around forefoot, anchor other end
3. Turn foot outward against resistance (eversion)
4. Slowly return to start
5. 3 sets of 15-20 reps

**Progression:** Yellow → Red → Green → Blue band

**Resisted Inversion**
Balance the peroneals by also strengthening the tibialis posterior.
1. Same setup, but turn foot inward
2. 3 sets of 15-20 reps

**Resisted Dorsiflexion**
1. Band around forefoot, anchored in front
2. Pull foot toward shin
3. 3 sets of 15-20 reps

**Resisted Plantarflexion**
1. Band around forefoot, push away
2. Point foot against resistance
3. 3 sets of 15-20 reps

### Heel Raises

**Bilateral Heel Raises**
1. Stand on flat surface
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly (3 seconds)
4. 3 sets of 15-20 reps

**Single-Leg Heel Raises**
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly
4. Use support initially
5. 3 sets of 10-15 reps

**Eccentric Heel Drops**
1. Stand on step, heels off edge
2. Rise on both feet
3. Shift weight to affected side
4. Lower heel below step level slowly (4-5 seconds)
5. 3 sets of 15 reps

### Functional Strengthening

**Step-Ups**
1. Start with low step (4-6 inches)
2. Step up leading with affected leg
3. Lower slowly
4. Progress step height
5. 3 sets of 12-15 reps

**Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift**
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Hinge forward at hip, reaching opposite leg back
3. Keep small knee bend
4. Return to standing
5. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Lateral Step-Downs**
1. Stand on step sideways
2. Slowly lower opposite foot toward floor
3. Touch lightly and return
4. 3 sets of 12 reps

## Phase 4: Balance and Proprioception (Weeks 6+)

### Single-Leg Balance Progressions

**Level 1:**
- Single-leg stance, eyes open, firm surface
- Goal: 60 seconds

**Level 2:**
- Single-leg stance, eyes closed, firm surface
- Goal: 30 seconds

**Level 3:**
- Single-leg stance, eyes open, foam pad or pillow
- Goal: 45 seconds

**Level 4:**
- Single-leg stance, eyes closed, unstable surface
- Goal: 20-30 seconds

### Dynamic Balance

**Single-Leg Reaches**
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Reach opposite foot forward, backward, and to sides
3. Maintain balance
4. 10 reaches each direction

**Star Excursion**
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Reach opposite leg to 8 points (like a star/compass)
3. Touch lightly and return to center
4. 2 sets around the star

**Balance Board/BOSU**
1. Start with bilateral stance
2. Progress to single-leg
3. Add catches, reaches, or perturbations

### Hop Progressions (When Ready)

**Criteria before hopping:**
- Pain-free walking and standing
- Single-leg balance 30+ seconds eyes closed
- Full ankle range of motion
- 25+ single-leg heel raises

**Bilateral Hops**
- Small vertical hops
- Forward/backward hops
- Side-to-side hops

**Single-Leg Hops**
- Small vertical hops
- Progress to directional hops
- Focus on controlled landing

## Phase 5: Return to Activity (Weeks 8-12+)

### Running Progression

**Week 1:**
- Walk 5 min, jog 1 min, repeat 4 times
- Every other day

**Week 2:**
- Walk 4 min, jog 2 min, repeat 4-5 times

**Week 3:**
- Walk 3 min, jog 3 min, repeat 4-5 times

**Week 4:**
- Walk 2 min, jog 4 min, repeat 4 times

**Progress:** Gradually increase jog time, decrease walk breaks

**Surface selection:**
- Start on flat, even surfaces
- Progress to trails/uneven terrain gradually
- Avoid cambered roads initially

### Sport-Specific Progression

**Cutting and direction changes:**
1. Start with controlled pivots
2. Progress speed gradually
3. Practice on stable surface before uneven terrain

**Hiking:**
1. Start with flat trails
2. Progress to mild hills
3. Then uneven terrain
4. Use trekking poles initially if helpful

## Addressing Contributing Factors

### Foot Type
**High arches:** May need cushioning and lateral support
**Flat feet:** May need stability and arch support

Consider orthotic evaluation if foot mechanics contribute.

### Footwear
- Replace worn shoes
- Match shoe to activity
- Consider shoes with lateral stability
- Avoid completely flat or minimalist shoes during recovery

### Training Errors
- Gradual progression (10% rule)
- Adequate rest days
- Avoid excessive running on cambered surfaces
- Vary terrain

### Muscle Imbalances
Weak hip abductors can affect ankle loading. Include:
- Clamshells
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Monster walks

## Exercises to Avoid During Recovery

**Initially avoid:**
- Running (until walking is pain-free)
- Hiking on uneven terrain
- Cutting and pivoting sports
- Jumping activities
- Walking on soft sand

**Return gradually** once basic exercises are pain-free.

## Sample Weekly Program: Phase 3

**Monday:**
- Band exercises (4-way): 3×15 each
- Single-leg heel raises: 3×12
- Balance: 3×30 sec each side

**Tuesday:**
- Walking 20-30 min
- Stretching: 10 min

**Wednesday:**
- Eccentric heel drops: 3×15
- Step-ups: 3×12
- Single-leg RDL: 3×10
- Balance board: 3×1 min

**Thursday:**
- Rest or gentle walking

**Friday:**
- Band exercises: 3×15 each
- Single-leg heel raises: 3×15
- Star excursion: 2 sets

**Saturday:**
- Walking 30 min varied terrain
- Stretching: 10 min

**Sunday:**
- Rest

## When to Seek Help

**See a healthcare provider if:**
- Pain doesn't improve after 4-6 weeks of exercises
- Snapping or popping sensation around outer ankle
- Significant swelling that doesn't resolve
- Weakness in foot eversion
- Pain at rest or at night
- History of repeated ankle sprains

**May need:**
- Imaging (ultrasound or MRI)
- Injection therapy
- Custom orthotics
- Physical therapy
- Rarely: surgery for tears or subluxation

## Key Takeaways

1. **Eversion strengthening is key** — the peroneals turn the foot outward
2. **Progress resistance gradually** — band colors indicate difficulty
3. **Balance training is essential** — single-leg progressions
4. **Eccentric work helps tendons** — slow, controlled lowering
5. **Address the cause** — footwear, foot type, training errors
6. **Return to running gradually** — walk-run progression
7. **Maintain gains** — continue exercises even after symptoms resolve
8. **Monitor for recurrence** — early treatment prevents chronic problems

Peroneal tendonitis responds well to consistent, progressive exercise. Stay patient, address contributing factors, and you'll return to your activities with a stronger, more stable ankle.
