# Stroke Recovery Exercises: Regain Movement and Function

Stroke recovery is possible. The brain has remarkable ability to rewire itself (neuroplasticity), and exercise is one of the most powerful tools to promote this recovery. Consistent practice of movements—even assisted movements—helps rebuild neural pathways and restore function.

**Key insight:** Repetition drives recovery. The brain learns through repeated practice. Even small movements, done consistently, signal the brain to rebuild connections.

*Work with your healthcare team, including physical and occupational therapists, to develop an individualized program. These exercises are general guidance, not a replacement for professional rehabilitation.*

---

## Understanding Stroke Recovery

### How Exercise Helps

- **Neuroplasticity:** Repetitive movement helps the brain rewire
- **Prevents complications:** Reduces risk of contractures, blood clots
- **Builds strength:** Weakness improves with practice
- **Improves function:** Daily activities become easier
- **Boosts mood:** Exercise helps with post-stroke depression

### Recovery Stages

| Stage | Timeframe | Focus |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Acute | Days to weeks | Medical stability, preventing complications |
| Subacute | Weeks to months | Intensive rehabilitation |
| Chronic | Months to years | Continued improvement, maintenance |

### Factors Affecting Recovery

- Location and size of stroke
- Time since stroke
- Intensity of rehabilitation
- Overall health
- Support system
- Motivation and practice

---

## Safety Considerations

### Before Exercising

- Get clearance from your healthcare team
- Know your blood pressure limits
- Understand any movement restrictions
- Have supervision as needed
- Know when to stop

### Stop and Seek Help If:

- New weakness or numbness
- Severe headache
- Vision changes
- Confusion or speech difficulty
- Chest pain or shortness of breath

### General Guidelines

- Start with supervision
- Progress gradually
- Focus on affected side, but use both
- Rest when needed
- Consistency matters more than intensity

## Early Recovery Exercises (Bed and Seated)

### Passive Range of Motion

If you can't move the affected limb yourself, a helper moves it for you.

**Why it matters:** Maintains joint mobility, prevents stiffness, provides sensory input to brain.

**Shoulder Flexion:**
1. Helper supports arm at wrist and elbow
2. Gently raises arm forward
3. Moves through comfortable range
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 times daily

**Hip and Knee Bending:**
1. Helper supports leg at ankle and behind knee
2. Gently bends knee toward chest
3. Straightens leg
4. 10-15 reps each leg

**Ankle Movements:**
1. Helper moves foot up and down
2. Rotates ankle gently
3. 10-15 reps in each direction

### Active-Assisted Movements

You do as much as you can; helper assists the rest.

**Arm Raise:**
1. Clasp hands together
2. Use stronger arm to help lift affected arm
3. Raise arms forward and up
4. Lower with control
5. 10-15 reps

**Leg Lifts (Supine):**
1. Helper provides support as needed
2. Lift leg a few inches
3. Lower slowly
4. 10 reps each leg

### Bed Mobility

**Rolling (With Assistance):**
1. Helper provides guidance
2. Practice rolling side to side
3. Use rails or helper for support

**Bridging:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift hips off bed
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. Lower slowly
5. 10 reps (helper assists as needed)

## Seated Exercises

### Upper Body

**Seated Arm Raises:**
1. Sit upright in chair
2. Clasp hands together
3. Raise arms forward, then up
4. Lower with control
5. 10-15 reps

**Seated Trunk Rotation:**
1. Sit tall
2. Rotate torso to one side
3. Return to center
4. Rotate other side
5. 10 reps each direction

**Weight Shifts:**
1. Sit on edge of sturdy chair
2. Shift weight to one side
3. Return to center
4. Shift to other side
5. 10 reps each direction

### Lower Body

**Seated Marching:**
1. Sit upright
2. Lift one knee
3. Lower, lift other knee
4. 20-30 total

**Seated Knee Extension:**
1. Straighten one knee
2. Hold 3-5 seconds
3. Lower slowly
4. 10 reps each leg

**Seated Ankle Pumps:**
1. Lift toes toward shin
2. Point toes down
3. 20-30 reps each foot

**Heel-Toe Raises:**
1. Raise heels off floor
2. Lower, then raise toes
3. 15-20 reps

## Standing Exercises (With Support)

Progress to standing only when safe and with appropriate support.

### Basic Standing

**Weight Shifts:**
1. Stand holding support (rail, counter, helper)
2. Shift weight to one leg
3. Return to center
4. Shift to other leg
5. 10 reps each direction

**Mini Squats:**
1. Hold support
2. Bend knees slightly (few inches)
3. Straighten
4. 10-15 reps

**Hip Abduction:**
1. Hold support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. Lower slowly
4. 10 reps each leg

**Heel Raises:**
1. Hold support
2. Rise onto balls of feet
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 reps

### Balance Progression

**Narrow Stance:**
1. Feet close together
2. Hold support lightly
3. Maintain for 30 seconds
4. Progress to less support

**Tandem Stance:**
1. One foot in front of other
2. Hold support as needed
3. Hold 10-30 seconds
4. Switch feet

**Single Leg (With Support):**
1. Hold support firmly
2. Lift one foot slightly
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Switch legs

## Arm and Hand Exercises

Hand function often recovers slower. Repetition is especially important.

### Gross Motor

**Arm Across Table:**
1. Sit at table, affected arm on towel
2. Slide arm forward
3. Slide back
4. Repeat sideways
5. 10-15 reps each direction

**Stacking Objects:**
1. Use blocks, cups, or cans
2. Practice stacking and unstacking
3. Use affected hand as able

### Fine Motor

**Finger Extension:**
1. Place hand flat on table
2. Lift each finger individually
3. Or lift all fingers together
4. 10 reps

**Finger Pinches:**
1. Touch thumb to each fingertip
2. Repeat sequence
3. 10 complete cycles

**Grip and Release:**
1. Hold soft ball or putty
2. Squeeze
3. Release
4. 10-15 reps

**Object Manipulation:**
1. Pick up small objects (coins, beans)
2. Move from one container to another
3. Use affected hand as able

## Walking Practice (When Cleared)

### Pre-Walking

**Standing Balance:**
Master standing balance before walking

**Weight Shifting:**
Forward/backward and side to side

**Stepping:**
1. Stand with support
2. Step one foot forward
3. Return
4. Alternate feet

### Walking with Assistance

**Parallel Bars:**
If available, ideal for early practice

**Walker or Cane:**
Use as recommended by therapist

**Helper Support:**
Helper at affected side as needed

### Focus Points

- Equal weight on both legs
- Look forward, not at feet
- Smooth, controlled steps
- Quality over distance

---

## Spasticity Management

Tightness (spasticity) is common after stroke.

### Stretching for Spasticity

**Slow, Sustained Stretches:**
- Hold stretches 30-60 seconds
- Gentle, not painful
- Multiple times daily

**Key Areas:**
- Elbow and wrist (if arm affected)
- Ankle (pointing down is common)
- Fingers (if curled)

### Positioning

- Affected arm supported and extended when possible
- Affected leg properly positioned when sitting/lying
- Use splints or braces as prescribed

---

## Creating a Home Program

### Daily Routine

**Morning:**
- Range of motion exercises
- Seated exercises
- Arm/hand practice

**Afternoon:**
- Standing exercises (if applicable)
- Walking practice (if applicable)
- Functional tasks

**Evening:**
- Stretching
- Range of motion
- Relaxation

### Tracking Progress

- Note what you can do
- Celebrate small improvements
- Don't compare to others
- Focus on your journey

---

## Working with Your Rehabilitation Team

### Who's Involved

- **Physical therapist:** Movement, walking, balance
- **Occupational therapist:** Daily activities, hand function
- **Speech therapist:** If speech/swallowing affected
- **Physician:** Medical management, recovery monitoring

### Getting the Most from Therapy

- Do your home exercises
- Ask questions
- Communicate challenges
- Practice between sessions

---

## The Bottom Line

Stroke recovery is hard work, but the brain's ability to adapt gives hope. Consistent practice, even of small movements, sends signals that help rebuild function. Progress may be slow, but improvement is possible months and even years after stroke.

**Recovery principles:**
1. Repetition drives brain rewiring
2. Practice the movements you want to recover
3. Start where you are, progress as able
4. Consistency matters more than intensity
5. Work with your rehabilitation team

Every movement is a signal to your brain. Keep sending those signals.
