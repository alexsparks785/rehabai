# What Muscles Do Seated Leg Curls Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The seated leg curl isolates the hamstrings through knee flexion while sitting upright. This position creates a unique stimulus compared to lying leg curls, emphasizing different portions of the hamstrings. Here's exactly what's working and why the seated position matters.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Hamstrings

**Activation Level: Very High (All Three Muscles)**

The hamstrings are the sole target:

**Biceps Femoris (Lateral Hamstring):**
- Two heads (long and short)
- Located on outer back of thigh
- Works in all hamstring exercises
- Major knee flexor

**Semimembranosus:**
- Medial (inner) hamstring
- Larger of the two medial muscles
- Important for knee flexion

**Semitendinosus:**
- Also medial hamstring
- Works with semimembranosus
- Completes the hamstring group

## Why Seated Position Matters

The seated leg curl creates a unique situation:

**Hip position:** When seated, hips are FLEXED (bent at ~90°)

**Hamstring anatomy:** Hamstrings cross both the hip AND knee joints (except short head of biceps femoris)

**The effect:** Seated hip flexion places hamstrings in a STRETCHED position at the hip, even before you curl.

This pre-stretch may:
- Increase overall muscle tension
- Change which fibers are most active
- Create different training stimulus than lying curls

## Seated vs Lying Leg Curl

| Aspect | Seated | Lying |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Hip position | Flexed (90°) | Extended (straight) |
| Hamstring length | More stretched | Less stretched |
| Peak difficulty | Different point in ROM | Different point in ROM |
| Comfort | Some prefer | Some prefer |
| Distal vs proximal emphasis | May differ | May differ |

**Neither is "better"**—they provide different stimuli. Ideally, include both in your training.

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Gastrocnemius

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The upper calf muscle:
- Crosses the knee joint
- Assists knee flexion
- Not primary target
- More active if toes pointed down

### Popliteus

**Activation Level: Low**

Small muscle behind the knee:
- Unlocks knee for flexion
- Stabilizes the joint
- Minor contributor

### Sartorius

**Activation Level: Low**

Crosses both hip and knee:
- Assists knee flexion
- Very minor role

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Seated with back against pad
- Pad behind ankles
- Legs extended
- Hamstrings stretched

### Curl Phase (Concentric)
- Hamstrings contract to flex knees
- Pull pad toward glutes
- Full contraction
- Squeeze at bottom of movement

### Bottom Position (Peak Contraction)
- Maximum knee flexion
- Hamstrings fully shortened
- Brief squeeze (1-2 seconds)
- Peak activation

### Extension Phase (Eccentric)
- Controlled return
- Resist the weight
- Full extension
- Maintain tension throughout

## Common Mistakes

**Not using full range of motion**: Partial reps limit development. Fully extend and fully contract.

**Going too heavy**: Leads to poor form and using momentum. Control the weight.

**Not squeezing at contraction**: Missing peak activation. Hold the squeeze.

**Rushing the negative**: Missing eccentric gains. Control the extension (2-3 seconds).

**Seat position wrong**: Knee should align with machine pivot. Adjust the seat.

**Back lifting off pad**: Should stay in contact. Reduce weight if needed.

## How to Maximize Hamstring Activation

1. **Full range of motion**: Complete extension to full flexion
2. **Squeeze at the bottom**: 1-2 second hold at peak contraction
3. **Control the negative**: 2-3 seconds on the return
4. **Align knees with pivot**: Proper machine setup
5. **Moderate weight**: Feel the hamstrings, not the momentum
6. **Mind-muscle connection**: Focus on hamstrings doing the work
7. **Consider toe position**: Dorsiflexed (toes up) may reduce calf assistance

## Toe Position Effects

| Position | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Toes pointed up (dorsiflexed) | May reduce calf contribution |
| Toes pointed down (plantarflexed) | May increase calf assistance |
| Neutral | Standard |

The effect is relatively minor—use what feels most comfortable.

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Standard seated leg curl | Bilateral, machine-based |
| Single-leg seated curl | Unilateral, addresses imbalances |
| Slow eccentric seated curl | Emphasizes lengthening phase |
| Pause rep seated curl | Hold at contraction |
| Partial rep seated curl | Can target different ROM portions |

### Single-Leg Seated Curl

One leg at a time:
- Identifies imbalances
- Equal work per leg
- Greater focus
- Good for bringing up weak side

## Programming Recommendations

**For Hamstring Hypertrophy:**
- 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps
- Moderate weight
- Full ROM with squeeze
- 2-3 times per week

**For Strength:**
- 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps
- Heavier weight
- Still controlled
- Full ROM

**In a Leg Program:**
Include with:
- Lying leg curl (different position)
- Romanian deadlift (hip extension emphasis)
- Good mornings (hip extension)

Variety in hamstring training = complete development

**Superset Ideas:**
- Seated leg curl + lying leg curl (two positions)
- Seated leg curl + leg extension (hamstrings + quads)
- Seated leg curl + glute-ham raise (isolation + compound)

## Complete Hamstring Training

Hamstrings have TWO functions:
1. **Knee flexion** (bending knee) ← Leg curls train this
2. **Hip extension** (straightening hip) ← RDLs, good mornings train this

**You need both movement patterns for complete hamstrings:**

| Function | Exercises |
|----------|-----------|
| Knee flexion | Seated curl, lying curl, Nordic curl |
| Hip extension | RDL, stiff-leg deadlift, good morning |

Don't rely on leg curls alone—include hip extension work.

## Seated Leg Curl vs Other Hamstring Exercises

| Exercise | Knee Flexion | Hip Extension | Equipment |
|----------|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| Seated leg curl | Isolated | None | Machine |
| Lying leg curl | Isolated | None | Machine |
| Nordic curl | Isolated | None | Bodyweight |
| Romanian deadlift | Minimal | High | Free weight |
| Glute-ham raise | High | High | GHD bench |

Seated leg curls excel at isolated knee flexion training.

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: All three hamstring muscles (very high, isolated)
- **Secondary**: Gastrocnemius (minor), popliteus
- **Seated position**: Hip flexion creates hamstring pre-stretch
- **vs Lying curl**: Different hip position = different stimulus—do both
- **Key form**: Full ROM, squeeze at contraction, control the negative
- **Not complete alone**: Must include hip extension work (RDLs, etc.)
- **Common error**: Partial ROM, going too heavy, rushing

The seated leg curl is an excellent hamstring isolation exercise that provides a unique stimulus due to the hip-flexed position. Combine it with lying curls and hip extension exercises for complete hamstring development.
