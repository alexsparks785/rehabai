You don't need a gym to get strong. Bodyweight training has built impressive physiques for centuries—from gymnasts to military personnel to prisoners with nothing but a cell floor.

This guide covers everything you need to start: fundamental movements, progressions for all fitness levels, and complete workout programs you can do anywhere.

## Why Bodyweight Training Works

### Always Available
Your body is the only equipment you need. No gym membership, no commute, no excuses.

### Builds Functional Strength
You learn to control your own body through space—the most practical form of strength.

### Natural Progression
Master easier variations before advancing. This builds a solid foundation and prevents injury.

### Joint-Friendly
Bodyweight exercises allow natural movement patterns. No machines forcing unnatural paths.

### Scalable Difficulty
Every exercise can be made easier or harder through leverage, tempo, and range of motion changes.

## The Fundamental Movement Patterns

All bodyweight training builds from these patterns:

1. **Push (horizontal)** – Push-ups and variations
2. **Push (vertical)** – Pike push-ups, handstand work
3. **Pull (horizontal)** – Rows
4. **Pull (vertical)** – Pull-ups, chin-ups
5. **Squat** – Bodyweight squats and progressions
6. **Hinge** – Hip hinges, glute bridges
7. **Core** – Planks, anti-rotation, flexion

Master these patterns, and you can train your entire body effectively.

## Push Exercises

### Push-Up Progression

**Level 1: Wall Push-Up**
1. Stand arm's length from wall
2. Place hands on wall at shoulder height
3. Lower chest toward wall
4. Push back to start

**Level 2: Incline Push-Up**
1. Hands on elevated surface (counter, bench, stairs)
2. Body straight from head to heels
3. Lower chest to surface
4. Push back up
5. Lower the surface as you get stronger

**Level 3: Knee Push-Up**
1. Hands on floor, knees down
2. Body straight from knees to head
3. Lower chest to floor
4. Push back up

**Level 4: Full Push-Up**
1. Hands slightly wider than shoulders
2. Body straight from head to heels
3. Lower until chest nearly touches floor
4. Push back up, arms fully extended

**Key form cues:**
- Core tight throughout—no sagging or piking
- Elbows at 45-degree angle, not flared out
- Full range of motion

**Level 5+: Advanced Variations**
- Diamond push-ups (hands close together)
- Archer push-ups (shift weight side to side)
- Decline push-ups (feet elevated)
- One-arm push-up progressions

### Pike Push-Up (Vertical Push)

Targets shoulders, builds toward handstand push-up.

1. Start in downward dog position
2. Hands shoulder-width, hips high
3. Lower head toward floor between hands
4. Push back up

**Progression:** Elevate feet to increase difficulty.

## Pull Exercises

### Inverted Row Progression

**Level 1: High Bar Row**
1. Bar at chest height (Smith machine, TRX straps, or sturdy table)
2. Hang underneath with body at angle
3. Pull chest to bar
4. Lower with control

**Level 2: Lower Bar Row**
1. Lower the bar closer to the ground
2. More horizontal body = harder
3. Pull chest to bar

**Level 3: Feet Elevated Row**
1. Put feet on a raised surface
2. Body nearly horizontal
3. Pull chest to bar

**Key cues:**
- Body stays straight like a plank
- Pull with back, not just arms
- Squeeze shoulder blades at top

### Pull-Up Progression

**Level 1: Dead Hang**
1. Hang from bar with straight arms
2. Build to 30-60 seconds
3. Builds grip and prepares shoulders

**Level 2: Flexed Arm Hang**
1. Jump or use assistance to get chin above bar
2. Hold position as long as possible
3. Lower slowly (negative)

**Level 3: Negative Pull-Up**
1. Jump to top position
2. Lower yourself slowly (5-10 seconds)
3. Repeat

**Level 4: Assisted Pull-Up**
1. Use a band or chair for assistance
2. Perform full pull-ups with reduced bodyweight

**Level 5: Full Pull-Up**
1. Hang with straight arms
2. Pull until chin clears bar
3. Lower with control

**Chin-up variation:** Palms facing you—easier for most beginners and more bicep involvement.

## Squat Exercises

### Squat Progression

**Level 1: Assisted Squat**
1. Hold onto something stable (doorframe, pole)
2. Squat as deep as possible
3. Use arms only as needed for balance

**Level 2: Box Squat**
1. Squat down to a chair or box
2. Touch briefly, don't sit
3. Stand back up
4. Lower box height as you progress

**Level 3: Full Bodyweight Squat**
1. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out
2. Squat until thighs are at least parallel
3. Keep chest up, knees tracking over toes
4. Stand by driving through whole foot

**Key cues:**
- Weight in mid-foot, not toes
- Knees push out, don't cave in
- Depth matters—go as low as you can with good form

**Level 4+: Advanced Variations**
- Jump squats (add explosive power)
- Pause squats (hold at bottom)
- Pistol squat progressions (single-leg)

### Lunge Variations

**Reverse Lunge**
1. Step backward with one foot
2. Lower until back knee nearly touches floor
3. Push through front foot to return
4. Safer for knees than forward lunges

**Walking Lunge**
1. Step forward into lunge
2. Push through front foot to bring back leg forward
3. Continue walking forward

**Bulgarian Split Squat**
1. Rear foot elevated on chair or couch
2. Lower until back knee nearly touches floor
3. Powerful single-leg builder

## Hinge Exercises

### Glute Bridge Progression

**Level 1: Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Push through heels to lift hips
3. Squeeze glutes at top
4. Lower with control

**Level 2: Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
1. One leg extended or held up
2. Bridge using one leg
3. Much harder than bilateral version

**Level 3: Hip Thrust (Elevated)**
1. Upper back on couch or bench
2. Feet flat on floor
3. Lower hips, then drive up
4. Greater range of motion

### Nordic Curl (Advanced)

Extremely challenging hamstring exercise.

1. Kneel, anchor feet under something heavy
2. Slowly lower body forward, using hamstrings to control descent
3. Catch yourself with hands, push back up
4. Progress by lowering further before catching

## Core Exercises

### Plank Progression

**Level 1: Incline Plank**
1. Forearms on elevated surface
2. Body straight from head to heels
3. Hold position

**Level 2: Full Plank**
1. Forearms on floor, elbows under shoulders
2. Body straight from head to heels
3. Don't let hips sag or pike up

**Level 3: Plank Variations**
- Side plank (lateral core)
- Plank shoulder taps (anti-rotation)
- Body saw (moving forward/back)

### Dead Bug

1. Lie on back, arms pointed at ceiling
2. Knees bent 90 degrees, shins parallel to floor
3. Extend opposite arm and leg, keeping back flat
4. Return, repeat other side

### Mountain Climbers

1. Push-up position
2. Drive one knee toward chest
3. Switch legs rapidly
4. Maintain plank position throughout

### Hollow Body Hold

Gymnastic core exercise.

1. Lie on back
2. Press lower back into floor
3. Lift shoulders and legs slightly off ground
4. Hold position—core engaged throughout

## Sample Beginner Programs

### Program 1: Minimalist (3x/week, 20 min)

**Circuit – 3 rounds:**
1. Push-ups (appropriate level) – 8-12 reps
2. Bodyweight squats – 15 reps
3. Inverted rows (or wall rows) – 8-12 reps
4. Glute bridges – 15 reps
5. Plank – 20-30 seconds

Rest 30-60 seconds between exercises, 2 minutes between rounds.

### Program 2: Full Body (3x/week, 30 min)

**Workout:**
1. Squats – 3 × 15
2. Push-ups – 3 × 8-12
3. Reverse lunges – 3 × 10 each leg
4. Inverted rows – 3 × 8-12
5. Glute bridges – 3 × 15
6. Pike push-ups – 2 × 8
7. Plank – 3 × 30 seconds
8. Dead bug – 2 × 10 each side

Rest 60 seconds between sets.

### Program 3: Upper/Lower Split (4x/week)

**Upper Body (Mon/Thu):**
1. Push-ups – 4 × 10-15
2. Inverted rows – 4 × 10
3. Pike push-ups – 3 × 8
4. Chin-up negatives – 3 × 5
5. Diamond push-ups – 2 × 8
6. Plank shoulder taps – 3 × 10 each

**Lower Body (Tue/Fri):**
1. Squats – 4 × 15
2. Reverse lunges – 3 × 12 each leg
3. Single-leg glute bridge – 3 × 10 each
4. Wall sit – 3 × 30 seconds
5. Calf raises – 3 × 20
6. Hollow body hold – 3 × 20 seconds

## How to Progress

### Add Reps
When you can complete all sets with good form, add 2-3 reps per set.

### Add Sets
Once you hit rep targets, add a set. Cap most exercises at 4-5 sets.

### Harder Variation
When an exercise becomes easy (15+ reps), progress to a harder variation.

### Slow Tempo
3-4 second lowering makes the same exercise much harder.

### Reduce Rest
Shorter rest periods increase difficulty and conditioning demands.

### Add Pauses
Hold the hardest position for 2-3 seconds.

## Common Mistakes

### Skipping Basics
Master each progression before advancing. A shaky push-up won't lead to a strong one-arm push-up.

### Ignoring Legs
Upper body exercises are more visible, but your legs contain your largest muscles. Train them.

### Poor Form for More Reps
5 good reps beat 15 sloppy ones. Quality always trumps quantity.

### No Pulling
Without equipment, pulling is harder to train. Find something to row and pull from—this is essential for balanced development.

### Same Routine Forever
Progress by making exercises harder or adding volume. Doing the same thing indefinitely stops working.

## Equipment That Helps (Optional)

**Pull-up bar:** Essential for serious bodyweight training. Doorframe versions are cheap and effective.

**Gymnastics rings or TRX:** Makes rows and many exercises adjustable and more challenging.

**Parallettes:** Allow deeper push-up variations and skill work.

**Resistance bands:** Can assist pull-ups or add resistance to other movements.

## The Bottom Line

Bodyweight training can build impressive strength and physique with zero equipment. The key is progressive overload—continuously making exercises harder as you adapt.

**Keys to success:**
- Master fundamentals before advancing
- Train all movement patterns (push, pull, squat, hinge, core)
- Progress through harder variations as you get stronger
- Be consistent—3 sessions per week beats sporadic training
- Find ways to do pulling exercises (this is the main limitation)

Start with Program 1 for 4-6 weeks, then progress to more volume and harder variations. Your body is a gym that's always open.
