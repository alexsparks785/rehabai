# Exercises for Long COVID: Safe Recovery and Reconditioning

Long COVID requires a different approach to exercise. The standard "push through" mentality can backfire, making symptoms worse. This guide covers safe, gentle exercises and pacing strategies specifically for long COVID recovery.

## Understanding Long COVID and Exercise

### What Makes Long COVID Different

**Key challenge: Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM)**
- Symptoms worsen 24-72 hours after activity
- Can last days or weeks
- Not the same as normal exercise soreness
- Triggered by physical, cognitive, or emotional exertion

**Other exercise-relevant symptoms:**
- Extreme fatigue
- Shortness of breath
- Brain fog
- Heart rate abnormalities
- Dizziness
- Muscle weakness
- Sleep disturbances

### Why Standard Exercise Advice Doesn't Apply

**Normal advice:** Push yourself, work through discomfort
**Long COVID reality:** Pushing causes crashes and setbacks

**Normal advice:** No pain, no gain
**Long COVID reality:** Pain and crashes mean you've done too much

**The goal:** Build capacity slowly without triggering PEM

## Before You Start

### Get Medical Clearance

**Important to rule out:**
- Cardiac issues (myocarditis, pericarditis)
- Blood clots
- Lung damage
- Autonomic dysfunction

**Testing to consider:**
- Heart monitoring if palpitations
- Pulmonary function if breathing issues
- Tilt table test if dizziness/POTS symptoms

### Establish Your Baseline

**Track for 1-2 weeks:**
- Daily activity level
- Symptoms and severity
- Heart rate patterns
- Sleep quality
- Crashes and triggers

**Find your "envelope":**
- What activities don't trigger crashes?
- What duration is safe?
- What heart rate is tolerable?

## The Pacing Approach

### What Is Pacing?

**Core principles:**
- Stay within your energy envelope
- Stop BEFORE you feel tired
- Rest proactively, not reactively
- Quality over quantity

### The 50% Rule

**Starting point:**
- Do 50% of what you think you can do
- If you could walk 20 minutes, walk 10
- If you could do 10 squats, do 5
- Assess for 48-72 hours
- If no crash, maintain; if crash, reduce

### Heart Rate Monitoring

**Particularly if you have POTS or dysautonomia:**
- Keep heart rate under threshold (often 100-120 for long COVID)
- Stop if heart rate spikes inappropriately
- Consider heart rate monitor
- Some use "HR = 60-70% max" as ceiling

## Starting Exercises (Weeks 1-4)

### Phase 1: Gentle Movement

**Goals:**
- Move without triggering symptoms
- Maintain some function
- Build confidence

**Daily routine (5-10 minutes maximum):**

**Lying Down:**
- Ankle pumps: 10 each direction
- Gentle leg slides: 5 each leg
- Arm raises: 5 reps
- Deep breathing: 5 breaths

**Seated:**
- Neck rotations: 3 each direction (slow)
- Shoulder shrugs: 5 reps
- Seated marching: 10 steps (slow)
- Ankle circles: 5 each

**Key points:**
- Stop if symptoms increase
- Rest between exercises
- Do lying down if sitting is too much
- Skip days if needed

### Breathing Exercises

**Particularly important for long COVID:**

**Diaphragmatic Breathing**
- Lie or sit comfortably
- Hand on belly
- Breathe so belly rises (not chest)
- Slow inhale: 4 counts
- Slow exhale: 6 counts
- 5 minutes, once or twice daily

**Box Breathing**
- Inhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- Exhale: 4 counts
- Hold: 4 counts
- Repeat 4-8 cycles

**Why this helps:**
- Activates parasympathetic system
- Supports autonomic recovery
- Can do even on bad days
- No crash risk

### Gentle Stretching

**If tolerated:**
- Neck stretches: 15 seconds each direction
- Seated side bends: 15 seconds each
- Gentle chest stretch: 15 seconds
- Seated hamstring stretch: 15 seconds each

**Total: 2-3 minutes**

## Progression (Weeks 5-12)

### How to Progress Safely

**Rule:** Only increase one variable at a time

**Variables:**
- Duration (add 1-2 minutes per week)
- Frequency (add one session per week)
- Intensity (add resistance or effort)

**Timeline:**
- Increase no more than 10% per week
- Wait 72 hours after increase to assess
- If crash, return to previous level for 2 weeks

### Phase 2 Exercises

**If Phase 1 is tolerated without crashes:**

**Daily routine (10-15 minutes):**

**Lying:**
- Bridge hold: 5 seconds × 3 (build to 10 sec)
- Dead bug (modified): 3 each side
- Knee to chest stretches: 15 seconds each

**Seated:**
- Seated marching: 20 steps
- Arm raises with light weights (1-2 lbs): 5 reps
- Seated row with band: 5 reps
- Seated spinal twist: 15 seconds each

**Standing (if tolerated):**
- Wall support standing: 1-2 minutes
- Slow weight shifts
- Calf raises (with support): 5 reps
- Mini squats (quarter depth): 5 reps

### Adding Walking

**Start extremely conservatively:**
- Week 1: Walk to mailbox and back
- Week 2: Add 1-2 minutes if no crash
- Week 3: Add another 1-2 minutes
- Continue slow progression

**Walking guidelines:**
- Flat terrain only initially
- Slow pace
- Take breaks
- Monitor heart rate
- Stop if symptoms increase

## Managing POTS and Dysautonomia

### What It Is

**POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome):**
- Heart rate spikes when standing
- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Blood pooling in legs
- Common in long COVID

### Exercise Modifications

**Start reclined or seated:**
- Lying exercises are safest
- Progress to seated before standing
- Use recumbent equipment if available

**Helpful exercises:**
- Recumbent cycling
- Swimming (if tolerated—water supports body)
- Rowing (seated)
- Resistance bands lying down

**Compression:**
- Wear compression stockings during exercise
- Abdominal binder may help some

**Hydration and salt:**
- Stay well hydrated
- Increase salt intake if advised by doctor
- Critical for POTS management

### Reconditioning the Autonomic System

**Gradual upright tolerance:**
- Start with brief standing (30 seconds)
- Gradually increase duration
- Use wall for support
- Progress to standing exercises slowly

## Signs to Stop or Reduce

### Stop Immediately If:

- Chest pain
- Severe shortness of breath
- Dizziness that doesn't resolve
- Heart rate spikes above safe threshold
- Feeling faint
- Significant symptom increase

### Reduce Activity If:

- Symptoms worse than baseline after exercise
- Crash 24-72 hours later
- Unable to do normal activities the next day
- Sleep significantly disrupted
- Brain fog worse than usual

### What Crashes Feel Like

- Extreme fatigue (different from exercise tiredness)
- Flu-like feeling
- Cognitive difficulties
- Muscle weakness
- May last hours to days

## Sample Weekly Schedule

### Week 1-4 (Baseline)

**Daily:**
- Breathing exercises: 5 minutes
- Gentle lying/seated movements: 5 minutes
- Optional gentle stretching: 2-3 minutes

**Every other day:**
- Very short walk (even 2-3 minutes counts)

**Rest days:**
- Listen to your body
- Some days may be complete rest

### Week 5-8 (If No Crashes)

**4-5 days per week:**
- Breathing exercises: 5 minutes
- Phase 2 exercises: 10-15 minutes
- Walking: 5-10 minutes (separate session)

**2-3 days per week:**
- Complete rest or stretching only

### Week 9-12 (If Progressing Well)

**4-5 days per week:**
- Breathing: 5 minutes
- Strength/movement: 15-20 minutes
- Walking: 10-15 minutes

**2 days per week:**
- Rest or very light activity

## Long-Term Progression

### When to Add More

**Only progress when:**
- Current level causes no crashes for 2+ weeks
- Baseline symptoms are stable or improving
- Sleep quality maintained
- You feel ready (not just think you should)

### Eventual Goals

**May take months to years:**
- Normal walking tolerance
- Light strength training
- Eventual return to previous activities
- Some may not return to pre-COVID levels (acceptance important)

### Working with Professionals

**Consider:**
- Physical therapist experienced with long COVID
- Cardiologist if heart issues
- Pulmonologist if lung issues
- Autonomic specialist if POTS
- ME/CFS specialists understand similar conditions

## Mental Health and Exercise

### The Challenge

- Frustration with limitations
- Grief for lost abilities
- Anxiety about making symptoms worse
- Depression from isolation and illness

### How Gentle Exercise Helps

- Provides sense of control
- Maintains some fitness
- Can improve mood (when not overdone)
- Structures recovery

### Mindfulness Integration

**Combine with exercise:**
- Focus on breath during movement
- Body awareness during stretching
- Gratitude for what you can do
- Acceptance of current limitations

## Practical Tips

### Create an Exercise Space

- Comfortable temperature (avoid overheating)
- Near place to rest
- Water accessible
- Heart rate monitor if using

### Track Everything

- Daily symptoms (1-10 scale)
- Activities and duration
- Sleep quality
- Crashes and triggers
- Heart rate during/after exercise

**Apps and journals help** - look for patterns

### Rest Proactively

- Schedule rest periods
- Don't wait until exhausted
- Pre-rest before activities
- Post-activity rest even if feeling okay

## What Not to Do

### Avoid

- High-intensity exercise (HIIT)
- Exercising through symptoms
- Comparing to pre-COVID fitness
- Ignoring warning signs
- Sudden increases in activity
- Exercise when having a bad day

### The "Push-Crash" Cycle

**What happens:**
1. Feel better, do too much
2. Crash 24-72 hours later
3. Rest until somewhat recovered
4. Feel better, do too much again
5. Repeat

**Break the cycle:**
- Pacing consistently
- Stay below your threshold even on good days
- Slow, steady progression

## Key Takeaways

**The long COVID exercise approach:**
- **Pacing** over pushing
- **Start extremely low** and progress slowly
- **Stop before** feeling tired
- **Monitor** for 48-72 hour crashes
- **Rest** is part of recovery, not weakness

**Safe starting points:**
- Breathing exercises (almost always tolerable)
- Lying down movements
- Very brief seated exercises
- Walking measured in minutes, not distance

**Progression rules:**
- 10% increase maximum
- Wait 72 hours to assess
- If crash, go back 2 weeks
- One variable at a time

**Most important:**
- Listen to your body
- Crashes mean you've done too much
- Recovery is measured in months, not weeks
- Some limitations may be long-term—adjust expectations

You're not being lazy or weak by going slowly. You're being smart about a complex condition. Gentle, consistent progress is the path forward.
