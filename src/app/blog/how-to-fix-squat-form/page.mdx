# How to Fix Squat Form: Common Mistakes and Corrections

The squat is fundamental—but it's also one of the most butchered exercises in the gym. Poor form limits your strength, causes pain, and increases injury risk. Here's how to identify and fix the most common squat mistakes.

## The Ideal Squat

Before fixing problems, know what good looks like:

- Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider
- Toes pointed slightly out (15-30 degrees)
- Knees tracking over toes
- Hips and knees break simultaneously
- Back stays neutral (not rounded or hyperextended)
- Depth: hip crease below knee (at minimum)
- Weight balanced mid-foot to heel
- Chest up throughout

## Common Squat Problems and Fixes

### Problem 1: Knees Caving In (Valgus)

**What it looks like:** Knees collapse toward each other, especially at the bottom or when coming up.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak glute medius
- Tight adductors
- Poor motor control
- Ankle mobility restrictions

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Push knees out" or "Spread the floor with your feet"

**Exercises:**
- Banded squats (band above knees forces you to push out)
- Clamshells: 3x20 per side
- Monster walks: 3x15 steps each direction
- Single-leg glute bridges: 3x12 per side

**Mobility:**
- Adductor stretches
- 90-90 hip stretch

### Problem 2: Heels Rising

**What it looks like:** Heels lift off the floor during the descent, shifting weight to toes.

**Why it happens:**
- Limited ankle dorsiflexion
- Tight calves
- Poor balance awareness

**How to fix it:**

**Temporary fix:** Elevate heels with small plates or squat shoes

**Long-term solutions:**
- Knee-to-wall ankle mobilization: 20 reps + 30-sec hold daily
- Calf stretches (straight and bent knee): 45 sec each
- Ankle circles and dorsiflexion stretches

**Cue:** "Drive through your whole foot" or "Grip the floor with your toes"

### Problem 3: Butt Wink (Posterior Pelvic Tilt)

**What it looks like:** Lower back rounds at the bottom of the squat; pelvis tucks under.

**Why it happens:**
- Limited hip flexion mobility
- Tight hamstrings (sometimes)
- Core stability issues
- Going too deep for current mobility
- Anatomical factors (hip structure)

**How to fix it:**

**First, determine the cause:**
- If it happens at full depth only: May be mobility or going past your safe range
- If it happens early: Core stability or hip mobility issue

**Exercises:**
- Hip flexor stretches: 90 sec per side daily
- 90-90 hip stretch: 60 sec per side
- Deep squat holds (only to depth where spine stays neutral)
- Dead bugs for core stability: 3x10 per side

**Cue:** "Keep your belt buckle pointing forward"

**Sometimes the fix is:** Don't squat as deep. Squat to where you can maintain neutral spine.

### Problem 4: Excessive Forward Lean

**What it looks like:** Torso tips forward significantly; looks like a good morning.

**Why it happens:**
- Weak quads (body shifts load to posterior chain)
- Tight ankles (limits knee travel forward)
- Long femurs / short torso (structural)
- Poor upper back position

**How to fix it:**

**Exercises:**
- Front squats (forces upright torso)
- Goblet squats (counterbalance keeps you upright)
- Leg press to build quad strength
- Ankle mobility work

**Cues:**
- "Chest up"
- "Drive your traps into the bar"
- "Lead with the chest"

**Consider:** Some forward lean is normal, especially in low-bar squats. Excessive is the problem.

### Problem 5: Not Hitting Depth

**What it looks like:** Stopping well above parallel; partial squats.

**Why it happens:**
- Mobility restrictions (hips, ankles)
- Fear of losing control
- Ego (can move more weight with partials)
- Not knowing what proper depth feels like

**How to fix it:**

**Use a target:**
- Box squats to consistent depth
- Touch and go to a bench

**Mobility work:**
- Deep squat holds: accumulate 5 minutes daily
- Hip opener stretches
- Ankle mobilization

**Build awareness:**
- Film yourself from the side
- Use a mirror to find your depth

### Problem 6: Knees Shooting Forward First

**What it looks like:** Knees travel forward before hips push back; ends up on toes.

**Why it happens:**
- Quad-dominant pattern
- Not initiating with hip hinge
- Lack of glute activation

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Sit back" or "Hips back first"

**Exercises:**
- Box squats (forces sitting back)
- Goblet squats with pause at bottom
- Romanian deadlifts (teaches hip hinge)

**Practice:** Squat to a chair, focusing on pushing hips back to sit.

### Problem 7: Bar Position Issues

**What it looks like:** Bar rolls up neck, slips down back, or causes wrist pain.

**Why it happens:**
- Wrong bar position for body type
- Poor upper back tightness
- Limited shoulder mobility

**How to fix it:**

**High bar position:**
- Bar sits on traps
- More upright torso
- Requires good ankle mobility

**Low bar position:**
- Bar sits on rear delts
- More forward lean
- Better for longer femurs/shorter torsos

**For both:**
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- Create a "shelf" with upper back muscles
- Keep elbows down (not flared back)

**If wrists hurt:** Widen grip, use wrist wraps, or try thumbless grip.

### Problem 8: Looking Up or Down

**What it looks like:** Hyperextending neck to look at ceiling or tucking chin to look at floor.

**Why it happens:**
- Trying to keep chest up by looking up
- Copying bad advice

**How to fix it:**

**Cue:** "Neutral neck" or "Pick a spot on the floor 6-10 feet ahead"

Your neck should stay aligned with your spine—chin slightly tucked, eyes forward or slightly down.

## Mobility Routine for Better Squats

Do this before squatting and/or daily:

### Ankles (3 minutes)
- Knee-to-wall mobilization: 15 reps each side
- Calf stretch (straight leg): 30 sec each
- Calf stretch (bent knee): 30 sec each

### Hips (5 minutes)
- 90-90 stretch: 45 sec each position
- Hip flexor stretch: 45 sec each side
- Deep squat hold: 60-90 sec total

### Thoracic Spine (2 minutes)
- Foam roller extension: 60 sec
- Thread the needle: 8 reps each side

## Warm-Up Progression

Before heavy squats:

1. **Bodyweight squats:** 10-15 reps (focus on form)
2. **Goblet squats:** 10 reps with light weight
3. **Empty bar squats:** 2x8
4. **Gradual loading:** Work up in 10-20% jumps

## When Form Breaks Down

Form often fails at certain points:

**Bottom of squat:** Mobility issue or core stability problem
**Coming out of the hole:** Weak quads or glutes
**Lockout:** Weak glutes or hip flexor tightness
**Throughout:** Too heavy, fatigue, or fundamental technique issue

**Rule:** If form breaks, reduce weight. Don't practice bad patterns.

## Quick Fixes to Try Today

1. **Widen your stance** slightly
2. **Point toes out** more (up to 30-45 degrees)
3. **Hold a light weight in front** (goblet position)
4. **Put plates under your heels** temporarily
5. **Focus on sitting BACK, not DOWN**
6. **Brace your core** before descending

## The Bottom Line

Squat form issues usually come from mobility restrictions, muscle weaknesses, or both. The fix involves:

1. **Identifying** your specific issue (film yourself)
2. **Mobilizing** what's restricted (ankles, hips, thoracic spine)
3. **Strengthening** what's weak (glutes, quads, core)
4. **Practicing** with lighter weight until the pattern is grooved

Don't just keep squatting with bad form and hope it improves. Address the underlying cause, and the squat will feel natural rather than forced.

Squat better, not just heavier. The weight will follow the form.
