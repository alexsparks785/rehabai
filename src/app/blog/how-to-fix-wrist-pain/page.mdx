      <h1>How to Fix Wrist Pain: Exercises for Stronger, Pain-Free Wrists</h1>

      <p>Whether it's from typing, lifting weights, or doing push-ups, wrist pain is incredibly common and frustrating. Your wrists are complex joints that we often neglect until they start hurting. Here's how to fix wrist pain and build wrists that can handle whatever you throw at them.</p>

      <h2>Common Causes of Wrist Pain</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Repetitive strain:</strong> Typing, mouse use, repetitive motions</li>
        <li><strong>Exercise:</strong> Push-ups, planks, front rack position, yoga</li>
        <li><strong>Weakness:</strong> Wrist muscles simply aren't strong enough</li>
        <li><strong>Poor mobility:</strong> Limited extension for weight-bearing positions</li>
        <li><strong>Carpal tunnel syndrome:</strong> Nerve compression in the wrist</li>
        <li><strong>Tendinitis:</strong> Inflammation from overuse</li>
        <li><strong>Sprains:</strong> Ligament damage from falls or trauma</li>
        <li><strong>Arthritis:</strong> Joint degeneration</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Doctor First</h2>

      <p>Get professional evaluation before self-treating if you have:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain after a fall or direct trauma</li>
        <li>Significant swelling or bruising</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling in fingers (especially thumb, index, middle)</li>
        <li>Weakness in grip that's sudden onset</li>
        <li>Visible deformity</li>
        <li>Pain that's severe or worsening</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Mobility Exercises</h2>

      <h4>1. Wrist Circles</h4>
      <p>Make fists and rotate your wrists in full circles, both directions. Go slowly through any stiff or tender spots. This lubricates the joint and identifies tight areas.</p>
      <p><strong>20 circles each direction, each wrist</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Prayer Stretch</h4>
      <p>Press palms together in front of your chest, fingers pointing up. Slowly lower your hands while keeping palms pressed together until you feel a stretch in your wrists and forearms. Hold.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Reverse Prayer Stretch</h4>
      <p>Press the backs of your hands together, fingers pointing down. Raise your elbows to increase the stretch on the top of your wrists (extensors).</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Tabletop Wrist Extension Stretch</h4>
      <p>Place palms flat on a table, fingers pointing toward you. Lean forward gently, keeping palms flat. This stretches the wrist flexors and improves extension mobility—crucial for push-ups and planks.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Quadruped Wrist Rocks</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees with fingers pointing forward, gently rock forward and back, moving through your wrist range of motion. Then try with fingers pointing sideways and backward (as tolerated).</p>
      <p><strong>10-15 rocks in each position</strong></p>

      <h2>Strengthening Exercises</h2>

      <h4>6. Wrist Curls</h4>
      <p>Rest your forearm on your thigh or a bench, palm up, wrist over the edge. Hold a light dumbbell. Curl your wrist up, then lower slowly. This strengthens the wrist flexors.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 each wrist</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Reverse Wrist Curls</h4>
      <p>Same position but palm facing down. Lift the back of your hand toward the ceiling. This strengthens the extensors—often weaker than the flexors.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 each wrist</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Radial and Ulnar Deviation</h4>
      <p>Hold a hammer or weighted object at the end of the handle, arm at your side. Tilt your wrist side to side (like a windshield wiper), moving only at the wrist. This strengthens the muscles that stabilize side-to-side movement.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 sets of 12 each direction, each wrist</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Wrist Roller</h4>
      <p>Hold a wrist roller (rod with rope and weight) with arms extended. Roll the weight up by rotating your wrists forward, then reverse. Works both flexors and extensors.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 sets of rolling up and down</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Finger Extension with Rubber Band</h4>
      <p>Place a rubber band around all fingertips. Spread your fingers apart against the resistance. This strengthens the extensors and balances grip strength.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 20 each hand</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Grip Strengthening</h4>
      <p>Squeeze a tennis ball, stress ball, or gripper. Hold for 3-5 seconds, release fully. Strong grip supports wrist stability.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10-15 each hand</strong></p>

      <h2>Exercises for Weight-Bearing Positions</h2>

      <h4>12. Fist Push-Ups</h4>
      <p>If regular push-ups hurt your wrists, try them on your fists. This keeps the wrist neutral and removes the extension demand. Use a padded surface.</p>
      <p><strong>Replace regular push-ups while building wrist strength</strong></p>

      <h4>13. Push-Up Handles or Parallettes</h4>
      <p>These keep your wrists neutral during push-ups and planks. Use while you build wrist mobility and strength.</p>

      <h4>14. Gradual Weight-Bearing Progression</h4>
      <p>Start with quadruped holds (on hands and knees) before progressing to planks and push-ups. Gradually increase the angle and load as wrists adapt.</p>
      <p><strong>Build tolerance slowly over weeks</strong></p>

      <h2>Self-Massage and Release</h2>

      <h4>15. Forearm Self-Massage</h4>
      <p>Use your opposite thumb to massage the muscles of your forearm—both the flexor side (palm up) and extensor side (palm down). Work from elbow toward wrist, finding and holding on tender spots.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 minutes each forearm</strong></p>

      <h4>16. Lacrosse Ball Forearm Roll</h4>
      <p>Place your forearm on a lacrosse ball on a table. Apply pressure and roll slowly, finding tight spots in the forearm muscles that can refer pain to the wrist.</p>
      <p><strong>1-2 minutes each side of each forearm</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Wrist circles: 20 each direction</li>
        <li>Prayer stretch: 30 seconds</li>
        <li>Reverse prayer stretch: 30 seconds</li>
        <li>Tabletop extension stretch: 30 seconds</li>
        <li>Rubber band finger extensions: 20 reps</li>
      </ul>

      <ul>

        <li>Grip work: 3x10</li>
        <li>Quadruped wrist rocks: 10-15 each position</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Before Exercise Involving Wrists</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Wrist circles: 10-15 each direction</li>
        <li>Quadruped rocks: 10 reps</li>
        <li>Light grip activation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>For Computer Users</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Keyboard position:</strong> Wrists should be neutral, not bent up or down</li>
        <li><strong>Mouse technique:</strong> Move from shoulder, not just wrist</li>
        <li><strong>Take breaks:</strong> Every 30-60 minutes, do wrist circles and stretches</li>
        <li><strong>Consider ergonomics:</strong> Vertical mouse, split keyboard, wrist rests (for resting, not while typing)</li>
        <li><strong>Watch typing force:</strong> You don't need to pound the keys</li>
      </ul>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>1-2 weeks:</strong> Improved mobility and reduced daily discomfort</li>
        <li><strong>4-6 weeks:</strong> Noticeable strength gains</li>
        <li><strong>8-12 weeks:</strong> Significant improvement in tolerance for weight-bearing</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance keeps wrists healthy</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to Modify Exercises</h2>

      <p>If certain exercises cause pain:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Reduce range of motion</li>
        <li>Use lighter weight</li>
        <li>Try neutral wrist positions (fist push-ups, handles)</li>
        <li>Focus on mobility before strength</li>
        <li>If pain persists, skip that exercise and reassess</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain that doesn't improve with rest and exercise</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling (possible carpal tunnel)</li>
        <li>Clicking, catching, or locking</li>
        <li>Significant swelling</li>
        <li>Pain that wakes you at night</li>
        <li>Weakness that affects daily function</li>
