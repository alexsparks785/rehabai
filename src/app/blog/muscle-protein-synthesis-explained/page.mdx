      <h2>What Is Muscle Protein Synthesis?</h2>

      <p>
        Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) is the biological process of creating new muscle proteins. Your muscles are constantly in a state of turnover—old, damaged proteins are broken down (muscle protein breakdown, or MPB), and new proteins are built to replace them.
      </p>

      <p>
        <strong>Muscle growth occurs when MPS exceeds MPB over time.</strong>
      </p>

      <p>
        Think of it like a construction site: workers are constantly demolishing old structures (breakdown) and building new ones (synthesis). If construction outpaces demolition, the building gets bigger. That's hypertrophy.
      </p>

      <h2>What Triggers Muscle Protein Synthesis?</h2>

      <p>
        Two primary factors stimulate MPS:
      </p>

      <h3>1. Resistance Training</h3>
      <p>
        Lifting weights is the most powerful stimulus for MPS. When you create mechanical tension in your muscles, you activate signaling pathways (particularly the mTOR pathway) that ramp up protein synthesis.
      </p>

      <p>
        After a training session, MPS is elevated for 24-48 hours, with the peak occurring in the first few hours post-workout. This is the window when your muscles are primed to build new tissue.
      </p>

      <h3>2. Protein Intake</h3>
      <p>
        Eating protein—specifically the amino acids it contains—also stimulates MPS. Leucine, one of the essential amino acids, is particularly powerful at triggering the synthesis response.
      </p>

      <p>
        Protein intake and training have a synergistic effect: consuming protein after training amplifies the MPS response beyond what either stimulus would produce alone.
      </p>

      <h2>Optimizing MPS Through Training</h2>

      <h3>Train Each Muscle Frequently</h3>
      <p>
        Since MPS returns to baseline within 24-48 hours, training each muscle only once per week means you're missing potential growth windows. Training each muscle 2-3 times per week keeps MPS elevated more consistently.
      </p>

      <h3>Volume Matters</h3>
      <p>
        More sets (up to a point) create a greater MPS response. However, there's a ceiling—doing 20 sets for biceps in one session doesn't produce 4x the MPS of 5 sets. Spreading volume across multiple sessions may be more effective.
      </p>

      <h3>Progressive Overload</h3>
      <p>
        Your muscles adapt to training stress. To continue stimulating MPS, you need to progressively increase the challenge—more weight, more reps, or better technique over time.
      </p>

      </p>

      <p>

      </p>

      <p>
        This suggests spreading protein across 3-5 meals rather than consuming it all in one or two large meals.
      </p>

      <p>
        Not all proteins are equal for stimulating MPS:
      </p>

        <li><strong>Plant proteins</strong> can work but may require higher amounts or combining sources</li>

      </p>

        <li>What matters most is total daily intake and distribution</li>
        <li>That said, consuming protein within a few hours post-workout is still a good practice</li>

      </p>

        What matters is the cumulative balance of synthesis over breakdown across weeks and months—not the response to any single meal or workout.
