      <h1>How to Fix Thoracic Stiffness: Exercises for Upper Back Mobility</h1>

      <p>If your upper back feels locked up, turning your torso is difficult, and you can't get your arms fully overhead without arching your lower back—you have thoracic spine stiffness. This extremely common issue affects everything from your posture to your shoulder health to your ability to breathe properly.</p>

      <h2>What Is the Thoracic Spine?</h2>

      <p>Your thoracic spine is the 12 vertebrae in your mid and upper back (T1-T12). It's designed for rotation and extension while providing attachment points for your ribs. Unlike the lower back which should be relatively stable, the thoracic spine should be mobile.</p>

      <p><strong>Problem:</strong> Modern life makes it stiff. Sitting, hunching over phones, and lack of varied movement causes the thoracic spine to lock up.</p>

      <h2>Signs of Thoracic Stiffness</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Difficulty rotating your torso without moving your hips</li>
        <li>Can't get arms fully overhead without arching lower back</li>
        <li>Rounded upper back posture</li>
        <li>Neck pain (compensating for lack of upper back mobility)</li>
        <li>Shoulder impingement symptoms</li>
        <li>Lower back pain (compensating for stiff upper back)</li>
        <li>Difficulty taking a full, deep breath</li>
        <li>Feeling "stuck" in your upper back</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Why Thoracic Mobility Matters</h2>

      <p>When your thoracic spine can't move, other areas compensate:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Shoulders:</strong> Have to work harder to get arms overhead, leading to impingement</li>
        <li><strong>Neck:</strong> Takes on rotation that should come from upper back</li>
        <li><strong>Lower back:</strong> Extends excessively to compensate for stiff upper back</li>
        <li><strong>Breathing:</strong> Ribcage can't expand properly, affecting breath depth</li>
        <li><strong>Posture:</strong> Hard to stand tall when upper back won't extend</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises to Restore Thoracic Mobility</h2>

      <h3>Extension Exercises</h3>

      <h4>1. Foam Roller Thoracic Extension</h4>
      <p>Lie with a foam roller across your upper back (not neck or lower back). Support your head with your hands. Let your upper back extend over the roller. Move the roller to different levels—each segment needs attention. Breathe and relax into the extension.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 minutes, hitting multiple levels</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Cat-Cow with Thoracic Focus</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, focus the movement on your upper back. For "cow," think about lifting your chest and pulling shoulder blades together. For "cat," push the area between your shoulder blades toward the ceiling. Keep lower back relatively still.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10 slow cycles</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Prone Press-Up</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with hands beside your shoulders. Press your chest up while keeping hips on the ground. Focus on extending through the upper back, not just the lower back. Hold at top for 2-3 seconds.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Bench Thoracic Extension</h4>
      <p>Kneel facing a bench or chair. Place your elbows on the surface with palms together (prayer position). Sit your hips back while letting your chest drop through your arms toward the floor. Feel the extension through your upper back.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds</strong></p>

      <h3>Rotation Exercises</h3>

      <h4>5. Thread the Needle</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, reach one arm under your body, rotating your thoracic spine. Follow your hand with your eyes. Then reverse, opening up and reaching that arm toward the ceiling. Focus on rotation coming from the upper back, not just the shoulder.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 8-10 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Open Book</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side with knees bent and stacked, arms extended in front of you. Keeping your knees together, rotate your top arm open toward the other side, following with your eyes and letting your upper back rotate. Keep knees pinned together so the rotation comes from the thoracic spine.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 8-10 each side, holding end position 2-3 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Seated Thoracic Rotation</h4>
      <p>Sit on the floor with legs extended or sit in a chair. Cross your arms over your chest. Rotate your upper body to one side as far as you can without moving your hips. Focus on wringing out your upper back like a towel. Return and go the other way.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10 each direction</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Quadruped Rotation</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, place one hand behind your head. Rotate that elbow toward your opposite arm, then rotate up toward the ceiling, opening your chest. Keep your hips still—all rotation should come from the upper back.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Combined Mobility</h3>

      <h4>9. Wall Windmill</h4>
      <p>Stand sideways to a wall, about arm's length away. Place your near hand on the wall at shoulder height. Reach your far arm up and over, rotating your thoracic spine as you reach toward the wall on the other side. Return slowly.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 8 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Kneeling Thoracic Extension-Rotation</h4>
      <p>Start in a half-kneeling position. Place one hand behind your head. Rotate toward the down knee (closing), then rotate up and back (opening), extending through the upper back as you open. Combine rotation with extension.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 8 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Breathing Integration</h3>

      <h4>11. Crocodile Breathing</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with forehead on hands. Breathe into your belly (you'll feel it press into the floor) and also into your back. Focus on expanding your ribcage in all directions, especially laterally and posteriorly. This mobilizes the ribcage and thoracic joints.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 minutes of relaxed breathing</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Foam Roller Snow Angels</h4>
      <p>Lie lengthwise on a foam roller (head to tailbone on the roller). Let your arms fall out to the sides. Slowly move arms in a snow angel pattern—from by your sides up toward overhead. This combines thoracic extension with shoulder mobility.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10 slow reps</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Daily Mobility (10-12 minutes)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Foam roller thoracic extension: 2-3 minutes</li>
        <li>Cat-cow with thoracic focus: 10 cycles</li>
        <li>Thread the needle: 8 each side</li>
        <li>Open book: 8 each side</li>
        <li>Bench thoracic extension: 45-60 seconds</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Enhanced Session (2-3x/week, add to daily)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Quadruped rotation: 2x10 each side</li>
        <li>Wall windmill: 2x8 each side</li>
        <li>Kneeling extension-rotation: 2x8 each side</li>
        <li>Crocodile breathing: 2-3 minutes</li>
        <li>Foam roller snow angels: 2x10</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Tips for Better Results</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Breathe:</strong> Exhale as you move into end ranges—it allows more mobility</li>
        <li><strong>Go slow:</strong> Rushing doesn't improve mobility</li>
        <li><strong>Focus on upper back:</strong> Don't let movement come from lower back or neck</li>
        <li><strong>Daily consistency:</strong> Short daily sessions beat occasional long ones</li>
        <li><strong>Don't force:</strong> Work at your available range and gradually expand it</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Throughout Your Day</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Sitting:</strong> Change positions frequently; don't stay hunched</li>
        <li><strong>Standing:</strong> Think about lifting your chest without arching lower back</li>
        <li><strong>Movement breaks:</strong> Every 30-60 minutes, do a few rotations or extensions</li>
        <li><strong>Deep breaths:</strong> Periodically take full breaths that expand your ribcage</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to See Improvement?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Immediately:</strong> Temporary improvement after mobility work</li>
        <li><strong>2-4 weeks:</strong> Noticeable and lasting improvement with daily practice</li>
        <li><strong>6-8 weeks:</strong> Significant gains in rotation and extension</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance required; stiffness returns without consistent work</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Mistakes</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Moving from the wrong segments:</strong> Make sure rotation and extension come from thoracic spine, not lower back or neck</li>
        <li><strong>Going too fast:</strong> Mobility work requires time in position</li>
        <li><strong>Only doing extension:</strong> Rotation is equally important</li>
        <li><strong>Inconsistency:</strong> Once a week won't create lasting change</li>
        <li><strong>Holding breath:</strong> Breathing helps mobility; don't brace and hold</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <p>Get evaluated if:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain is severe or sharp</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling in arms or torso</li>
        <li>History of spinal injury or surgery</li>
        <li>Stiffness came on suddenly</li>
        <li>No improvement after consistent work</li>
        <li>Difficulty breathing that's not improved by mobility work</li>
      </ul>

When your upper back moves properly, your neck, shoulders, and lower back all function better.
