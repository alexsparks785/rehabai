# How to Fix Knock Knees: Exercises for Better Knee Alignment

Knock knees—when your knees angle inward and touch while your ankles stay apart—isn't just cosmetic. It affects how you walk, run, and squat, and can lead to knee pain, hip issues, and ankle problems. Here's how to fix it.

## What Are Knock Knees?

Medically called "genu valgum," knock knees is when the legs angle inward at the knees:
- Knees touch or nearly touch when standing
- Ankles remain apart
- Feet may turn outward to compensate
- Often more visible during movement (squatting, stairs)

## Is Your Knock Knees Structural or Functional?

**Structural** – Bone shape is different (harder to change)
**Functional** – Muscle weakness/tightness causes collapse (very fixable)

**How to tell:** If your knees align better when you consciously squeeze your glutes, it's likely functional.

**Most adult knock knees is functional** – caused by weak hips and tight muscles.

## What Causes Functional Knock Knees?

The knee collapses inward because of imbalances above and below:

**Weak muscles:**
- Gluteus medius and maximus (can't control hip rotation)
- Hip external rotators
- VMO (inner quad)

**Tight muscles:**
- Adductors (inner thigh)
- IT band / TFL
- Hip internal rotators

**Result:** When you squat, jump, or walk, weak hips can't control the femur, and knees cave inward.

## The Fix: Strengthen Hips, Stretch Tight Muscles

The solution focuses on:
1. Strengthening glute medius and external rotators
2. Stretching tight adductors and TFL
3. Practicing proper alignment in movement

## Strengthening Exercises for Knock Knees

### 1. Clamshell

**Foundational glute medius exercise.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, knees bent, feet together
2. Open top knee toward ceiling
3. Keep feet touching and don't rotate hips
4. Hold briefly at top
5. Lower with control

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 15-20 each side

**Progression:** Add resistance band above knees.

### 2. Side-Lying Hip Abduction

**Direct glute medius strengthening.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, bottom leg bent, top leg straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. Keep toes pointing forward (not up)
4. Control the lowering

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 15 each side

**Key:** Don't rotate leg outward—maintain neutral hip.

### 3. Monster Walk (Lateral Band Walk)

**Functional glute medius activation.**

**How to do it:**
1. Band around legs (above knees or ankles)
2. Quarter squat position
3. Step sideways, maintaining tension
4. Keep knees pushed out
5. Don't let knees cave

**Sets/reps:** 2-3 sets × 15 steps each direction

### 4. Banded Squat

**Teaches proper knee alignment.**

**How to do it:**
1. Band above knees
2. Squat with band trying to pull knees in
3. Actively push knees out against band
4. Maintain outward pressure throughout

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 12 reps

**Why it works:** Trains glutes to fire and resist knee valgus during squatting.

### 5. Single-Leg Glute Bridge

**Unilateral hip strength.**

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, one knee bent, other leg extended
2. Drive through heel, lift hips
3. Keep pelvis level (don't drop on lifted side)
4. Lower with control

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 10-12 each side

### 6. Step-Down with Alignment Focus

**Functional eccentric control.**

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on box or step
2. Slowly lower one foot toward ground
3. Keep standing knee tracking over toes—not inward
4. Lightly tap heel, return to top

**Sets/reps:** 3 sets × 10 each leg

**Key:** Control the knee—don't let it collapse inward.

### 7. Fire Hydrant

**External rotation strengthening.**

**How to do it:**
1. On hands and knees
2. Keeping knee bent, lift leg out to side
3. Like a dog at a fire hydrant
4. Control the lowering

**Sets/reps:** 2 sets × 12-15 each side

### 8. Standing Hip External Rotation

**Targets external rotators.**

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on one leg, knee slightly bent
2. Rotate standing leg outward (knee turns out)
3. Use hip muscles, not just turning foot
4. Hold 5 seconds, return

**Sets/reps:** 2 sets × 10 each side

## Stretches for Knock Knees

### 1. Adductor Stretch (Butterfly)

**Opens tight inner thighs.**

**How to do it:**
1. Sit, soles of feet together
2. Knees fall out to sides
3. Gently press knees down
4. Lean forward for deeper stretch
5. Hold 30-60 seconds

### 2. Standing Adductor Stretch

**Alternative inner thigh stretch.**

**How to do it:**
1. Wide stance, toes forward
2. Shift weight to one side
3. Bend that knee, feel stretch in other inner thigh
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### 3. IT Band / TFL Stretch

**Releases lateral tightness.**

**How to do it:**
1. Cross one leg behind the other
2. Lean toward the back leg's side
3. Reach arm overhead for more stretch
4. Feel stretch on outside of hip
5. Hold 30 seconds each side

### 4. Foam Roll IT Band

**Releases lateral tension.**

**How to do it:**
1. Side-lying on foam roller (outer thigh)
2. Roll from hip to just above knee
3. Pause on tender spots
4. 1-2 minutes each side

**Note:** This is uncomfortable but effective.

## Complete Knock Knee Correction Routine

### Daily Quick Routine (10 minutes)

**Strengthening (6 minutes):**
- Clamshells: 15 each side
- Side-lying hip abduction: 12 each side
- Monster walks: 15 steps each direction
- Fire hydrants: 10 each side

**Stretching (4 minutes):**
- Butterfly stretch: 45 seconds
- IT band stretch: 30 seconds each side
- Standing adductor stretch: 30 seconds each side

### Full Correction Session (25 minutes)

**Strengthening (15 minutes):**
1. Clamshells (banded): 3 × 15 each side
2. Side-lying hip abduction: 3 × 12 each side
3. Monster walks: 3 × 15 steps each direction
4. Banded squats: 3 × 12
5. Single-leg glute bridge: 2 × 12 each side
6. Step-downs: 2 × 10 each side

**Stretching (8 minutes):**
1. Foam roll IT band: 1.5 min each side
2. Butterfly stretch: 1 minute
3. Standing adductor stretch: 45 sec each side
4. IT band/TFL stretch: 30 sec each side

**Alignment Practice (2 minutes):**
- Bodyweight squats with focus on knees out
- Single-leg balance with knee alignment awareness

## Movement Cues for Better Alignment

### Squatting
- "Spread the floor" with your feet
- "Knees out" throughout the movement
- Don't let knees cave at bottom or on way up

### Walking/Running
- Avoid feet turning out excessively
- Think of tracking knee over middle of foot
- Strengthen before adding running volume

### Stairs
- Knee tracks over toes, not inward
- Control each step down
- Use for practice

### Standing
- Weight distributed evenly
- Slight activation of glutes
- Avoid hyperextending knees

## How Long Until Improvement?

**Week 1-2:** Learn exercises, feel muscles activating

**Week 3-4:** Better awareness of knee position during movement

**Month 2:** Visible improvement in knee alignment during activities

**Month 3+:** New alignment becomes more automatic

**Consistency is key:** Daily exercises, even brief ones, create change faster than occasional longer sessions.

## When to See a Professional

Consult a physical therapist or doctor if:
- Significant pain with knock knees
- One knee notably worse than other
- History of injury to hip, knee, or ankle
- Knock knees developed suddenly
- No improvement after consistent work
- Under 7 or over 18 with severe knock knees

## Common Mistakes

### Only Stretching
- Knock knees is primarily a weakness problem
- Must strengthen glute medius and external rotators
- Stretching alone doesn't fix it

### Ignoring Movement Patterns
- Can do all the exercises but still squat with knees caving
- Practice proper alignment during movement
- Use banded squats to retrain pattern

### Expecting Quick Results
- Functional issues take time to correct
- Muscles need to strengthen
- Patterns need to change
- Give it 2-3 months minimum

### Using Too Much Weight
- Focus on form first
- Heavy weight before proper alignment reinforces bad patterns

## The Bottom Line

Knock knees is usually fixable with consistent hip strengthening. Clamshells, side-lying hip abduction, and monster walks build the glute medius strength needed to control knee position.

Stretch tight adductors and IT band. Practice keeping knees tracking properly during squats and stairs. Be patient—alignment changes take months of consistent work.

Strong hips = straight knees. Start today.
