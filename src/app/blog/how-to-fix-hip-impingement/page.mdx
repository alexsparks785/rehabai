      <h1>How to Fix Hip Impingement: Exercises for FAI Relief</h1>

      <p>Sharp pain in your groin when you squat deep, bring your knee to your chest, or sit for long periods? You might have hip impingement—also called femoroacetabular impingement (FAI). While this condition involves bone shape, the right exercises can significantly reduce symptoms and improve function for many people.</p>

      <h2>What Is Hip Impingement?</h2>

      <p>Hip impingement occurs when the ball (femoral head) and socket (acetabulum) of your hip joint don't fit together smoothly. Extra bone on one or both creates friction and pinching, especially in certain positions.</p>

      <p><strong>Types of FAI:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Cam:</strong> Extra bone on the ball (femoral head) creates a bump</li>
        <li><strong>Pincer:</strong> Extra bone on the socket (acetabulum) creates overcoverage</li>
        <li><strong>Combined:</strong> Both cam and pincer morphology (most common)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Symptoms</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Groin pain, especially with hip flexion</li>
        <li>Pain at end range of motion</li>
        <li>Pinching sensation in the front of the hip</li>
        <li>Stiffness after sitting</li>
        <li>Pain with squatting, especially deep squats</li>
        <li>Discomfort bringing knee to chest</li>
        <li>Pain with prolonged sitting</li>
        <li>Clicking or catching sensation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Important: Get a Diagnosis First</h2>

      <p>Hip impingement symptoms overlap with many other conditions (labral tears, hip flexor strains, sports hernias). Before assuming you have FAI, get proper imaging and diagnosis from a healthcare provider. These exercises are for confirmed or suspected impingement—not a substitute for diagnosis.</p>

      <h2>Can Exercise Fix Hip Impingement?</h2>

      <p>Exercise can't change the bone shape that causes impingement. However, exercise can:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Reduce muscle tightness that worsens symptoms</li>
        <li>Strengthen muscles that improve hip mechanics</li>
        <li>Improve movement patterns to reduce irritation</li>
        <li>Increase functional mobility within your available range</li>
        <li>Decrease pain and improve quality of life</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Many people with FAI morphology (bone shape) are completely asymptomatic. The goal is to become one of them—managing symptoms without surgery.</p>

      <h2>What to Avoid</h2>

      <p>Before we get to helpful exercises, know what makes impingement worse:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Deep squatting (past where pain starts)</li>
        <li>Bringing knee to chest forcefully</li>
        <li>Hip flexion + internal rotation + adduction (the impingement position)</li>
        <li>Aggressive hip stretching into painful ranges</li>
        <li>Prolonged sitting with hips below knees</li>
        <li>Exercises that repeatedly jam the hip into end range</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises for Hip Impingement</h2>

      <h3>Reduce Muscle Tightness (Gently)</h3>

      <h4>1. Supine Hip Flexor Stretch (Modified)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back at the edge of a bed or table. Pull one knee toward your chest (gently—don't force it). Let the other leg hang off the edge. This stretches the hip flexor without putting the hip in the impingement position. Don't pull the knee aggressively.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Piriformis Stretch</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back with knees bent. Cross one ankle over the opposite knee. Gently pull the bottom thigh toward you. This stretches the deep hip rotators without hip flexion. If you feel pinching in the front of the hip, back off.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Quadriceps Foam Roll</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with a foam roller under your thighs. Roll slowly from hip to just above the knee. Tight quads can contribute to anterior hip tightness. Don't roll directly over the hip joint.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Adductor Rockback Stretch</h4>
      <p>Kneel with one leg extended out to the side, foot flat. Gently rock your hips back, feeling a stretch in the inner thigh of the extended leg. This addresses adductor tightness that can contribute to hip mechanics issues.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Strengthen Supporting Muscles</h3>

      <h4>5. Glute Bridge</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Squeeze your glutes and lift your hips. Focus on glute engagement, not pushing through your lower back. Strong glutes improve hip mechanics and take stress off the joint. Hold 2-3 seconds at top.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Single-Leg Glute Bridge</h4>
      <p>Same as above but with one leg extended or held toward chest. This increases glute demand. Keep hips level—don't let the unsupported side drop.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10-12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Clamshell</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side, hips and knees bent about 45 degrees. Keep feet together and lift your top knee toward the ceiling. Don't let your hips roll back. This strengthens the hip external rotators and abductors.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Side-Lying Hip Abduction</h4>
      <p>Lie on your side with bottom leg bent for stability. Lift the top leg toward the ceiling, leading with the heel. Keep the leg slightly behind your body (in slight hip extension), not in front. This avoids the impingement position while strengthening glute medius.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Quadruped Hip Extension</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend one leg straight back. Keep your spine neutral—don't arch your lower back. This strengthens the glutes and hamstrings in a hip-friendly position. Hold 2 seconds at the top.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Standing Hip Extension with Band</h4>
      <p>With a band around your ankles, stand on one leg and extend the other leg behind you against the band resistance. Keep your torso upright. This builds hip extensor strength without flexion.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Core Stability</h3>

      <h4>11. Dead Bug (Modified Range)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, arms toward ceiling, knees bent at 90 degrees. Press your lower back into the floor. Slowly lower one leg (don't go too far—stop before hip flexion becomes excessive). Return and switch. This builds core stability while controlling hip range.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 8-10 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Bird Dog</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg while keeping your spine neutral. This trains core stability and hip extension strength simultaneously. Move slowly and with control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Hip Mobility (Within Pain-Free Range)</h3>

      <h4>13. Hip Circles (Quadruped)</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, make small circles with one knee. Start with tiny circles and gradually increase size—but stop before you feel pinching. This lubricates the joint and explores available range gently.</p>
      <p><strong>10 circles each direction, each side</strong></p>

      <h4>14. 90-90 Hip Switches (Modified)</h4>
      <p>Sit with one leg in front, one to the side, both at 90-degree angles. Slowly switch positions, windshield-wipering your legs. Only go as far as comfortable—don't force into pinching. This moves the hip through rotation without end-range flexion.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 8 transitions</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Daily (10-12 minutes)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Supine hip flexor stretch: 45 seconds each side</li>
        <li>Piriformis stretch: 45 seconds each side</li>
        <li>Hip circles (quadruped): 10 each direction, each side</li>
        <li>Glute bridge: 15 reps</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Strength Sessions (3-4x/week)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Single-leg glute bridge: 3x10 each side</li>
        <li>Clamshell: 3x15 each side</li>
        <li>Side-lying hip abduction: 3x15 each side</li>
        <li>Quadruped hip extension: 3x12 each side</li>
        <li>Dead bug: 3x8 each side</li>
        <li>Bird dog: 3x10 each side</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Modifying Activities</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Squatting:</strong> Use a wider stance, toes pointed out more; don't go as deep</li>
        <li><strong>Sitting:</strong> Keep hips at or above knee level; take frequent breaks</li>
        <li><strong>Cycling:</strong> Raise seat height to reduce hip flexion angle</li>
        <li><strong>Sleeping:</strong> Pillow between knees if side sleeping</li>
        <li><strong>Stretching:</strong> Avoid aggressive hip flexor and groin stretches</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to See Improvement?</h2>

      <ul>

        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Significant functional improvement for many people</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance exercises remain important</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When Surgery Is Considered</h2>

      <p>Conservative management (exercise + activity modification) should be tried for at least 3-6 months before considering surgery. Surgery may be appropriate if:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Significant labral damage is present</li>
        <li>Symptoms persist despite consistent conservative care</li>
        <li>Pain significantly limits daily activities</li>
        <li>You're an athlete with specific performance demands</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Many people manage FAI successfully without surgery through consistent exercise, activity modification, and patience.</p>

        <li>Pain is severe or worsening</li>
        <li>Symptoms don't improve with conservative care</li>
        <li>You have locking, catching, or giving way</li>
        <li>Pain at rest or at night</li>
      </ul>

Focus on what you can control—muscle strength and movement patterns—and work within your available range rather than fighting against your anatomy.</p>
