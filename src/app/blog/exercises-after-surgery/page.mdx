# Exercises After Surgery: Safe Recovery Movement Guide

Movement after surgery speeds recovery, prevents complications, and helps you return to normal life faster. This guide covers general principles and exercises that apply to most surgical recoveries. Always follow your surgeon's specific instructions, as they supersede general guidelines.

## Why Exercise After Surgery Matters

**Benefits of appropriate post-surgical movement:**
- Prevents blood clots (deep vein thrombosis)
- Reduces risk of pneumonia
- Prevents muscle atrophy
- Maintains joint mobility
- Speeds healing (circulation brings nutrients)
- Reduces constipation
- Improves mood and mental health
- Shortens recovery time

**Risks of too little movement:**
- Blood clots in legs
- Lung complications
- Muscle weakness
- Joint stiffness
- Slower healing
- Depression

## General Timeline

**Note:** Your surgeon's guidance takes precedence. This is a general framework.

### Phase 1: Immediate Post-Op (Days 1-7)

**Goals:**
- Prevent blood clots
- Maintain basic circulation
- Gentle breathing exercises
- Basic mobility (getting out of bed if cleared)

**Typical activities:**
- Ankle pumps (most important)
- Deep breathing exercises
- Gentle walking (if cleared)
- Basic range of motion

### Phase 2: Early Recovery (Weeks 1-4)

**Goals:**
- Gradually increase activity
- Restore basic function
- Maintain range of motion
- Begin gentle strengthening

**Typical activities:**
- Extended walking
- Light stretching
- Gentle strengthening of unaffected areas
- Physical therapy exercises if prescribed

### Phase 3: Intermediate Recovery (Weeks 4-12)

**Goals:**
- Rebuild strength
- Restore full range of motion
- Progress activity level
- Return to modified daily activities

**Typical activities:**
- Progressive strengthening
- Cardio progression
- Functional movements
- Sport-specific prep (later in phase)

### Phase 4: Full Recovery (Months 3-6+)

**Goals:**
- Return to normal activities
- Full strength restoration
- Sport/activity return (if applicable)
- Long-term maintenance

## Immediate Post-Surgery Exercises

**Safe for most surgeries (confirm with your care team):**

### Breathing Exercises (Critical)

**Deep Breathing**
- Inhale slowly and deeply through nose
- Fill lungs completely
- Exhale slowly through mouth
- 10 breaths every hour while awake
- Prevents pneumonia, especially after abdominal/chest surgery

**Incentive Spirometer** (if given)
- Follow instructions provided
- Use 10 times every hour while awake
- Aim to reach your target volume

**Coughing** (if safe for your surgery)
- Support incision with pillow if abdominal surgery
- Cough gently to clear lungs
- Important after general anesthesia

### Circulation Exercises

**Ankle Pumps**
- Point toes down, then pull up toward shin
- 10-20 reps every hour
- Most important exercise for DVT prevention
- Can do in bed

**Ankle Circles**
- Rotate ankles in circles
- 10 each direction
- Each foot

**Quad Sets**
- Tighten thigh muscles
- Push back of knee into bed
- Hold 5 seconds
- 10 reps each leg

**Glute Squeezes**
- Squeeze buttocks together
- Hold 5 seconds
- 10 reps

### Getting Out of Bed

**When cleared to get up:**

**Technique:**
1. Roll onto your side
2. Use arms to push up while swinging legs off bed
3. Sit at edge for a moment (prevent dizziness)
4. Stand when stable
5. Have support nearby

**First walks:**
- Short distances (to bathroom, around room)
- Use assistive device if provided
- Have someone with you
- Don't overdo it

## Early Recovery Exercises

**Typically safe during weeks 1-4 (confirm with surgeon):**

### Walking Program

**Week 1:**
- Walk to bathroom and back
- Short walks in home/hospital
- Rest as needed

**Week 2:**
- Multiple short walks (5-10 minutes)
- Increase distance gradually
- Total 15-30 minutes daily

**Week 3-4:**
- Longer continuous walks (10-20 minutes)
- Multiple times daily
- Total 30-45 minutes daily

### Gentle Range of Motion

**For unaffected joints:**

**Neck:**
- Gentle rotations
- Side tilts
- Chin tucks
- 5-10 reps each, slowly

**Shoulders** (if not surgical site):
- Shrugs
- Gentle circles
- Pendulum swings
- 10 reps each

**Wrists and Hands:**
- Wrist circles
- Finger spreads
- Make fists
- 10 reps each

**Ankles:**
- Continue pumps and circles
- Increase to 20-30 reps

### Gentle Strengthening (Non-Surgical Areas)

**Seated Exercises:**
- Seated marching
- Knee extensions
- Heel raises
- Arm exercises with light weights (if upper body not affected)

### Stretching

**Gentle stretches for areas not affected by surgery:**
- Hold 20-30 seconds
- No bouncing
- Stop if any pulling on surgical site
- Focus on maintaining flexibility

## Exercises by Surgery Type

### After Abdominal Surgery

**Precautions:**
- Avoid heavy lifting (usually 6-8 weeks)
- No abdominal exercises initially
- Support incision when coughing
- Watch for hernia signs

**Safe early exercises:**
- Walking (most important)
- Ankle pumps
- Deep breathing
- Gentle lower body stretches

**Progress to:**
- Pelvic tilts (when cleared)
- Gentle core activation
- Light upper body exercise
- Gradually increase walking

### After Orthopedic Surgery (General)

**Follow specific protocol for your surgery** (ACL, hip replacement, etc. have different requirements)

**General principles:**
- Follow weight-bearing restrictions
- Use prescribed assistive devices
- Attend physical therapy
- Don't rush progression

**Common exercises:**
- Ankle pumps
- Quad sets
- Glute squeezes
- Range of motion as allowed

### After Cardiac Surgery

**Supervised progression is important**

**Early:**
- Deep breathing critical
- Ankle pumps
- Very gentle walking
- Monitor symptoms closely

**Cardiac rehab:**
- Usually begins 2-6 weeks post-surgery
- Supervised exercise program
- Gradual progression
- Education on heart-healthy lifestyle

### After Breast Surgery

**Precautions:**
- Follow restrictions on arm movement
- Watch for lymphedema
- Gradual progression

**Early:**
- Walking
- Gentle hand/wrist exercises
- Deep breathing
- Lower body exercises

**Progress to:**
- Gentle shoulder range of motion (when cleared)
- Light stretching
- Specific exercises for lymphedema prevention

## Signs to Stop Exercising

**Stop and contact your surgeon if:**
- Increased pain at surgical site
- New swelling or redness
- Fever
- Discharge from incision
- Chest pain
- Shortness of breath beyond normal exertion
- Bleeding
- Dizziness or fainting
- Any concerning symptoms

## Pain Management and Exercise

### When Pain is Barrier

**Strategies:**
- Time exercise with pain medication peak
- Start very gently
- Use ice after exercise if appropriate
- Short sessions multiple times vs. one long session
- Communicate with healthcare team

### Distinguishing Good and Bad Pain

**Normal discomfort:**
- Muscle fatigue
- Mild soreness
- Slight tightness
- Improves with gentle movement

**Warning pain:**
- Sharp, stabbing pain
- Pain at surgical site that worsens
- Pain that doesn't improve with rest
- New or different pain

## Returning to Normal Activity

### Timeline Expectations

**Light daily activities:** 2-6 weeks (varies by surgery)
**Driving:** When off pain medications and can react quickly
**Work (desk job):** 1-4 weeks typically
**Work (physical):** 6-12 weeks typically
**Exercise:** Gradual return, often 6-12 weeks
**Sports:** 3-12 months depending on surgery and sport

### Gradual Progression Principles

**Increase slowly:**
- Add 10-15% per week maximum
- Listen to your body
- Rest days are important
- Expect setbacks

**Signs you're progressing well:**
- Decreased pain over time
- Increased energy
- Better function
- Meeting physical therapy goals

**Signs you're doing too much:**
- Increased pain
- Swelling
- Fatigue lasting days
- Regression in function

## Working with Healthcare Team

### Physical Therapy

**When prescribed, PT provides:**
- Exercises specific to your surgery
- Hands-on treatment
- Progression guidance
- Functional training

**Maximize PT by:**
- Attending all sessions
- Doing home exercises consistently
- Asking questions
- Communicating honestly about pain/progress

### Follow-Up Appointments

**Prepare by noting:**
- Current activity level
- Pain levels
- Questions about progression
- Any concerns

### Communication

**Don't hesitate to ask:**
- When can I do [specific activity]?
- Is this symptom normal?
- Can I progress faster/should I slow down?
- What should I watch for?

## Mental Health During Recovery

**Surgery recovery can be mentally challenging:**

### Normal Feelings
- Frustration with limitations
- Anxiety about recovery
- Sadness or depression
- Impatience

### Exercise Benefits for Mental Health
- Even gentle movement improves mood
- Creates sense of progress
- Provides structure to day
- Releases endorphins

### Coping Strategies
- Set small, achievable goals
- Celebrate progress
- Stay connected with others
- Ask for help when needed

## Sample Recovery Schedule

### Week 1 Post-Surgery

**Multiple times daily:**
- Deep breathing: 10 breaths
- Ankle pumps: 20 reps
- Glute squeezes: 10 reps

**2-3 times daily:**
- Short walks (as cleared): 5-10 minutes each

### Week 2-3 Post-Surgery

**Daily:**
- Walking: 15-20 minutes total (can be broken up)
- Breathing exercises
- Ankle and circulation exercises
- Gentle stretching (non-surgical areas)

### Week 4-6 Post-Surgery

**Daily:**
- Walking: 20-30 minutes
- Range of motion exercises
- Beginning strengthening (as cleared)
- Stretching routine

### Week 6-12 Post-Surgery

**Following surgeon/PT guidance:**
- Progressive strengthening
- Cardio progression
- Functional training
- Activity-specific exercises

## Key Takeaways

**Essential principles:**
1. **Move early, move gently**: Appropriate movement speeds recovery
2. **Follow your surgeon's instructions**: They know your specific situation
3. **Ankle pumps are critical**: DVT prevention is life-saving
4. **Deep breathing matters**: Prevents lung complications
5. **Progress gradually**: Patience prevents setbacks
6. **Communicate concerns**: Don't suffer in silence

**The recovery mindset:**
- Every surgery is different
- Compare yourself to yesterday, not to others
- Setbacks are normal
- Rest is part of recovery
- Movement is medicine

**Most important exercises:**
- Ankle pumps (circulation)
- Deep breathing (lung health)
- Walking (overall recovery)
- Exercises prescribed by your care team

Your surgeon and physical therapist are your best resources for specific guidance. This guide provides general principles, but your individualized plan is what matters most. Follow your medical team's advice, be patient with yourself, and trust the process.
