# How to Fix Diastasis Recti: Exercises That Actually Work

Diastasis recti—the separation of the abdominal muscles—is incredibly common after pregnancy, but it's not just a cosmetic issue. It affects core function, can contribute to back pain, and won't fix itself without targeted work. Here's how to actually close the gap and rebuild a functional core.

## What Is Diastasis Recti?

Diastasis recti occurs when the two sides of the rectus abdominis (your "six-pack" muscles) separate along the linea alba—the connective tissue running down the center of your abdomen. This tissue stretches and thins, creating a gap.

### Who Gets It?
- **Pregnant women:** Extremely common—up to 60% at 6 weeks postpartum
- **Postpartum women:** Many still have it months or years later
- **Men:** Can develop from obesity, improper exercise, or surgery
- **Anyone:** Chronic abdominal pressure can cause it

### Signs of Diastasis Recti
- Visible bulge or "doming" along the midline when doing ab work
- A gap you can feel between the muscles
- Lower back pain
- Poor posture
- Pelvic floor issues
- Weak core that doesn't respond to traditional ab exercises
- Belly that looks pregnant long after delivery

## Self-Assessment: Do You Have It?

### The Finger Test
1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Place your fingers horizontally across your belly button
3. Lift your head and shoulders slightly off the floor (small crunch)
4. Feel for a gap between the muscles
5. Check above and below the belly button too

**What the gap means:**
- 1-2 finger widths: Normal or mild
- 2-3 finger widths: Moderate diastasis
- 3+ finger widths: Significant diastasis

**Also assess:**
- Depth: How deep can your fingers sink? Shallow is better.
- Tension: Can you generate tension in the tissue when you contract? This matters as much as width.

## What Makes Diastasis Worse

Before focusing on exercises, stop doing things that increase the problem:

### Movements to Avoid (Initially)
- Traditional crunches and sit-ups
- Planks (until you can control the bulging)
- Any exercise that causes abdominal doming
- Heavy lifting that spikes intra-abdominal pressure
- Twisting movements with load
- Getting up from lying by sitting straight up

### Habits to Address
- Chronic constipation (straining)
- Breath-holding during exertion
- Poor posture (rib flare, swayback)
- Sucking in your stomach constantly
- Heavy lifting without proper core engagement

### How to Get Up from Lying Down
1. Roll to your side first
2. Use your arm to push yourself up
3. Don't sit straight up from your back

## The Foundation: Breathing and Connection

Before any exercises, you need to reconnect with your deep core.

### Diaphragmatic Breathing
**Why:** Proper breathing coordinates the diaphragm, pelvic floor, and deep abdominals.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back, knees bent
2. Place one hand on chest, one on belly
3. Breathe so your belly rises (not your chest)
4. As you exhale, feel your belly gently fall
5. Practice 5 minutes daily

### Connection Breath (Core Engagement)
**How to do it:**
1. Inhale and let your belly expand
2. As you exhale, gently draw your belly button toward your spine
3. Think of "hugging" your baby (or where your baby was) with your abs
4. The movement is subtle—not a hard brace
5. Try to feel your pelvic floor gently lift as you exhale

**Key:** This is NOT sucking in. It's a gentle engagement that happens WITH the breath.

## Exercises to Fix Diastasis Recti

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

#### 1. Heel Slides with Core Breath
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Exhale and gently engage your core
3. Slowly slide one heel away from you along the floor
4. Keep your back flat (don't let it arch)
5. Inhale and slide the heel back
6. Alternate legs
7. Do 10 reps per leg

**Key:** If your back arches or your belly bulges, you've gone too far.

#### 2. Dead Bug (Modified)
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet off the floor (tabletop position)
2. Arms reaching toward the ceiling
3. Exhale, engage core, and slowly lower ONE leg toward the floor
4. Only go as far as you can without your back arching or belly doming
5. Inhale and return
6. Do 8-10 reps per leg

**Progression:** Eventually lower opposite arm and leg together.

#### 3. Pelvic Tilts with Core Activation
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Exhale and gently flatten your lower back into the floor
3. Feel your deep abs engage
4. Hold for 5-10 seconds
5. Relax and repeat
6. Do 15-20 reps

#### 4. Bird Dog (Hands and Knees)
**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Exhale and engage core
3. Extend ONE arm forward (keep hips level)
4. Return and switch arms
5. Progress to extending one leg back
6. Eventually: opposite arm and leg together
7. Do 8-10 per side

### Phase 2: Building Strength (Weeks 4-8)

#### 5. Modified Plank (On Knees or Incline)
**How to do it:**
1. Start in plank position on your knees (or hands on a bench/wall)
2. Engage your core without letting belly sag or dome
3. Hold for 10-20 seconds
4. If you see doming, go to an easier variation
5. Build up hold time gradually

**Progression:** Lower the incline or move toward full plank as control improves.

#### 6. Toe Taps
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, legs in tabletop (knees at 90 degrees)
2. Exhale, engage core
3. Slowly lower one foot to tap the floor
4. Keep the other leg stable
5. Inhale and return
6. Alternate legs
7. Do 10-12 per leg

#### 7. Glute Bridge with Core Focus
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Exhale and engage core FIRST
3. Then lift hips off the floor
4. Squeeze glutes at top
5. Lower slowly
6. Do 15 reps

**Key:** The core engagement comes before and during the hip lift.

#### 8. Side-Lying Core Activation
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your side, knees bent
2. Exhale and gently draw in your lower belly
3. Lift your waist slightly off the floor (small movement)
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. Do 10 reps per side

### Phase 3: Functional Integration (Weeks 8+)

#### 9. Pallof Press (Anti-Rotation)
**How to do it:**
1. Stand sideways to a cable or resistance band
2. Hold the band/cable at your chest
3. Exhale, engage core, and press arms straight out
4. Don't let your body rotate
5. Hold 3 seconds and return
6. Do 10 reps per side

#### 10. Farmer's Carries
**How to do it:**
1. Hold a weight in each hand (or one hand for added challenge)
2. Engage your core
3. Walk with good posture
4. Don't let your belly sag or rib cage flare
5. Walk 30-40 steps

#### 11. Squat with Core Awareness
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
2. Exhale and engage core as you squat down
3. Keep ribs down (no flaring)
4. Stand and squeeze glutes
5. Do 12-15 reps

#### 12. Full Plank (When Ready)
**Readiness criteria:**
- Can do modified plank without doming
- Can maintain core engagement throughout
- No bearing down or breath-holding

**How to do it:**
1. Full plank position on hands or forearms
2. Keep hips level (not sagging or piking)
3. Maintain gentle core engagement
4. Build up to 30-60 seconds

## What About the Gap?

Here's the truth: **The gap may not fully close, and that's often okay.**

What matters more is:
- Can you generate tension across the gap?
- Can you control your core during movement?
- Has your function improved?
- Is your back pain better?

A 2-finger gap with good tension and function is better than a 1-finger gap with no control.

## Your Weekly Schedule

### Daily (5-10 minutes)
- Diaphragmatic breathing: 2-3 minutes
- Connection breath practice: 10-15 reps
- Heel slides or dead bugs: 10 per leg

### 3x Per Week (15-20 minutes)
- Full exercise routine appropriate to your phase
- Progress gradually as control improves

### Ongoing
- Integrate core engagement into daily activities
- Practice getting up correctly (roll to side)
- Maintain awareness during lifting

## Additional Strategies

### Posture Awareness
- Avoid rib flare (ribs should stack over pelvis)
- Don't stand in swayback (pelvis forward, ribs back)
- Practice stacking: ribs over hips, head over shoulders

### Abdominal Support
- Belly bands or compression garments can provide support during activity
- Not a substitute for exercises but can help during recovery
- Gradually reduce reliance as strength improves

### Pelvic Floor Attention
- The pelvic floor and deep core work together
- If you have pelvic floor issues, address them in parallel
- Consider pelvic floor physical therapy

## When to See a Professional

**Seek help if:**
- Gap is more than 3 fingers wide
- You have bulging or herniation
- Significant back pain
- Pelvic floor symptoms (incontinence, prolapse)
- No improvement after 8-12 weeks of consistent work
- You're unsure if you're doing exercises correctly

**Helpful professionals:**
- Pelvic floor physical therapist (gold standard)
- Women's health physiotherapist
- Certified postpartum fitness specialist
- Urogynecologist (for severe cases)

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Weeks 1-4:** Foundation, reconnection, learning proper engagement
- **Weeks 4-8:** Building strength, gap may start to decrease
- **Weeks 8-12:** Functional integration, noticeable improvement
- **3-6 months:** Significant changes in strength and appearance
- **6-12 months:** Ongoing progress with consistent work

**Important:** Progress isn't linear. Be patient and consistent.

## The Bottom Line

Diastasis recti is a functional problem, not just a cosmetic one—and it requires a functional approach to fix. Traditional ab exercises often make it worse. The key is rebuilding from the inside out: breathing, deep core connection, and progressive loading without doming.

Start with breathing and connection work. Add foundational exercises. Progress only when you can maintain control without bulging. Be patient—you're rebuilding tissue and neuromuscular connections, not just building muscle.

Most women see meaningful improvement within 3-6 months of consistent, correct exercise. Some degree of separation may remain, but function can be fully restored.

Your core was designed to adapt—it stretched to grow a human. Now help it adapt back.
