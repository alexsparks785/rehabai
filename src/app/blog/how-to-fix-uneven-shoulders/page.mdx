      <h1>How to Fix Uneven Shoulders: Exercises for Better Alignment</h1>

      <p>Look in the mirror—is one shoulder noticeably higher than the other? Uneven shoulders are incredibly common and can cause neck pain, headaches, and shoulder problems. While some asymmetry is normal, significant unevenness often stems from muscle imbalances that respond well to targeted exercise.</p>

      <h2>What Causes Uneven Shoulders?</h2>

      <p><strong>Structural causes:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Scoliosis (spinal curvature)</li>
        <li>Leg length difference affecting the whole chain</li>
        <li>Previous injury or surgery</li>
        <li>Congenital differences</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Functional causes (more common and fixable):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Tight upper trapezius on one side</li>
        <li>Weak lower trapezius on one side</li>
        <li>Habitual carrying (bag always on same shoulder)</li>
        <li>One-sided activities (tennis, throwing)</li>
        <li>Always sleeping on the same side</li>
        <li>Workstation setup favoring one side</li>
        <li>Compensation from neck or upper back issues</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Identifying Your Pattern</h2>

      <h3>Which Shoulder Is High?</h3>
      <p>Stand in front of a mirror with arms relaxed. Note which shoulder sits higher. This is often the side with the tight upper trap that's pulling the shoulder up.</p>

      <h3>Check Your Habits</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Which shoulder do you carry your bag on?</li>
        <li>Do you cradle your phone on one side?</li>
        <li>Which arm do you lean on at your desk?</li>
        <li>Which side do you sleep on?</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Often the high shoulder is the side that gets more use or tension throughout the day.</p>

      <h2>The Muscle Imbalance Pattern</h2>

      <p><strong>High shoulder side typically has:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Tight, overactive upper trapezius</li>
        <li>Tight levator scapulae</li>
        <li>May have weak lower trapezius</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Low shoulder side typically has:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Weak upper trapezius (relatively)</li>
        <li>May have tighter latissimus dorsi pulling shoulder down</li>
        <li>Potentially weak serratus anterior</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises to Balance Your Shoulders</h2>

      <h3>Release the High Side</h3>

      <h4>1. Upper Trap Stretch (High Side)</h4>
      <p>Tilt your head away from the high shoulder, bringing your ear toward the opposite shoulder. Gently assist with your hand on the head. Keep the high shoulder actively pressed down. Hold and breathe.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Levator Scapulae Stretch (High Side)</h4>
      <p>Turn your head 45 degrees toward your armpit on the opposite side of the high shoulder. Then tilt your head down, looking toward your armpit. Assist gently with your hand on the back of your head.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Upper Trap Foam Roll/Ball Release (High Side)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back with a tennis or lacrosse ball under your upper trap (between neck and shoulder point). Let your arm rest at your side. Relax and breathe into the tight spot. Move the ball to find different tender areas.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds</strong></p>

      <h3>Strengthen the Low Side</h3>

      <h4>4. Single-Arm Shrug (Low Side Only)</h4>
      <p>Hold a moderate weight in the hand of your low shoulder side. Shrug that shoulder up toward your ear, hold 2 seconds at the top, lower slowly. This specifically strengthens the upper trap on the weaker side.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 (low side only)</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Single-Arm Farmer Carry (Low Side)</h4>
      <p>Hold a heavy weight in the hand of your low shoulder side only. Walk while keeping both shoulders level—don't let the weighted side drop. This builds strength and teaches proper positioning.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 30-40 seconds (low side only)</strong></p>

      <h3>Strengthen Lower Traps (Both Sides, Focus on Weak)</h3>

      <h4>6. Prone Y Raise</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with arms extended overhead at 45 degrees, thumbs up. Raise both arms while squeezing shoulder blades down and back. If one side is weaker, you'll feel it. Focus on equal effort.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Single-Arm Prone Y Raise (Weak Side Focus)</h4>
      <p>Same position but one arm at a time. This allows you to do extra reps on the weaker/low shoulder side to build symmetry.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 weak side, 2 sets of 12 strong side</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Face Pulls with External Rotation</h4>
      <p>Use a cable or band at face height. Pull toward your face, externally rotating so hands end beside your ears. Squeeze shoulder blades together. Watch in a mirror to ensure both shoulders are working equally.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15</strong></p>

      <h3>Core and Global Stability</h3>

      <h4>9. Side Plank (Both Sides, Note Differences)</h4>
      <p>Perform side planks on each side. Note if one side feels significantly harder—this can indicate asymmetry in your lateral stabilizers that affects shoulder position. Work to equalize.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 20-30 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Pallof Press (Both Sides)</h4>
      <p>Stand sideways to a cable or band. Press the handle straight out, resisting rotation. Keep shoulders square and level. Note if one side is harder—do extra work on that side.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Dead Bug with Arm Emphasis</h4>
      <p>Lie on back, arms reaching to ceiling. As you lower opposite arm and leg, focus on keeping your shoulders pressing evenly into the floor. Don't let the high side hike up.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Lat Stretch (If Low Side Has Tight Lat)</h3>

      <h4>12. Kneeling Lat Stretch</h4>
      <p>Kneel facing a bench. Place elbows on the bench, palms together. Sit hips back while letting chest drop between arms. If one lat is tighter, you may need extra time on that side.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds, extra on tight side</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Daily (5-10 minutes)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Upper trap stretch (high side): 2 x 45 seconds</li>
        <li>Levator scapulae stretch (high side): 2 x 45 seconds</li>
        <li>Posture check and correction: multiple times</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Strength Sessions (3x/week)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Single-arm shrug (low side): 3x15</li>
        <li>Single-arm farmer carry (low side): 3x30 seconds</li>
        <li>Prone Y raises: 3x12 (extra set on weak side)</li>
        <li>Face pulls: 3x15</li>
        <li>Side planks: 3x20 seconds each</li>
        <li>Pallof press: 3x12 each side</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Habit Changes</h2>

      <p>Exercise alone won't fix the problem if you keep reinforcing the imbalance:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Bag carrying:</strong> Alternate shoulders or use a backpack</li>
        <li><strong>Phone use:</strong> Don't cradle between ear and shoulder; use speaker or headphones</li>
        <li><strong>Sleep position:</strong> Try alternating sides or sleeping on your back</li>
        <li><strong>Desk setup:</strong> Center your keyboard and monitor; don't twist to one side</li>
        <li><strong>Driving:</strong> Keep both hands on wheel; don't rest one arm on door for extended periods</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Mirror Check</h2>

      <p>Throughout the day, use mirrors or windows to check your shoulder position. When you notice the imbalance:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Actively press the high shoulder down</li>
        <li>Gently lift the low shoulder slightly</li>
        <li>Breathe and try to feel "even"</li>
      </ol>

      <p>This conscious correction builds awareness and helps retrain the default position.</p>

      <h2>How Long to See Results?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>2-4 weeks:</strong> Reduced tension on high side, improved awareness</li>
        <li><strong>6-8 weeks:</strong> Noticeable strength gains on weak side</li>
        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Visible improvement in shoulder symmetry</li>
        <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Maintenance needed to prevent return</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <p>Get evaluated if:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>You have significant scoliosis</li>
        <li>Pain accompanies the unevenness</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling in arms</li>
        <li>The asymmetry is severe or worsening</li>
        <li>You've had recent injury or trauma</li>
        <li>No improvement after 2-3 months of consistent work</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Some degree of shoulder asymmetry is normal—perfect symmetry is rare. The goal is to reduce problematic differences that cause pain or dysfunction. With consistent stretching of the tight side, strengthening of the weak side, and modification of habits that reinforce the imbalance, most people achieve meaningful improvement.</p>
