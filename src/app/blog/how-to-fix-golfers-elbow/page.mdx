      <h1>How to Fix Golfer's Elbow: Exercises for Inner Elbow Pain</h1>

      <p>Golfer's elbow is tennis elbow's less famous cousin—same type of problem, opposite side of the elbow. That nagging pain on the inside of your elbow that flares with gripping, throwing, or lifting can be just as debilitating. Here's how to fix it.</p>

      <h2>What Is Golfer's Elbow?</h2>

      <p>Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) affects the tendons on the inside of your elbow. The wrist flexor and forearm pronator muscles attach to the medial epicondyle (bony bump on the inner elbow). Overuse causes tendon degeneration, leading to pain and weakness.</p>

      <p>Despite the name, golfers are actually more likely to get tennis elbow. Golfer's elbow is common in:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Climbers</li>
        <li>Weightlifters (especially pulling movements)</li>
        <li>Throwing athletes</li>
        <li>Manual laborers</li>
        <li>Anyone with repetitive gripping or wrist flexion</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Symptoms</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain on the inner elbow, may radiate into forearm</li>
        <li>Pain worsens with gripping, wrist flexion, or pronation</li>
        <li>Weak grip strength</li>
        <li>Pain with throwing motions</li>
        <li>Tenderness when pressing on inner elbow</li>
        <li>Stiffness in the elbow</li>
        <li>Possible numbness or tingling in ring and pinky fingers (if ulnar nerve involved)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Causes</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Rock climbing and bouldering</li>
        <li>Weightlifting (deadlifts, pull-ups, rows, curls)</li>
        <li>Golf (especially with poor swing mechanics)</li>
        <li>Throwing sports (baseball, javelin)</li>
        <li>Racquet sports (forehand, serving)</li>
        <li>Manual labor with repetitive gripping</li>
        <li>Sudden increase in training volume or intensity</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Solution: Eccentric Exercise</h2>

      <p>Like tennis elbow, golfer's elbow responds best to eccentric loading—where the muscle lengthens under resistance. This stimulates tendon healing and remodeling.</p>

      <h3>Key Exercises</h3>

      <h4>1. Eccentric Wrist Flexion</h4>
      <p>This is your main exercise:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Rest your forearm on a table, wrist and hand over the edge, palm UP</li>
        <li>Hold a light weight (1-3 lbs to start)</li>
        <li>Use your other hand to help curl the weight up</li>
        <li>Slowly lower the weight down over 3-5 seconds using only the affected arm</li>
        <li>Assist back up with your other hand and repeat</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 reps, twice daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Reverse Tyler Twist</h4>
      <p>Using a Flexbar (same tool as for tennis elbow, opposite motion):</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Hold the Flexbar in front of you with your affected arm, wrist flexed (curled toward you)</li>
        <li>Grip the other end with your unaffected hand</li>
        <li>Twist the bar with your unaffected hand</li>
        <li>Keeping arms in front, slowly let your affected wrist extend (straighten) against the resistance</li>
        <li>This eccentric loading targets the wrist flexors</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 reps, twice daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Wrist Flexor Stretch</h4>
      <p>Extend your affected arm in front of you, palm up. Use your other hand to pull your fingers back toward you, straightening the elbow. You should feel a stretch along the inner forearm.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds, 3-4 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Eccentric Forearm Pronation</h4>
      <p>The pronator muscles also attach to the medial epicondyle:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Hold a hammer or weighted bar at the end of the handle</li>
        <li>Rest your elbow on your knee, forearm vertical, palm facing in</li>
        <li>Let the weight slowly rotate your forearm outward (supination) over 3-5 seconds</li>
        <li>Use your other hand to rotate back to start and repeat</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, once daily</strong></p>

      <h3>Progressive Loading (Later Stages)</h3>

      <h4>5. Full Range Wrist Curls</h4>
      <p>Once pain decreases, do full wrist curls: curl UP and lower down using only the affected arm. Progress weight slowly.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, increase weight when pain-free</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Forearm Pronation/Supination</h4>
      <p>Hold a hammer at the end of the handle. Rotate forearm palm up, then palm down, using full motion. This strengthens both pronators and supinators.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 sets of 15 each direction</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Grip Strengthening</h4>
      <p>Use a stress ball or light gripper. Start easy and progress gradually as the tendon heals.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10-15, progress as tolerated</strong></p>

      <h3>Self-Massage</h3>

      <h4>8. Flexor Muscle Release</h4>
      <p>Use your thumb or a massage ball to work the muscles on the palm side of your forearm. Find tight, tender spots and apply sustained pressure for 20-30 seconds each.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 minutes daily</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Phase 1: Pain Management (Weeks 1-2)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Wrist flexor stretch: 30 seconds, 3-4x daily</li>
        <li>Self-massage: 2-3 minutes daily</li>
        <li>Ice after aggravating activities: 10-15 minutes</li>
        <li>Modify or avoid aggravating activities</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 2: Eccentric Loading (Weeks 2-8)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Eccentric wrist flexion: 3x15, twice daily</li>
        <li>Reverse Tyler Twist: 3x15, twice daily (if using Flexbar)</li>
        <li>Eccentric pronation: 3x15, once daily</li>
        <li>Continue stretching and massage</li>
        <li>Some discomfort (3-4/10) during exercise is acceptable</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 8+)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Full wrist curls: 3x15, progress weight</li>
        <li>Pronation/supination: 2x15 each way</li>
        <li>Grip strengthening: 3x10</li>
        <li>Gradual return to sport/activity</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Activity Modification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Climbing:</strong> Reduce volume, avoid aggressive grips, focus on technique</li>
        <li><strong>Lifting:</strong> Reduce weight on pulling exercises, use straps temporarily</li>
        <li><strong>Golf:</strong> Check grip pressure (shouldn't be death grip), lessons for mechanics</li>
        <li><strong>Throwing:</strong> Reduce volume and intensity, check mechanics</li>
        <li><strong>Consider a counterforce brace:</strong> Worn below elbow to reduce tendon load</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Ulnar Nerve Involvement</h2>

      <p>The ulnar nerve runs close to the medial epicondyle. If you have numbness or tingling in your ring and pinky fingers along with elbow pain, the nerve may be irritated. Be gentler with exercises and see a healthcare provider if nerve symptoms are significant.</p>

      <h2>How Long to Heal?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>4-6 weeks:</strong> Noticeable improvement with consistent eccentric work</li>
        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Significant healing for most cases</li>
        <li><strong>6-12 months:</strong> Full resolution of stubborn cases</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Tendons heal slowly due to limited blood supply. Consistency matters more than intensity.</p>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling that's persistent or worsening</li>
        <li>Severe pain limiting daily activities</li>
        <li>Symptoms after acute injury</li>
        <li>Elbow instability</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Preventing Recurrence</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Continue maintenance exercises even after healing</li>
        <li>Warm up forearms before gripping activities</li>
        <li>Progress training volume gradually</li>
        <li>Address technique issues in sport</li>
        <li>Don't grip harder than necessary</li>
        <li>Balance flexor work with extensor exercises</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Golfer's elbow responds well to patient, consistent eccentric loading. Focus on the exercises, modify aggravating activities, and give your tendon time to heal. Most people recover fully—it just takes time and consistency.</p>
