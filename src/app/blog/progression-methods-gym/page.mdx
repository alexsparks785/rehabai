      <p className="lead">
        "Add weight to the bar" is the classic advice for progression. But what happens when you can't add weight? Or when adding 5 lbs would take you from 8 reps to 3? There are many ways to progress—and knowing your options keeps gains coming when simple weight increases stall.
      </p>

      <h2>What Is Progressive Overload?</h2>

      <p>
        Progressive overload is the gradual increase in stress placed on the body during training. Your body adapts to the demands you place on it—to continue adapting, those demands must increase over time.
      </p>

      <p>
        Adding weight is just one form of overload. The principle is broader: <strong>do more than you did before</strong>.
      </p>

      <h2>Progression Methods</h2>

      <h3>1. Add Weight (Load Progression)</h3>
      <p>
        The classic approach: same reps, more weight.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Add 2.5-10 lbs when you hit your rep target</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Beginners, compound lifts, strength-focused training</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Eventually you can't add weight every session</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Week 1: Squat 200×5. Week 2: Squat 205×5. Week 3: Squat 210×5.
      </p>

      <h3>2. Add Reps (Rep Progression)</h3>
      <p>
        Same weight, more reps over time.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Keep weight constant, add 1-2 reps per session or week</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Intermediate lifters, accessory exercises, hypertrophy</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Eventually you exceed your target rep range</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Week 1: Bench 185×6. Week 2: Bench 185×7. Week 3: Bench 185×8.
      </p>

      <h3>3. Double Progression</h3>
      <p>
        Add reps within a range, then add weight and start over.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Work within a rep range (e.g., 8-12). When you hit the top of the range, add weight and drop back to the bottom</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Most intermediate lifters, balances strength and hypertrophy</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Can be slow, requires patience</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Week 1: Row 135×8. Week 2: Row 135×10. Week 3: Row 135×12. Week 4: Row 140×8. Repeat.
      </p>

      <h3>4. Add Sets (Volume Progression)</h3>
      <p>
        Same weight and reps, more sets.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Add a set when current volume becomes manageable</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Increasing volume over a mesocycle, hypertrophy focus</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Sessions get longer, recovery demands increase</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Week 1: Lunges 3×10. Week 3: Lunges 4×10. Week 5: Lunges 5×10.
      </p>

      <h3>5. Reduce Rest Periods (Density Progression)</h3>
      <p>
        Same work in less time, or more work in the same time.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Gradually reduce rest between sets (e.g., 3 min → 2.5 min → 2 min)</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Conditioning, work capacity, time-efficient training</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Can compromise strength performance</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>6. Improve Technique (Quality Progression)</h3>
      <p>
        Same weight, better execution.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Focus on range of motion, control, muscle engagement</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> All levels, especially when load progression stalls</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Harder to measure objectively</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Same weight, but deeper squats, more controlled eccentrics, better mind-muscle connection.
      </p>

      <h3>7. Increase Range of Motion</h3>
      <p>
        Same weight, greater ROM.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Progress from partial to full range, or add deficit/elevation</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Building strength at end ranges, improving flexibility</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> May require weight reduction initially</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Progress from box squats to parallel squats to ATG squats.
      </p>

      <h3>8. Harder Exercise Variations</h3>
      <p>
        Progress to more challenging versions of the same movement pattern.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>How:</strong> Move from easier to harder variations systematically</li>
        <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Bodyweight training, addressing weak points</li>
        <li><strong>Limitation:</strong> Requires knowing exercise progressions</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example:</strong> Knee push-ups → Regular push-ups → Deficit push-ups → One-arm push-ups.
      </p>

      <h2>Matching Progression to Training Level</h2>

      <h3>Beginners (0-1 year)</h3>
      <p>
        Simple load progression works. Add weight every session (linear progression) until it stops working. Don't overcomplicate things.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Primary method: Add weight each session</li>
        <li>Progression rate: 2.5-10 lbs per week depending on the lift</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Intermediate (1-3 years)</h3>
      <p>
        Session-to-session progression slows. Use double progression or weekly load increases.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Primary methods: Double progression, weekly load increases, rep progression</li>
        <li>Progression rate: Weight increases every 1-4 weeks</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Advanced (3+ years)</h3>
      <p>
        Progress becomes incremental and requires sophisticated programming.
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Primary methods: Periodized progression across mesocycles, technique refinement, varied methods</li>
        <li>Progression rate: Monthly or longer cycles</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to Use Each Method</h2>

      <table>
        <thead>
          <tr>
            <th>Situation</th>
            <th>Best Progression Method</th>
          </tr>
        </thead>
        <tbody>
          <tr>
            <td>New to lifting</td>
            <td>Simple load progression</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Can't add weight anymore</td>
            <td>Double progression or rep progression</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Isolation exercises</td>
            <td>Double progression, volume progression</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Bodyweight training</td>
            <td>Rep progression, harder variations</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Hypertrophy focus</td>
            <td>Volume progression, double progression</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Strength focus</td>
            <td>Load progression with periodization</td>
          </tr>
          <tr>
            <td>Form issues</td>
            <td>Quality progression (same weight, better technique)</td>
          </tr>
        </tbody>
      </table>

      <h2>Tracking Progression</h2>

      <p>
        You can't progress what you don't track. Keep a training log that records:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Exercises, sets, reps, and weight</li>
        <li>RPE or RIR for each set</li>
        <li>Notes on form, energy level, or issues</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        Review your log to identify what's working and where you're stalling.
      </p>

      <h2>Common Mistakes</h2>

      <h3>Adding Weight Too Fast</h3>
      <p>
        Forcing weight increases leads to form breakdown and stalls. Let progression happen; don't rush it.
      </p>

      <h3>No Progression Plan</h3>
      <p>
        Randomly adding weight or reps without a system leads to inconsistent progress. Have a clear method for each exercise.
      </p>

      <h3>Only Using One Method</h3>
      <p>
        If adding weight is your only tool, you'll get stuck when it stops working. Expand your progression toolkit.
      </p>

      <h3>Ignoring Quality</h3>
      <p>
        Adding reps or weight with deteriorating form isn't real progress. Sometimes maintaining weight while improving technique is the right move.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Progressive overload is essential, but adding weight isn't the only way. Having multiple progression methods in your toolkit ensures continuous progress regardless of training level.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Progressive overload = doing more than before (in some form)</li>
        <li>Methods include: load, reps, sets, density, technique, ROM, variations</li>
        <li>Beginners: simple load progression works best</li>
        <li>Intermediate: double progression and rep progression</li>
        <li>Advanced: periodized, multi-method approaches</li>
        <li>Track your training to know if you're actually progressing</li>
        <li>Match the progression method to the exercise and your goals</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        When one progression method stalls, switch to another. Progress is always possible—you just need to find the right path forward.
      </p>
