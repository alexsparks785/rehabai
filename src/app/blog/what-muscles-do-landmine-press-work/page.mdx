# What Muscles Do Landmine Press Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

The landmine press—performed with one end of a barbell anchored to the floor—is one of the most versatile and shoulder-friendly pressing exercises available. The unique arc path creates a different stimulus than traditional pressing while being easier on cranky shoulders.

Let's break down exactly what muscles the landmine press targets and why it deserves a place in your program.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Anterior Deltoid (Front Shoulder) - Maximum Activation

The front delts are the primary mover:

**Why activation is so high:**
- Initiates and drives the press
- Works through full range of motion
- Arc path keeps constant tension

**Activation level:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### Upper Chest (Clavicular Head) - Very High Activation

The upper portion of the pecs works hard:

**Why upper chest is emphasized:**
- The pressing angle (forward and up) targets upper chest
- Similar angle to a high incline press
- Gets excellent activation throughout

**Activation level:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### Triceps - Very High Activation

The back of the arm:

**Role in the landmine press:**
- Extends the elbow to complete the press
- Works hard through the lockout
- All three heads contribute

**Activation level:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Serratus Anterior - High Activation

The "boxer's muscle":
- Protracts the scapula at the top
- Works during the reaching portion
- Important for shoulder health

### Core Muscles - High Activation

Your entire core stabilizes:
- **Obliques:** Resist rotation (especially single-arm)
- **Rectus abdominis:** Maintains posture
- **Transverse abdominis:** Creates stability

### Lateral Deltoid - Moderate Activation

The side shoulder:
- Assists with the press
- More active than in flat pressing
- Contributes to shoulder development

### Lower Trapezius - Moderate Activation

Helps with scapular movement:
- Allows proper shoulder blade mechanics
- Works during the press

### Rotator Cuff - Moderate Activation

The shoulder stabilizers:
- Stabilize the humeral head
- Work throughout the movement
- Less stress than some other presses

## Why the Landmine Press Is Special

### The Arc Path Advantage

Unlike vertical or horizontal pressing, the landmine creates an **arc path**:
- Starts near shoulder, travels forward AND up
- Natural scapular movement allowed
- Doesn't force shoulder into problematic positions

### Shoulder-Friendly Mechanics

The landmine press is easier on shoulders because:
1. **Neutral/angled grip:** Less external rotation stress
2. **Arc path:** Allows natural scapular rhythm
3. **No lockout overhead:** Reduces impingement risk
4. **Unilateral option:** Work around asymmetries

### Muscle Emphasis Comparison

| Exercise | Front Delt | Upper Chest | Triceps | Shoulder Stress |
|----------|-----------|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| Landmine Press | Maximum | Very High | High | Low |
| Overhead Press | Very High | Low | High | Moderate-High |
| Incline Bench | High | Maximum | High | Moderate |
| Flat Bench | Moderate | Moderate | High | Moderate |

The landmine press offers excellent front delt and upper chest work with minimal shoulder stress.

## How to Perform the Landmine Press

### Setup
1. Anchor barbell in landmine attachment or corner
2. Stand facing the end of the bar
3. Feet shoulder-width apart
4. Grip end of bar with one or both hands at shoulder height

### Single-Arm Execution
1. **Start position:** Bar at shoulder, elbow bent
2. **Brace:** Engage core, prevent rotation
3. **Press:** Drive bar forward and up in an arc
4. **Lockout:** Arm extended, reaching slightly
5. **Lower:** Control the return to shoulder
6. **Repeat:** All reps one side, then switch

### Two-Arm Execution
1. **Grip:** Interlace fingers or cup bar end with both hands
2. **Start:** Bar at chest level
3. **Press:** Drive forward and up together
4. **Lower:** Controlled return

### Key Technique Points

- **Don't lean back:** Stay upright or lean slightly forward
- **Control the arc:** Don't turn it into a push
- **Full range:** From shoulder to full extension
- **Core engaged:** Especially for single-arm version
- **Let scapula move:** Allow natural protraction at top

## Stance Variations

### Standing (Standard)
- Most common version
- Full body engagement
- Moderate core demand

### Half-Kneeling
- Inside knee down, outside foot forward
- Increased core stability demand
- Better hip flexor stretch

### Tall Kneeling
- Both knees down
- Eliminates leg drive
- Maximum core requirement

### Split Stance
- Staggered feet
- Athletic position
- Good for sports transfer

### Seated
- On bench or floor
- Removes lower body
- Isolates pressing muscles

## Equipment Setup Options

### Landmine Attachment
- Purpose-built device
- Mounts to rack or floor
- Smoothest option

### Corner
- Stick bar in corner of two walls
- Free option
- May damage walls over time

### Heavy Dumbbell
- Wedge bar end against heavy dumbbell
- Improvised but works
- Dumbbell must be heavy enough

### Landmine Post
- Standalone floor anchor
- Portable option
- Works well

## Programming Recommendations

### For Shoulder Development
- 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps each arm
- 2x per week
- Focus on squeeze at top

### For Upper Chest
- Include as part of chest training
- 3 sets of 10-15 reps
- Superset with flies for pump

### For Shoulder Rehab/Prehab
- Light weight, higher reps
- 2-3 sets of 15-20 reps
- Focus on control and scapular movement

### As Primary Press
- When overhead pressing is problematic
- Heavy singles/triples possible
- Progress weight over time

### In Athletic Training
- Explosive reps for power
- Single-arm for rotational transfer
- Include with other functional movements

## Common Mistakes

### Excessive Back Lean
- **Problem:** Turns into an incline press, stresses lower back
- **Fix:** Stay upright or lean slightly forward

### Limited Range of Motion
- **Problem:** Missing the stretch and full contraction
- **Fix:** From shoulder to full extension, let scapula move

### Rotating the Torso (Single-Arm)
- **Problem:** Cheating, reduces core demand
- **Fix:** Keep hips and shoulders square

### Going Too Heavy
- **Problem:** Form breaks down
- **Fix:** Weight you can control for target reps

### Gripping Too Tight
- **Problem:** Unnecessary tension
- **Fix:** Firm but not death grip

## Landmine Press Variations

### Landmine Push Press
- Add leg drive
- Allows heavier loading
- More power-focused

### Landmine Rotational Press
- Allow rotation through press
- Trains rotational power
- Athletic transfer

### Landmine Squeeze Press
- Two dumbbells, squeeze together
- Extra chest activation
- Challenging variation

### Landmine Floor Press
- Lying on floor
- Different angle
- Tricep emphasis

### Single-Arm Landmine Row to Press
- Compound movement
- Row, then press
- Full upper body

## Who Should Do Landmine Press?

### Great For:
- Anyone with shoulder issues
- People who can't press overhead
- Athletes wanting rotational power
- Upper chest development
- General pressing strength

### Especially Beneficial For:
- Shoulder impingement history
- AC joint issues
- People who find overhead pressing painful
- Combat athletes (punching transfer)

### May Not Be Ideal For:
- Those with wrist issues (single-arm grip)
- When overhead strength is specifically needed
- Limited equipment access

## Complementary Exercises

Pair landmine press with:

**For Complete Shoulder Development:**
- Lateral raises
- Rear delt work
- Face pulls

**For Pressing Balance:**
- Rows (match pressing volume)
- Pull-ups or pulldowns
- Horizontal pulling

**For Chest Development:**
- Flat pressing
- Flies
- Dips

## Key Takeaways

1. **Landmine press targets front delts and upper chest** primarily
2. **Arc path is shoulder-friendly** - great for cranky shoulders
3. **Single-arm version adds core demand** - anti-rotation work
4. **Multiple stance options** for different goals
5. **Doesn't require overhead mobility** - accessible to more people
6. **Can be used heavy or light** - versatile for strength or hypertrophy

## The Bottom Line

The landmine press is one of the most underrated pressing exercises available. It builds the front delts and upper chest effectively while being remarkably easy on the shoulders. The single-arm version adds valuable core work.

If overhead pressing bothers your shoulders, or you want to add variety to your pressing, the landmine press deserves serious consideration. It's effective, versatile, and shoulder-friendly—a rare combination.
