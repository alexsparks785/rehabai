      <h1>How to Fix Winged Scapula: Exercises to Flatten Your Shoulder Blades</h1>

      <p>Notice your shoulder blade sticking out from your back? That's scapular winging—and it's more than a cosmetic issue. When your scapula doesn't sit flat against your rib cage, it affects every pushing and pulling movement you do and can lead to shoulder pain and injury.</p>

      <h2>What Causes Winged Scapula?</h2>

      <p>Your scapula is held against your ribcage by the serratus anterior muscle, which wraps around your ribs and attaches to the inner edge of your shoulder blade. When this muscle is weak or the nerve controlling it (long thoracic nerve) is compromised, the scapula loses its anchor.</p>

      <p><strong>Common causes include:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Serratus anterior weakness (most common)</li>
        <li>Prolonged desk posture</li>
        <li>Nerve damage from injury or surgery</li>
        <li>Muscular imbalances from poor training</li>
        <li>Trapezius weakness</li>
        <li>Excessive forward shoulder position</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How to Tell If You Have It</h2>

      <p><strong>Wall push-up test:</strong> Stand facing a wall, place your hands on it, and do a slow push-up. Have someone watch your back. If one or both shoulder blades pop away from your ribs, you have winging.</p>

      <p><strong>Push-up test:</strong> Do a regular push-up and pause at the bottom. Scapular winging often becomes obvious in this position.</p>

      <h2>Best Exercises for Winged Scapula</h2>

      <p>The key is strengthening the serratus anterior while also addressing the supporting muscles. Focus on quality over quantity—proper scapular movement matters more than reps.</p>

      <h3>1. Wall Slides with Serratus Press</h3>
      <p>Stand with your back against a wall, arms in a "goal post" position. Slide your arms up while actively pressing your entire forearm into the wall. At the top, push extra hard like you're trying to press through the wall. Lower with control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>2. Serratus Push-Up</h3>
      <p>Start in a push-up position. Without bending your elbows, let your shoulder blades squeeze together (chest drops slightly toward floor), then push the floor away by spreading your shoulder blades apart. Your upper back should round slightly at the top.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15-20 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>3. Bear Position Serratus Reach</h3>
      <p>Get on hands and knees with your knees hovering an inch off the ground. Keeping your elbow straight, reach one hand forward while pushing through the other hand to protract that scapula. Return and repeat on the other side.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>4. Banded Serratus Punch</h3>
      <p>Anchor a resistance band at chest height. Face away from the anchor, holding the band in one hand. Punch straight forward, keeping your elbow locked, focusing on reaching through your shoulder blade at the end. Control the return.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 each arm</strong></p>

      <h3>5. High Plank with Shoulder Protraction</h3>
      <p>Hold a high plank position. Without bending your elbows, let your chest sink between your shoulder blades, then push the floor away hard, spreading your shoulder blades apart. Your upper back rounds slightly. Repeat rhythmically.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>6. Incline Serratus Press</h3>
      <p>Set a bench to 30-45 degrees. Hold light dumbbells (start with 5-10 lbs) straight up. Lower them normally, then press up. At the top, push extra by reaching the weights toward the ceiling—you'll feel your shoulder blades spread apart.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>7. Prone Y Raise</h3>
      <p>Lie face down on the floor or an incline bench. Raise your arms into a Y position (45 degrees from overhead) with thumbs up. Focus on pulling your shoulder blades down and back as you lift. Hold 2 seconds at the top.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>8. Side-Lying Serratus Press</h3>
      <p>Lie on your side with a light dumbbell in your top hand. Start with your arm reaching toward the ceiling. Push the weight straight up toward the ceiling by protracting your shoulder blade (reaching your shoulder forward). Lower with control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 each side</strong></p>

      <h2>Lower Trap Strengtheners</h2>

      <p>The lower trapezius works with serratus anterior to control scapular position. If it's weak, fixing winging becomes harder.</p>

      <h3>9. Prone T Raise</h3>
      <p>Lie face down. Raise your arms out to the side (T position) with thumbs up. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and hold 2 seconds. Lower with control.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>10. Face Pull with External Rotation</h3>
      <p>Use a cable or band at face height. Pull toward your face while externally rotating so your hands end up beside your ears, knuckles pointing back. Squeeze your shoulder blades together hard. Return slowly.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 reps</strong></p>

      <h2>What About Stretching?</h2>

      <p>Stretching is secondary to strengthening for winged scapula. However, if you have a tight pec minor (most people with forward shoulders do), it can pull the scapula forward and make winging worse.</p>

      <p><strong>Pec Minor Stretch:</strong> Stand in a doorway with your elbow bent 90 degrees, forearm on the door frame, elbow slightly above shoulder height. Step through gently until you feel a stretch in the front of your shoulder and upper chest. Hold 30-60 seconds each side.</p>
