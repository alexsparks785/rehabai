# What Muscles Do Reverse Grip Barbell Rows Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Reverse grip barbell rows (also called underhand or supinated rows) use an underhand grip instead of the traditional overhand grip. This change shifts muscle emphasis, increasing bicep involvement and potentially targeting the lower lats more effectively. Here's exactly what the grip change does.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Latissimus Dorsi

**Activation Level: Very High**

The lats remain the primary target:
- Shoulder extension (pulling arm back)
- Adduction (pulling arm toward body)
- Works through full rowing range
- Lower lat fibers may be more emphasized

**Lower lat emphasis theory:**
The supinated grip and more forward arm position may better target the lower lat fibers. While research is mixed, many lifters report a better lower lat "feel" with this grip.

### Biceps Brachii

**Activation Level: Very High**

Significantly more than overhand grip:
- Supinated position = biceps in strongest pulling position
- Both heads work hard
- Can become a limiting factor
- Similar to a chin-up grip

### Brachialis

**Activation Level: High**

The muscle beneath biceps:
- Primary elbow flexor
- Works regardless of grip
- Contributes to arm pulling power
- Active throughout movement

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Rhomboids

**Activation Level: High**

Between the shoulder blades:
- Retract scapulae at the top
- Pull shoulder blades together
- Important for full back contraction
- Work hard in rows

### Middle and Lower Trapezius

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The traps assist:
- Middle: Scapular retraction
- Lower: Scapular depression
- Both contribute to back contraction
- Active at end range

### Erector Spinae

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The spinal erectors:
- Maintain neutral spine during the bent-over position
- Work isometrically throughout
- Can be a limiting factor
- Essential for row safety

### Teres Major

**Activation Level: Moderate to High**

The "little lat":
- Assists the lats
- Pulls arm back and down
- Works throughout movement

### Rear Deltoids

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The posterior delts:
- Assist shoulder extension
- Contribute to the pulling motion
- More involved than in pulldowns

### Brachioradialis

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Forearm muscle:
- Assists elbow flexion
- Active in all rowing
- Contributes to grip

## How Grip Affects the Row

| Grip | Lat Activation | Bicep Involvement | Common Cue |
|------|----------------|-------------------|------------|
| Overhand (pronated) | Very High | Moderate | Pull to lower chest |
| Underhand (supinated) | Very High | Very High | Pull to navel |
| Neutral | Very High | High | Pull to lower chest |

The underhand grip:
1. Puts biceps in mechanically strongest position
2. Often allows pulling to a slightly lower point (belly)
3. May target lower lats more effectively
4. Increases arm involvement

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Bent over, back flat
- Barbell hanging at arm's length
- Underhand grip (palms forward)
- Knees slightly bent

### Pull Phase (Concentric)
- Lats and biceps drive the pull
- Pull bar toward lower abdomen/navel
- Elbows travel close to body
- Shoulder blades begin retracting

### Top Position (Peak Contraction)
- Bar at navel/lower stomach area
- Elbows behind body
- Scapulae fully retracted
- Squeeze the back contraction

### Lowering Phase (Eccentric)
- Controlled descent
- Maintain tension
- Full arm extension at bottom
- Don't bounce at bottom

## Reverse Grip vs Overhand Row

| Aspect | Reverse Grip | Overhand Grip |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Bicep involvement | Very High | Moderate |
| Bar path | To navel | To lower chest |
| Elbow path | Close to body | More flared option |
| Lower lat emphasis | Potentially higher | Standard |
| Weight used | Often slightly less | Standard |
| Grip fatigue | Can be higher | Standard |

Both are effective—they complement each other.

## Dorian Yates' Preference

Dorian Yates, 6x Mr. Olympia, famously preferred reverse grip rows:
- Believed they targeted lower lats better
- Used them as a primary back builder
- Credited them for his legendary back development
- Pulled to lower stomach/navel area

While this is anecdotal, it's influenced many bodybuilders to include this variation.

## Common Mistakes

**Rounding the lower back**: Dangerous under load. Keep back flat, chest up.

**Too upright**: Reduces lat involvement. Stay bent over (45-60° from floor).

**Using too much body momentum**: Heaving the weight. Control the movement.

**Pulling to the wrong spot**: Should go to navel, not chest. Let grip dictate bar path.

**Grip failing**: Underhand can fatigue grip faster. Consider straps for heavy sets.

**Elbows flaring**: Should stay relatively close to body. Track naturally.

## How to Maximize Back Activation

1. **Pull to navel**: Natural path for underhand grip
2. **Keep elbows close**: Don't flare wide
3. **Squeeze at top**: Retract scapulae, pause briefly
4. **Maintain hip hinge**: Stay bent over throughout
5. **Control the negative**: 2-3 seconds down
6. **Chest up, back flat**: Protect the spine
7. **Think "pull with elbows"**: Not hands

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Standard reverse grip row | Baseline |
| Pendlay reverse grip row | From floor, explosive |
| Yates row | More upright (45°), heavy |
| Single-arm reverse grip dumbbell row | Unilateral |
| Reverse grip cable row | Constant tension |
| Reverse grip T-bar row | Different equipment |

### Yates Row

Dorian Yates' specific style:
- More upright torso (about 45°)
- Underhand grip
- Pull to navel
- Heavy weight
- Slightly reduced ROM but allows more load

### Single-Arm Dumbbell Version

Using dumbbells with supinated grip:
- Addresses imbalances
- Can use bench for support
- Different feel
- Good unilateral option

## Programming Recommendations

**For Back Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps
- Moderate to heavy weight
- Full ROM with squeeze
- 1-2 times per week

**For Lower Lat Emphasis:**
- Include with overhand rows
- Focus on the pull to navel
- Mind-muscle connection
- Quality over quantity

**For Bicep Assistance:**
- Counts as significant bicep work
- May reduce direct bicep volume needed
- Good compound arm movement

**Integration Ideas:**
- Alternate with overhand rows week to week
- Use both in same workout (different emphases)
- Superset with straight arm pulldowns

## Who Should Use Reverse Grip Rows

**Those wanting more bicep involvement:**
- Kills two birds with one stone
- Heavy compound pulling for arms
- Efficient training

**Lower lat development focus:**
- May better target this area
- Worth experimenting with
- Many bodybuilders prefer it

**Anyone wanting variety:**
- Different stimulus
- Breaks plateaus
- Keeps training interesting

**Those with shoulder issues on overhand:**
- Different shoulder position
- May be more comfortable
- Worth trying if overhand bothers you

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscles**: Lats (very high), biceps (very high), brachialis (high)
- **Secondary**: Rhomboids, traps, erector spinae, teres major, rear delts
- **vs Overhand**: More bicep, pull to navel, potentially more lower lat
- **Key form**: Pull to navel (not chest), stay bent over, back flat
- **Made famous by**: Dorian Yates—a back development staple
- **Common error**: Pulling too high (should be navel area)
- **Use alongside**: Overhand rows for complete back development

Reverse grip barbell rows are an excellent back builder that increases bicep involvement and may better target the lower lats. Include them alongside overhand rows for complete back development.
