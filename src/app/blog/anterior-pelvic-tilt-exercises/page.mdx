# Anterior Pelvic Tilt Exercises: Fix Your Posture and Relieve Pain

Anterior pelvic tilt (APT) is one of the most common postural issues affecting people today. If you've ever been told you have excessive lower back curve, a "duck butt" posture, or experience chronic lower back tightness, anterior pelvic tilt may be the culprit.

## What Is Anterior Pelvic Tilt?

Anterior pelvic tilt occurs when the front of your pelvis drops forward while the back rises up. Imagine your pelvis as a bowl of water—with APT, the water would spill out the front.

### Signs You Have Anterior Pelvic Tilt

- Your lower back has an exaggerated curve (hyperlordosis)
- Your belly appears to stick out even when you're lean
- Your butt appears more prominent
- You experience chronic lower back tightness
- Hip flexors feel constantly tight
- Standing for long periods causes back discomfort

### The Thomas Test (Self-Assessment)

1. Lie on your back at the edge of a table or bed
2. Pull one knee to your chest and hold it
3. Let the other leg hang off the edge
4. If the hanging thigh rises above horizontal or the knee can't bend to 90°, you likely have tight hip flexors contributing to APT

## What Causes Anterior Pelvic Tilt?

APT typically develops from muscle imbalances:

**Tight muscles:**
- Hip flexors (psoas, iliacus, rectus femoris)
- Lower back extensors (erector spinae)

**Weak muscles:**
- Glutes (gluteus maximus)
- Abdominals (especially lower abs)
- Hamstrings

The most common cause? Prolonged sitting. When you sit for hours, your hip flexors stay shortened, your glutes stay inactive, and over time this becomes your default posture.

## Phase 1: Release Tight Muscles

Before strengthening, you need to release the muscles pulling your pelvis forward.

### Hip Flexor Foam Rolling

**Target:** Rectus femoris and TFL

1. Place a foam roller on the front of your thigh
2. Roll from just below your hip to just above your knee
3. Spend extra time on tender spots
4. Roll side to side to hit the TFL (outer hip)

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds per leg

### Psoas Release

**The psoas is deep and hard to stretch—try this release:**

1. Lie face down with a softball or lacrosse ball under your hip, 2 inches inside your hip bone
2. Gently lower your weight onto the ball
3. Breathe deeply and let the muscle relax
4. Small movements side to side can help release tension

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds per side

### Lower Back Release

1. Lie on your back with knees bent
2. Place two tennis balls taped together (or a peanut roller) under your lower back, on either side of your spine
3. Gently roll up and down, never directly on the spine
4. Focus on areas of tightness

**Duration:** 2-3 minutes

## Phase 2: Stretch Tight Hip Flexors

### Half-Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch

**The foundation stretch for APT:**

1. Kneel on one knee with the other foot in front (90/90 position)
2. **Critical:** Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt) and squeeze the glute on the kneeling side
3. Shift your hips forward slightly until you feel a stretch in the front of the back hip
4. Keep your torso upright—don't lean forward

**Hold:** 30-60 seconds per side, 2-3 sets

**Common mistakes:**
- Arching the lower back (defeats the purpose)
- Not engaging the glute
- Leaning forward instead of tucking the pelvis

### Couch Stretch (Rectus Femoris Focus)

**More intense hip flexor stretch:**

1. Face away from a wall or couch
2. Place one knee on the ground with your shin up against the wall/couch
3. Step the other foot forward into a lunge
4. Tuck your tailbone and engage your glute
5. Squeeze your butt and keep core tight

**Hold:** 30-60 seconds per side

**Progression:** Once comfortable, you can work toward a more upright torso

### Prone Quad Stretch

1. Lie face down
2. Bend one knee and grab your ankle
3. Pull your heel toward your glute
4. Press your hip into the ground (prevents compensation)

**Hold:** 30 seconds per side

## Phase 3: Strengthen Weak Muscles

### Glute Bridge with Posterior Pelvic Tilt

**Teaches proper pelvic positioning while strengthening glutes:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. **Before lifting:** Tuck your tailbone, flattening your lower back against the floor (posterior pelvic tilt)
3. Maintaining this tilt, squeeze your glutes to lift your hips
4. At the top, your body forms a straight line—no arching the lower back
5. Hold for 2 seconds, lower with control

**Reps:** 15-20 reps, 3 sets

**Key:** The posterior pelvic tilt position is more important than how high you lift

### Dead Bug

**Core exercise that teaches pelvic control:**

1. Lie on your back with arms reaching toward the ceiling, knees bent 90°
2. Press your lower back firmly into the floor (posterior pelvic tilt)
3. Slowly extend one arm overhead while extending the opposite leg
4. **The moment your back starts to arch, you've gone too far**
5. Return and repeat on the other side

**Reps:** 8-10 per side, 3 sets

**Progression:** Add more range of motion as you can maintain the flat back

### Reverse Crunch

**Targets lower abdominals:**

1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet off the floor
2. Using your lower abs, curl your pelvis toward your ribcage
3. Your hips should lift slightly off the ground
4. Lower with control—don't let your back arch

**Reps:** 12-15 reps, 3 sets

**Focus:** Small, controlled movement—this is not a leg lift

### Glute Ham Raise Alternative (Floor Version)

**Strengthens glutes and hamstrings:**

1. Lie face down with something heavy anchoring your feet (or have a partner hold them)
2. Place hands behind your head or crossed on your chest
3. Squeeze your glutes and hamstrings to lift your torso
4. Keep your hips pressed into the ground
5. Lower with control

**Reps:** 10-12 reps, 3 sets

### Plank with Posterior Tilt

**Core stability with proper pelvic position:**

1. Get into a forearm plank position
2. **Key adjustment:** Tuck your tailbone, squeezing your glutes and drawing your belly button in
3. Your lower back should be flat or slightly rounded—never arched
4. Hold this position

**Hold:** 30-60 seconds, 3 sets

**This is harder than a regular plank because you can't cheat with your lower back**

## Phase 4: Strengthen in Standing

### Wall Hip Flexor March

**Trains hip flexion without using the lower back:**

1. Stand with your back against a wall
2. Keep your entire spine pressed against the wall
3. March in place, lifting knees to 90°
4. **Challenge:** Don't let your lower back leave the wall

**Reps:** 20 marches (10 per leg), 3 sets

### Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

**Strengthens glutes and hamstrings while teaching hip hinge:**

1. Stand on one leg, slight bend in the knee
2. Hinge at the hip, reaching your other leg back
3. Keep your back flat (neutral spine)
4. Feel the stretch in your standing leg hamstring
5. Squeeze your glute to return to standing

**Reps:** 8-10 per leg, 3 sets

### Pallof Press with Posterior Tilt

**Anti-rotation core work with pelvic control:**

1. Stand sideways to a cable or band anchored at chest height
2. Hold the handle at your chest
3. Tuck your tailbone (posterior tilt) and brace your core
4. Press the handle straight out in front of you
5. Resist the rotation and the urge to arch your back
6. Hold for 2 seconds, return

**Reps:** 10 per side, 3 sets

## Daily Habits to Fix APT

### Sitting Modifications

- **Use a lumbar support** that keeps your pelvis neutral
- **Sit on the edge of your chair** with feet flat, practicing a slight posterior tilt
- **Set a timer** to stand every 30-45 minutes
- **Avoid crossing your legs**—it promotes pelvic asymmetry

### Standing Modifications

- **Squeeze your glutes** slightly when standing
- Practice a **subtle posterior tilt** throughout the day
- If standing for long periods, **alternate feet on a small step**

### Walking Cue

Think about "tucking your tail" slightly when walking. Your glutes should be doing more of the work propelling you forward.

### Sleeping Position

- **Side sleepers:** Place a pillow between your knees to keep pelvis neutral
- **Back sleepers:** Place a pillow under your knees to reduce lower back arch
- **Avoid sleeping on your stomach**—it forces your lower back into extension

## Sample APT Correction Routine

### Morning Routine (5 minutes)

1. Hip flexor foam rolling: 60 seconds per side
2. Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch: 30 seconds per side
3. Glute bridge with posterior tilt: 15 reps
4. Dead bug: 8 per side

### Evening Routine (10 minutes)

1. Psoas release: 60 seconds per side
2. Couch stretch: 45 seconds per side
3. Reverse crunch: 15 reps
4. Plank with posterior tilt: 45 seconds
5. Single-leg RDL: 8 per side

### During the Day

- Glute squeezes at your desk: 10 reps, hourly
- Standing with slight posterior tilt
- Walking with glute engagement

## How Long Does It Take to Fix APT?

With consistent work:
- **2-4 weeks:** Noticeable improvement in awareness and control
- **6-8 weeks:** Visible postural changes, reduced pain
- **3-6 months:** Long-term correction with new movement patterns

**Important:** Even after correction, maintenance is key. The muscles that caused APT will try to revert if you return to old habits.

## When to Seek Help

Consult a physical therapist if:
- You have significant pain that doesn't improve with exercise
- You notice numbness or tingling in your legs
- Your symptoms worsen with the exercises
- You've had previous spine surgeries or injuries
- You're unsure if your technique is correct

## Key Takeaways

1. **APT is a muscle imbalance issue**—tight hip flexors and weak glutes/abs
2. **Release before stretching, stretch before strengthening**
3. **The posterior pelvic tilt cue is essential** for all exercises
4. **Daily habits matter as much as exercises**—sitting, standing, and walking patterns need to change
5. **Consistency beats intensity**—5 minutes daily beats 30 minutes once a week

Anterior pelvic tilt is correctable with the right approach. Focus on the fundamentals, be patient with the process, and you'll see improvements in both your posture and any associated pain.
