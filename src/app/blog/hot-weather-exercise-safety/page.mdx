Summer heat doesn't have to sideline your fitness—but it does require smart adjustments. Exercising in hot weather carries real risks if you're not prepared. Here's how to keep training safely when the temperature rises.

## Understanding Heat and Exercise

### How Your Body Cools

During exercise, your body generates heat. To cool down, it:

1. **Sends blood to the skin** (to release heat to the air)
2. **Produces sweat** (evaporation cools the body)

In hot weather, these systems work harder. If they can't keep up, body temperature rises dangerously.

### Why Heat Makes Exercise Harder

- Heart works harder (pumping blood to skin AND muscles)
- More fluid loss through sweat
- Performance naturally decreases
- Core temperature rises faster
- Greater risk of heat illness

### Heat Index

The "feels like" temperature matters more than actual temperature. Humidity prevents sweat from evaporating, reducing your body's cooling ability.

| Heat Index | Risk Level |
|------------|------------|
| 80-90°F | Caution |
| 90-103°F | Extreme caution |
| 103-124°F | Danger |
| 124°F+ | Extreme danger |

## Heat-Related Illnesses

Know the signs and what to do.

### Heat Cramps

**What it is:** Painful muscle spasms during or after exercise

**Signs:**
- Painful cramping, usually in legs or abdomen
- Heavy sweating

**What to do:**
- Stop exercising
- Move to cooler area
- Drink fluids with electrolytes
- Gently stretch and massage cramped muscles

### Heat Exhaustion

**What it is:** Body overheating, unable to cool itself

**Signs:**
- Heavy sweating
- Cool, pale, clammy skin
- Fast, weak pulse
- Nausea or vomiting
- Muscle cramps
- Tiredness, weakness
- Dizziness, fainting
- Headache

**What to do:**
- Stop exercising immediately
- Move to air conditioning or shade
- Lie down, elevate legs
- Apply cool, wet cloths
- Sip water
- Seek medical help if vomiting, symptoms worsen, or last more than 1 hour

### Heat Stroke (MEDICAL EMERGENCY)

**What it is:** Body temperature regulation fails completely

**Signs:**
- High body temperature (103°F+)
- Hot, red, dry OR damp skin
- Fast, strong pulse
- Confusion, altered mental state
- Slurred speech
- Loss of consciousness

**What to do:**
- Call 911 immediately
- Move to cooler environment
- Cool the person rapidly (cold water, ice, wet sheets)
- Do NOT give fluids
- This is life-threatening

## Hydration Strategies

### Before Exercise

- Drink 17-20 oz of water 2-3 hours before
- Drink 8 oz 20-30 minutes before
- Start exercise well-hydrated

### During Exercise

- Drink 7-10 oz every 10-20 minutes
- For exercise over 60 minutes, include electrolytes
- Don't wait until thirsty (thirst is a late signal)

### After Exercise

- Drink 16-24 oz for every pound lost during exercise
- Include sodium (sports drink, salty snack, or food)
- Continue hydrating for several hours

### Signs of Dehydration

- Dark urine (should be pale yellow)
- Decreased urination
- Dry mouth
- Headache
- Fatigue
- Dizziness

### Electrolytes

Sweat contains salt (sodium), potassium, and other electrolytes. In hot weather or long exercise:

- Sports drinks
- Electrolyte tablets
- Coconut water
- Salty foods after exercise

## Timing Your Workouts

### Best Times

**Early morning:**
- Coolest part of the day
- Lower humidity typically
- Beat the heat and crowds

**Evening:**
- After peak heat (usually 3-5 PM)
- Sun lower in sky
- Can still be warm—check conditions

### Worst Times

- 10 AM to 4 PM (peak sun and heat)
- Avoid if heat index is in danger zone

### Check the Forecast

- Look at heat index, not just temperature
- Check humidity
- Plan around weather
- Have indoor backup plan

## Clothing and Gear

### What to Wear

**Fabric:**
- Lightweight, moisture-wicking synthetics
- Loose fit for airflow
- Light colors (reflect heat)
- Avoid cotton (holds moisture)

**Coverage:**
- Cover skin from sun OR
- Use sunscreen on exposed skin
- Wide-brimmed hat or visor
- Sunglasses

### Sunscreen

- SPF 30 or higher
- Water/sweat resistant
- Apply 15-30 minutes before going out
- Reapply every 2 hours or after sweating

### Cooling Gear

- Cooling towels
- Frozen water bottles
- Ice bandanas
- Misting fans

## Workout Modifications

### Reduce Intensity

- Work at lower intensity than normal
- Heart rate will be higher at same pace—that's normal
- Expect decreased performance
- Listen to your body

### Shorten Duration

- Do 30 minutes instead of 60
- Split workouts (morning and evening)
- Quality over quantity

### Take More Breaks

- Rest in shade
- Drink fluids
- Cool down before continuing
- Don't push through warning signs

### Choose Activities Wisely

**Better in heat:**
- Swimming (built-in cooling)
- Water aerobics
- Indoor exercise
- Early morning or evening outdoor activity
- Shaded trails

**More challenging in heat:**
- Running
- Cycling (wind can deceive you)
- Outdoor sports
- Any high-intensity activity

## Heat Acclimatization

Your body can adapt to heat—but it takes time.

### How It Works

Over 10-14 days of heat exposure:
- Sweating starts sooner
- Sweat becomes more dilute (lose less sodium)
- Blood volume increases
- Heart becomes more efficient
- Core temperature rises more slowly

### How to Acclimatize

- Start with 10-15 minutes in heat
- Increase by 5-10 minutes each day
- Reduce intensity initially
- Stay well hydrated
- Takes about 2 weeks for full adaptation

### Who Needs Extra Care

- Those new to heat
- Returning after time away
- Traveling to hotter climate
- Beginning of summer

## Indoor Alternatives

When it's too hot outside:

### Home Options

- Home workout videos
- Bodyweight circuits
- Yoga/stretching
- Strength training
- Dance workouts

### Gym/Facility Options

- Air-conditioned gym
- Indoor pool
- Indoor track
- Fitness classes

### When to Stay Inside

- Heat index above 103°F
- Air quality alerts
- If you're not heat acclimatized
- If you're feeling unwell
- During peak sun hours

## Special Populations

### Older Adults

- Reduced ability to regulate temperature
- May have medications that affect heat response
- Higher risk of heat illness
- Exercise in early morning or indoors
- Extra hydration attention

### Children

- Less efficient sweating
- Higher surface area to body weight
- May not recognize heat stress
- Need reminders to drink
- Shorter outdoor sessions

### Those Taking Medications

Some medications affect heat response:
- Diuretics
- Beta-blockers
- Antihistamines
- Psychiatric medications
- Check with doctor about heat precautions

### Pregnant Women

- Already have elevated body temperature
- Extra caution in heat
- Stay well hydrated
- Avoid overheating
- Consult healthcare provider

## Planning Your Hot Weather Workout

### Before

- Check weather/heat index
- Plan timing (early or late)
- Prepare hydration (cold water, electrolytes)
- Lay out appropriate clothing
- Have indoor backup plan

### During

- Start easy, assess how you feel
- Drink regularly before thirsty
- Monitor for warning signs
- Be willing to cut it short
- Find shade when possible

### After

- Get to cool environment
- Continue hydrating
- Cool shower
- Monitor recovery
- Rest before next session

## Quick Reference

**Heat index actions:**
- 80-90°F: Exercise with caution, hydrate well
- 90-103°F: Reduce intensity and duration, extra hydration
- 103°F+: Consider indoor exercise or skip

**Hydration:**
- Before: 17-20 oz (2-3 hours before)
- During: 7-10 oz every 10-20 minutes
- After: 16-24 oz per pound lost

**Warning signs (stop immediately):**
- Dizziness
- Nausea
- Headache
- Confusion
- Excessive fatigue
- Stopped sweating (dangerous!)

**Prevention:**
- Time workouts wisely
- Hydrate proactively
- Dress appropriately
- Reduce intensity
- Acclimatize gradually
- Know when to stop

Summer fitness is absolutely possible—you just need to respect the heat. Train smart, stay hydrated, and don't be afraid to take it inside when conditions are dangerous. Your fitness will still be there when the heat breaks.
