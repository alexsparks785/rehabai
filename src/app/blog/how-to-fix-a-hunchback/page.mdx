# How to Fix a Hunchback: Exercises for Thoracic Kyphosis

That rounded upper back—whether from years of desk work, phone use, or just life—can make you look older, feel stiffer, and even affect your breathing. The good news: postural kyphosis (the fixable kind) responds well to consistent exercise. Here's how to straighten up.

## What Is a Hunchback?

A "hunchback" refers to excessive rounding of the upper back (thoracic spine). The medical term is hyperkyphosis—too much kyphotic curve. Some curve is normal; too much creates problems.

**Signs of excessive kyphosis:**
- Rounded upper back visible in profile
- Head that juts forward
- Shoulders that roll inward
- Difficulty standing fully upright
- Upper back stiffness or discomfort
- Appearance of a "hump" at the base of the neck

## Types of Kyphosis

### Postural Kyphosis (Most Common)
Caused by poor posture habits—slouching at desks, looking at phones, weak muscles. This type is fully correctable with exercise and posture awareness.

### Scheuermann's Kyphosis
A structural condition where vertebrae develop wedge-shaped during adolescence. More rigid than postural kyphosis and may require medical management.

### Age-Related Kyphosis
Develops in older adults due to disc degeneration, vertebral compression fractures, or muscle weakness. Partially correctable depending on cause.

**Important:** If you're unsure which type you have, or if you have significant pain, see a healthcare provider for evaluation.

## What Causes Postural Kyphosis?

### Muscle Imbalances

**Tight muscles (front of body):**
- Chest (pectoralis major and minor)
- Front shoulders (anterior deltoids)
- Neck flexors (sternocleidomastoid)

**Weak muscles (back of body):**
- Mid-back (rhomboids, middle trapezius)
- Lower trapezius
- Deep neck flexors
- Thoracic extensors

### Lifestyle Factors
- Hours spent hunched over computers
- Phone use (looking down constantly)
- Reading, studying, crafting in flexed positions
- Driving
- Carrying heavy bags on shoulders

### Habits
- Slouching when tired
- Sitting in unsupportive chairs
- Not engaging postural muscles

## Can You Actually Fix a Hunchback?

**Postural kyphosis:** Yes, often significantly. With consistent exercise over weeks to months, you can restore more normal curvature.

**Structural kyphosis:** Limited correction, but exercise can prevent worsening, reduce pain, and improve function.

**Age-related kyphosis:** Depends on cause. If primarily muscular and postural, significant improvement is possible. If due to compression fractures, focus is on preventing progression.

## Exercises to Fix a Hunchback

### Step 1: Mobilize the Thoracic Spine

#### 1. Foam Roller Extension
**Why:** Directly extends the rounded thoracic segments.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie with a foam roller across your upper back
2. Support your head with your hands
3. Let your upper back extend over the roller
4. Hold for 3 breaths
5. Move the roller up or down to target different levels
6. Spend 2-3 minutes total

#### 2. Cat-Cow (Emphasis on Cow)
**Why:** Moves the thoracic spine through flexion and extension.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Cow: Drop belly, lift chest, look up—focus on arching your UPPER back
3. Cat: Round back, tuck chin
4. Spend extra time in the cow position
5. Do 15-20 reps

#### 3. Thread the Needle
**Why:** Adds rotation to thoracic mobility work.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Reach right arm under your body to the left
3. Let your right shoulder and ear rest on the floor
4. Hold for 20-30 seconds
5. Then reach right arm toward the ceiling, rotating open
6. Do 8-10 reps per side

#### 4. Thoracic Extension on Chair
**Why:** Easy to do anywhere, targets mid-back.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit backward on a chair, facing the backrest
2. Hold the backrest with your hands
3. Let your chest sink forward and down
4. Lift your chest and arch your upper back
5. Hold for 5-10 seconds
6. Do 10 reps

### Step 2: Open the Chest and Front Shoulders

#### 5. Doorway Pec Stretch
**Why:** Tight pecs pull your shoulders forward and your upper back into flexion.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in a doorway
2. Place forearms on the door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step one foot forward and lean through
4. Feel the stretch across your chest
5. Hold for 45-60 seconds
6. Repeat with arms higher and lower to target different fibers

#### 6. Floor Angel Stretch
**Why:** Opens the chest in a passive, prolonged stretch.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back on the floor
2. Bend your knees, feet flat, to flatten your lower back
3. Place arms out to the sides, elbows bent at 90 degrees (goal post)
4. Let gravity pull your arms toward the floor
5. Hold for 2-3 minutes
6. If shoulders are very tight, place a small towel under each hand

#### 7. Lying Chest Opener on Foam Roller
**Why:** Combines thoracic extension with chest stretch.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie lengthwise on a foam roller (along your spine)
2. Head and tailbone supported on the roller
3. Let arms fall out to the sides, palms up
4. Relax and breathe
5. Hold for 3-5 minutes

### Step 3: Strengthen the Back

#### 8. Prone Y-T-W Raises
**Why:** Strengthens lower traps and mid-back in their shortened range.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down on the floor or an incline bench
2. Y: Raise arms at 45 degrees overhead, thumbs up—hold 3 seconds
3. T: Raise arms straight out to sides, thumbs up—hold 3 seconds
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades as you raise—hold 3 seconds
5. Do 10 reps of each position

**Key:** Keep your neck neutral—don't crane your head up.

#### 9. Wall Angels
**Why:** Strengthens upper back while maintaining chest opening.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against a wall
2. Feet 4-6 inches from wall
3. Press lower back, upper back, and head into the wall
4. Raise arms to goal post position against the wall
5. Slide arms up and down, keeping contact with the wall
6. Do 10-15 reps

**Note:** If you can't keep everything on the wall, work on mobility first.

#### 10. Band Pull-Aparts
**Why:** Strengthens the muscles that pull your shoulder blades together.

**How to do it:**
1. Hold a resistance band in front of you at shoulder height
2. Arms straight, light tension on the band
3. Pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades together
4. Control the return
5. Do 15-20 reps

#### 11. Face Pulls
**Why:** Strengthens rear deltoids and rotator cuff while training shoulder retraction.

**How to do it:**
1. Use a cable or resistance band at face height
2. Pull toward your face, elbows high
3. At the end, rotate hands outward and squeeze shoulder blades
4. Control the return
5. Do 12-15 reps

#### 12. Rows with Scapular Focus
**Why:** Trains the mid-back through a full range of motion.

**How to do it:**
1. Use cables, bands, or dumbbells
2. Start by squeezing your shoulder blade back (retraction)
3. Then complete the row by bending your elbow
4. Reverse: extend arm first, then let shoulder blade protract
5. Do 12-15 reps per arm

### Step 4: Strengthen the Neck

#### 13. Chin Tucks
**Why:** Strengthens deep neck flexors and corrects forward head posture.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit or stand tall
2. Pull your chin straight back (make a double chin)
3. Hold for 5-10 seconds
4. Release and repeat
5. Do 15-20 reps, multiple times daily

#### 14. Chin Tuck with Wall Press
**Why:** Adds resistance to the chin tuck.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back of head against a wall
2. Perform a chin tuck, pressing your head into the wall
3. Hold for 5-10 seconds
4. Do 10-15 reps

## Your Daily Routine

### Morning (5-7 minutes)
- Foam roller thoracic extension: 2 minutes
- Cat-cow (emphasis on cow): 15 reps
- Doorway chest stretch: 45 seconds
- Chin tucks: 15 reps
- Wall angels: 10 reps

### Throughout the Day
- Chin tucks: Every hour, 10 reps
- Chest stretch: Brief stretch during breaks
- Posture check: Reset every 30-60 minutes

### Evening (10-15 minutes)
- Foam roller lying chest opener: 3-5 minutes
- Thread the needle: 8 each side
- Prone Y-T-W: 10 reps each
- Band pull-aparts: 15 reps
- Face pulls or rows: 12 reps
- Floor angel stretch: 2 minutes

## Posture Cues and Daily Habits

### The Stacking Cue
Imagine stacking your body: ears over shoulders, shoulders over hips, hips over ankles. Check this throughout the day.

### The String Cue
Imagine a string attached to the top of your head, gently pulling you upward. This lifts your chest without forcing your shoulders back.

### Screen Height
- Computer screen at eye level
- Phone brought up to face (not face down to phone)
- Laptop on a stand with external keyboard

### Sleeping Position
- Avoid thick pillows that push your head forward
- Side sleepers: pillow should keep head neutral
- Back sleepers: thin pillow or cervical pillow

### Strengthen Your Awareness
Set hourly reminders to check your posture. Over time, good posture becomes more automatic.

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Week 1-2:** Improved awareness, some mobility gains
- **Week 3-4:** Noticeable improvements in how you feel
- **Week 6-8:** Visible postural improvements
- **3-6 months:** Significant, lasting changes

**Consistency matters more than intensity.** Daily short sessions beat occasional long ones.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Forcing shoulders back:** Squeezing your shoulders back artificially is exhausting and unsustainable. Build the strength to hold proper posture naturally.

**Overarching the lower back:** When trying to stand tall, don't compensate by arching your lower back. That's just shifting the problem.

**Expecting fast results:** Postural patterns develop over years. They take weeks to months to meaningfully change.

**Only stretching the back:** Your back muscles are often already overstretched. You need to open the front AND strengthen the back.

**Ignoring the neck:** Forward head posture is part of the kyphotic pattern. Address it with chin tucks.

## When to Seek Help

See a professional if:
- You have significant pain
- Your kyphosis is severe or rigid
- You're not improving after 8-12 weeks of consistent work
- You have breathing difficulties
- The curve appeared suddenly or is progressing rapidly
- You're over 65 and concerned about vertebral fractures

## The Bottom Line

Postural kyphosis—the rounded upper back from desk life—is fixable. The formula is simple: mobilize the stiff thoracic spine, open the tight chest, strengthen the weak back muscles, and practice better posture daily.

Start with foam roller extension and chest stretches for quick mobility wins. Add strengthening (Y-T-W raises, band pull-aparts) for lasting change. Practice chin tucks throughout the day.

Most people see meaningful improvement within 6-8 weeks. Your spine didn't round overnight, and it won't straighten overnight—but with consistent work, you can stand taller, move better, and feel more confident in your posture.

The best time to fix your posture was years ago. The second best time is now.
