      <p className="lead">
        The deadlift has a reputation for being dangerous for the back. But when performed correctly, it's actually one of the best exercises for building a strong, resilient spine. Lower back pain during deadlifts usually signals a technique problem—here's how to identify and fix it.
      </p>

      <h2>Why Deadlifts Can Hurt Your Back</h2>

      <p>
        The deadlift loads the spine significantly. When technique is good, this load is distributed safely across muscles and joints. When technique breaks down, stress concentrates on vulnerable structures:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Disc compression and shear:</strong> Especially with spinal flexion under load</li>
        <li><strong>Ligament strain:</strong> When muscles can't maintain position</li>
        <li><strong>Muscle strain:</strong> Erector spinae and other back muscles overworked</li>
        <li><strong>Facet joint irritation:</strong> From excessive extension or rotation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Form Mistakes That Cause Back Pain</h2>

      <h3>1. Rounding the Lower Back</h3>
      <p>
        The most common issue. When the lower back rounds (lumbar flexion) under load, disc pressure increases dramatically and muscles are in a weakened position.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Causes:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Weight too heavy</li>
        <li>Poor bracing / weak core</li>
        <li>Limited hip mobility</li>
        <li>Starting with hips too low</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Reduce weight until you can maintain a neutral spine</li>
        <li>Learn to brace properly (see below)</li>
        <li>Work on hip hinge mechanics with lighter loads</li>
        <li>Address hip mobility if that's the limiting factor</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>2. Hyperextending (Over-Arching)</h3>
      <p>
        Some lifters overcompensate by arching excessively, especially at lockout. This loads the facet joints and can cause extension-based pain.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Finish the lift by squeezing your glutes and standing tall—not by leaning back. Your spine should be neutral at the top, not hyperextended.
      </p>

      <h3>3. Not Bracing Properly</h3>
      <p>
        The core muscles create intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes the spine. Without proper bracing, the spine is vulnerable.
      </p>

      <p><strong>How to Brace:</strong></p>
      <ol>
        <li>Take a deep breath into your belly (not chest)</li>
        <li>Brace your core as if preparing to get punched</li>
        <li>Push your abs out against your belt (if wearing one) or against your own tension</li>
        <li>Hold this brace throughout the lift</li>
        <li>Exhale only after completing the rep</li>
      </ol>

      <h3>4. Bar Too Far from Body</h3>
      <p>
        When the bar drifts away from your body, the lever arm increases and back stress multiplies. The bar should stay in contact with your legs throughout the lift.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Start with the bar over mid-foot, touching your shins</li>
        <li>Drag the bar up your legs (wear long socks or pants)</li>
        <li>Keep lats engaged to pull the bar back into your body</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>5. Jerking the Weight</h3>
      <p>
        Yanking the bar off the floor creates sudden force that the back may not be prepared to handle.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Take the slack out of the bar before lifting</li>
        <li>Build tension gradually, then drive through the floor</li>
        <li>Think "push the floor away" rather than "pull the bar up"</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>6. Treating It Like a Squat</h3>
      <p>
        Sitting too deep with hips too low turns the deadlift into a squat-pull hybrid. This often causes the hips to rise faster than the shoulders, leading to back rounding.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> The deadlift is a hip hinge. Hip height at the start depends on your proportions, but generally your hips should be higher than a squat position. Shoulders should be over or slightly in front of the bar.
      </p>

      <h2>Proper Deadlift Setup</h2>

      <h3>Step-by-Step:</h3>
      <ol>
        <li><strong>Stance:</strong> Feet hip-width apart, toes slightly out</li>
        <li><strong>Bar position:</strong> Over mid-foot, about 1 inch from shins</li>
        <li><strong>Grip:</strong> Bend down and grip the bar just outside your legs</li>
        <li><strong>Shins to bar:</strong> Bend your knees until shins touch the bar</li>
        <li><strong>Chest up:</strong> Lift your chest to straighten your back</li>
        <li><strong>Brace:</strong> Big breath, brace core hard</li>
        <li><strong>Take slack out:</strong> Pull up on the bar until it clicks against the plates</li>
        <li><strong>Drive:</strong> Push the floor away while keeping chest up</li>
        <li><strong>Lockout:</strong> Stand tall, squeeze glutes—don't hyperextend</li>
      </ol>

      <h2>Exercises to Support Pain-Free Deadlifting</h2>

      <h3>Hip Hinge Practice</h3>
      <p>
        Learn the movement pattern with lighter loads:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Romanian deadlifts</li>
        <li>Kettlebell deadlifts</li>
        <li>Cable pull-throughs</li>
        <li>Good mornings (light)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Core Strengthening</h3>
      <p>
        A strong core protects the spine:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Planks and side planks</li>
        <li>Dead bugs</li>
        <li>Pallof press</li>
        <li>Loaded carries</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Hip Mobility</h3>
      <p>
        If tight hips prevent you from getting into position:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>90/90 stretches</li>
        <li>Pigeon pose</li>
        <li>Hip flexor stretches</li>
        <li>Goblet squat holds</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Alternative Deadlift Variations</h2>

      <p>
        If conventional deadlifts consistently cause pain despite technique work, try alternatives:
      </p>

      <h3>Trap Bar Deadlift</h3>
      <p>
        The handles at your sides reduce lower back stress. Great option for those who struggle with conventional positioning.
      </p>

      <h3>Sumo Deadlift</h3>
      <p>
        Wide stance, hands inside legs. More upright torso position may be easier on the back for some people.
      </p>

      <h3>Romanian Deadlift</h3>
      <p>
        Targets the hinge pattern with less spinal load than pulling from the floor. Good for building posterior chain strength.
      </p>

      <h3>Block/Rack Pulls</h3>
      <p>
        Elevating the bar reduces range of motion and may be easier to maintain spine position. Build up range over time.
      </p>

      <h2>When to Seek Help</h2>

      <p>
        See a professional if:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain persists despite form corrections</li>
        <li>You have radiating pain, numbness, or tingling in legs</li>
        <li>There's significant weakness</li>
        <li>Pain affects daily activities</li>
        <li>There was an acute injury during lifting</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Should You Deadlift Through Back Pain?</h2>

      <p>
        Some general guidelines:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Mild muscle soreness:</strong> Generally okay to continue training</li>
        <li><strong>Dull ache (1-3/10) that doesn't worsen:</strong> May be okay with reduced load</li>
        <li><strong>Sharp pain:</strong> Stop immediately</li>
        <li><strong>Pain that worsens during lifting:</strong> Stop and reassess</li>
        <li><strong>Pain with radiating symptoms:</strong> Stop and seek evaluation</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        When in doubt, reduce load, work on technique, or substitute a less demanding variation.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        The deadlift isn't inherently bad for your back—but poor technique is. A neutral spine, proper bracing, and appropriate loading make deadlifts safe and effective for building back strength.

        Key takeaways:
      </p>
