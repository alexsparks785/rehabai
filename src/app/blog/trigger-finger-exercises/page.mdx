# Trigger Finger Exercises: Relieve Locking and Stiffness

Trigger finger (stenosing tenosynovitis) causes your finger to catch, click, or lock when you bend it—sometimes getting stuck in a bent position. While severe cases may need medical intervention, many people find significant relief through targeted exercises and stretches.

## Understanding Trigger Finger

Trigger finger occurs when the tendon sheath (the tunnel the tendon glides through) becomes inflamed and narrowed. The tendon develops a nodule that catches as it passes through, creating that characteristic clicking or locking sensation.

**Common causes include:**
- Repetitive gripping (tools, sports equipment, steering wheels)
- Inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes)
- Occupations requiring prolonged gripping
- Middle age (40-60 years most common)

**Which fingers are affected?**
- Ring finger and thumb most common
- Middle finger also frequently affected
- Multiple fingers can be involved simultaneously

## Before You Start: Important Guidelines

**Exercise timing matters:**
- Best done when the finger is warm (after a shower or warm soak)
- Avoid exercising when the finger is severely locked or inflamed
- Morning stiffness is common—gentle motion helps

**Stop if you experience:**
- Increased catching or locking
- Sharp pain during exercises
- Significant swelling
- Complete inability to straighten the finger

## Warm-Up: Prepare Your Tendons

### Warm Water Soak
Before exercises, soak your hand in warm (not hot) water for 5-10 minutes. This:
- Increases blood flow to the tendons
- Reduces stiffness
- Makes the sheath more pliable

### Gentle Fist Pumps
1. Open your hand fully, spreading fingers
2. Slowly make a loose fist (don't squeeze tight)
3. Open again
4. Repeat 10 times slowly

## Stretching Exercises

### 1. Finger Extension Stretch
This is your most important daily exercise.

**How to do it:**
1. Place your palm flat on a table
2. Using your other hand, gently lift the affected finger
3. Keep the base of the finger on the table
4. Lift until you feel a gentle stretch (no pain)
5. Hold for 15-30 seconds
6. Release slowly

**Frequency:** 5-6 times per day, 3-5 repetitions each time

### 2. Passive Finger Flexion
Keeps the full range of motion.

**How to do it:**
1. Use your other hand to gently bend the affected finger
2. Bend at each joint: tip, middle, and base
3. Move slowly to avoid triggering
4. Hold each position for 5-10 seconds

**Note:** If the finger locks, don't force it. Gently massage the palm and try again.

### 3. Finger Spread Stretch
Stretches the web spaces and interosseous muscles.

**How to do it:**
1. Place your hand flat, palm down
2. Spread all fingers apart as wide as possible
3. Hold for 10 seconds
4. Relax and repeat 5-10 times

### 4. Wrist Flexor Stretch
The finger flexor tendons run through the wrist—keeping them supple helps.

**How to do it:**
1. Extend your arm, palm up
2. Use your other hand to gently pull fingers back toward you
3. Keep elbow straight
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. Repeat 2-3 times

## Strengthening Exercises

Strengthening should begin only after acute inflammation subsides. Start gently.

### 1. Tendon Gliding Exercises
These systematically move the tendon through its full range.

**The sequence (hold each position 5 seconds):**
1. **Straight:** All fingers extended
2. **Hook:** Bend the tip and middle joints, base straight (like a hook)
3. **Fist:** Full fist, thumb outside
4. **Table top:** Bend at base joints only, fingers straight
5. **Straight fist:** Fingers folded into palm, thumb over

**Repeat:** 10 complete cycles, 3-4 times daily

### 2. Finger Walking
**How to do it:**
1. Place your hand flat on a table
2. "Walk" each finger toward your thumb, one at a time
3. Return each finger to starting position
4. Move slowly and deliberately
5. Repeat 10 times per finger

### 3. Rubber Band Finger Extensions
Strengthens the finger extensors (opposites of the affected flexors).

**How to do it:**
1. Place a rubber band around all fingers at the nail level
2. Spread fingers apart against resistance
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. Relax slowly
5. Repeat 15-20 times

**Progression:** Use thicker bands or double up

### 4. Putty Exercises
Therapy putty allows progressive resistance.

**Exercises include:**
- Full grip squeeze (hold 5 seconds)
- Finger pinches (thumb to each finger)
- Finger extension (push through the putty)
- Finger spreads (place putty between fingers, spread)

**Time:** 5-10 minutes, 2-3 times daily

## Self-Massage Techniques

### Palm Massage
The A1 pulley (where triggering occurs) is at the base of the finger in your palm.

**How to do it:**
1. Locate the base of the affected finger in your palm
2. Using your opposite thumb, apply firm but comfortable pressure
3. Make small circles over the nodule
4. Massage for 1-2 minutes
5. Follow the tendon into the palm and up the finger

**Timing:** Before and after exercises, and when stiff

### Finger Milking
**How to do it:**
1. Starting at the fingertip, use thumb and index finger
2. Apply gentle pressure and "milk" toward the palm
3. This encourages fluid movement and reduces swelling
4. Repeat 10 times per finger

## Splinting and Support

Night splinting keeps the finger extended during sleep, preventing morning locking.

**Types of splints:**
- **Full finger splint:** Keeps entire finger straight
- **MCP blocking splint:** Keeps base joint extended while allowing tip movement
- **Ring splints:** Comfortable for daytime wear

**How to use:**
- Wear during sleep and during aggravating activities
- Don't wear 24/7—movement is important
- Gradually reduce splinting as symptoms improve

## Activity Modifications

**Reduce triggering activities:**
- Avoid prolonged gripping when possible
- Use power tools instead of manual tools
- Build up handles with foam or tape for easier grip
- Take frequent breaks during repetitive hand tasks

**Ergonomic tips:**
- Use larger pens/pencils
- Choose tools with cushioned handles
- Switch hands when possible
- Avoid sleeping with hands in a fist

## Condition-Specific Advice

### Trigger Thumb
The thumb is especially important functionally.

**Additional exercises:**
- Opposition stretch: Touch thumb tip to each fingertip
- Thumb circles: Gentle rotations in both directions
- Thumb extension: Place hand flat, lift only the thumb

### Multiple Trigger Fingers
Work on all affected fingers but don't overdo it.

**Strategy:**
- Divide exercises between affected fingers
- Total hand fatigue is a signal to stop
- Prioritize the most problematic finger

### Diabetic Trigger Finger
Diabetes increases risk and can slow healing.

**Considerations:**
- Blood sugar control aids tissue healing
- Be patient—recovery may take longer
- More likely to need medical intervention
- Extra important to avoid re-injury

## When Exercises Aren't Enough

Seek medical evaluation if:
- Finger remains locked and you can't straighten it
- Symptoms persist beyond 4-6 weeks of conservative care
- You have multiple affected fingers
- Pain is severe or worsening
- There's significant loss of function

**Medical treatments include:**
- Corticosteroid injections (often very effective)
- Ultrasound-guided injections
- Percutaneous release (needle technique)
- Surgical release (outpatient procedure)

## Sample Daily Routine

### Morning (5-7 minutes)
1. Warm water soak: 5 minutes
2. Gentle fist pumps: 10 reps
3. Finger extension stretch: 3 reps, 20-second hold
4. Tendon glides: 5 cycles

### Midday (3-5 minutes)
1. Tendon glides: 10 cycles
2. Palm massage: 1-2 minutes
3. Rubber band extensions: 15 reps

### Evening (5-7 minutes)
1. Warm soak (if very stiff): 5 minutes
2. All stretches: 2-3 reps each
3. Putty exercises: 5 minutes
4. Self-massage: 2 minutes
5. Apply night splint

## Progress Expectations

**Week 1-2:**
- May feel worse initially as you mobilize stiff tissues
- Focus on gentle motion and stretching
- No strengthening yet

**Week 3-4:**
- Catching/clicking should start reducing
- Begin gentle strengthening
- Morning stiffness improves

**Week 5-8:**
- Significant symptom reduction for many
- Increase strengthening intensity
- Return to normal activities gradually

**Week 8+:**
- Continue maintenance exercises
- Some cases need longer or medical intervention
- Prevent recurrence with ongoing care

## Prevention of Recurrence

Once improved, prevent trigger finger from returning:
- Continue stretches daily (takes only 2-3 minutes)
- Avoid sustained gripping when possible
- Use ergonomic tools
- Take breaks during repetitive hand work
- Address any underlying conditions (diabetes, arthritis)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Warm before stretching** — tendons respond better when warm
2. **Tendon glides daily** — the single most important exercise
3. **Don't force through locking** — gentle pressure, not force
4. **Massage the palm** — target the A1 pulley where triggering occurs
5. **Consider night splinting** — prevents morning locking
6. **Be patient** — improvement takes 4-8 weeks of consistent effort
7. **Seek help if stuck** — medical treatments are effective when exercises aren't enough

Trigger finger responds well to conservative care in many cases. Stay consistent with your exercises, modify activities that aggravate symptoms, and give your tendons time to heal.
