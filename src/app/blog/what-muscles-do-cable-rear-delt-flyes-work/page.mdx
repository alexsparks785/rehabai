# What Muscles Do Cable Rear Delt Flyes Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Cable rear delt flyes target the posterior (rear) deltoids—the often-neglected back portion of the shoulder. Using cables provides constant tension throughout the movement, making this an excellent choice for rear delt isolation. Here's exactly what's working.

## Primary Muscle Worked

### Posterior Deltoid (Rear Delt)

**Activation Level: Very High (Maximum Isolation)**

The rear delts are the primary target:
- Back portion of the shoulder
- Creates shoulder roundness from behind
- Performs shoulder horizontal abduction
- Often underdeveloped compared to front/side delts

**Why rear delts need direct work:**
Pressing movements (bench, overhead press) primarily hit front delts. Rows work rear delts but not exclusively. Direct rear delt work is essential for balanced shoulders.

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Rhomboids

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Between the shoulder blades:
- Retract the scapulae
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- Work at end range
- Contribute to the pulling motion

### Middle Trapezius

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The middle portion of traps:
- Assist scapular retraction
- Work with rhomboids
- Pull shoulder blades together
- Active at peak contraction

### Infraspinatus and Teres Minor

**Activation Level: Moderate**

Rotator cuff muscles:
- External rotation component
- Shoulder stabilization
- Work throughout movement
- Important for shoulder health

### Lateral Deltoid

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The side delt contributes slightly:
- Some abduction component
- Depends on exact angle
- Not primary focus

## Cable Setup Options

### High Cable Position
- Cables at head height or above
- Pull downward and back
- Different feel
- Some prefer this angle

### Low Cable Position
- Cables at low position
- Pull upward and back
- Similar to bent-over dumbbell version
- Mimics gravity-based angle

### Mid-Height Cable
- Cables at shoulder height
- Pull straight back
- Horizontal path
- Clean rear delt isolation

**Most common:** Shoulder height, crossing cables in front.

## The Cross-Body Setup

The most popular cable rear delt fly setup:

1. Set both cables at shoulder height
2. Grab left cable with right hand, right cable with left hand
3. Cables cross in front of you
4. Pull arms out and back
5. Cables cross/uncross during movement

This creates a smooth, effective resistance curve.

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Arms extended in front, crossed
- Holding opposite cables
- Slight elbow bend
- Shoulders relaxed

### Fly Phase (Concentric)
- Pull arms out and back
- Lead with elbows
- Rear delts contract
- Shoulder blades squeeze together

### Peak Contraction
- Arms extended to sides/slightly behind
- Maximum rear delt contraction
- Rhomboids fully retracted
- Brief squeeze (1-2 seconds)

### Return Phase (Eccentric)
- Control arms forward
- Cables cross again
- Maintain tension
- Don't let weight stack crash

## Common Mistakes

**Using too much weight**: Leads to momentum and poor form. Rear delts are small—use light to moderate weight.

**Bending elbows excessively**: Turns into a row. Maintain slight, fixed elbow bend.

**Shrugging shoulders**: Traps taking over. Keep shoulders down.

**Short range of motion**: Not fully extending arms. Full ROM for full activation.

**Pulling too low**: Should stay at shoulder level. Don't pull toward hips.

**Rushing**: Missing the contraction. Slow and controlled wins.

## How to Maximize Rear Delt Activation

1. **Keep elbows slightly bent but fixed**: Don't change the angle
2. **Lead with elbows**: Think "elbows back" not "hands back"
3. **Squeeze at peak**: 1-2 second hold
4. **Control the negative**: 2-3 seconds return
5. **Keep shoulders down**: Fight the shrug
6. **Light weight**: This is isolation, not strength
7. **Feel the rear delts**: Mind-muscle connection

## Cable vs Dumbbell Rear Delt Flyes

| Aspect | Cable | Dumbbell |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Tension at bottom | High | Low |
| Tension at top | High | High |
| Constant tension | Yes | No |
| Setup | Requires machine | Anywhere |
| Balance | Stable | Requires control |
| Feel | Often smoother | Gravity-based |

**Cables advantage:** Constant tension throughout. Dumbbells have minimal tension at the bottom of bent-over flyes.

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Cross-body cable fly | Standard, cables crossed |
| Single-arm cable rear delt fly | Unilateral focus |
| Seated cable rear delt fly | Removes momentum |
| Rope face pull | Different grip, external rotation |
| High cable rear delt fly | Different angle |
| Prone cable rear delt fly | Lying face-down |

### Single-Arm Cable Rear Delt Fly

One arm at a time:
- Better focus on each side
- Identifies imbalances
- Can brace with other hand
- Excellent variation

### Rope Face Pull Comparison

Face pulls include external rotation—rear delt flyes don't:

| Exercise | Rear Delt | External Rotation | Traps |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Cable rear delt fly | Very High | Low | Moderate |
| Face pull | High | High | Higher |

Both are valuable—they complement each other.

## Programming Recommendations

**For Rear Delt Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 12-20 reps
- Light to moderate weight
- Full ROM with squeeze
- 2-3 times per week

**For Shoulder Balance:**
- Include with every shoulder session
- Can also do on back day
- Frequency helps rear delts grow
- Quality over quantity

**Superset Ideas:**
- Cable rear delt fly + cable lateral raise (rear + side delts)
- Cable rear delt fly + face pulls (isolation + external rotation)
- Cable rear delt fly + band pull-aparts (cables + bands)

**Integration:**
- After compound pressing
- With back work
- As shoulder finisher
- Anytime—rear delts recover quickly

## Why Rear Delts Are Often Weak

**Typical training overemphasizes:**
- Front delts (all pressing)
- Chest (bench-heavy programs)
- Mirror muscles generally

**Rear delts get neglected because:**
- You can't see them easily
- Pressing is more popular than pulling
- Direct work often skipped
- "I do rows" isn't enough

**Result:** Shoulder imbalance, poor posture, injury risk.

## Benefits of Strong Rear Delts

**Shoulder health:**
- Balances the joint
- Reduces injury risk
- Supports pressing movements

**Posture:**
- Pulls shoulders back
- Counteracts desk posture
- Opens the chest

**Appearance:**
- 3D shoulder look
- Roundness from all angles
- Complete development

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscle**: Posterior deltoid (very high, isolated)
- **Secondary**: Rhomboids, middle traps, rotator cuff
- **Cable advantage**: Constant tension throughout movement
- **Common setup**: Cross-body, cables at shoulder height
- **Key cues**: Fixed elbow bend, lead with elbows, squeeze at peak
- **Weight**: Light to moderate—rear delts are small
- **Why essential**: Pressing doesn't hit them—direct work required

Cable rear delt flyes are one of the best exercises for isolating the posterior deltoids. The constant cable tension provides an advantage over dumbbells, and the movement is easy to learn. Include them regularly for balanced, healthy shoulders.
