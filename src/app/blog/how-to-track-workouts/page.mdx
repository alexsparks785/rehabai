      <p className="lead">
        If you're not tracking your workouts, you're guessing. You can't know if you're progressing, what's working, or why you're stuck. A workout log is one of the simplest tools that separates people who make consistent gains from those who spin their wheels.
      </p>

      <h2>Why Track Your Workouts?</h2>

      <h3>1. Progressive Overload Requires Knowledge</h3>
      <p>
        To progressively overload, you need to know what you did last time. Did you squat 185×8 or 190×7? Without a log, you're relying on memory—which is unreliable after even a few days.
      </p>

      <h3>2. Identify What's Working</h3>
      <p>
        When you look back at periods of good progress, you can see what you were doing. Training frequency, exercise selection, volume—it's all there to analyze.
      </p>

      <h3>3. Identify What's Not Working</h3>
      <p>
        Stuck in a plateau? Your log reveals patterns. Maybe you've been doing the same weight for weeks, or your volume crept too high, or you skipped deloads.
      </p>

      <h3>4. Motivation and Accountability</h3>
      <p>
        Seeing your progress in black and white is motivating. A visible streak of consistent workouts encourages you to keep going.
      </p>

      <h3>5. Long-Term Reference</h3>
      <p>
        Months or years from now, you can look back and see where you started, how far you've come, and what approaches worked best for your body.
      </p>

      <h2>What to Track</h2>

      <h3>The Essentials</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Date:</strong> When did you train?</li>
        <li><strong>Exercises:</strong> What movements did you do?</li>
        <li><strong>Sets:</strong> How many sets per exercise?</li>
        <li><strong>Reps:</strong> How many reps per set?</li>
        <li><strong>Weight:</strong> What load did you use?</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        <strong>Example entry:</strong><br/>
        March 15, 2024<br/>
        Squat: 185×8, 185×8, 185×7<br/>
        Romanian Deadlift: 135×10, 135×10, 135×10<br/>
        Leg Press: 270×12, 270×12, 270×11
      </p>

      <h3>Useful Additions</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>RPE/RIR:</strong> How hard was each set? (e.g., RPE 8, 2 reps in reserve)</li>
        <li><strong>Rest periods:</strong> How long between sets?</li>
        <li><strong>Bodyweight:</strong> Especially useful if tracking body composition</li>
        <li><strong>Notes:</strong> How you felt, form issues, pain, energy level</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Optional (for Detailed Tracking)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Sleep quality/hours the night before</li>
        <li>Nutrition notes (fasted, pre-workout meal, etc.)</li>
        <li>Time of day</li>
        <li>Mood/stress level</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How to Track: Methods</h2>

      <h3>Paper Notebook</h3>
      <p>
        <strong>Pros:</strong> Simple, no battery needed, tactile satisfaction, no distractions
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Cons:</strong> Can be lost, harder to analyze trends, no backups
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Best for:</strong> People who prefer analog, those distracted by phones
      </p>

      <h3>Spreadsheet (Google Sheets, Excel)</h3>
      <p>
        <strong>Pros:</strong> Free, highly customizable, easy to analyze data, accessible anywhere
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Cons:</strong> Requires setup, can be cumbersome on mobile
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Best for:</strong> Data-oriented people, those who want to analyze trends
      </p>

      <h3>Workout Apps</h3>
      <p>
        Popular options: Strong, JEFIT, Hevy, GymBook, FitNotes
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Pros:</strong> Purpose-built, quick entry, built-in analysis, exercise libraries
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Cons:</strong> Learning curve, some cost money, dependent on your phone
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Best for:</strong> Most people, especially those who always have their phone
      </p>

      <h3>Notes App</h3>
      <p>
        <strong>Pros:</strong> Simple, already on your phone, syncs to cloud
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Cons:</strong> No analysis features, can get disorganized
      </p>
      <p>
        <strong>Best for:</strong> Minimalists, casual tracking
      </p>

      <h2>Making Tracking a Habit</h2>

      <h3>Track During Rest Periods</h3>
      <p>
        Enter your data between sets while you're resting. It takes seconds and becomes part of your workout rhythm.
      </p>

      <h3>Keep It Simple at First</h3>
      <p>
        Don't track 15 variables from day one. Start with the basics (exercise, sets, reps, weight) and add more later if useful.
      </p>

      <h3>Review Regularly</h3>
      <p>
        A log you never look at isn't helping. Review before each workout to know what you're trying to beat. Review weekly or monthly to spot trends.
      </p>

      <h3>Make It Accessible</h3>
      <p>
        Your tracking method should be easy to access at the gym. If it's inconvenient, you'll stop doing it.
      </p>

      <h2>Using Your Log for Progress</h2>

      <h3>Before Each Workout</h3>
      <p>
        Check what you did last time for each exercise. Your goal: beat it somehow—more weight, more reps, better form.
      </p>

      <h3>Weekly Review</h3>
      <p>
        Did you complete all planned workouts? Did lifts improve? Any recurring issues or fatigue?
      </p>

      <h3>Monthly Review</h3>
      <p>
        Look at trends over the month. Where did you progress? Where did you stall? Do you need to adjust volume, exercises, or recovery?
      </p>

      <h3>Long-Term Review</h3>
      <p>
        Every few months, zoom out. Compare current performance to 3-6 months ago. Celebrate progress and identify what drove it.
      </p>

      <h2>Sample Tracking Formats</h2>

      <h3>Simple Notebook Format</h3>
      <pre>
{`Monday, April 14
Bench Press: 155x8, 155x8, 155x7 (RPE 8)
Incline DB Press: 50x10, 50x10, 50x9
Cable Flyes: 30x12, 30x12, 30x12
Tricep Pushdown: 50x12, 50x12
Notes: Felt strong today, sleep was good`}
      </pre>

      <h3>Spreadsheet Columns</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Date | Exercise | Set 1 | Set 2 | Set 3 | RPE | Notes</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>App Entry</h3>
      <p>
        Most apps have you select the exercise, then enter weight and reps for each set. The app stores history and shows your previous performance.
      </p>

      <h2>Common Mistakes</h2>

      <h3>Not Tracking at All</h3>
      <p>
        The biggest mistake. Even imperfect tracking beats no tracking.
      </p>

      <h3>Tracking but Not Reviewing</h3>
      <p>
        A log you never look at is just data hoarding. Use it actively.
      </p>

      <h3>Overcomplicating</h3>
      <p>
        Tracking 20 variables creates friction. Keep it sustainable.
      </p>

      <h3>Inconsistent Tracking</h3>
      <p>
        Logging some workouts but not others creates gaps that reduce usefulness.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Tracking your workouts turns guessing into knowing. It enables progressive overload, helps you identify what works, and keeps you accountable.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Track at minimum: exercises, sets, reps, weight</li>
        <li>Add RPE and notes for more insight</li>
        <li>Choose a method you'll actually use (paper, app, spreadsheet)</li>
        <li>Track during rest periods to build the habit</li>
        <li>Review your log before workouts and periodically</li>
        <li>Use historical data to guide progression decisions</li>
        <li>Start simple—you can add detail later</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
