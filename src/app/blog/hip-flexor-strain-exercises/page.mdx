# Hip Flexor Strain Exercises: Recover from Pulled Hip Flexor

A hip flexor strain can sideline athletes and active individuals with front hip and groin pain. Proper rehabilitation restores flexibility, rebuilds strength, and prevents re-injury.

## Understanding Hip Flexor Strains

The hip flexors lift your knee toward your chest. The main muscles involved are:

**Iliopsoas:** Deep hip flexor (iliacus + psoas major)
- Primary hip flexor
- Deep groin/front hip pain

**Rectus Femoris:** Part of quadriceps, crosses hip
- Front thigh pain
- Pain with both hip flexion and knee extension

**Strain grades:**
- **Grade I:** Mild strain, minimal fiber damage
- **Grade II:** Moderate, partial tear
- **Grade III:** Severe, complete tear (rare)

**Common causes:**
- Sprinting and kicking
- Sudden acceleration
- Overuse (runners, cyclists)
- Sit-ups and leg raises
- Inadequate warm-up

## Symptoms

- Front hip or groin pain
- Pain with lifting knee
- Pain with resisted hip flexion
- Tightness after sitting
- Pain with sprinting or kicking
- Possible swelling or bruising (severe strains)

## Phase 1: Acute Management (Days 1-7)

### PRICE Protocol

**Protect:** Avoid aggravating activities
**Rest:** Relative rest (not complete bed rest)
**Ice:** 15-20 minutes, several times daily
**Compression:** If swelling present
**Elevation:** When possible

### Activity Modification
- Avoid running and kicking
- Limit stairs (take one at a time)
- Avoid sit-ups and leg raises
- Reduce sitting time (shortens hip flexors)

### Gentle Movement

**Knee to Chest** (Passive)
1. Lie on back
2. Use hands to pull knee toward chest
3. Gentle stretch, no pain
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. 3-5 repetitions

**Gentle Hip Circles**
1. Lie on back, knee bent
2. Small circles with knee
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Pain-free range only

**Pelvic Tilts**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Flatten low back to floor
3. Then arch slightly
4. 15-20 repetitions

## Phase 2: Early Mobility (Weeks 1-2)

### Stretching (Gentle)

**Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch**
1. Kneel on affected side (use padding)
2. Front foot forward, knee at 90°
3. Tuck pelvis under (posterior tilt)
4. Lean forward gently
5. Should feel stretch, not pain
6. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times

**Supine Hip Flexor Stretch**
1. Lie on back at edge of bed
2. Pull unaffected knee to chest
3. Let affected leg hang off edge
4. Feel stretch in front of hip
5. Hold 30 seconds

**Prone Lying**
1. Simply lie face down
2. Extends the hip
3. 5-10 minutes
4. Gentle sustained stretch

### Active Range of Motion

**Supine Marching**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift one knee toward chest
3. Lower slowly
4. Alternate sides
5. 10-15 each side

**Standing Knee Raise**
1. Hold support
2. Lift knee gently
3. Only to comfortable range
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Core Activation

**Abdominal Bracing**
1. Lie on back
2. Gently contract abs
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Don't hold breath
5. 10-15 repetitions

**Glute Squeezes**
1. Lie on back
2. Squeeze glutes together
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 2-4)

### Hip Flexor Strengthening (Gradual)

**Supine Hip Flexion** (Gravity Assisted)
1. Lie on back
2. Slide heel toward buttock
3. Then lift knee slightly
4. Lower slowly
5. 2-3 sets of 10-15

**Standing Hip Flexion**
1. Hold support
2. Lift knee toward chest
3. Control the descent
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Seated Knee Raise**
1. Sit in chair
2. Lift thigh off seat
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. 2-3 sets of 15

### Resistance Progression

**Band Hip Flexion** (Light)
1. Band around foot, anchored behind
2. Lift knee against resistance
3. Control return
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Slider Marching**
1. Lie on back, feet on sliders
2. Slide one foot in, then out
3. Engages hip flexors with less load
4. 2 sets of 15 each side

### Core Strengthening

**Dead Bug**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees bent
2. Lower opposite arm and leg
3. Keep low back flat
4. 10 reps each side

**Bird Dog**
1. Hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep spine neutral
4. 10 reps each side

**Plank** (When Comfortable)
1. On forearms and toes
2. Hold straight line
3. Start with 20-30 seconds
4. Progress duration

### Glute Strengthening

**Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Lift hips, squeeze glutes
3. 3 sets of 15

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Lift top knee
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

## Phase 4: Functional Strengthening (Weeks 4-6)

### Progressive Hip Flexor Loading

**Mountain Climbers** (Slow)
1. Push-up position
2. Bring knee toward chest slowly
3. Alternate
4. 10-15 each side

**Leg Raises** (Progressive)
1. Lie on back
2. Lift straight legs slightly
3. Progress height as tolerated
4. 2-3 sets of 10-15

**Hanging Knee Raise** (Advanced)
1. Hang from bar
2. Lift knees toward chest
3. Control descent
4. 2-3 sets of 10

### Single-Leg Exercises

**Split Squat**
1. Staggered stance
2. Lower into lunge
3. Don't let back knee touch
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Step-Ups**
1. Step onto box
2. Drive through front leg
3. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Single-Leg RDL**
1. Hinge on one leg
2. Reach opposite leg back
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Dynamic Movements

**Walking Lunges**
1. Step forward into lunge
2. Drive forward to next step
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

**Lateral Lunges**
1. Step to side, hinge at hip
2. Push back to start
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

## Phase 5: Return to Sport (Weeks 6-8+)

### Running Progression

**Week 1:** Walk-jog intervals (30 sec jog, 60 sec walk)
**Week 2:** Extend jog intervals (60 sec jog, 30 sec walk)
**Week 3:** Continuous easy jogging (10-15 min)
**Week 4:** Progress distance, add strides
**Week 5:** Introduce faster running
**Week 6:** Sport-specific training

### Kicking/Sprinting Progression

**Level 1:** Easy passing/shooting at 50%
**Level 2:** Moderate intensity (70%)
**Level 3:** Near full intensity (90%)
**Level 4:** Full intensity in controlled setting
**Level 5:** Full game/competition

### Return Criteria

- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Strength equal to uninjured side
- No pain with sport-specific movements
- Completed running progression
- Passed sport-specific testing

## Preventing Re-Injury

### Dynamic Warm-Up
- Leg swings (forward/back and side/side)
- Walking lunges
- High knees (progressive)
- Butt kicks
- Hip circles

### Maintain Flexibility
- Daily hip flexor stretches
- Don't neglect after sitting
- Stretch after activity

### Strength Maintenance
- Continue hip flexor strengthening
- Maintain core and glute strength
- Address any imbalances

### Training Principles
- Gradual progression
- Adequate recovery
- Avoid sudden increases in sprinting/kicking volume

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 4)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Kneeling hip flexor stretch: 3×30 sec each
3. Supine hip flexion: 3×15
4. Standing band hip flexion: 3×15
5. Dead bug: 3×10 each side
6. Glute bridge: 3×15

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Split squats: 3×12 each
3. Step-ups: 3×12 each
4. Bird dog: 3×10 each side
5. Clamshells: 3×15 each
6. Hip flexor stretch: 3×30 sec

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Walking lunges: 2×10 each
3. Slow mountain climbers: 2×10 each
4. Single-leg RDL: 3×10 each
5. Plank: 3×30 sec
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 5
1. Light jog (if cleared): 10-15 min
2. Dynamic movements
3. Sport-specific drills (low intensity)
4. Cool-down and stretch

## When to Seek Help

**See a healthcare provider if:**
- Severe pain or inability to walk
- No improvement after 2-3 weeks
- Significant swelling or bruising
- Pain at rest
- Numbness or weakness

**Imaging may be needed for:**
- Grade III tears
- Suspected avulsion fracture
- Not improving with rehabilitation

## Key Takeaways

1. **Rest initially, but not too long** — early gentle movement helps
2. **Stretch the hip flexors** — multiple times daily
3. **Progress strengthening gradually** — start easy, build up
4. **Don't neglect core and glutes** — support the hip flexors
5. **Warm up thoroughly** — especially before sprinting/kicking
6. **Return to sport progressively** — follow a structured plan
7. **Maintain gains** — continue exercises after returning to sport
8. **Prevent re-injury** — address why it happened

Hip flexor strains respond well to proper rehabilitation. Be patient through the phases, address flexibility and strength, and you'll return to full activity with a healthier, more resilient hip.
