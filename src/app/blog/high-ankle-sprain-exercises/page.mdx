# High Ankle Sprain Exercises: Syndesmosis Injury Rehabilitation

A high ankle sprain (syndesmosis injury) is different from a typical ankle sprain and requires longer recovery. Understanding this injury and following proper rehabilitation is crucial for full return to activity.

## Understanding High Ankle Sprains

The syndesmosis is the ligament complex that holds the tibia and fibula together above the ankle joint. Unlike lateral ankle sprains (which affect the outside of the ankle), high ankle sprains affect the area above the joint.

**Anatomy involved:**
- Anterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (AITFL)
- Posterior inferior tibiofibular ligament (PITFL)
- Interosseous membrane
- Transverse ligament

**Mechanism of injury:**
- External rotation of foot
- Dorsiflexion (foot forced upward)
- Common in football, hockey, skiing
- Often from planted foot with body rotation

## How It's Different from Regular Ankle Sprains

| Feature | Lateral Ankle Sprain | High Ankle Sprain |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Location | Outside of ankle | Above ankle joint |
| Swelling | Usually significant | Often less visible |
| Recovery | 1-4 weeks | 6-12 weeks |
| Pain pattern | Outer ankle | Above ankle, with rotation |

**Key point:** High ankle sprains take significantly longer to heal—be patient.

## Grades of Injury

**Grade I:** Ligaments stretched, stable joint
**Grade II:** Partial tear, some widening
**Grade III:** Complete tear, unstable joint (may need surgery)

## Treatment Overview

**Conservative (Grades I-II):**
- Protected weight-bearing
- Walking boot
- Progressive rehabilitation
- 6-12 weeks recovery

**Surgical (Grade III or unstable):**
- Screw or tightrope fixation
- Longer protected period
- Structured rehabilitation
- 3-6 months recovery

## Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 1-3)

### Initial Management
- Walking boot or cast
- Crutches as needed
- Ice and elevation
- Protect healing ligaments

### What to Avoid
- Dorsiflexion (foot up) — stresses syndesmosis
- External rotation — mechanism of injury
- Weight-bearing beyond recommendations

### Allowed Exercises

**Toe Movements**
1. Wiggle toes
2. Curl and spread
3. Maintains circulation

**Knee and Hip Exercises**
1. Quad sets
2. Straight leg raises
3. Hip abduction
4. Maintain leg strength

**Gentle Plantarflexion** (If Allowed)
1. Point toes gently
2. Avoid dorsiflexion
3. Very gentle range

## Phase 2: Early Mobility (Weeks 3-6)

### Protected Range of Motion

**Plantarflexion Focus**
1. Sit with foot supported
2. Point toe gently
3. Avoid pulling foot up aggressively
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Gradual Dorsiflexion** (Week 4+)
1. Very gentle, pain-free
2. Progress slowly
3. Don't force

**Inversion/Eversion** (Gentle)
1. Turn foot in and out
2. Small movements
3. 15-20 repetitions

### Early Strengthening

**Isometric Exercises**
1. Push foot against resistance (no movement)
2. All directions except dorsiflexion initially
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10 repetitions each

**Seated Heel Raises**
1. Sit with feet flat
2. Lift heels
3. 2-3 sets of 20

**Toe Raises** (Seated)
1. Lift toes, heels stay down
2. 2-3 sets of 20

### Weight-Bearing Progression
- Follow physician guidelines exactly
- Boot weaning as directed
- Progress from partial to full weight-bearing

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 6-10)

### Calf Strengthening

**Bilateral Heel Raises** (Standing)
1. Rise onto toes
2. Lower slowly
3. 3 sets of 15
4. Progress to single leg

**Eccentric Heel Drops**
1. Rise on both feet
2. Lower on one foot
3. 3 sets of 12

### Band Exercises

**4-Way Ankle**
1. Plantarflexion: Point against band
2. Dorsiflexion: Pull toward shin (progress carefully)
3. Inversion: Turn in
4. Eversion: Turn out
5. 3 sets of 15 each

**External Rotation Caution:** Progress external rotation exercises slowly—this is the injury mechanism.

### Functional Exercises

**Mini Squats**
1. Partial depth
2. Equal weight distribution
3. 3 sets of 15

**Step-Ups** (Low)
1. 4-6 inch step
2. Control the movement
3. 3 sets of 12 each

**Balance Training**
1. Single-leg stance
2. Progress: eyes closed, foam pad
3. 3×30 seconds each leg

## Phase 4: Advanced Rehabilitation (Weeks 10-16)

### Continued Strengthening

**Single-Leg Heel Raises**
1. Full range
2. Control eccentric
3. 3 sets of 12-15

**Lunges**
1. Forward and reverse
2. Good ankle control
3. 3 sets of 10 each

**Single-Leg Squats**
1. Partial to full depth
2. 2-3 sets of 10 each

### Balance Progression

**Dynamic Balance**
1. Single-leg reaches
2. Star excursion
3. Perturbation training

**Unstable Surfaces**
1. Foam pad
2. BOSU ball
3. Balance board

### Agility Introduction

**Controlled Movements**
1. Walking patterns (figure-8s)
2. Gradual lateral movements
3. No cutting yet

## Phase 5: Return to Sport (Weeks 12-20+)

### Agility Progression

**Lateral Shuffles**
1. Start slow
2. Progress speed
3. 2-3 sets of 20 yards

**Carioca**
1. Crossover movements
2. Progress gradually
3. 2-3 sets of 20 yards

**Cutting Drills**
1. Start at 45 degrees
2. Progress angles
3. Progress speed
4. **Key stress test for syndesmosis**

**Rotational Movements**
1. Progress carefully
2. External rotation is the injury mechanism
3. Build confidence gradually

### Plyometrics

**Box Jumps**
1. Low to moderate height
2. Focus on landing
3. 2-3 sets of 8

**Lateral Hops**
1. Side-to-side
2. Controlled landing
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each direction

**Single-Leg Hops** (Late)
1. Forward and lateral
2. 2-3 sets of 6 each

### Running Progression

1. Walk-jog intervals
2. Continuous jogging
3. Tempo runs
4. Sprints
5. Direction changes (last to add)

## Return Criteria

**Before full return:**
- Full, pain-free ROM
- Strength equal to other side
- No pain with dorsiflexion stress
- No pain with external rotation stress
- Completed cutting and agility drills
- Sport-specific movements pain-free

**Timeline:** Often 12-20 weeks for athletes (longer than lateral sprains)

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 8)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Bike 10 min
2. Band exercises (4-way): 3×15 each
3. Heel raises: 3×15
4. Step-ups: 3×12 each
5. Balance: Single leg, 3×30 sec each

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: Walking 10 min
2. Squats: 3×12
3. Lunges: 2×10 each
4. Calf stretch: 3×30 sec
5. Balance board: 5 min

### Day 3: Rest or Upper Body

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: 10 min
2. Single-leg heel raises: 2×12 each
3. Single-leg balance: Progressions 10 min
4. Step-downs: 3×12 each
5. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 5
1. Light cardio: 20-30 min (bike or walking)
2. Mobility work
3. Balance practice

## When to Be Concerned

**Contact your physician if:**
- Pain worsening rather than improving
- Significant swelling returning
- Inability to progress with weight-bearing
- Pain with walking doesn't improve
- Popping, clicking, or catching

**May indicate:**
- More severe injury than initially assessed
- Need for imaging
- Possible surgical consideration

## Key Takeaways

1. **High ankle sprains take longer** — 2-4× longer than lateral sprains
2. **Protect early** — avoid dorsiflexion and external rotation
3. **Progress ROM carefully** — especially dorsiflexion
4. **Cutting is the test** — last movement to return
5. **Don't compare to lateral sprains** — different injury
6. **Balance training essential** — prevents future injuries
7. **Return when ready** — meet objective criteria
8. **Surgery if unstable** — Grade III may need fixation

High ankle sprains require patience and disciplined rehabilitation. The injury heals more slowly than typical sprains, but with proper progression, most athletes return to full activity.
