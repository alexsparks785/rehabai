# How to Fix Knee Valgus: Exercises for Knee Caving

Knee valgus—when your knees cave inward during squats, lunges, or even walking—is one of the most common movement dysfunctions. It increases injury risk (especially ACL tears), limits your strength potential, and can lead to chronic knee pain. The good news: it's fixable with the right targeted work.

## What Is Knee Valgus?

Knee valgus occurs when your knee collapses inward toward the midline of your body while your foot stays planted. You'll notice it most during:

- Squatting (knees buckle in at the bottom)
- Landing from a jump
- Walking up stairs
- Single-leg movements
- Running (knees angle inward with each stride)

This position puts excessive stress on the ACL, MCL, and the inside of the knee joint. It's a leading predictor of non-contact ACL injuries, particularly in female athletes.

## Why Your Knees Cave In

Understanding the cause helps you fix the problem at its source:

### 1. Weak Hip External Rotators
The gluteus medius and other hip external rotators control knee position from above. When they're weak, they can't keep the knee tracking over the toes.

### 2. Weak Glutes Overall
Your gluteus maximus is your primary hip extensor. Weak glutes force other muscles to compensate, often resulting in poor knee alignment.

### 3. Tight or Weak Hip Adductors
Overly tight inner thigh muscles can pull the knee inward. Paradoxically, weak adductors can also contribute to instability.

### 4. Limited Ankle Dorsiflexion
If your ankles are stiff, your body compensates by letting the knees collapse inward to create more depth in squats.

### 5. Poor Motor Control
Sometimes the strength is there, but the brain hasn't learned to activate the right muscles at the right time.

### 6. Structural Factors
Wide hips (more common in women), foot pronation, and natural femoral angle can predispose you to valgus. These can't be changed, but can be managed.

## Self-Assessment: Do You Have Knee Valgus?

### The Overhead Squat Test
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
2. Raise your arms overhead
3. Squat as deep as you comfortably can
4. Watch your knees in a mirror or have someone video you

**Signs of valgus:**
- Knees move inward at any point during the squat
- More pronounced at the bottom of the squat
- Worse when fatigued or moving quickly

### The Single-Leg Squat Test
1. Stand on one leg
2. Slowly squat down as far as you can
3. Watch your knee

This test often reveals valgus that doesn't show up in two-legged movements.

## Exercises to Fix Knee Valgus

### Phase 1: Activation and Awareness

#### 1. Banded Glute Bridges with Abduction
**Why:** Teaches your glutes to fire while maintaining external rotation.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a resistance band above your knees
2. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
3. Push your knees outward against the band
4. Lift your hips while maintaining outward pressure
5. Squeeze at the top for 2 seconds
6. Lower slowly
7. Do 15-20 reps

#### 2. Side-Lying Clamshells
**Why:** Isolates the gluteus medius in a non-weight-bearing position.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your side with knees bent at 45 degrees
2. Keep your feet together
3. Lift your top knee as high as possible
4. Don't let your hips roll back
5. Lower slowly
6. Do 20 reps per side

**Progression:** Add a resistance band above your knees.

#### 3. Quadruped Hip Circles
**Why:** Builds hip control and strengthens external rotators.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Lift your right knee off the ground
3. Make big circles with your knee (forward, out, back, in)
4. Keep your core stable—don't shift your hips
5. Do 10 circles each direction per leg

### Phase 2: Strength Building

#### 4. Monster Walks
**Why:** Trains hip abduction and external rotation in a functional standing position.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a resistance band above your knees (or around ankles for more challenge)
2. Get into a quarter-squat position
3. Take small steps forward and outward at 45-degree angles
4. Keep tension on the band constantly
5. Walk 10-15 steps forward, then backward

#### 5. Lateral Band Walks
**Why:** Strengthens hip abductors through full range.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a band around your ankles or above your knees
2. Get into a quarter-squat position
3. Step sideways, leading with your heel
4. Don't let your stance leg collapse inward
5. Keep your toes pointing forward
6. Do 15-20 steps each direction

#### 6. Single-Leg Romanian Deadlifts
**Why:** Builds hip stability and glute strength in a single-leg stance.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on your right leg
2. Hinge forward at the hips
3. Let your left leg extend behind you for balance
4. Keep your hips square (don't open up)
5. Feel the stretch in your right hamstring
6. Drive through your heel to stand
7. Do 10 reps per leg

**Key:** Watch that your stance knee doesn't cave in during the movement.

#### 7. Bulgarian Split Squats
**Why:** Develops single-leg strength while training proper knee tracking.

**How to do it:**
1. Place your back foot on a bench or chair
2. Hop your front foot forward into a lunge stance
3. Lower your back knee toward the ground
4. Actively push your front knee outward over your toes
5. Drive through your front heel to stand
6. Do 10-12 reps per leg

**Cue:** "Push your knee toward your pinky toe."

### Phase 3: Movement Integration

#### 8. Goblet Squats with Band
**Why:** Trains proper squat mechanics with external feedback.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a resistance band above your knees
2. Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell at your chest
3. Squat down while pushing your knees out against the band
4. Keep your chest up and knees tracking over toes
5. Stand and squeeze your glutes at the top
6. Do 12-15 reps

#### 9. Box Squats with Knee Focus
**Why:** The box provides a target and allows you to focus on knee position.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a box or bench behind you
2. Squat down until you lightly touch the box
3. Actively drive your knees outward throughout
4. Pause briefly on the box (don't relax)
5. Stand up explosively
6. Do 10-12 reps

#### 10. Step-Downs with Control
**Why:** Eccentric single-leg control is crucial for fixing valgus.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on a step or box (6-12 inches high)
2. Slowly lower your non-stance foot toward the ground
3. Tap gently and return to start
4. Focus intensely on keeping your stance knee over your toes
5. Do 10-12 reps per leg

## Ankle Mobility Work

Limited ankle mobility is a hidden cause of knee valgus. If your ankles can't dorsiflex, your knees compensate.

### Ankle Dorsiflexion Stretch
1. Place your foot on a step or against a wall
2. Drive your knee forward over your toes
3. Keep your heel down
4. Hold for 30 seconds
5. Do 3 reps per side

### Elevated Heel Squats (Temporary Fix)
While you work on ankle mobility, elevating your heels with small plates or squat wedges can help you squat with better knee alignment.

## Your Weekly Program

### 3x Per Week (10-15 minutes):

**Warm-Up (3 minutes):**
- Ankle circles: 10 each direction
- Hip circles: 10 each direction per leg
- Bodyweight glute bridges: 15 reps

**Activation (3 minutes):**
- Clamshells: 15 reps per side
- Banded glute bridges: 15 reps

**Strength (5-7 minutes):**
- Monster walks: 15 steps each direction
- Single-leg RDLs: 10 per leg
- Goblet squats with band: 12 reps

**Integration (2 minutes):**
- Step-downs: 10 per leg
- Bodyweight squats focusing on knee tracking: 10 reps

## Cueing Yourself During Movement

Mental cues help reinforce proper patterns:

- **"Spread the floor"** – Push your feet outward as if trying to tear the floor apart
- **"Knees to pinkies"** – Drive your knees toward your pinky toes
- **"Screw your feet in"** – Rotate your feet into the ground (external rotation)
- **"Make space"** – Create space between your thighs at the bottom of the squat

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Overcorrecting:** Pushing your knees too far out (varus) is also problematic. Knees should track over the 2nd-3rd toe.

**Going too heavy too soon:** Master the pattern with light weight before adding load.

**Neglecting ankle mobility:** All the glute work won't help if stiff ankles are the root cause.

**Only training bilateral movements:** Single-leg work reveals and fixes asymmetries.

**Expecting overnight results:** Changing movement patterns takes 4-8 weeks of consistent work.

## When to See a Professional

Seek help if:
- You have pain with the exercises
- Your knee valgus doesn't improve after 6-8 weeks
- You have a history of knee injury or surgery
- One leg is significantly worse than the other
- You're an athlete returning from ACL injury

## The Bottom Line

Knee valgus is usually a hip problem, not a knee problem. Weak glutes—especially the gluteus medius—are the most common culprit. Add in some ankle mobility work and conscious movement practice, and most people can fix their knee caving within 6-8 weeks.

Start with the activation exercises (clamshells, banded bridges) to wake up the right muscles. Progress to strength work (monster walks, single-leg RDLs) to build capacity. Then integrate those patterns into your squats and lunges.

The key is consistency. A few minutes of targeted work several times per week beats an occasional hour-long session. Your brain needs repetition to rewire the movement pattern.

Fix the hips, free the knees.
