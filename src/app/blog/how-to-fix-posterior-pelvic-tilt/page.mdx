      <h1>How to Fix Posterior Pelvic Tilt: Exercises for Pelvic Alignment</h1>

      <p>While anterior pelvic tilt (excessive arch) gets most of the attention, posterior pelvic tilt—where your pelvis tucks under and your lower back flattens—is equally problematic. It can cause back pain, limit hip mobility, and affect everything from your squat to your posture. Here's how to fix it.</p>

      <h2>What Is Posterior Pelvic Tilt?</h2>

      <p>Posterior pelvic tilt occurs when the front of your pelvis rises and the back drops down, causing your lower back to flatten or even round. Think of your pelvis as a bowl of water—in posterior tilt, the water would spill out the back.</p>

      <p><strong>Signs you have posterior pelvic tilt:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Flat or rounded lower back (loss of natural curve)</li>
        <li>Tailbone tucked under when standing</li>
        <li>Difficulty arching your lower back</li>
        <li>Lower back rounds at bottom of squat ("butt wink")</li>
        <li>Tight feeling in lower back</li>
        <li>Difficulty sitting up straight</li>
        <li>Belt line tilts backward</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How to Check Your Pelvic Position</h2>

      <p>Stand sideways to a mirror. Place one hand on your lower back. Is there a natural inward curve, or is your lower back flat? Now place your hands on your hip bones—if they tilt backward (top of pelvis behind the bottom), you have posterior tilt.</p>

      <h2>What Causes Posterior Pelvic Tilt?</h2>

      <p><strong>Tight muscles:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Hamstrings (pull pelvis down from behind)</li>
        <li>Glutes (when chronically shortened)</li>
        <li>Abdominals (pull pelvis up from front)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Weak muscles:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Hip flexors (don't pull pelvis forward enough)</li>
        <li>Lower back extensors (can't maintain curve)</li>
        <li>Often part of a pattern with weak hip flexors</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Habitual causes:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Slouching while sitting</li>
        <li>Overcorrection from fear of anterior tilt</li>
        <li>Excessive "tucking" during exercises</li>
        <li>Prolonged sitting with poor posture</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Problems Caused by Posterior Pelvic Tilt</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Lower back pain:</strong> Loss of natural curve loads the spine poorly</li>
        <li><strong>Limited hip mobility:</strong> Pelvis position affects hip range</li>
        <li><strong>Butt wink in squats:</strong> Pelvis tucks at the bottom, stressing lower back</li>
        <li><strong>Disc issues:</strong> Rounding under load increases disc pressure</li>
        <li><strong>Hamstring strains:</strong> Constantly tight hamstrings are injury-prone</li>
        <li><strong>Poor posture:</strong> Affects entire spinal alignment</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises to Fix Posterior Pelvic Tilt</h2>

      <h3>Release Tight Muscles</h3>

      <h4>1. Hamstring Stretch (Standing)</h4>
      <p>Place one heel on a low surface (step, bench). Keep your back straight—don't round forward. Hinge at the hips until you feel a stretch in the back of your thigh. Tight hamstrings are often the main culprit.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Lying Hamstring Stretch</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, one leg flat. Use a strap or your hands to pull the other leg up while keeping it straight. Keep your lower back in neutral (slight arch), not pressed flat to the floor.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 45-60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Foam Roll Hamstrings</h4>
      <p>Sit on a foam roller with it under your thighs. Roll slowly from just above the knee to just below the glutes. Cross one leg over the other for more pressure.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each leg</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Glute Foam Roll</h4>
      <p>Sit on the foam roller, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, and lean toward the crossed leg side. Roll through the glute muscle.</p>
      <p><strong>60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Strengthen Weak Muscles</h3>

      <h4>5. Hip Flexor March (Supine)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back with a slight arch in your lower back (don't flatten it). Lift one knee toward your chest using your hip flexor, hold 3 seconds, lower slowly. Keep your lower back in neutral throughout.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Standing Hip Flexor Lift</h4>
      <p>Stand tall and lift one knee toward your chest without rounding your lower back or leaning backward. This strengthens the hip flexors that help maintain proper pelvic position.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Prone Back Extension (Controlled)</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with hands beside your shoulders. Lift your chest a few inches off the ground using your lower back muscles. Don't push with your hands—let your back extensors do the work. Hold 2-3 seconds.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Superman Hold</h4>
      <p>Lie face down with arms extended overhead. Lift your arms and legs simultaneously, holding at the top for 2-3 seconds. This strengthens the entire posterior chain including the lower back extensors.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10-12</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Quadruped Arm and Leg Lift</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend one arm and the opposite leg while maintaining a neutral spine (with a slight arch in lower back). Don't let your back round. This trains the back extensors to work with the core.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Pelvic Position Training</h3>

      <h4>10. Pelvic Tilts (Finding Neutral)</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back with knees bent. Practice tilting your pelvis forward (increasing arch) and backward (flattening back). Find the middle—neutral position—with a small natural curve. Hold neutral and breathe.</p>
      <p><strong>10 tilts each way, then hold neutral for 30 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Standing Pelvic Tilts</h4>
      <p>Stand with your back against a wall. Practice tilting your pelvis forward and backward. Notice how this feels and find neutral. Practice throughout the day.</p>
      <p><strong>Practice multiple times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Cat-Cow with Emphasis on Cow</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, alternate between arching your back (cow—what you need more of) and rounding it (cat). Spend extra time in the cow position, focusing on creating an arch in your lower back.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 10, emphasizing the cow portion</strong></p>

      <h3>Functional Integration</h3>

      <h4>13. Goblet Squat with Neutral Spine</h4>
      <p>Hold a weight at your chest. Squat down while maintaining a neutral spine—keep that natural curve in your lower back throughout. Don't let your pelvis tuck at the bottom. Go only as deep as you can maintain neutral.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10</strong></p>

      <h4>14. Romanian Deadlift</h4>
      <p>Hold weights at thighs. Hinge at hips, pushing them back while keeping spine neutral (slight arch maintained). This teaches you to hip hinge without losing pelvic position.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10</strong></p>

      <h4>15. Good Morning</h4>
      <p>Barbell on upper back or hands behind head. Hinge forward from hips while keeping lower back in neutral (not rounding). Return by driving hips forward. This strengthens the ability to maintain lumbar curve during hip flexion.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10-12</strong></p>

      <h2>Sitting and Standing Awareness</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Sitting:</strong> Don't slouch or round your lower back. Sit on your sit bones with a slight arch. Use a lumbar support if needed.</li>
        <li><strong>Standing:</strong> Find neutral pelvis—don't tuck under. Weight balanced on whole foot.</li>
        <li><strong>Lifting:</strong> Maintain lumbar curve. Never round your lower back under load.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Mistakes</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Over-tucking during exercises:</strong> "Tuck your pelvis" is overcued and creates problems</li>

        <li><strong>Only stretching:</strong> You need to strengthen hip flexors and back extensors too</li>
        <li><strong>Forcing an arch:</strong> Goal is neutral, not excessive extension</li>
        <li><strong>Flattening back during planks:</strong> A slight curve is okay and often better</li>
      </ul>
