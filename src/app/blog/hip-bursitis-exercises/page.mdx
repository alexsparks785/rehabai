# Hip Bursitis Exercises: Relieve Trochanteric Pain Syndrome

Hip bursitis—pain on the outer hip—is one of the most common causes of hip pain. While called "bursitis," current understanding suggests it's often a tendon problem. Either way, the right exercises can provide significant relief.

## Understanding Hip Bursitis

The greater trochanter is the bony bump on the outer hip. The area contains bursae (fluid-filled sacs) and the gluteal tendon attachments.

**Current terminology:**
- **Trochanteric bursitis:** Traditional name (inflammation of bursa)
- **Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS):** More accurate term
- **Gluteal tendinopathy:** Often the actual pathology

**What's really happening:**
Most cases involve the gluteal tendons (gluteus medius and minimus) rather than just bursa inflammation. Treatment should address both possibilities.

**Common causes:**
- Weakness in hip abductors
- Overuse (running, walking)
- Direct trauma
- Prolonged side-lying
- Leg length discrepancy
- Hip or knee problems affecting gait

## Symptoms

- Pain on outer hip (over bony prominence)
- Worse lying on affected side
- Pain with stairs, especially going up
- Worse with prolonged standing or walking
- May radiate down outer thigh
- Tender to touch at greater trochanter
- Pain with single-leg standing

## What Makes It Worse

**Avoid these initially:**
- Lying directly on affected hip
- Crossing legs
- Standing with hip dropped (hip hanging posture)
- Excessive stretching of IT band/outer hip
- Running (temporarily)

**Position modifications:**
- Sleep on unaffected side with pillow between knees
- Avoid crossing legs when sitting
- Stand with weight evenly distributed

## Phase 1: Pain Reduction (Weeks 1-2)

### Isometric Hip Abduction

**Critical exercise** — builds strength without tendon compression.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand sideways to wall
2. Affected hip toward wall
3. Push outer hip/knee against wall
4. Hold 30-45 seconds
5. 5 repetitions
6. 3 times daily

**Why it works:** Isometrics provide pain relief and begin strengthening without the compression that occurs with movement.

### Avoid Stretching (Initially)

**Counterintuitive but important:**
- Stretching the IT band compresses the tendons
- Avoid pigeon pose, IT band stretches
- The "tight" feeling is often from tendon irritation

### Posture Correction

**Standing:**
- Avoid hip drop (hanging on one hip)
- Stand with weight evenly distributed
- Engage glutes slightly

**Sitting:**
- Avoid crossing legs
- Sit symmetrically
- Use armrests to reduce hip compression

### Ice

- Apply to outer hip for 15-20 minutes
- After activity or when painful
- Can help reduce inflammation

## Phase 2: Early Strengthening (Weeks 2-4)

### Progressive Isometrics

**Wall Push** (Increased Duration)
1. Same as before
2. Increase to 45-60 second holds
3. 5 reps, 2-3 times daily

**Bridge with Isometric Abduction**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Band around knees
3. Press out against band while bridging
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. 5-8 repetitions

### Supine Exercises

**Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 5 seconds at top
4. 3 sets of 15

**Bridge with March** (Gentle)
1. Hold bridge position
2. Lift one foot slightly
3. Set down, lift other
4. 10 marches total

### Side-Lying (Non-Affected Side)

**Supported Hip Abduction**
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Affected leg on pillows (supported)
3. Gently lift leg (small range)
4. 2-3 sets of 10-15

**Note:** Avoid aggressive side-lying exercises on affected side initially.

### Standing Exercises

**Mini Squats**
1. Partial squat (quarter depth)
2. Weight evenly distributed
3. Squeeze glutes at top
4. 3 sets of 15

**Weight Shifts**
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width
2. Shift weight side to side
3. Control the movement
4. 2 minutes total

## Phase 3: Progressive Loading (Weeks 4-8)

### Hip Abductor Strengthening

**Standing Hip Abduction**
1. Hold support
2. Lift leg out to side
3. Control the descent
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction** (Affected Side Up)
1. Lie on unaffected side
2. Top leg straight
3. Lift toward ceiling
4. Don't go too high
5. 3 sets of 15

**Clamshells** (Progressive)
1. Side-lying, knees bent 45°
2. Lift top knee, keep feet together
3. Add band as tolerated
4. 3 sets of 15

### Functional Strengthening

**Step-Ups**
1. Step onto low box/step
2. Drive through heel
3. Control descent
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Lateral Step-Downs**
1. Stand on step sideways
2. Lower opposite foot toward floor
3. Don't touch—control the motion
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Single-Leg Mini Squat**
1. Partial squat on one leg
2. Keep pelvis level (no hip drop)
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Band Exercises

**Lateral Band Walks**
1. Band around ankles or above knees
2. Side-step with tension
3. Stay low, knees slightly bent
4. 20 steps each direction

**Monster Walks**
1. Band above knees
2. Walk forward in wide stance
3. Knees apart throughout
4. 20 steps

**Band Squats**
1. Band above knees
2. Press out against band during squat
3. 3 sets of 12

## Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 8-12)

### Single-Leg Focus

**Single-Leg Bridge**
1. One leg extended
2. Bridge on single leg
3. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Single-Leg RDL**
1. Hinge on one leg
2. Keep hips level
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

**Bulgarian Split Squat**
1. Rear foot elevated
2. Lower into lunge
3. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Balance and Stability

**Single-Leg Balance**
1. Stand on affected leg
2. Progress: eyes closed, unstable surface
3. 3×30 seconds each side

**Single-Leg Balance with Movement**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Reach other leg forward, back, and side
3. Maintain pelvic stability
4. 10 reaches each direction

### Running Preparation (If Applicable)

**Start with:**
1. Walk-jog intervals
2. Flat surfaces
3. Short distances
4. Build gradually

**Watch for:**
- Return of symptoms
- Hip drop while running
- Fatigue leading to poor form

## Stretching Guidelines

### After Initial Pain Settles

**Hip Flexor Stretch**
1. Kneeling position
2. Tuck pelvis, lean forward
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Generally safe and helpful

**Gentle Piriformis Stretch**
1. Lie on back
2. Ankle over opposite knee
3. Pull knee toward chest
4. Don't overstretch
5. Hold 30 seconds

**Avoid or Modify:**
- IT band stretches (can compress)
- Figure-4 stretch (if painful)
- Aggressive hip stretching

## Activity Modifications

### Running
- May need temporary break
- Address running mechanics (hip drop)
- Gradual return when pain-free
- May need gait retraining

### Walking
- Usually fine, but monitor symptoms
- Avoid excessive distance during flares
- Focus on form (no hip drop)

### Stairs
- Use handrail for support
- Step up with uninvolved leg first
- Strengthen before challenging with stairs

### Sleep
- Don't lie on affected side
- Pillow between knees
- May need to train new sleep position

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 6)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Wall isometric abduction: 5×45 sec
3. Glute bridge: 3×15
4. Standing hip abduction: 3×15 each
5. Lateral band walks: 2×20 each direction

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Step-ups: 3×12 each
3. Lateral step-downs: 3×12 each
4. Single-leg mini squat: 2×10 each
5. Hip flexor stretch: 3×30 sec each

### Day 3: Rest or Light Walking

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Side-lying abduction: 3×15
3. Clamshells with band: 3×15
4. Monster walks: 2×20
5. Single-leg balance: 3×30 sec each

### Day 5
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Single-leg bridge: 3×10 each
3. Single-leg RDL: 2×10 each
4. Band squats: 3×12
5. Stretching: 5 min

## When to Seek Help

**Consult a healthcare provider if:**
- No improvement after 6-8 weeks
- Pain getting worse
- Significant night pain
- Unable to perform daily activities
- Considering injection therapy

**Additional treatments:**
- Corticosteroid injection (temporary relief)
- Physical therapy (supervised progression)
- Shockwave therapy
- PRP injection (in some cases)
- Surgery (rarely needed)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Isometrics are your best friend** — pain relief + strengthening
2. **Avoid stretching the IT band** — compresses the tendons
3. **Sleep position matters** — pillow between knees, avoid affected side
4. **Strengthen the hip abductors** — address the root cause
5. **Correct posture habits** — no hip hanging
6. **Progress gradually** — build tendon tolerance slowly
7. **Running needs care** — address form, return gradually
8. **Most cases resolve** — with consistent rehabilitation

Hip bursitis (GTPS) responds well to progressive strengthening, especially targeting the gluteal muscles. Be patient, focus on isometrics early, and build strength systematically for lasting relief.
