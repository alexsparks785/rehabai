# SLAP Tear Exercises: Manage Labral Injury Conservative or Post-Surgery

A SLAP tear (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior) affects the top of the shoulder's labrum where the biceps tendon attaches. Whether managing conservatively or recovering from surgery, targeted exercises are essential for return to function.

## Understanding SLAP Tears

The labrum is a ring of cartilage that deepens the shoulder socket and provides stability. A SLAP tear occurs at the top (superior) of this ring, affecting the biceps tendon anchor.

**SLAP Tear Types:**
- **Type I:** Fraying, no detachment
- **Type II:** Labrum and biceps anchor detached (most common surgical type)
- **Type III:** Bucket handle tear, biceps intact
- **Type IV:** Bucket handle extending into biceps tendon

**Common causes:**
- Repetitive overhead motion (throwing, swimming)
- Fall on outstretched arm
- Sudden traction on arm
- Lifting heavy objects
- Age-related degeneration

## Symptoms

- Deep shoulder pain (often hard to localize)
- Pain with overhead activities
- Catching, locking, or popping
- Pain with biceps activities
- Decreased throwing velocity (athletes)
- Night pain
- Feeling of instability

## Conservative vs. Surgical Treatment

**Conservative management often works for:**
- Type I tears
- Older adults with degenerative tears
- Lower-demand individuals
- Those who respond to rehabilitation

**Surgery more often needed for:**
- Type II tears in athletes
- Failed conservative treatment
- High-demand overhead athletes
- Significant mechanical symptoms

## Conservative Management Phase 1: Reduce Inflammation

### Rest and Activity Modification
- Avoid aggravating overhead activities
- Limit heavy lifting and biceps loading
- Modify work and sports as needed

### Pain Management
- Ice after activity
- NSAIDs as directed by physician
- Avoid painful positions

### Gentle Range of Motion

**Pendulum Exercises**
1. Lean forward, arm hanging
2. Make small circles
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Several times daily

**Passive Flexion**
1. Use other arm to lift affected arm
2. Raise to tolerance
3. 10-15 repetitions

**Passive External Rotation**
1. Use stick or other arm
2. Gently rotate outward
3. Stay within pain-free range
4. 10-15 repetitions

## Conservative Phase 2: Restore Mobility (Weeks 2-6)

### Active-Assisted Range of Motion

**Pulley Exercises**
1. Over-door pulley system
2. Use good arm to assist affected arm up
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Wand Exercises**
1. Hold stick with both hands
2. Use good arm to move affected arm
3. Flexion, extension, rotation
4. 15 reps each direction

### Stretching (Gentle)

**Posterior Capsule Stretch**
1. Cross-body stretch (may need to limit)
2. Hold arm across chest
3. 30 seconds, 3 repetitions

**Pec Stretch**
1. Doorway stretch
2. Hold 30 seconds
3. Repeat 3 times

### Avoid Aggressive Stretching

**Be careful with:**
- Sleeper stretch (can stress labrum)
- Behind-back stretches
- End-range positions

## Conservative Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 4-12)

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. 3 sets of 15

**Standing External Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate outward against band
3. 3 sets of 15

**Standing Internal Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate inward against band
3. 3 sets of 15

### Scapular Strengthening

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Face down on bench
2. Y: Arms overhead, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze and lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Rows**
1. Band or cable at chest height
2. Pull elbows back
3. Squeeze shoulder blades
4. 3 sets of 15

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. 3 sets of 15

**Serratus Punches**
1. Arm straight up
2. Push toward ceiling
3. Shoulder blade protracts
4. 3 sets of 15

### Biceps Caution

**Early:** Avoid biceps-specific exercises
**Later:** Gradual biceps strengthening when cleared
**Always:** Control eccentric loading

## Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

### Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0-4)

**Sling use:**
- Typically 2-4 weeks
- Remove for exercises

**Allowed exercises:**
- Elbow, wrist, hand motion
- Gentle pendulums (may wait until week 2-3)
- Scapular squeezes
- Grip exercises

**Restrictions:**
- No active biceps
- No lifting
- No reaching behind back
- Limited external rotation

### Phase 2: Early Motion (Weeks 4-8)

**Progressive ROM:**
- Passive then active-assisted flexion
- External rotation as cleared (often limited to 30-45°)
- Extension and internal rotation gradually

**Exercises:**
- Pendulums (larger)
- Pulley flexion
- Wand exercises
- Begin isometric rotator cuff

### Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 8-16)

**Progress to:**
- Active ROM full
- Isotonic rotator cuff strengthening
- Scapular exercises
- Begin light biceps (week 12+)

**Exercises:**
- Side-lying ER
- Band ER/IR
- Prone Y-T-W
- Rows, face pulls
- Push-up progression

### Phase 4: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 16-24)

**Add:**
- Full shoulder strengthening
- Sport-specific preparation
- Plyometrics (for athletes)
- Progressive biceps loading

### Phase 5: Return to Sport (Months 6-12)

**For throwers:**
- Interval throwing program
- Very gradual progression
- May take 9-12 months

## Key Exercises Explained

### Rhythmic Stabilization

**How to do it:**
1. Arm supported at 90° flexion
2. Partner provides gentle, alternating pushes
3. Resist without large movements
4. 30-60 second sets

**Why it helps:** Builds dynamic stability and neuromuscular control.

### External Rotation at 90/90

**How to do it:**
1. Arm abducted 90°, elbow bent 90°
2. Rotate forearm up toward ceiling
3. Control the descent
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**When:** Later phases—mimics throwing position.

### Biceps Loading Progression

**Phase 1:** Isometric biceps (no movement)
**Phase 2:** Light concentric curls
**Phase 3:** Eccentric focus (slow lowering)
**Phase 4:** Functional loading

### Thrower's Ten Program

A standardized program for overhead athletes including:
1. External rotation variations
2. Internal rotation variations
3. Shoulder flexion/abduction
4. Scapular exercises
5. Trunk and core work

**Ask your therapist** for the full protocol.

## Activity Modifications

### Overhead Athletes
- May never return to pre-injury level
- Throwing athletes: 6-12 month return
- Modify technique if needed
- Consider position changes

### Weightlifters
- Modify exercises that stress labrum
- Avoid extreme positions
- Progress slowly

### Daily Activities
- Avoid reaching high shelves
- Don't carry heavy bags on affected side
- Use step stool instead of reaching

## Sample Weekly Program (Conservative, Week 8)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, 2 min
2. Pulley flexion: 20 reps
3. Side-lying ER: 3×15
4. Standing IR band: 3×15
5. Prone T raises: 2×12
6. Scapular clocks: 2×10 each

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Rows: 3×15
3. Serratus punches: 3×15
4. Face pulls: 3×15
5. Wall push-ups: 2×15
6. Posterior capsule stretch: 3×30 sec

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, circles
2. Standing ER band: 3×15
3. Prone Y raises: 2×12
4. Push-up plus: 2×12
5. Ball on wall stability: 3×30 sec

### Day 5
1. Full warm-up
2. All strengthening exercises
3. Progress intensity if tolerated
4. Stretching: 5 min

## Monitoring Progress

### Good Signs
- Decreasing pain
- Improving strength
- Better overhead tolerance
- Return to activities

### Warning Signs
- Persistent clicking/catching
- No improvement in 6-8 weeks
- Pain getting worse
- Mechanical symptoms

## When to Consider Surgery

**If conservative treatment fails:**
- After 3-6 months of rehabilitation
- Continued mechanical symptoms
- Unable to return to desired activities
- High-demand overhead athlete with type II tear

## Key Takeaways

1. **Many SLAP tears respond to conservative care** — give rehab a chance
2. **Protect the biceps anchor** — avoid heavy biceps loading early
3. **Strengthen rotator cuff and scapula** — compensates for labral deficiency
4. **Progress gradually** — especially overhead activities
5. **Post-surgical rehab is lengthy** — 6-12 months for full recovery
6. **Overhead athletes need specific progression** — throwing programs
7. **Monitor for mechanical symptoms** — clicking/catching may need surgery
8. **Be patient** — labral healing takes time

SLAP tears can be challenging, but many people return to full function with proper rehabilitation. Whether managing conservatively or recovering from surgery, consistent exercise and patience are key.
