      <h1>How to Fix Carpal Tunnel Syndrome: Exercises for Wrist and Hand Relief</h1>

      <p>That numbness, tingling, and pain in your hand that wakes you up at night? If it's in your thumb, index, middle, and half your ring finger, you likely have carpal tunnel syndrome. While severe cases may need surgery, many people find significant relief through exercises and lifestyle changes.</p>

      <h2>What Is Carpal Tunnel Syndrome?</h2>

      <p>The carpal tunnel is a narrow passageway in your wrist formed by bones and ligaments. The median nerve passes through this tunnel along with tendons. When the tunnel narrows or contents swell, the nerve gets compressed, causing symptoms.</p>

      <p><strong>Classic symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Numbness and tingling in thumb, index, middle, and half of ring finger</li>
        <li>Pain in wrist, hand, or forearm</li>
        <li>Symptoms worse at night (often wake you up)</li>
        <li>Weakness in grip and tendency to drop things</li>
        <li>Shaking out hands provides temporary relief</li>
        <li>Symptoms worse with driving, phone use, or typing</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What Causes Carpal Tunnel?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Repetitive hand motions:</strong> Typing, assembly work, using tools</li>
        <li><strong>Wrist position:</strong> Prolonged flexion or extension</li>
        <li><strong>Pregnancy:</strong> Fluid retention increases pressure</li>
        <li><strong>Medical conditions:</strong> Diabetes, thyroid disorders, rheumatoid arthritis</li>
        <li><strong>Anatomy:</strong> Some people have smaller carpal tunnels</li>
        <li><strong>Obesity:</strong> Increases risk</li>
        <li><strong>Wrist injury:</strong> Previous fracture or dislocation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Important: Get a Diagnosis</h2>

      <p>Other conditions mimic carpal tunnel (cervical radiculopathy, thoracic outlet syndrome). See a healthcare provider for proper diagnosis before assuming your symptoms are carpal tunnel. Nerve conduction studies can confirm the diagnosis.</p>

      <h2>Can Exercises Fix Carpal Tunnel?</h2>

      <p>For mild to moderate carpal tunnel, exercises combined with lifestyle modifications can significantly reduce symptoms and may prevent progression. Severe cases with constant numbness or muscle wasting typically need surgical release.</p>

      <h2>Nerve Gliding Exercises</h2>

      <p>These exercises help the median nerve move freely through the carpal tunnel:</p>

      <h4>1. Median Nerve Glide - Basic</h4>
      <ol>
        <li>Start with fist closed, wrist neutral</li>
        <li>Extend fingers straight, keeping wrist neutral</li>
        <li>Extend wrist back (fingers pointing up)</li>
        <li>Extend thumb away from palm</li>
        <li>Gently turn forearm palm-up</li>
        <li>Use other hand to gently extend thumb further</li>
      </ol>
      <p>Move through positions slowly, holding each 3-5 seconds.</p>
      <p><strong>5-10 repetitions, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Median Nerve Glide - Simplified</h4>
      <p>Make a fist, then open fingers straight while extending wrist back. Return to fist. This simpler version provides nerve gliding without as many steps.</p>
      <p><strong>10-15 repetitions, several times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Tendon Gliding</h4>
      <p>Move through these hand positions in sequence:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Straight fingers (table top)</li>
        <li>Hook fist (fingers bent at middle joints only)</li>
        <li>Full fist</li>
        <li>Straight fist (fingers bent at knuckles, straight otherwise)</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>5-10 cycles, 3-4 times daily</strong></p>

      <h2>Stretching Exercises</h2>

      <h4>4. Wrist Flexor Stretch</h4>
      <p>Extend arm in front, palm up. Use other hand to gently pull fingers back toward you. Feel stretch in forearm and wrist.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 3-4 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Wrist Extensor Stretch</h4>
      <p>Extend arm, palm down. Use other hand to gently push hand down. Feel stretch on top of forearm.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 3-4 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Prayer Stretch</h4>
      <p>Press palms together in front of chest, fingers up. Slowly lower hands while keeping palms pressed together until you feel a stretch in wrists.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds</strong></p>

      <h2>Strengthening Exercises</h2>

      <h4>7. Grip Strengthening</h4>
      <p>Squeeze a soft ball or therapy putty. Hold 5 seconds, release. Don't overdo it—keep intensity low to moderate.</p>
      <p><strong>10-15 squeezes, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Finger Spread</h4>
      <p>Place a rubber band around all five fingers. Spread fingers apart against the resistance. This strengthens the muscles that open the hand.</p>
      <p><strong>15-20 repetitions, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Wrist Curls (Light)</h4>
      <p>Rest forearm on thigh, palm up. Hold light weight (1-2 lbs). Curl wrist up, lower slowly. Keep movements controlled.</p>
      <p><strong>2 sets of 15, with light weight</strong></p>

      <h2>Wrist Splinting</h2>

      <p>Wearing a wrist splint at night keeps your wrist in neutral position and prevents the flexion that compresses the nerve during sleep. This is one of the most effective conservative treatments.</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Wear a neutral wrist splint during sleep</li>
        <li>Consider wearing during aggravating activities</li>
        <li>Don't wear 24/7—you need some wrist movement</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Workplace and Lifestyle Modifications</h2>

      <h4>Computer Setup</h4>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Keyboard position:</strong> Keep wrists neutral, not bent up or down</li>
        <li><strong>Mouse:</strong> Consider ergonomic or vertical mouse</li>
        <li><strong>Wrist rests:</strong> For resting, not while actively typing</li>
        <li><strong>Take breaks:</strong> Every 30-60 minutes, rest hands and do exercises</li>
      </ul>

      <h4>General Tips</h4>
      <ul>
        <li>Avoid prolonged gripping or repetitive motions</li>
        <li>Keep wrists neutral during activities</li>
        <li>Reduce force—don't grip harder than necessary</li>
        <li>Keep hands warm (cold increases symptoms)</li>
        <li>Sleep with arms straight, not curled</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Daily (5-10 minutes)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Nerve gliding: 10 reps, 2-3 times</li>
        <li>Tendon gliding: 10 cycles, 2-3 times</li>
        <li>Wrist stretches: 15-30 seconds each, 2-3 times</li>
        <li>Wear night splint during sleep</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Throughout the Day</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Take regular breaks from repetitive tasks</li>
        <li>Do quick nerve glides during breaks</li>
        <li>Shake out hands when symptoms appear</li>
        <li>Maintain neutral wrist position</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Strengthening (3x/week)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Grip strengthening: 2 x 10-15</li>
        <li>Finger spreads: 2 x 15-20</li>
        <li>Light wrist curls: 2 x 15</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long Until Improvement?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>1-2 weeks:</strong> Some symptom relief with splinting and exercises</li>
        <li><strong>4-6 weeks:</strong> Noticeable improvement for mild cases</li>
        <li><strong>3-6 months:</strong> Maximum benefit from conservative treatment</li>
      </ul>

      <p>If symptoms don't improve in 2-3 months of consistent treatment, surgery may be needed.</p>

      <h2>When to See a Doctor</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Constant numbness (not just intermittent)</li>
        <li>Weakness or muscle wasting at base of thumb</li>
        <li>Dropping objects frequently</li>
        <li>Symptoms not improving with conservative care</li>
        <li>Symptoms after trauma</li>
        <li>Need for diagnosis confirmation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What About Surgery?</h2>

      <p>Carpal tunnel release surgery is highly effective for moderate to severe cases. It involves cutting the ligament that forms the roof of the carpal tunnel, giving the nerve more room. Recovery is typically 2-6 weeks. Surgery is usually recommended when:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Conservative treatment fails after 3-6 months</li>
        <li>Symptoms are severe</li>
        <li>Muscle wasting is present</li>
        <li>Nerve conduction studies show significant compression</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Carpal tunnel syndrome is highly treatable. For mild to moderate cases, consistent nerve gliding exercises, night splinting, and ergonomic modifications can provide significant relief. The key is catching it early and being consistent with conservative treatment before the nerve sustains permanent damage.</p>
