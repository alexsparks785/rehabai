      <h1>How to Fix Achilles Tendinitis: Exercises for Heel Pain Relief</h1>

      <p>That painful, stiff Achilles that hurts with every step—especially first thing in the morning? Achilles tendinitis is one of the most common and frustrating running injuries. But with the right approach, especially eccentric exercises, most cases heal without surgery or injections.</p>

      <h2>What Is Achilles Tendinitis?</h2>

      <p>The Achilles tendon connects your calf muscles to your heel bone. Achilles tendinopathy (the more accurate term) involves degeneration and micro-tears in the tendon from overuse. It typically occurs in two locations:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Mid-portion:</strong> 2-6 cm above the heel (most common)</li>
        <li><strong>Insertional:</strong> Where the tendon attaches to the heel bone</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain at the back of the heel or lower calf</li>
        <li>Stiffness and pain in the morning (first steps)</li>
        <li>Pain that warms up with activity but returns after</li>
        <li>Thickening or nodule in the tendon</li>
        <li>Pain with walking, running, or climbing stairs</li>
        <li>Tenderness when squeezing the tendon</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What Causes Achilles Tendinitis?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Overuse:</strong> Too much running, jumping, or walking too soon</li>
        <li><strong>Rapid training increase:</strong> The classic "too much, too fast"</li>
        <li><strong>Tight calves:</strong> Increased load on the tendon</li>
        <li><strong>Weak calves:</strong> Tendon compensates for muscle weakness</li>
        <li><strong>Poor footwear:</strong> Worn shoes, sudden change in heel height</li>
        <li><strong>Hill running:</strong> Especially uphills</li>
        <li><strong>Age:</strong> Tendons become less resilient over 30</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Why Rest Alone Fails</h2>

      <p>Complete rest makes tendons weaker. Achilles tendinopathy is a degenerative condition—the tendon needs progressive loading to stimulate healing and rebuilding. Rest may reduce pain temporarily, but symptoms return when you resume activity because the tendon never got stronger.</p>

      <h2>The Solution: Eccentric Loading</h2>

      <p>The Alfredson protocol (eccentric heel drops) is the gold standard treatment for Achilles tendinopathy, supported by decades of research. Eccentric exercise—where the muscle lengthens under load—stimulates tendon healing.</p>

      <h2>Key Exercises</h2>

      <h3>The Alfredson Protocol</h3>

      <h4>1. Eccentric Heel Drop (Straight Knee)</h4>
      <p>This is your primary exercise:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Stand on a step with the balls of your feet on the edge, heels hanging off</li>
        <li>Rise up onto your toes using BOTH legs</li>
        <li>Shift weight to the AFFECTED leg only</li>
        <li>Slowly lower your heel below the step over 3-5 seconds</li>
        <li>Use both legs to rise back up, then repeat</li>
        <li>Keep knee straight to target gastrocnemius</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, twice daily. Some pain (up to 5/10) is expected and acceptable.</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Eccentric Heel Drop (Bent Knee)</h4>
      <p>Same exercise but with knee slightly bent. This shifts load to the soleus muscle and targets the tendon differently.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, twice daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Adding Weight</h4>
      <p>Once you can do the exercises with minimal pain, add weight (backpack with books, weighted vest). Progressive overload continues tendon adaptation.</p>
      <p><strong>Progress when 3x15 is easy—add 5-10 lbs at a time</strong></p>

      <h3>For Insertional Achilles Tendinitis</h3>

      <p>If your pain is at the heel bone attachment, modify the exercises:</p>

      <h4>4. Flat Ground Heel Raises</h4>
      <p>Don't let your heel drop below neutral (below floor level). This reduces compression at the insertion. Do heel raises on flat ground instead of off a step.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, twice daily</strong></p>

      <h3>Supporting Exercises</h3>

      <h4>5. Isometric Calf Raise Hold</h4>
      <p>Rise onto your toes and hold. Isometrics can reduce pain and begin loading the tendon early in rehab.</p>
      <p><strong>5 holds of 30-45 seconds</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Seated Calf Raise</h4>
      <p>Sit with weight on your knees, rise onto toes. This targets the soleus with less Achilles stretch.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Single-Leg Calf Raise (Later Stage)</h4>
      <p>Once eccentric drops are easy, progress to full single-leg calf raises—both up AND down on one leg.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15 each leg</strong></p>

      <h3>Stretching (Gentle)</h3>

      <h4>8. Wall Calf Stretch</h4>
      <p>Gentle stretching only—don't aggressively stretch an irritated tendon. Face a wall, back leg straight, heel down. Lean in gently.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30 seconds each side, don't push into pain</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Soleus Stretch</h4>
      <p>Same position but bend the back knee. Feel stretch lower in the calf.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h3>Foam Rolling</h3>

      <h4>10. Calf Foam Roll</h4>
      <p>Roll your calves—NOT directly on the Achilles tendon. Reducing calf tightness decreases tendon load.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each calf</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Phase 1: Load Introduction (Weeks 1-2)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Isometric holds: 5 x 30-45 seconds, daily</li>
        <li>Gentle calf stretches: 30 seconds each, 2x daily</li>
        <li>Reduce aggravating activities</li>
        <li>Avoid walking barefoot or in flat shoes</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 2: Eccentric Loading (Weeks 2-12)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Eccentric heel drops (straight knee): 3 x 15, twice daily</li>
        <li>Eccentric heel drops (bent knee): 3 x 15, twice daily</li>
        <li>Continue stretching and foam rolling</li>
        <li>Some pain during exercise is normal (up to 5/10)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 3: Strength Building (Weeks 8-16)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Add weight to eccentric exercises</li>
        <li>Progress to full single-leg calf raises</li>
        <li>Begin gradual return to running</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 4: Return to Activity (Weeks 12+)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Gradual return to running (walk-run protocol)</li>
        <li>Continue maintenance calf strengthening</li>
        <li>Progress distance and speed slowly</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Managing Pain During Rehab</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>0-3/10 pain:</strong> Good, continue</li>
        <li><strong>4-5/10 pain:</strong> Acceptable during exercise</li>
        <li><strong>6+/10 pain:</strong> Too much—reduce load</li>
        <li><strong>Pain worse next morning:</strong> Did too much—back off</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Activity Modification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Heel lifts:</strong> Temporary heel inserts reduce tendon stretch</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid flat shoes:</strong> Some heel elevation helps during healing</li>
        <li><strong>Reduce running:</strong> Cross-train with cycling or swimming</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid hills:</strong> Especially uphill running</li>
        <li><strong>Morning routine:</strong> Do gentle calf raises before getting out of bed</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to Heal?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>4-6 weeks:</strong> Noticeable improvement</li>
        <li><strong>3 months:</strong> Significant healing for most</li>
        <li><strong>6-12 months:</strong> Full recovery for stubborn cases</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Tendon healing is slow—be patient and consistent.</p>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Sudden pop or snap in the Achilles (possible rupture—urgent)</li>
        <li>Severe pain or inability to walk</li>
        <li>No improvement after 8-12 weeks</li>
        <li>Significant swelling</li>
        <li>Pain at rest that doesn't improve</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Preventing Recurrence</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Continue calf strengthening long-term</li>
        <li>Progress training loads gradually (10% rule)</li>
        <li>Replace worn shoes regularly</li>
        <li>Warm up before running</li>
        <li>Address calf tightness consistently</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Achilles tendinitis is stubborn but responds well to eccentric loading. The Alfredson protocol has helped countless runners and athletes recover. Stay consistent with your heel drops, accept that some discomfort is part of healing, and give the tendon time to rebuild stronger than before.</p>
