# How to Fix a Sedentary Body: Exercises to Undo Sitting Damage

Years of sitting take a toll—tight hips, weak glutes, rounded shoulders, stiff spine, and a body that feels older than it should. But here's the good news: your body is remarkably adaptable. With consistent work, you can reverse much of the damage and feel dramatically better. Here's your roadmap.

## What Sitting Does to Your Body

### The Sedentary Pattern

**Front of body (gets tight):**
- Hip flexors
- Chest muscles
- Front shoulders
- Neck flexors (from forward head)

**Back of body (gets weak):**
- Glutes (essentially turn off)
- Hamstrings (weak despite feeling "tight")
- Mid-back muscles
- Deep core stabilizers

**Joints (lose mobility):**
- Hips (especially extension and rotation)
- Thoracic spine (upper back)
- Ankles
- Shoulders (overhead reach)

### Common Symptoms
- Lower back pain or stiffness
- Tight hips that don't want to open
- Rounded shoulders and forward head
- Weak core that can't stabilize
- Poor balance
- Feeling "old" or creaky
- Fatigue and low energy
- Difficulty with basic movements (squatting, reaching overhead)

## The Recovery Framework

Think of fixing a sedentary body in three phases:

1. **Restore Mobility** — Unlock joints and lengthen tight muscles
2. **Activate and Strengthen** — Wake up dormant muscles and build strength
3. **Integrate and Maintain** — Put it together into functional movement

You don't finish one phase before starting the next—you work on all three, but the emphasis shifts over time.

## Phase 1: Restore Mobility

Focus here first. You can't strengthen through a range you don't have.

### Hip Mobility

#### 1. 90-90 Hip Stretch
**Why:** Opens hips in multiple directions.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with both knees bent at 90 degrees
2. Front shin perpendicular to your body
3. Back shin behind you, also at 90 degrees
4. Sit tall and feel the stretch in your front hip
5. Hold 60-90 seconds, then switch sides

#### 2. Hip Flexor Stretch (Couch Stretch)
**Why:** Deeply stretches hip flexors shortened by sitting.

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel with one knee in the corner where wall meets floor
2. Other foot forward in a lunge
3. Squeeze your back glute and tuck your tailbone
4. Stay upright (don't lean forward)
5. Hold 90-120 seconds per side

#### 3. Deep Squat Hold
**Why:** Restores the fundamental human resting position.

**How to do it:**
1. Squat as deep as you can with heels down
2. Hold onto something for balance if needed
3. Work up to 2-3 minutes total per day
4. Break into smaller holds if needed

### Thoracic (Upper Back) Mobility

#### 4. Foam Roller Extension
**How to do it:**
1. Lie with foam roller across upper back
2. Support head with hands
3. Extend backward over the roller
4. Move to different segments
5. Spend 2-3 minutes total

#### 5. Cat-Cow
**How to do it:**
1. On all fours, alternate arching and rounding
2. Focus on moving the upper back, not just the lower back
3. Do 15-20 reps daily

#### 6. Thread the Needle
**How to do it:**
1. On all fours, reach one arm under your body
2. Let shoulder come to floor, feel the rotation
3. Return and reach toward ceiling
4. 8-10 reps per side

### Shoulder Mobility

#### 7. Doorway Chest Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Forearm on door frame, elbow at shoulder height
2. Step through and lean forward
3. Hold 45-60 seconds
4. Vary arm height to target different fibers

#### 8. Wall Slides
**How to do it:**
1. Back against wall
2. Arms in goal post position
3. Slide up and down, keeping everything on wall
4. 10-15 reps

### Ankle Mobility

#### 9. Ankle Rocks
**How to do it:**
1. Kneel with one foot forward
2. Drive knee forward over toes, keeping heel down
3. Rock back and forth
4. 15-20 reps per side

## Phase 2: Activate and Strengthen

Now wake up the muscles that have been sleeping.

### Glute Activation

#### 10. Glute Bridges
**Why:** The most important exercise for reversing sitting damage.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Push through heels to lift hips
3. Squeeze glutes hard at top
4. Hold 3 seconds, lower slowly
5. Do 15-20 reps

**Progression:** Single-leg bridges, weighted bridges

#### 11. Clamshells
**Why:** Targets gluteus medius, often severely weakened.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, knees bent
2. Lift top knee while keeping feet together
3. Don't let hips roll back
4. 20 reps per side

**Progression:** Add resistance band

#### 12. Bird Dog
**Why:** Glute activation with core stability.

**How to do it:**
1. On all fours
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep hips level, don't arch back
4. Hold 3 seconds, switch sides
5. 10 per side

### Core Activation

#### 13. Dead Bug
**Why:** Teaches core stability in a safe position.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees at 90 degrees
2. Press lower back into floor
3. Lower opposite arm and leg slowly
4. Return without letting back arch
5. 10 per side

#### 14. Plank
**Why:** Builds endurance in the full anterior core.

**How to do it:**
1. Forearm plank position
2. Body in straight line
3. Don't let hips sag or pike up
4. Build to 60 seconds

### Upper Back Strengthening

#### 15. Prone Y-T-W
**Why:** Strengthens the mid-back muscles that get weak from sitting.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down
2. Y: Arms overhead at 45 degrees, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades, lift
5. Hold each 3 seconds, 10 reps each

#### 16. Band Pull-Aparts
**How to do it:**
1. Hold band at shoulder height
2. Pull apart by squeezing shoulder blades
3. Control the return
4. 15-20 reps

### Building Basic Strength

#### 17. Goblet Squat
**Why:** Teaches proper squat mechanics with counterbalance.

**How to do it:**
1. Hold weight at chest
2. Squat down, keeping chest up
3. Knees tracking over toes
4. Drive through heels to stand
5. 10-15 reps

#### 18. Romanian Deadlift
**Why:** Strengthens the posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, back).

**How to do it:**
1. Hold weights in front of thighs
2. Hinge at hips, pushing them back
3. Keep slight knee bend, back flat
4. Feel stretch in hamstrings
5. Drive hips forward to stand
6. 10-12 reps

#### 19. Push-Up (Modified if Needed)
**Why:** Upper body pushing strength.

**How to do it:**
1. Hands shoulder-width apart
2. Lower chest toward floor
3. Push back up
4. Start on knees if needed
5. 8-15 reps

#### 20. Row
**Why:** Upper body pulling to balance the pushing.

**How to do it:**
1. Use dumbbells, cables, or bands
2. Pull toward your body, squeezing shoulder blades
3. Control the return
4. 10-15 reps per arm

## Phase 3: Integrate and Maintain

Combine everything into movement patterns and make it sustainable.

### Movement Patterns to Master
- **Squat:** Goblet squat, eventually back or front squat
- **Hinge:** Romanian deadlift, eventually conventional deadlift
- **Push:** Push-up, overhead press
- **Pull:** Rows, pull-ups or lat pulldowns
- **Carry:** Farmer's walks, suitcase carries
- **Rotation:** Wood chops, pallof press

### Sample Weekly Schedule

**Day 1: Lower Body Focus**
- Hip mobility work (10 min)
- Goblet squats: 3x12
- Romanian deadlifts: 3x10
- Glute bridges: 3x15
- Dead bugs: 2x10 each side

**Day 2: Upper Body Focus**
- Thoracic mobility (10 min)
- Push-ups: 3x8-12
- Rows: 3x12
- Band pull-aparts: 3x15
- Plank: 3x30-45 sec

**Day 3: Active Recovery**
- Full mobility routine (20 min)
- Walking (20-30 min)

**Day 4: Full Body**
- Brief mobility warm-up
- Squats: 3x10
- Hinges: 3x10
- Push-ups: 2x10
- Rows: 2x12
- Farmer's walks: 3x30 seconds

**Day 5: Mobility and Core**
- Deep mobility work (20 min)
- Core circuit: dead bugs, bird dogs, planks
- Stretching

**Days 6-7: Light Activity**
- Walking, casual movement
- Brief stretching as needed

## Daily Non-Negotiables

Whatever else you do, commit to these daily:

1. **Hip flexor stretch:** 60 seconds per side
2. **Glute bridges:** 15-20 reps
3. **Cat-cow:** 10-15 reps
4. **Chin tucks:** 15-20 reps
5. **Movement breaks:** Every 30-60 minutes during sitting

**Total time:** 10 minutes. Do this every single day.

## Timeline for Change

- **Week 1-2:** Things feel different, some soreness
- **Week 3-4:** Noticeable mobility improvements
- **Week 6-8:** Strength gains begin, movements feel easier
- **Month 3:** Significant changes in how you move and feel
- **Month 6:** Substantial reversal of sedentary damage
- **Year 1:** Transformation complete, maintenance mode

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Starting too hard:** Your body needs time to adapt. Start easier than you think.

**Skipping mobility:** Strength without mobility creates new problems.

**Inconsistency:** Three times a week consistently beats six times a week for two weeks.

**All or nothing thinking:** 10 minutes is better than zero. Don't skip entirely if you can't do a full workout.

**Ignoring daily movement:** Exercise doesn't offset 8+ hours of sitting. Move throughout the day.

## The Bottom Line

A sedentary body isn't broken—it's adapted to what you've asked of it. Ask it to do more, and it will adapt again.

Start with mobility work and glute activation. Add basic strength progressively. Move daily, not just during workouts.

The goal isn't perfection—it's progress. Every squat, every stretch, every walk adds up. In six months, you won't recognize the body that felt stiff and weak today.

Your body wants to move. Give it permission, give it consistency, and watch what happens.
