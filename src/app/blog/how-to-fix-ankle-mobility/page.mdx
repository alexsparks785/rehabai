# How to Fix Ankle Mobility: Exercises for Better Dorsiflexion

Poor ankle mobility is a hidden limiting factor for many people. It affects your squat depth, causes knee pain, and makes your body compensate in ways that lead to injury. If your heels rise during squats or you can't get into a deep squat without falling backward, your ankles are likely the problem.

## Why Ankle Mobility Matters

Ankle dorsiflexion—the ability to bring your toes toward your shin—is crucial for:

- **Deep squatting:** Your knees need to travel forward, which requires ankle bend
- **Walking and running:** Proper push-off and landing mechanics
- **Knee health:** Limited ankles force knees into compensatory positions
- **Balance:** Ankle mobility affects your ability to recover from perturbations
- **Hip and back health:** Restrictions cascade up the chain

## Signs of Poor Ankle Mobility

- Heels rise during squats
- Can't squat deep without falling backward
- Knees cave inward during squats (compensation)
- Excessive forward lean when squatting
- Shin splints or Achilles issues
- Difficulty walking up hills or stairs
- Feel "stuck" in the ankle when trying to stretch it

## Self-Assessment

### Wall Test
1. Face a wall, one foot about 4-5 inches away
2. Keep heel down and try to touch knee to wall
3. If you can touch the wall, move foot back an inch and repeat
4. Note the maximum distance where you can still touch with heel down

**Targets:**
- Minimum: 4 inches (10 cm)
- Good: 5 inches (12-13 cm)
- Excellent: 6+ inches (15+ cm)

### Deep Squat Assessment
1. Squat as deep as possible with heels down
2. Note:
   - Do heels rise?
   - Do you fall backward?
   - Does your back round excessively?
   - Do knees collapse inward?

Any of these suggest ankle restriction.

## What Limits Ankle Mobility?

### Calf Tightness
The gastrocnemius and soleus (calf muscles) cross the ankle and limit dorsiflexion when tight.

### Joint Capsule Restriction
The ankle joint capsule can become stiff from lack of movement or previous injury.

### Bony Block
In some people, bone shape limits range—this is harder to change.

### Previous Injury
Ankle sprains can leave scar tissue and joint restrictions.

### Footwear
Constantly wearing shoes with heels (even small ones) shortens calf muscles over time.

## Ankle Mobility Exercises

### Calf Stretches

#### 1. Wall Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius)
**How to do it:**
1. Face a wall, hands on wall
2. Step one foot back 2-3 feet
3. Keep back knee STRAIGHT, heel down
4. Lean forward until you feel stretch in upper calf
5. Hold 45-60 seconds per side
6. Repeat 2-3 times

#### 2. Bent-Knee Calf Stretch (Soleus)
**How to do it:**
1. Same position as above
2. Bend your BACK knee while keeping heel down
3. Feel stretch lower in the calf, near Achilles
4. Hold 45-60 seconds per side
5. Repeat 2-3 times

**Both stretches are important—the soleus is often more restricted.**

#### 3. Step Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on a step with heels hanging off
2. Let heels drop below the step
3. Can do one leg at a time for more intensity
4. Hold 45-60 seconds

### Joint Mobilizations

#### 4. Knee-to-Wall Ankle Mobilization
**The most important ankle mobility exercise.**

**How to do it:**
1. Face a wall in a half-kneeling position
2. Front foot a few inches from wall
3. Drive your knee forward OVER your toes toward the wall
4. Keep your heel firmly planted
5. Rock in and out of the stretch
6. Do 15-20 reps, then hold for 30 seconds
7. Move foot back an inch and repeat

**Key:** Don't let heel come up. Keep foot straight (not turned out).

#### 5. Banded Ankle Mobilization
**How to do it:**
1. Anchor a resistance band low behind you
2. Loop band around front of ankle (at the joint)
3. Step forward to create tension
4. Perform knee-to-wall mobilization with band pulling ankle back
5. This helps the talus (ankle bone) glide properly
6. Do 15-20 reps per side

**This is excellent for joint capsule restrictions.**

#### 6. Ankle Circles
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with one ankle crossed over knee
2. Rotate ankle in full circles
3. Make circles as large as possible
4. 15-20 circles each direction per foot

#### 7. Alphabet Writing
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with foot elevated
2. Use your big toe to "write" the alphabet in the air
3. This moves the ankle through all ranges
4. Do full alphabet with each foot

### Active Mobilization

#### 8. Rocking Ankle Mobilization
**How to do it:**
1. Get in a half-kneeling position
2. Lean forward, driving knee over toes
3. Rock back to starting position
4. Keep heel down throughout
5. 20-30 rocks per side

#### 9. Elevated Heel Squat Hold
**How to do it:**
1. Place heels on a 1-2 inch elevation (plates, board)
2. Squat as deep as possible
3. Focus on driving knees forward over toes
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

**This builds strength and control in your available range.**

#### 10. Deep Squat Hold (Working on Ankle)
**How to do it:**
1. Squat as deep as possible, heels down
2. Use arms inside knees to push them out
3. Rock side to side, shifting weight
4. Work toward holding 2-3 minutes total

### Self-Massage

#### 11. Calf Foam Rolling
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with calf on foam roller
2. Cross other leg on top for more pressure
3. Roll slowly from ankle to below knee
4. Pause on tender spots for 20-30 seconds
5. Rotate leg in and out to hit different areas
6. 60-90 seconds per leg

#### 12. Lacrosse Ball to Calf
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with calf on a lacrosse ball
2. Move around to find tight spots
3. When you find one, flex and point foot while maintaining pressure
4. Spend 1-2 minutes per leg

#### 13. Sole of Foot Release
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with a tennis/lacrosse ball under your foot
2. Roll ball under foot, applying pressure
3. Spend extra time on tender areas
4. 60-90 seconds per foot

**Tight plantar fascia can limit ankle mobility.**

## Strengthening for Ankle Mobility

Mobility without strength doesn't last.

#### 14. Tibialis Raises (For Front of Ankle)
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Feet about a foot from wall
3. Lift toes while keeping heels down
4. Lower slowly
5. 2-3 sets of 15-20 reps

#### 15. Calf Raises (Full Range)
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on edge of step, heels off
2. Lower heels as far as possible (stretch)
3. Rise all the way up on toes
4. 2-3 sets of 15-20 reps

**This strengthens calves through full range, not just shortened position.**

#### 16. Single-Leg Balance
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on one foot
2. Focus on stability through the ankle
3. Progress to unstable surfaces
4. 30-60 seconds per leg

## Your Daily Ankle Mobility Routine

### Quick Version (5 minutes)
- Knee-to-wall mobilization: 15 reps + 30-second hold per side
- Bent-knee calf stretch: 45 seconds per side
- Ankle circles: 10 each direction per foot

### Full Version (15 minutes)
- Foam roll calves: 60 seconds per leg
- Wall calf stretch (straight leg): 45 seconds per side
- Bent-knee calf stretch: 45 seconds per side
- Banded ankle mobilization: 15 reps per side
- Knee-to-wall mobilization: 20 reps + 30-second hold per side
- Deep squat hold: 2 minutes
- Tibialis raises: 15 reps
- Single-leg balance: 30 seconds per leg

### When to Do It
- **Before squatting:** Quick version as warm-up
- **Daily:** Full version for fastest improvement
- **After sitting:** Quick mobilization to restore range

## Common Mistakes

**Letting heel lift:** The whole point is to keep the heel down. If it lifts, you're not working ankle mobility.

**Turning foot out:** Keep foot straight ahead. Turning out is a compensation.

**Only stretching, not mobilizing:** Static stretches help, but loaded movement through range (like knee-to-wall) is most effective.

**Ignoring the soleus:** The bent-knee stretch targets the soleus, which limits mobility more than the gastrocnemius in many people.

**Rushing:** Tissue change takes time. Consistent daily work beats occasional intense sessions.

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Week 1-2:** Stretches feel less intense, small gains
- **Week 3-4:** Noticeable improvement in wall test
- **Week 6-8:** Significant functional improvement (better squats)
- **3+ months:** Major changes possible with consistency

**Note:** Bony restrictions can't be changed. If you've worked consistently for months with no progress, you may have a structural limit.

## When to Seek Help

**See a professional if:**
- Pain with ankle mobilization (not just tightness)
- History of significant ankle injury
- Clicking, catching, or locking
- No improvement after 8-12 weeks of consistent work
- Asymmetry that doesn't improve

## The Bottom Line

Ankle mobility—specifically dorsiflexion—is a fundamental movement capacity that affects everything from squats to walking. Limited ankles force compensations in your knees, hips, and back.

The knee-to-wall mobilization is your most important exercise. Do it daily. Add calf stretching (both straight and bent knee) and some strengthening work.

Most people can significantly improve ankle mobility within 6-8 weeks of consistent daily work. Your squat depth, knee health, and overall movement quality will thank you.

Free your ankles, and the rest of your body can finally move the way it's supposed to.
