# Shoulder Injury Prevention: Exercises to Protect Your Shoulders

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in your body—and also one of the most vulnerable. From rotator cuff tears to impingement to instability, shoulder injuries are common and can take a long time to heal. Prevention through targeted exercises is far better than treatment after injury.

## Understanding Shoulder Injuries

### Common Shoulder Problems

**Rotator Cuff Tears/Tendinopathy:**
- The four rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) stabilize and move the shoulder
- Overuse, acute injury, or degeneration can damage these tendons
- Can range from tendinopathy to partial to full tears

**Shoulder Impingement:**
- Structures get "pinched" under the acromion during overhead movements
- Causes pain, especially when raising arm
- Often related to poor scapular mechanics or tight structures

**Instability/Labral Tears:**
- The shoulder "ball" can slip or dislocate from the "socket"
- Labrum (cartilage ring) can tear
- Often from trauma or repetitive overhead activities

**SLAP Tears:**
- Tear of the superior labrum (where biceps attaches)
- Common in throwing athletes
- Overhead pain and catching

### Risk Factors

**Modifiable:**
- Rotator cuff weakness
- Scapular instability
- Poor posture (rounded shoulders)
- Muscle imbalances (push > pull)
- Inadequate warm-up
- Training errors (too much, too fast)
- Poor mobility

**Less Modifiable:**
- Age (degeneration increases)
- Previous injury
- Anatomical variations
- Hypermobility

## Keys to Shoulder Prevention

### 1. Rotator Cuff Strength

These muscles must be strong enough to stabilize the humeral head during all movements.

### 2. Scapular Stability

The shoulder blade must move correctly for the shoulder joint to function properly.

### 3. Balanced Strength

Pulling exercises should balance or exceed pushing exercises.

### 4. Thoracic Mobility

A stiff upper back forces the shoulder to compensate.

### 5. Proper Warm-Up

Prepare the shoulder before demanding activities.

## Rotator Cuff Strengthening

### Side-Lying External Rotation

**The foundational rotator cuff exercise:**

1. Lie on your side, elbow bent 90°, arm at your side
2. Hold light weight (2-5 lbs)
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. Keep elbow glued to your side
5. Lower with control

*Reps: 15-20 per arm, 2-3 sets*

### Standing External Rotation (Band)

1. Band attached at elbow height
2. Elbow at side, bent 90°
3. Rotate forearm outward against band
4. Control return

*Reps: 15-20 per arm, 2-3 sets*

### Standing Internal Rotation (Band)

1. Same setup as external rotation
2. Rotate forearm inward (across body) against band
3. Control return

*Reps: 15-20 per arm, 2-3 sets*

### High External Rotation (90/90 Position)

1. Arm out to side at shoulder height
2. Elbow bent 90°
3. Rotate forearm up (like a football throw position)
4. Control return

*Reps: 15, 2-3 sets*

### Prone Y-T-W Raises

**Targets supraspinatus and scapular stabilizers:**

1. Lie face down on bench or floor
2. **Y:** Raise arms at 45° angles, thumbs up
3. **T:** Raise arms straight to sides, thumbs up
4. **W:** Elbows bent, rotate thumbs up/back

*Reps: 10 each position, 2-3 sets*

## Scapular Stability Exercises

### Wall Slides

1. Back against wall
2. Arms in W position (elbows and hands touching wall)
3. Slide arms up toward Y position
4. Maintain wall contact
5. Lower to W

*Reps: 12-15, 3 sets*

### Push-Up Plus

1. Top of push-up position
2. Without bending elbows, protract shoulder blades (push floor away)
3. Let shoulder blades come back together
4. Repeat

*Reps: 15-20, 3 sets*

### Serratus Punch

1. Lie on back, arm toward ceiling
2. Push arm further up by protracting shoulder blade
3. Let shoulder blade retract
4. Repeat

*Reps: 15 per arm, 3 sets*

### Face Pulls

**Essential for scapular health:**

1. Band or cable at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. At end, externally rotate (thumbs back)
4. Squeeze shoulder blades

*Reps: 15-20, 3 sets*

### Band Pull-Aparts

1. Hold band in front at shoulder height
2. Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades
3. Control return

*Reps: 20-25, 3 sets*

### Prone Trap Raise

1. Lie face down, arms hanging off bench
2. Raise arms toward ceiling (like a prone Y)
3. Hold 2-3 seconds at top

*Reps: 12-15, 3 sets*

## Thoracic Mobility

### Thoracic Extension on Foam Roller

1. Roller horizontal under upper back
2. Hands behind head
3. Extend over the roller
4. Move roller to different levels

*Duration: 2-3 minutes total*

### Thread the Needle

1. On all fours
2. Reach one arm under your body
3. Let shoulder rest on floor
4. Feel rotation through upper back

*Hold: 30 seconds per side*

### Open Book

1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Arms stacked in front
3. Open top arm toward ceiling/floor behind you
4. Follow hand with eyes
5. Return

*Reps: 10 per side*

### Cat-Cow

1. On all fours
2. Arch back, look up (cow)
3. Round back, tuck chin (cat)
4. Move through full range

*Reps: 15-20 cycles*

## Shoulder Mobility Exercises

### Shoulder CARs

1. Arm at side, make fist
2. Slowly raise arm forward
3. At top, rotate and continue in full circle
4. Make it as large as possible
5. Reverse direction

*Reps: 3-5 each direction per arm*

### Sleeper Stretch

1. Lie on your side, shoulder stacked
2. Arm in front, elbow bent 90°
3. Use other hand to push forearm toward floor
4. Don't force—gentle stretch

*Hold: 30 seconds per side*

### Cross-Body Stretch

1. Bring arm across body at chest height
2. Use other hand to pull it closer
3. Don't let shoulder hike up

*Hold: 30 seconds per side*

### Doorway Pec Stretch

1. Forearm on doorframe
2. Step through
3. Adjust elbow height:
   - Low (below shoulder): lower pec
   - High (above shoulder): upper pec/pec minor

*Hold: 30 seconds each position per side*

### Lat Stretch

1. Kneel facing bench/chair
2. Elbows on surface
3. Push hips back
4. Let chest drop toward floor

*Hold: 45-60 seconds*

## Warm-Up Protocol for Shoulders

### Before Any Overhead Activity (5-7 minutes)

1. **Arm circles:** 10 forward, 10 backward
2. **Shoulder CARs:** 3 each direction per arm
3. **Band pull-aparts:** 15 reps
4. **Band external rotation:** 15 per arm
5. **Face pulls:** 15 reps
6. **Push-up plus:** 10 reps
7. **Cat-cow:** 10 cycles
8. **Light overhead movement:** Specific to activity

## Training Guidelines

### Balancing Push and Pull

**Rule:** For every pushing exercise, do at least one pulling exercise.

**Better:** Do 2-3 pulls for every push (especially if you sit at a desk).

**Example Week:**
- Monday: Bench press → Row, Face pulls
- Wednesday: Overhead press → Pull-ups, Band pull-aparts
- Friday: Push-ups → Rows, Y-T-W raises

### Volume and Intensity

- Don't increase shoulder training volume by more than 10% per week
- Include adequate rest between shoulder-intensive sessions
- Avoid training shoulders when fatigued
- Stop if you feel pain (not just muscle burn)

### Exercise Selection

**Safer alternatives:**
- Neutral grip pressing (vs. wide grip)
- Dumbbell pressing (vs. barbell—allows natural rotation)
- Cable lateral raises (vs. dumbbells past 90°)
- Full range of motion with control

**Higher risk movements:**
- Behind-the-neck press (minimize or avoid)
- Upright rows (elbows above shoulders)
- Dips with excessive depth
- Bench press with extreme arch and wide grip

## Sport-Specific Prevention

### For Overhead Athletes (Baseball, Tennis, Volleyball)

- Year-round rotator cuff strengthening
- Thoracic mobility work daily
- Eccentric emphasis for decelerators
- Adequate rest between throwing/serving sessions
- Off-season training to address weaknesses

### For Swimmers

- Scapular stability is paramount
- Address muscle imbalances from repetitive motion
- External rotation strengthening
- Posture correction for rounded shoulders
- Adequate recovery between hard sessions

### For Weightlifters

- Warm up thoroughly before pressing
- Balance push/pull ratio
- Include overhead mobility work
- Don't push through pain
- Address weaknesses before they become injuries

### For CrossFitters

- Volume management for high-rep overhead work
- Kipping pull-up preparation (strict strength first)
- Shoulder stability before gymnastics movements
- Mobility work for overhead positions

## Complete Shoulder Prevention Program

### Daily (5 minutes)

1. Shoulder CARs: 3 each direction per arm
2. Band pull-aparts: 15 reps
3. External rotation: 15 per arm
4. Thoracic extension: 10 reps

### 3x Per Week (15 minutes)

**Rotator Cuff:**
1. Side-lying external rotation: 3x15
2. Band internal rotation: 3x15

**Scapular Stability:**
1. Face pulls: 3x15
2. Y-T-W raises: 3x10 each
3. Push-up plus: 3x15

**Mobility:**
1. Sleeper stretch: 30 seconds per side
2. Doorway pec stretch: 30 seconds per position
3. Thread the needle: 30 seconds per side

### Before Upper Body Training

1. Arm circles: 10 each direction
2. Band pull-aparts: 15
3. External rotation: 15 per arm
4. Cat-cow: 10 cycles
5. Light pressing movements to warm up

## Warning Signs

Seek evaluation if you experience:

- Pain with overhead movements that persists
- Night pain (waking you up)
- Weakness or inability to raise arm
- Popping or clicking with pain
- Sudden sharp pain during activity
- Shoulder instability (feeling it will "slip out")

## Key Takeaways

1. **Rotator cuff strength is foundational**—daily or every-other-day external rotation work
2. **Scapular stability matters**—the shoulder blade must move correctly for the shoulder to work
3. **Balance push and pull**—most people push too much; prioritize pulling exercises
4. **Thoracic mobility affects the shoulder**—a stiff upper back stresses the shoulder joint
5. **Warm up before demanding activities**—cold shoulders are vulnerable shoulders
6. **Progress gradually**—sudden increases in training volume cause many injuries
7. **Don't train through pain**—shoulder problems worsen when ignored

Your shoulders allow incredible movement and function—but that mobility comes with vulnerability. A consistent prevention program keeps your shoulders healthy for decades of pain-free movement.
