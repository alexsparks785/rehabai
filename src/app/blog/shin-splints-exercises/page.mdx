Shin splints are one of the most common overuse injuries, affecting runners, dancers, military recruits, and anyone who rapidly increases their training load. The good news: they're highly treatable and preventable with the right approach.

This guide covers what shin splints actually are, how to treat them, and—most importantly—how to prevent them from coming back.

## What Are Shin Splints?

"Shin splints" is a general term for pain along the shinbone (tibia). The medical term is medial tibial stress syndrome (MTSS).

**What's happening:**
The muscles, tendons, and bone tissue around the tibia become irritated and inflamed from repetitive stress. The bone itself may develop microdamage that hasn't progressed to a stress fracture.

**Common locations:**
- **Medial (inside) shin:** Most common, along the inner edge of the tibia
- **Anterior (front) shin:** Front of the lower leg, involving the tibialis anterior muscle

## Symptoms

**Typical shin splint symptoms:**
- Dull, aching pain along the shin
- Pain that starts during exercise and may ease with continued activity
- Tenderness when pressing along the shin
- Mild swelling in some cases
- Pain that initially goes away with rest but returns with activity

**Warning signs of something more serious (stress fracture):**
- Pinpoint pain in one specific spot
- Pain that worsens throughout activity and doesn't ease
- Pain at rest or at night
- Pain with everyday walking

If you suspect a stress fracture, see a healthcare provider for proper diagnosis.

## Causes and Risk Factors

### Training Errors
- **Too much, too soon:** Rapid increases in volume or intensity
- **Hard surfaces:** Running primarily on concrete
- **Worn-out shoes:** Inadequate cushioning and support
- **Sudden changes:** New terrain, hills, or speed work

### Biomechanical Factors
- **Overpronation:** Excessive inward foot roll
- **Flat feet or high arches**
- **Tight calf muscles**
- **Weak hip and core muscles**
- **Poor running mechanics**

### Other Factors
- Previous shin splint history
- Female sex (higher risk)
- Low bone density
- Being new to running or impact activity

## Treatment Approach

### Phase 1: Acute Phase (Weeks 1-2)

**Reduce load:**
- Decrease or stop the aggravating activity
- Don't push through significant pain
- Consider low-impact cross-training (swimming, cycling)

**Manage symptoms:**
- Ice: 15-20 minutes several times daily
- Compression: Compression sleeves may help
- Elevation: When resting
- NSAIDs: Short-term use if needed (consult healthcare provider)

**Gentle movement:**
- Continue walking if it doesn't cause pain
- Begin gentle stretching and mobility work

### Phase 2: Rehabilitation (Weeks 2-6)

**Gradual loading:**
- Begin rehab exercises (see below)
- Slowly reintroduce activity as symptoms allow
- Follow the 10% rule for increasing volume

**Address contributing factors:**
- Assess footwear
- Evaluate running mechanics
- Build strength in weak areas

### Phase 3: Return to Activity (Weeks 4-8+)

**Progressive return:**
- Start with walk/run intervals
- Gradually increase running volume
- Monitor symptoms closely
- Continue prevention exercises

## Exercises for Shin Splints

### Stretching Exercises

**Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius)**
1. Stand facing wall, hands on wall
2. Step one foot back, keep it straight
3. Press heel into floor
4. Feel stretch in upper calf
5. Hold 30-45 seconds, 2-3 times each side

**Calf Stretch (Soleus)**
1. Same position as above
2. Bend back knee slightly while keeping heel down
3. Feel stretch lower in the calf
4. Hold 30-45 seconds, 2-3 times each side

**Kneeling Shin Stretch**
1. Kneel on soft surface, tops of feet flat on floor
2. Sit back toward heels
3. Feel stretch in front of shins
4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 2-3 times

**Standing Shin Stretch**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Point other foot, placing top of toes on floor behind you
3. Gently press down to feel stretch in front of shin
4. Hold 20-30 seconds each side

### Strengthening Exercises

**Toe Raises (Tibialis Anterior)**
Critical for anterior shin splints.

1. Stand with back against wall
2. Walk feet out 6-12 inches from wall
3. Lift toes toward shins, keeping heels down
4. Lower with control
5. 3 sets of 15-20 reps
6. Progress by moving feet further from wall

**Heel Walks**
1. Walk on heels, toes lifted off ground
2. Keep ankles dorsiflexed throughout
3. 3 sets of 30-60 seconds

**Calf Raises (Eccentric Focus)**
1. Stand on step, heels hanging off edge
2. Rise up on both feet
3. Lower slowly on ONE foot (3-4 seconds)
4. 3 sets of 10-15 each leg

**Single-Leg Calf Raise**
1. Stand on one leg
2. Rise up onto toes
3. Lower with control
4. 3 sets of 12-15 each leg

**Resisted Ankle Dorsiflexion**
1. Sit with band around forefoot
2. Anchor band to something sturdy
3. Pull toes toward shin against resistance
4. 3 sets of 15-20 each foot

**Resisted Ankle Inversion**
1. Sit with band around forefoot, anchored to outside
2. Turn foot inward against resistance
3. 3 sets of 15-20 each foot

### Hip and Core Strengthening

Weak hips and core contribute to poor lower leg mechanics.

**Clamshells**
1. Lie on side, knees bent 45 degrees
2. Keep feet together, open top knee
3. Don't let hips roll back
4. 3 sets of 15-20 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Lie on side, legs straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. Keep hips stacked
4. 3 sets of 15-20 each side

**Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, one foot flat, other leg extended
2. Push through heel to lift hips
3. Keep hips level
4. 3 sets of 12-15 each side

**Monster Walks**
1. Band around thighs
2. Quarter squat position
3. Walk forward/backward and sideways
4. 3 sets of 20 steps each direction

### Foot Strengthening

**Towel Scrunches**
1. Sit with foot on towel
2. Scrunch towel toward you using toes
3. Repeat until towel is gathered
4. 2-3 sets each foot

**Marble Pickups**
1. Pick up marbles with toes
2. Transfer to a cup
3. 2 sets of 20 each foot

**Short Foot Exercise**
1. Sit or stand, foot flat
2. Try to "shorten" your foot by lifting the arch
3. Don't curl toes—the movement is subtle
4. Hold 5 seconds, 15-20 reps each foot

## Sample Rehabilitation Program

### Week 1-2 (Acute Phase)
**Daily:**
- Ice: 3-4 times × 15-20 minutes
- Calf stretches: 3 × 30 seconds each
- Kneeling shin stretch: 2 × 30 seconds

**Every other day:**
- Toe raises: 2 × 15
- Heel walks: 2 × 30 seconds
- Clamshells: 2 × 15

**Activity:** Low-impact only (swimming, cycling)

### Week 3-4 (Building Strength)
**Daily:**
- Stretching routine (all stretches)

**3-4 times per week:**
- Toe raises: 3 × 20
- Eccentric calf raises: 3 × 12
- Resisted dorsiflexion: 3 × 15
- Clamshells: 3 × 20
- Single-leg glute bridge: 3 × 12
- Short foot exercise: 2 × 15

**Activity:** Begin walk/run intervals if pain-free

### Week 5+ (Return to Running)
**2-3 times per week (ongoing prevention):**
- Full exercise routine from Week 3-4
- Add single-leg calf raises: 3 × 15
- Add monster walks: 3 × 20 steps

**Running:**
- Start with 1:1 walk/run (1 min run, 1 min walk)
- Progress by increasing run intervals
- Follow 10% rule for weekly volume increases

## Return to Running Protocol

**Week 1:** Walk 20 min, no pain → Progress
**Week 2:** 1 min run / 1 min walk × 10 cycles (20 min total)
**Week 3:** 2 min run / 1 min walk × 7 cycles (21 min)
**Week 4:** 3 min run / 1 min walk × 6 cycles (24 min)
**Week 5:** 4 min run / 1 min walk × 5 cycles (25 min)
**Week 6:** 5 min run / 1 min walk × 5 cycles (30 min)
**Week 7+:** Continue gradual progression to continuous running

**Rules:**
- No pain during or after running
- Mild discomfort that resolves quickly is okay
- If pain returns, drop back a week
- Run every other day, not consecutive days

## Prevention Strategies

### Training Modifications
- **10% rule:** Don't increase weekly mileage more than 10%
- **Vary surfaces:** Mix soft trails with harder surfaces
- **Avoid sudden changes:** Introduce hills, speed work gradually
- **Rest days:** Include at least 1-2 per week

### Footwear
- **Replace shoes:** Every 300-500 miles
- **Proper fit:** Get fitted at a running specialty store
- **Consider orthotics:** If you have significant flat feet or overpronation

### Running Form
- **Increase cadence:** Aim for 170-180 steps per minute
- **Shorter strides:** Overstriding increases impact
- **Land softly:** Reduce pounding

### Ongoing Exercises
Continue strengthening even after symptoms resolve:
- Calf raises: 2-3 × 15, twice weekly
- Toe raises: 2 × 20, twice weekly
- Hip strengthening: 2-3 exercises, twice weekly

## The Bottom Line

Shin splints are frustrating but highly treatable. The key is addressing the problem early, not pushing through pain, and building the strength and mechanics that prevent recurrence.

**Key takeaways:**
- Reduce training load when symptoms appear
- Stretch tight calves and strengthen the shin muscles
- Address hip and foot weakness
- Return to running gradually with a structured protocol
- Continue prevention exercises even when pain-free

Most cases resolve within 4-8 weeks with proper management. If symptoms persist despite treatment, see a healthcare provider to rule out stress fracture or other conditions.
