      <p className="lead">
        Knee pain during squats is one of the most common gym complaints. The good news: most squat-related knee pain is fixable. The cause is usually technique, mobility, or weakness—not damaged joints. Here's how to identify the problem and fix it.
      </p>

      <h2>Where Does Your Knee Hurt?</h2>

      <p>
        The location of pain gives clues about the cause:
      </p>

      <h3>Front of the Knee (Anterior)</h3>
      <p>
        The most common location. Usually indicates:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Patellofemoral pain syndrome (kneecap tracking issues)</li>
        <li>Patellar tendinopathy (tendon below the kneecap)</li>
        <li>Quad tendon issues (above the kneecap)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Inside of the Knee (Medial)</h3>
      <p>
        Often related to:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Knee valgus (knees caving inward)</li>
        <li>MCL stress</li>
        <li>Medial meniscus irritation</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Outside of the Knee (Lateral)</h3>
      <p>
        May indicate:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>IT band tightness</li>
        <li>Lateral meniscus issues</li>
        <li>Excessive external rotation</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Behind the Knee (Posterior)</h3>
      <p>
        Less common with squatting, but can involve:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Hamstring tendon issues</li>
        <li>Baker's cyst</li>
        <li>Deep squat compression</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Causes of Squat Knee Pain</h2>

      <h3>1. Knee Valgus (Knees Caving In)</h3>
      <p>
        When your knees collapse inward during the squat, it creates shearing forces on the joint. This is often caused by:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Weak glutes (especially gluteus medius)</li>
        <li>Tight adductors</li>
        <li>Poor motor control</li>
        <li>Too narrow a stance</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Cue "knees out" or "spread the floor"</li>
        <li>Strengthen glutes with banded squats, clamshells, lateral band walks</li>
        <li>Try a slightly wider stance</li>
        <li>Use lighter weight until you can control knee position</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>2. Knees Traveling Too Far Forward</h3>
      <p>
        Some forward knee travel is normal and necessary. But excessive forward travel (heels lifting, knees way past toes) increases patellofemoral stress.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Causes:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Limited ankle mobility</li>
        <li>Weak or underactive glutes/hamstrings</li>
        <li>Quad-dominant squatting pattern</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Work on ankle mobility (ankle stretches, elevated heel squats)</li>
        <li>Cue "sit back" more into the hips</li>
        <li>Consider heel-elevated shoes or plates under heels</li>
        <li>Strengthen posterior chain (deadlifts, RDLs, hip thrusts)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>3. Limited Ankle Mobility</h3>
      <p>
        If your ankles don't flex enough, your body compensates—often with excessive forward knee travel, heel lifting, or torso collapse.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Signs:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Heels lift during squats</li>
        <li>Feeling "stuck" at the bottom</li>
        <li>Excessive forward lean</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Ankle mobility exercises (wall ankle stretches, banded distractions)</li>
        <li>Squat with heels elevated (plates, squat wedges, or weightlifting shoes)</li>
        <li>Foam roll or massage calves</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>4. Hip Mobility Limitations</h3>
      <p>
        Tight hips can cause compensations that stress the knees. Limited hip flexion, internal rotation, or abduction can all contribute.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Hip flexor stretches</li>
        <li>90/90 stretches</li>
        <li>Pigeon pose</li>
        <li>Adjust stance width and toe angle to match your hip anatomy</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>5. Weak Quads</h3>
      <p>
        Paradoxically, weak quads can contribute to knee pain. The quads help control knee movement and absorb force. Underdeveloped quads may not adequately support the joint.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Gradual progressive overload on squats</li>
        <li>Supplementary quad work (leg extensions, split squats)</li>
        <li>Build up volume over time</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>6. Weak or Underactive Glutes</h3>
      <p>
        The glutes control hip and knee position. Weak glutes lead to knee valgus, excessive quad reliance, and altered mechanics.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Glute activation before squatting (bridges, banded walks)</li>
        <li>Hip thrusts, Romanian deadlifts</li>
        <li>Focus on "pushing through the floor" during squats</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>7. Too Much, Too Soon</h3>
      <p>
        Rapid increases in weight or volume can irritate tissues before they adapt. Tendons, in particular, adapt slowly.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Progress load gradually (5-10% per week max)</li>
        <li>Monitor total squatting volume</li>
        <li>Include deloads</li>
        <li>If pain flares, reduce load and build back up</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Quick Fixes to Try</h2>

      <h3>Warm Up Properly</h3>
      <p>
        Cold tissues are more vulnerable. Include:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>5-10 minutes light cardio</li>
        <li>Dynamic stretches (leg swings, hip circles)</li>
        <li>Empty bar or goblet squat warm-up sets</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Elevate Your Heels</h3>
      <p>
        A quick test: squat with small plates or a wedge under your heels. If pain decreases, ankle mobility is likely a factor. Weightlifting shoes with elevated heels are a long-term solution.
      </p>

      <h3>Adjust Your Stance</h3>
      <p>
        Experiment with:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Wider stance</li>
        <li>Toes pointed out more</li>
        <li>Different squat variations (goblet, front, box squat)</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Hip anatomy varies—find what works for your body.
      </p>

      <h3>Use a Box or Depth Limiter</h3>
      <p>
        If pain occurs at depth, temporarily limit depth while building strength and mobility. Box squats or squatting to a bench can help.
      </p>

      <h3>Reduce Weight and Focus on Form</h3>
      <p>
        Drop to a weight you can squat pain-free with good form. Build back up from there.
      </p>

      <h2>When to Seek Help</h2>

      <p>
        See a healthcare provider or physical therapist if:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain persists despite technique corrections</li>
        <li>You have swelling, locking, or giving way</li>
        <li>Pain occurs with daily activities (stairs, walking)</li>
        <li>There was an acute injury or trauma</li>
        <li>Pain is getting progressively worse</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Should You Squat Through Pain?</h2>

      <p>
        Mild discomfort (1-3/10) that doesn't worsen during or after training is usually okay to work through while addressing the cause.
      </p>

      <p>
        Pain above 4/10, pain that worsens during the session, or pain that affects you the next day suggests you need to modify or stop.
      </p>

      <p>
        "No pain, no gain" doesn't apply to joint pain. Listen to your body.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Knee pain during squats usually comes from technique issues, mobility limitations, or weakness—not inherent joint damage. Most cases can be resolved with proper adjustments.
      </p>

        Key takeaways:
      </p>

        <li>Identify where the pain is (front, inside, outside)</li>

        <li>Warm up properly and progress load gradually</li>
        <li>Strengthen weak links (glutes, quads, hip stabilizers)</li>
        <li>Seek help if pain persists or worsens</li>
      </ul>
