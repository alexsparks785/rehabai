      <p className="lead">
        You probably spend 6-10 hours a day sitting. That's thousands of hours per year in a position that, done poorly, contributes to back pain, neck tension, and long-term postural changes. Here's how to sit in a way that minimizes damage.
      </p>

      <h2>The Truth About "Perfect" Posture</h2>

      <p>
        First, a reality check: there's no single "perfect" sitting position. The best posture is a varied posture—your next position is your best position. That said, some positions are better starting points than others.
      </p>

      <p>
        The goal isn't rigid, military posture. It's a sustainable, supported position that you can maintain without constant effort, combined with regular movement.
      </p>

      <h2>The Fundamentals of Good Sitting Posture</h2>

      <h3>Feet</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Flat on the floor (or on a footrest)</li>
        <li>Knees at approximately 90 degrees</li>
        <li>Thighs roughly parallel to the floor</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        If your feet dangle, your chair is too high. If your knees are much higher than your hips, it's too low.
      </p>

      <h3>Hips and Pelvis</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Sit back in the chair so your back contacts the backrest</li>
        <li>Hips slightly higher than knees (or level)</li>
        <li>Pelvis in neutral—not excessively tilted forward or tucked under</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Sitting on the edge of your chair unsupported puts all the work on your back muscles.
      </p>

      <h3>Lower Back</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Maintain the natural inward curve (lordosis)</li>
        <li>Use lumbar support if available</li>
        <li>A small cushion or rolled towel can help if your chair lacks support</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Slumping flattens this curve and loads the discs and ligaments.
      </p>

      <h3>Upper Back and Shoulders</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Shoulders relaxed, not hunched up toward ears</li>
        <li>Shoulder blades gently back (not forcefully squeezed)</li>
        <li>Upper back supported by the chair when possible</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Head and Neck</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Head balanced over shoulders, not jutting forward</li>
        <li>Ears roughly aligned with shoulders from the side view</li>
        <li>Chin slightly tucked, not poking forward</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Forward head posture is extremely common with computer work and puts significant strain on neck muscles.
      </p>

      <h2>Setting Up Your Workstation</h2>

      <h3>Chair Height</h3>
      <p>
        Adjust so your feet are flat on the floor with thighs parallel to the ground. If you can't lower your chair enough, use a footrest.
      </p>

      <h3>Monitor Position</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Height:</strong> Top of the screen at or slightly below eye level</li>
        <li><strong>Distance:</strong> Arm's length away (about 20-26 inches)</li>
        <li><strong>Angle:</strong> Tilted slightly back so you're looking straight at it, not down</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Laptops are problematic because the screen and keyboard can't be positioned independently. Consider an external monitor or laptop stand with separate keyboard.
      </p>

      <h3>Keyboard and Mouse</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Keyboard at elbow height</li>
        <li>Elbows at about 90 degrees</li>
        <li>Wrists neutral (not bent up or down)</li>
        <li>Mouse close to the keyboard to avoid reaching</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Document Placement</h3>
      <p>
        If you reference papers frequently, use a document holder next to your monitor rather than flat on the desk (which requires looking down).
      </p>

      <h2>Common Sitting Mistakes</h2>

      <h3>Slouching/Slumping</h3>
      <p>
        The most common error. The spine curves into a C-shape, loading discs and stretching ligaments. Feels relaxed initially but causes pain over time.
      </p>

      <h3>Perching on the Edge</h3>
      <p>
        Sitting on the front of the chair without back support forces your muscles to work constantly. Use your chair's backrest.
      </p>

      <h3>Forward Head Position</h3>
      <p>
        Head drifting forward toward the screen. For every inch your head moves forward, it effectively weighs 10 additional pounds to your neck muscles.
      </p>

      <h3>Crossed Legs</h3>
      <p>
        Sitting cross-legged for extended periods can create asymmetry and reduce circulation. Fine occasionally, not as a default position.
      </p>

      <h3>Leaning to One Side</h3>
      <p>
        Consistent leaning (toward armrest, toward mouse) creates muscle imbalances.
      </p>

      <h2>The Most Important Tip: Move</h2>

      <p>
        No sitting posture is good enough to hold for 8 hours straight. The human body is designed to move. Build movement into your day:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Every 30-60 minutes:</strong> Stand up, even briefly</li>
        <li><strong>Microbreaks:</strong> Look away from the screen, roll your shoulders</li>
        <li><strong>Position changes:</strong> Shift your weight, adjust your posture</li>
        <li><strong>Walking meetings:</strong> When possible</li>
        <li><strong>Standing work:</strong> Alternate sitting and standing if you have an adjustable desk</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        Set a timer if you need a reminder. Movement is more protective than any perfect position.
      </p>

      <h2>Exercises to Counteract Sitting</h2>

      <h3>Hip Flexor Stretches</h3>
      <p>
        Sitting shortens the hip flexors. Stretch them daily with lunging stretches or the couch stretch.
      </p>

      <h3>Thoracic Extension</h3>
      <p>
        Counter the rounded upper back with exercises that extend the thoracic spine: foam rolling, cat-cow stretches, or thoracic rotations.
      </p>

      <h3>Chin Tucks</h3>
      <p>
        Strengthen the deep neck flexors and correct forward head posture. Draw your chin back (making a "double chin") and hold.
      </p>

      <h3>Glute Activation</h3>
      <p>
        Sitting turns off your glutes. Activate them with bridges, squats, or glute squeezes throughout the day.
      </p>

      <h3>Chest Stretches</h3>
      <p>
        The pecs shorten with desk work. Doorway stretches or corner stretches open the chest.
      </p>

      <h2>Standing Desks: Are They Better?</h2>

      <p>
        Standing desks have benefits but aren't a complete solution:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Standing all day has its own problems (leg fatigue, varicose veins)</li>
        <li>The best setup alternates sitting and standing</li>
        <li>You can still have bad posture while standing</li>
        <li>Movement matters more than sitting vs. standing</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        If you have a standing desk, aim for a 1:1 or 2:1 ratio of sitting to standing, changing positions every 30-60 minutes.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Good sitting posture isn't about maintaining one rigid position. It's about starting from a supported position and moving frequently.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Feet flat, knees at 90°, back against the chair</li>
        <li>Maintain natural spinal curves, especially the lower back</li>
        <li>Keep your head over your shoulders, not forward</li>
        <li>Set up your monitor at eye level, arm's length away</li>
        <li>Move every 30-60 minutes—this is the most important thing</li>
        <li>Counteract sitting with stretches and strengthening exercises</li>
        <li>The next position is your best position</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        Your body can tolerate a lot of sitting if you balance it with movement and don't lock yourself into one poor position for hours. Sit better, move more, and your body will thank you.
      </p>
