      <h1>How to Fix Sway Back Posture: Exercises for Better Alignment</h1>

      <p>If you stand with your hips pushed forward, a flat lower back, and your shoulders slouching behind your pelvis, you likely have sway back posture. It's one of the most common postural deviations—and one of the most misunderstood. Let's break down what's actually happening and how to fix it.</p>

      <h2>What Is Sway Back Posture?</h2>

      <p>Sway back is characterized by:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Hips pushed forward in front of the ankles</li>
        <li>A long, flat lower back (reduced lumbar curve)</li>
        <li>Upper body leaning backward</li>
        <li>Head jutting forward to compensate</li>
        <li>Chest appears sunken</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Unlike anterior pelvic tilt (which has an excessive arch), sway back actually has a reduced or flattened lumbar curve. The key identifier is the hip position—if your hips sit well in front of your ankles when you stand relaxed, you're likely in sway back.</p>

      <h2>Why Does Sway Back Happen?</h2>

      <p><strong>The lazy standing pattern:</strong> Sway back is essentially your body's way of standing with minimal effort. By pushing the hips forward and hanging on the ligaments, you use less muscle to stay upright. It's comfortable in the short term but problematic long term.</p>

      <p><strong>Common contributors:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Weak core and hip flexors</li>
        <li>Tight, dominant hamstrings</li>
        <li>Weak upper back (thoracic extensors)</li>
        <li>Prolonged standing without active posture</li>
        <li>Habit from adolescence (especially during growth spurts)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Problems It Causes</h2>

      <p>Sway back isn't just an aesthetic issue:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Lower back pain (from ligament stress)</li>
        <li>Hip joint stress</li>
        <li>Neck pain and headaches (from forward head)</li>
        <li>Reduced core function and stability</li>
        <li>Poor movement patterns in exercises</li>
        <li>Difficulty activating glutes properly</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Fix: Strengthen What's Weak, Mobilize What's Tight</h2>

      <p>Correcting sway back requires a specific approach that differs from fixing anterior pelvic tilt. Here's what to target:</p>

      <h3>Strengthen:</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Hip flexors (yes, really—they're often weak in sway back)</li>
        <li>Lower abdominals</li>
        <li>Upper back extensors</li>
        <li>External obliques</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Lengthen/Mobilize:</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Hamstrings (often tight and dominant)</li>
        <li>Upper abs and chest (pulling you forward)</li>
        <li>Lower back (where it grips)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Best Exercises for Sway Back</h2>

      <h3>1. Dead Bug with Posterior Pelvic Tilt</h3>
      <p>Lie on your back, knees bent 90 degrees, feet off the floor. Press your lower back firmly into the floor (posterior pelvic tilt). Slowly extend one leg out while keeping your back flat. Return and switch sides. If your back arches, you've gone too far.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>2. Hip Flexor March (Supine)</h3>
      <p>Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat. Keeping your lower back pressed to the floor, lift one knee toward your chest using your hip flexor strength. Hold 3 seconds, lower slowly. Focus on keeping the pelvis stable—no rocking.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>3. Standing Hip Flexor Lift</h3>
      <p>Stand tall with weight on one leg. Lift the other knee toward your chest without leaning back or letting your hips shift forward. This trains the hip flexor to work while you maintain proper pelvic position.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>4. Thoracic Extension on Foam Roller</h3>
      <p>Lie with a foam roller across your mid-back, hands supporting your head. Let your upper back extend over the roller (don't force it). Hold 30 seconds, then move the roller slightly and repeat at different levels of the thoracic spine.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 minutes total</strong></p>

      <h3>5. Prone Cobra Hold</h3>
      <p>Lie face down with arms at your sides. Lift your chest, shoulders, and arms off the ground. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and rotate your palms outward. Hold for 20-30 seconds. This strengthens the upper back extensors.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 20-30 seconds</strong></p>

      <h3>6. Wall Supported Standing Posture</h3>
      <p>Stand with your back against a wall, heels 2-3 inches from the wall. Your glutes, upper back, and head should touch the wall. Draw your hips slightly back (they'll want to push forward). This teaches you what proper alignment feels like.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-60 seconds, several times daily</strong></p>

      <h3>7. Supine Hamstring Stretch</h3>
      <p>Lie on your back, one leg flat. Use a strap or your hands to gently pull the other leg straight up. Keep your knee straight but don't force it. Focus on relaxing the hamstring. Hold 30-60 seconds each side.</p>
      <p><strong>2-3 sets each side</strong></p>

      <h3>8. Pallof Press with Proper Stance</h3>
      <p>Use a cable or band at chest height. Stand perpendicular to the anchor in proper posture (hips back over ankles, not pushed forward). Press the handle straight out, resisting the rotation. This trains core stability in good alignment.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>9. Quadruped Hip Extension</h3>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend one leg straight back, keeping your spine neutral. Don't let your lower back arch or your hips shift. This retrains the glutes to work without compensation. Hold the top for 2 seconds.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each side</strong></p>

      <h3>10. Seated Good Morning</h3>
      <p>Sit on a bench or chair, feet flat, hands behind your head. Hinge forward from your hips while keeping your spine neutral (slight arch in lower back). This strengthens the lower back in a controlled way while teaching proper hip hinge mechanics.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15 reps</strong></p>
