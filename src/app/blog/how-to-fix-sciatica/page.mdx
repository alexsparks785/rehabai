      <h1>How to Fix Sciatica: Exercises for Nerve Pain Relief</h1>

      <p>That shooting pain from your lower back down through your buttock and leg? That's sciatica—and it can range from annoying to debilitating. The good news: most cases resolve with the right exercises, often without surgery or injections.</p>

      <h2>What Is Sciatica?</h2>

      <p>Sciatica refers to pain along the sciatic nerve, which runs from your lower back, through your buttock, and down each leg. It's not a diagnosis itself—it's a symptom of an underlying condition affecting the nerve.</p>

      <p><strong>Typical symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain radiating from lower back into buttock and down the leg</li>
        <li>Usually affects one side only</li>
        <li>Pain can be sharp, burning, or electric-shock-like</li>
        <li>Numbness or tingling in the leg or foot</li>
        <li>Weakness in the affected leg</li>
        <li>Pain worsens with sitting, coughing, or sneezing</li>
        <li>May have lower back pain, but leg pain is usually worse</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What Causes Sciatica?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Herniated disc:</strong> Disc material presses on nerve (most common)</li>
        <li><strong>Spinal stenosis:</strong> Narrowing of the spinal canal</li>
        <li><strong>Piriformis syndrome:</strong> Piriformis muscle irritates the nerve</li>
        <li><strong>Degenerative disc disease:</strong> Age-related changes</li>
        <li><strong>Spondylolisthesis:</strong> Vertebra slips over another</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Important: When to See a Doctor Immediately</h2>

      <p>Seek immediate medical attention if you have:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Loss of bladder or bowel control</li>
        <li>Numbness in the groin/saddle area</li>
        <li>Progressive weakness in the leg</li>
        <li>Severe pain that doesn't respond to anything</li>
        <li>Symptoms after significant trauma</li>
      </ul>

      <p>These could indicate cauda equina syndrome—a surgical emergency.</p>

      <h2>Finding Your Direction Preference</h2>

      <p>Different causes of sciatica respond to different movements. Pay attention to what helps and what worsens your symptoms:</p>

      <p><strong>Extension-preferring (often disc-related):</strong> Feels better standing, walking, lying flat, or arching back. Worse with sitting or bending forward.</p>

      <p><strong>Flexion-preferring (often stenosis):</strong> Feels better sitting, bending forward, or lying curled up. Worse with standing or walking long distances.</p>

      <p>Match your exercises to your direction preference.</p>

      <h2>Exercises for Extension-Preferring Sciatica</h2>

      <p>These are often most helpful when a disc is involved:</p>

      <h4>1. Prone Lying</h4>
      <p>Simply lie flat on your stomach. This mild extension can help push disc material forward, away from the nerve. Stay here 5-10 minutes if comfortable.</p>
      <p><strong>5-10 minutes, several times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Prone on Elbows</h4>
      <p>Lie face down, prop up on your elbows. This increases extension. Keep hips on the ground. If this centralizes your pain (moves it from leg toward back), that's a good sign.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-60 seconds, 10 reps, several times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Press-Up (McKenzie Extension)</h4>
      <p>Lie face down, hands beside shoulders. Press up, lifting your chest while keeping hips on the ground. Your lower back arches. Go only as far as comfortable.</p>
      <p><strong>10 reps, hold top for 1-2 seconds, repeat frequently throughout day</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Standing Extension</h4>
      <p>Stand with hands on lower back. Gently lean backward, extending your spine. Useful when you can't lie down.</p>
      <p><strong>10 reps, throughout the day</strong></p>

      <h2>Exercises for Flexion-Preferring Sciatica</h2>

      <p>These may help when stenosis is the cause:</p>

      <h4>5. Knee to Chest</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back. Pull one knee toward your chest, hold. Then try both knees. This opens the spinal canal.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30 seconds, 3-5 reps each</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Child's Pose</h4>
      <p>Kneel and sit back on your heels, reaching arms forward on the floor. Let your spine round. This is a comfortable flexion position.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-60 seconds, repeat as needed</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Seated Flexion</h4>
      <p>Sit on a chair, let your torso fold forward between your knees. This opens up the lower spine.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30 seconds, repeat as needed</strong></p>

      <h2>Nerve Gliding Exercises</h2>

      <p>These help the sciatic nerve move freely:</p>

      <h4>8. Sciatic Nerve Floss</h4>
      <p>Sit on a chair. Slump your back and look down while extending one leg and pointing toes up. Then straighten your back, look up, and point toes down. Alternate back and forth—the nerve slides through the tissues.</p>
      <p><strong>10-15 reps each leg, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Supine Nerve Glide</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back, affected leg raised, knee slightly bent. Alternate between straightening the knee (flexing foot up) and bending the knee (pointing toes). Keep movements gentle.</p>
      <p><strong>10-15 reps, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h2>Piriformis Syndrome Exercises</h2>

      <p>If the piriformis muscle is compressing the nerve:</p>

      <h4>10. Piriformis Stretch</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back. Cross affected leg over the other knee. Pull the bottom thigh toward your chest. Feel a deep stretch in the buttock.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-60 seconds, 3-4 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Figure-4 Stretch</h4>
      <p>Same as above but done seated—cross ankle over opposite knee and lean forward.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-60 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Foam Roll Piriformis</h4>
      <p>Sit on a foam roller with one ankle crossed over the opposite knee. Roll through the buttock of the crossed leg. Find tender spots and hold.</p>
      <p><strong>60-90 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h2>Core Stabilization</h2>

      <p>A stable core protects the spine:</p>

      <h4>13. Bird Dog</h4>
      <p>On hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg while keeping spine neutral. Don't let your back arch or rotate. This builds core stability without loading the spine.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>14. Dead Bug</h4>
      <p>Lie on back, arms up, knees bent 90 degrees. Lower opposite arm and leg while keeping lower back pressed to floor.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 10 each side</strong></p>

      <h4>15. Modified Plank</h4>
      <p>If tolerated, hold a plank on forearms with neutral spine. This builds endurance in the stabilizing muscles.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 20-30 seconds</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Acute Phase (High Pain)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Find your direction preference</li>
        <li>Do preferred exercises frequently (every 1-2 hours)</li>
        <li>Nerve gliding: 2-3 times daily, gently</li>
        <li>Avoid movements that send pain down the leg</li>
        <li>Walking is usually helpful</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Subacute Phase (Improving)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Continue direction-specific exercises: 3-4 times daily</li>
        <li>Add core stabilization: Bird dog, dead bug</li>
        <li>Piriformis stretching if relevant</li>
        <li>Gradually increase walking</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Recovery Phase</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Maintenance stretching daily</li>
        <li>Core strengthening: 3x/week</li>
        <li>Gradual return to normal activities</li>
        <li>Address contributing factors (posture, lifting technique)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What to Avoid</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Prolonged sitting (get up every 30 minutes)</li>
        <li>Heavy lifting during acute phase</li>
        <li>Movements that send pain further down the leg</li>
        <li>Aggressive stretching of the hamstring (can irritate nerve)</li>
        <li>Bed rest beyond 1-2 days</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long Until Relief?</h2>

        <li><strong>Most cases:</strong> Improve within 4-6 weeks</li>

        <li><strong>Full recovery:</strong> Can take 3-6 months for some</li>
        <li><strong>Stubborn cases:</strong> May need medical intervention</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to Seek Medical Care</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>No improvement after 4-6 weeks of exercises</li>
        <li>Progressive weakness</li>
        <li>Severe pain not responding to conservative care</li>
        <li>Bladder or bowel changes (emergency)</li>
        <li>Need for diagnosis confirmation</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Sciatica is painful but usually temporary. Finding your direction preference and doing the right exercises frequently can dramatically speed recovery. Most people improve without surgery—stay consistent with the exercises, stay active with walking, and give your body time to heal.</p>
