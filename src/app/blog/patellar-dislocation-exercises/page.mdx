# Patellar Dislocation Exercises: Stabilize Your Kneecap

A patellar dislocation—when the kneecap slides out of its groove—is painful and often leads to recurrent instability. Proper rehabilitation strengthens the muscles that keep your kneecap tracking correctly.

## Understanding Patellar Dislocation

The patella (kneecap) sits in a groove at the front of the femur. During dislocation, it typically slides to the outside (lateral) of the knee.

**Risk factors:**
- Shallow trochlear groove
- Ligament laxity (loose joints)
- Weak VMO (vastus medialis obliquus)
- Tight lateral structures
- Femoral anteversion (hip anatomy)
- Previous dislocation (high recurrence)

**Symptoms after dislocation:**
- Severe initial pain
- Swelling (often significant)
- Feeling that knee "gave out"
- Visible deformity (if still dislocated)
- Apprehension with certain movements

## Recurrence Risk

**First-time dislocation:**
- Recurrence rate: 15-45%
- Higher in young athletes

**After second dislocation:**
- Recurrence rate: 50%+

**Prevention through rehabilitation is critical.**

## Anatomy of Patellar Stability

**Dynamic stabilizers (muscles):**
- VMO (medial quadriceps)
- Hip external rotators and abductors
- Core muscles

**Static stabilizers (ligaments):**
- MPFL (medial patellofemoral ligament) — often torn in dislocation
- Trochlear groove shape

**Rehabilitation focuses on dynamic stabilizers** since you can strengthen muscles but not change ligaments or bones without surgery.

## Phase 1: Protection and Recovery (Weeks 1-3)

### Initial Management
- May be immobilized in brace or splint
- Crutches for comfort
- Ice and elevation
- Follow physician recommendations

### Range of Motion (When Cleared)

**Passive Knee Flexion**
1. Seated, let knee bend with gravity
2. Or assist with hands/strap
3. Progress range gradually
4. 0-90 degrees typically allowed early

**Heel Slides**
1. Lie on back
2. Slide heel toward buttock
3. Progress range as tolerated
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Quad Activation

**Quad Sets**
1. Sit or lie with leg straight
2. Push knee down, tighten quad
3. Hold 10 seconds
4. 30+ repetitions daily

**Straight Leg Raise**
1. Tighten quad first (no lag)
2. Lift leg 12 inches
3. 3 sets of 15

### Avoid
- Activities that stress patella laterally
- Resisted knee extension through full range
- Quick pivoting or cutting

## Phase 2: VMO and Hip Strengthening (Weeks 3-6)

### VMO-Focused Exercises

The VMO is the innermost part of the quadriceps and pulls the patella medially, countering lateral forces.

**Terminal Knee Extension** (TKE)
1. Band around knee, anchored behind
2. Start with knee slightly bent
3. Fully straighten against resistance
4. Focus on VMO contraction
5. 3 sets of 20

**Mini Squats with Ball Squeeze**
1. Ball between knees
2. Partial squat while squeezing ball
3. Activates VMO
4. 3 sets of 15

**Short Arc Quads with Adduction**
1. Roll under knee
2. Squeeze ball between knees
3. Straighten knee over roll
4. 3 sets of 15

**Leg Press** (Limited Range)
1. Focus on inner quad
2. Don't go past 90 degrees initially
3. 3 sets of 15

### Hip Strengthening (Critical)

Hip weakness contributes to knee valgus (inward collapse), which stresses the patella laterally.

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent
2. Lift top knee, keep feet together
3. Use band for resistance
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Bottom knee bent, top leg straight
2. Lift top leg toward ceiling
3. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Hip External Rotation**
1. Seated with band around knees
2. Push knees apart
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

**Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. 3 sets of 15

## Phase 3: Functional Strengthening (Weeks 6-12)

### Progressive Quad Exercises

**Squats** (Progress Depth)
1. Start partial, increase depth
2. Don't let knees collapse inward
3. 3 sets of 12

**Step-Ups** (Various Heights)
1. Start low (4-6 inches)
2. Progress height
3. Control knee alignment
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Forward Step-Downs**
1. Stand on step
2. Lower opposite foot toward floor
3. Control with quad
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

**Single-Leg Press**
1. Progress to single leg
2. Watch knee alignment
3. 2-3 sets of 12 each

### Lunges (When Ready)

**Walking Lunges**
1. Forward lunges
2. Watch knee tracking
3. 3 sets of 10 each leg

**Lateral Lunges**
1. Step to side
2. Hinge at hip
3. 3 sets of 10 each side

### Single-Leg Exercises

**Single-Leg Squat to Box**
1. High box initially
2. Maintain knee alignment
3. 2-3 sets of 8 each side

**Single-Leg RDL**
1. Hinge on one leg
2. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

**Single-Leg Balance**
1. Progress difficulty
2. Add movements
3. Challenge stability

## Phase 4: Agility and Sport Preparation (Weeks 10-16)

### Neuromuscular Training

**Lateral Band Walks**
1. Band around ankles or knees
2. Side-step with tension
3. 20 steps each direction

**Monster Walks**
1. Band above knees
2. Wide-stance forward walking
3. 20 steps

**Balance Board/BOSU**
1. Single-leg stance
2. Progress perturbations
3. Sport-specific challenges

### Agility Drills

**Lateral Shuffles**
1. Athletic stance
2. Quick side movements
3. Control knee alignment
4. 2-3 sets of 20 yards

**Cutting Progression**
1. Start with gentle angles
2. Progress to sharper cuts
3. Control knee position
4. Watch for apprehension

**Change of Direction**
1. Sport-specific patterns
2. Gradual intensity increase
3. Focus on proper mechanics

### Plyometrics

**Box Jumps** (Low)
1. Focus on landing alignment
2. Knees track over toes
3. 2-3 sets of 8

**Lateral Hops**
1. Side-to-side over line
2. Soft landing
3. Control knee
4. 2-3 sets of 10 each direction

## Patellar Taping and Bracing

### Taping
- McConnell taping may help
- Pulls patella medially
- Can be used during activity
- Technique taught by physical therapist

### Bracing
- Patellar stabilizing braces available
- May help with confidence
- Not substitute for strengthening
- Use during return to sport

## Return to Sport Criteria

**Before full return:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- Quad strength >85% of other side
- Single-leg squat with good alignment
- Completed agility/cutting progression
- No apprehension during activities
- Hop testing near normal

**Timeline:**
- Conservative: 3-4 months typically
- Post-surgical (MPFL repair): 4-6 months

## Surgical Considerations

**Surgery may be needed for:**
- Recurrent dislocations despite rehab
- Significant anatomical abnormalities
- MPFL tear with instability
- Associated cartilage damage

**Common procedures:**
- MPFL reconstruction
- Tibial tubercle osteotomy
- Trochleoplasty (groove deepening)
- Combined procedures

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 8)

### Day 1: Quad/VMO Focus
1. Warm-up: Bike 10 min
2. TKE: 3×20
3. Mini squats with ball: 3×15
4. Leg press: 3×15
5. Step-downs: 3×12 each
6. VMO stretch: 3×30 sec

### Day 2: Hip/Glute Focus
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Clamshells with band: 3×15 each
3. Side-lying abduction: 3×15 each
4. Glute bridge: 3×15
5. Single-leg RDL: 2×10 each
6. Hip flexor stretch: 3×30 sec

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4: Integrated
1. Warm-up: 10 min
2. Squats: 3×12
3. Step-ups: 3×12 each
4. Single-leg balance: 3×30 sec each
5. Lateral band walks: 2×20 each
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 5: Agility (When Cleared)
1. Full warm-up
2. Lateral shuffles
3. Cutting drills
4. Sport-specific movements
5. Cool-down

## Preventing Recurrence

- Continue hip and quad strengthening
- Maintain flexibility
- Proper warm-up before activity
- Consider bracing for high-risk sports
- Address biomechanical issues
- Listen to warning signs (apprehension)

## Key Takeaways

1. **VMO strengthening is essential** — pulls patella medially
2. **Don't forget the hips** — control knee alignment
3. **Control knee position** — no valgus collapse
4. **Progress through phases** — don't rush
5. **Taping/bracing can help** — supplement to strengthening
6. **Recurrence risk is real** — commit to rehab
7. **Surgery if needed** — for recurrent instability
8. **Prevention continues** — maintain strength lifelong

Patellar dislocation rehabilitation requires dedicated strengthening of the VMO and hip stabilizers. With consistent effort, most people return to full activity—but prevention of recurrence demands ongoing commitment to exercise.
