# How to Fix Foot Drop: Exercises to Restore Ankle Function

Foot drop—the inability to lift the front of your foot—makes walking difficult and increases fall risk. Whether caused by nerve damage, muscle weakness, or other conditions, targeted exercises can help improve function and restore more normal movement patterns.

## What Is Foot Drop?

Foot drop (or drop foot) is a condition where you have difficulty lifting the front part of your foot (dorsiflexion). This causes the toes to drag along the ground when walking, often leading to a compensatory "steppage gait" where you lift your knee higher than normal to clear the foot.

**Key symptoms:**
- Inability to lift the front of your foot
- Toes dragging when walking
- Slapping sound when your foot hits the ground
- High-stepping gait pattern
- Tripping or falling, especially on uneven surfaces
- Numbness on the top of the foot or shin

## What Causes Foot Drop?

### Nerve Damage (Most Common)
The peroneal nerve controls the muscles that lift your foot. It can be damaged by:
- Compression (crossing legs frequently, tight casts, prolonged bed rest)
- Injury (knee injury, hip/knee surgery)
- Diabetes (diabetic neuropathy)
- Other conditions affecting nerves

### Muscle Disorders
- Muscular dystrophy
- ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease)
- Polio
- Severe muscle weakness from disuse

### Spinal or Brain Conditions
- Herniated disc (L4-L5 or L5-S1)
- Spinal stenosis
- Stroke
- Multiple sclerosis
- Other central nervous system conditions

### Important Note
**Foot drop requires medical evaluation.** The underlying cause determines treatment options. The exercises in this guide can help improve function, but they don't replace proper diagnosis and medical care.

## Can Exercises Fix Foot Drop?

It depends on the cause:

**Good prognosis for recovery:**
- Compression injury to peroneal nerve (if addressed early)
- Mild nerve damage
- Weakness from disuse
- Some cases of disc herniation (if nerve recovers)

**May improve but not fully resolve:**
- Moderate nerve damage
- Some progressive conditions

**May have limited recovery:**
- Severe nerve damage
- Progressive neuromuscular diseases
- Stroke (though compensation strategies help)

Even when full recovery isn't possible, exercises can:
- Maintain or improve remaining function
- Prevent further weakening
- Build compensatory strength
- Improve balance and walking safety

## Exercises for Foot Drop

### Passive and Active-Assisted Range of Motion

Start here if your muscles are very weak.

#### 1. Passive Ankle Dorsiflexion
**Why:** Maintains joint mobility even if muscles can't move the joint.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with your leg extended
2. Use your hand or a towel to pull your foot toward your shin
3. Hold for 15-30 seconds
4. Relax and repeat
5. Do 10-15 reps, 3x daily

#### 2. Ankle Circles (Assisted)
**Why:** Maintains full ankle mobility.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with your leg supported
2. Use your hands to move your foot in circles
3. If you have some strength, let your muscles help as much as possible
4. 10 circles each direction, 3x daily

#### 3. Active-Assisted Dorsiflexion
**Why:** Helps build the neural connection as strength returns.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with your leg extended
2. Use a towel or resistance band around the ball of your foot
3. Try to pull your toes toward you while the band/towel assists
4. Focus on the mental effort—try to activate the muscle even if movement is small
5. Do 15-20 reps, 3x daily

### Active Strengthening Exercises

Progress to these as you regain some active movement.

#### 4. Toe Raises (Seated)
**Why:** Directly strengthens the anterior tibialis (the main dorsiflexion muscle).

**How to do it:**
1. Sit in a chair with feet flat on the floor
2. Keep your heels on the ground
3. Lift your toes and forefoot toward your shins
4. Hold for 3-5 seconds
5. Lower slowly
6. Do 15-20 reps, 3 sets

**Progression:** Add a light resistance band over your toes for resistance.

#### 5. Heel Walks
**Why:** Functional strengthening for dorsiflexion.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with support nearby for balance
2. Lift your toes off the ground, standing on your heels only
3. Walk forward for 10-20 steps
4. Rest and repeat

**Note:** Only do this if you have enough strength to safely lift your toes.

#### 6. Resistance Band Dorsiflexion
**Why:** Progressive strengthening with measurable resistance.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Loop a resistance band around the ball of your foot
3. Anchor the other end to a stable object in front of you
4. Pull your toes toward you against the resistance
5. Slowly return to start
6. Do 15-20 reps, 3 sets

**Progression:** Use stronger bands as you improve.

#### 7. Ankle Eversion (Outward)
**Why:** Strengthens the peroneal muscles, which also assist in foot clearance.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Loop a resistance band around the outside of your foot
3. Anchor the band to the inside
4. Turn your foot outward against the resistance
5. Do 15-20 reps, 3 sets per foot

#### 8. Marble Pickups
**Why:** Builds intrinsic foot muscle strength and fine motor control.

**How to do it:**
1. Place marbles or small objects on the floor
2. Pick them up with your toes
3. Transfer them to a different location
4. Spend 3-5 minutes practicing

### Balance and Functional Exercises

These exercises help improve walking safety and compensatory strength.

#### 9. Single-Leg Balance
**Why:** Improves overall stability and proprioception.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand near a wall or counter for support
2. Lift your unaffected foot slightly
3. Balance on your affected leg for as long as possible
4. Use support as needed
5. Work up to 30-60 seconds

**Progression:** Close your eyes (with support nearby).

#### 10. Step-Overs
**Why:** Practices the high-stepping pattern needed to clear the foot.

**How to do it:**
1. Place a small obstacle on the ground (rolled towel, small box)
2. Practice stepping over it with control
3. Focus on lifting your knee high and placing your foot carefully
4. Step forward and backward over the obstacle
5. Do 10-15 steps each direction

#### 11. Treadmill Walking (Slow)
**Why:** Provides a controlled environment to practice gait.

**How to do it:**
1. Set treadmill to very slow speed (1-2 mph)
2. Hold the handrails for support
3. Focus on lifting your foot and clearing the toes
4. Practice for 5-10 minutes

### Stretching to Prevent Contracture

If foot drop is chronic, the calf muscles can tighten, making recovery harder.

#### 12. Calf Stretch (Standing)
**How to do it:**
1. Face a wall, hands on the wall
2. Step your affected leg back
3. Keep your heel on the ground
4. Lean forward until you feel a stretch
5. Hold for 30-60 seconds
6. Do 3 reps, 2-3 times daily

#### 13. Gastrocnemius Stretch (Straight Knee)
Focus on stretching with the knee straight to target the gastrocnemius.

#### 14. Soleus Stretch (Bent Knee)
Same position but bend your back knee to target the deeper soleus muscle.

## Assistive Devices

While working on recovery, these devices can help:

### Ankle-Foot Orthosis (AFO)
A brace that holds your foot in a neutral position, preventing toe drag. Your doctor or physical therapist can help determine if you need one.

### Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES)
A device that stimulates the muscles to lift your foot at the right time during walking. Often used in rehabilitation.

## Your Daily Routine

### Morning (5 minutes)
- Passive ankle circles: 10 each direction
- Calf stretch: 30 seconds
- Seated toe raises: 20 reps
- Assisted dorsiflexion: 15 reps

### Midday (5 minutes)
- Resistance band dorsiflexion: 15 reps, 2 sets
- Ankle eversion: 15 reps, 2 sets
- Single-leg balance: 30 seconds (with support)

### Evening (10 minutes)
- Full stretching sequence: calves, 60 seconds each
- All strengthening exercises: 2 sets each
- Step-over practice: 10 reps each direction
- Marble pickups: 3 minutes

## Tips for Daily Life

### Walking Safety
- Use an AFO if prescribed
- Wear supportive shoes with good tread
- Avoid loose-fitting shoes or flip-flops
- Use a cane or walker if needed for balance
- Remove tripping hazards from your home
- Use handrails on stairs

### Prevent Pressure on the Peroneal Nerve
If compression caused your foot drop:
- Don't cross your legs
- Avoid kneeling for long periods
- Make sure casts or braces aren't too tight
- Change positions frequently

### Be Patient
Nerve recovery is slow. Expect weeks to months for improvement, depending on severity. Consistent daily exercise gives the best results.

## When to Seek Medical Attention

See a doctor immediately if:
- Foot drop came on suddenly
- You also have back pain or sciatica
- Weakness is spreading to other areas
- You have bowel or bladder problems
- There's no improvement after 4-6 weeks of exercise

Get a specialist evaluation if:
- The cause isn't clear
- You're not improving with conservative treatment
- You need help with bracing or electrical stimulation
- Surgery might be an option

## Realistic Expectations

- **Mild compression injuries:** Often recover fully in weeks to months
- **Moderate nerve damage:** May take 6-12 months; full recovery possible but not guaranteed
- **Severe damage or chronic conditions:** Focus may shift to maximizing remaining function and compensation

Even if full recovery isn't possible, most people can improve their walking safety and independence with consistent work.

## The Bottom Line

Foot drop is a challenging condition, but exercises can make a real difference. The key is consistency: daily stretching prevents tightening, and daily strengthening gives your muscles and nerves the best chance to recover.

Start with passive range of motion if you're very weak. Progress to active strengthening as able. Always maintain calf flexibility to prevent permanent shortening.

Work with a physical therapist if possible—they can assess your specific situation, help with assistive devices, and progress your exercises appropriately.

Your body has remarkable capacity to adapt and recover. Give it the stimulus it needs, be patient, and focus on what you can do today.
