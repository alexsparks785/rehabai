      <p className="lead">
        The bench press is notorious for causing shoulder pain. But the exercise itself isn't the problem—it's usually how it's performed. With the right technique and positioning, most people can bench press without shoulder issues. Here's how.
      </p>

      <h2>Why Does Bench Press Hurt Shoulders?</h2>

      <p>
        The shoulder is a highly mobile joint that sacrifices stability for range of motion. During bench press, the shoulder is loaded in a vulnerable position—especially if technique is poor. Common issues include:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Impingement:</strong> Tissues get pinched between bones</li>
        <li><strong>Rotator cuff strain:</strong> Small stabilizer muscles are overworked</li>
        <li><strong>Labral stress:</strong> The cartilage rim gets loaded awkwardly</li>
        <li><strong>AC joint irritation:</strong> The joint at the top of the shoulder</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Common Form Mistakes That Cause Shoulder Pain</h2>

      <h3>1. Elbows Flared at 90 Degrees</h3>
      <p>
        Benching with your elbows straight out to the sides (perpendicular to your body) puts the shoulder in its most vulnerable position for impingement.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Tuck your elbows to about 45-75 degrees from your torso. This puts the shoulder in a stronger, safer position. Think about tucking your elbows toward your ribs, not flaring them out.
      </p>

      <h3>2. Flat Back / No Arch</h3>
      <p>
        Benching with a completely flat back puts the shoulders at a disadvantage and increases the range of motion in a vulnerable position.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Create a slight arch in your upper back by:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Retracting (squeezing) your shoulder blades together</li>
        <li>Depressing (pulling down) your shoulder blades</li>
        <li>Maintaining a natural arch in your lower back</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        This creates a stable "shelf" for your shoulders and reduces range of motion in the most vulnerable positions.
      </p>

      <h3>3. Shoulder Blades Not Set</h3>
      <p>
        If your shoulder blades move around during the lift (especially coming forward at the top), your shoulders lose stability and take excessive stress.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Before unracking:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pull your shoulder blades together (retraction)</li>
        <li>Pull them down toward your back pockets (depression)</li>
        <li>Keep them locked in this position throughout the entire set</li>
      </ul>
      <p>
        Your shoulder blades should stay pinned to the bench the whole time.
      </p>

      <h3>4. Bar Path Too Straight</h3>
      <p>
        Pressing the bar straight up and down puts more stress on the shoulders. The natural bar path has a slight diagonal.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Lower the bar to your lower chest/sternum area. Press up and slightly back toward your face (ending roughly over your shoulders). This J-curve or diagonal path is more shoulder-friendly.
      </p>

      <h3>5. Grip Too Wide</h3>
      <p>
        Very wide grips increase the stretch on the shoulder and can contribute to impingement.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Use a grip where your forearms are roughly vertical when the bar touches your chest. For most people, this is 1.5-2x shoulder width. Experiment to find what feels best for your anatomy.
      </p>

      <h3>6. Going Too Deep</h3>
      <p>
        While full range of motion is generally good, some people don't have the shoulder mobility to safely touch the bar to their chest without pain.
      </p>

      <p><strong>Fix:</strong> Temporarily limit range of motion using a board or foam pad on your chest. Work on shoulder mobility separately and gradually increase depth as mobility improves.
      </p>

      <h2>Proper Bench Press Setup for Shoulder Health</h2>

      <h3>Step-by-Step:</h3>
      <ol>
        <li>Lie on the bench with eyes under the bar</li>
        <li>Pull shoulder blades together and down—really exaggerate this</li>
        <li>Create a slight arch by driving your upper back into the bench</li>
        <li>Plant feet firmly (flat or on toes depending on preference)</li>
        <li>Grip the bar—forearms should be vertical at the bottom</li>
        <li>Unrack by pressing up, keeping shoulder blades set</li>
        <li>Lower to lower chest with elbows at 45-75 degrees</li>
        <li>Press up and slightly back (J-curve)</li>
        <li>Keep shoulder blades pinned throughout</li>
      </ol>

      <h2>Exercises to Support Shoulder Health</h2>

      <h3>Strengthen the Rotator Cuff</h3>
      <p>
        The small rotator cuff muscles stabilize the shoulder. Include:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>External rotations (band or dumbbell)</li>
        <li>Face pulls</li>
        <li>Band pull-aparts</li>
        <li>Prone Y-T-W raises</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Balance Pushing with Pulling</h3>
      <p>
        Many lifters bench press far more than they row. This creates imbalances. Aim for at least a 1:1 ratio of horizontal pushing to pulling (ideally 2:1 pulling to pushing).
      </p>

      <h3>Work on Shoulder Mobility</h3>
      <p>
        Tight pecs, lats, and posterior capsule limit shoulder function. Include:
      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Doorway pec stretches</li>
        <li>Sleeper stretches</li>
        <li>Lat stretches</li>
        <li>Thoracic spine mobility work</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Alternative Exercises</h2>

      <p>
        If barbell bench press consistently hurts despite technique fixes, consider alternatives:
      </p>

      <h3>Dumbbell Bench Press</h3>
      <p>
        Allows more natural shoulder movement. Hands can rotate, and range of motion can be adjusted. Often better tolerated than barbell.
      </p>

      <h3>Neutral Grip Press</h3>
      <p>
        Using dumbbells with palms facing each other reduces shoulder external rotation demands.
      </p>

      <h3>Floor Press</h3>
      <p>
        Limits range of motion at the bottom, reducing shoulder stretch. Good for those who feel pain at the bottom of the lift.
      </p>

      <h3>Push-Ups</h3>
      <p>
        Allow the shoulder blades to move naturally. Can be progressed with deficit, weight, or bands.
      </p>

      <h3>Landmine Press</h3>
      <p>
        The angled pressing path is often more shoulder-friendly than horizontal pressing.
      </p>

      <h2>When to Seek Help</h2>

      <p>
        See a healthcare provider or physical therapist if:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Pain persists despite technique corrections</li>
        <li>You have weakness or instability in the shoulder</li>
        <li>Pain occurs with daily activities</li>
        <li>There's swelling, clicking, or catching</li>
        <li>Pain is sharp or worsening</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Should You Bench Through Pain?</h2>

      <p>
        Mild discomfort (1-3/10) that doesn't worsen may be okay to work through while fixing technique. However:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Sharp pain = stop</li>
        <li>Pain that worsens during the set = stop</li>
        <li>Pain that affects you the next day = reduce load or modify</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        There's no award for benching through injury. Protect your shoulders for the long term.
      </p>

      <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>

      <p>
        Bench press shoulder pain is usually a technique issue, not an inherent problem with the exercise. Proper scapular position, elbow angle, and bar path make a huge difference.
      </p>

      <p>
        Key takeaways:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li>Retract and depress shoulder blades before every rep</li>
        <li>Tuck elbows to 45-75 degrees—don't flare to 90</li>
        <li>Use a slight arch to create a stable base</li>
        <li>Press in a J-curve, not straight up and down</li>
        <li>Balance pushing with pulling exercises</li>
        <li>Strengthen rotator cuff and improve mobility</li>
        <li>Consider dumbbell or alternative presses if pain persists</li>
      </ul>
