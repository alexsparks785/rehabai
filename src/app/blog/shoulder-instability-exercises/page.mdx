# Shoulder Instability Exercises: Prevent Dislocations and Subluxations

Shoulder instability—whether from a previous dislocation or natural looseness—increases your risk of the shoulder "slipping" out of place. The right exercises strengthen the dynamic stabilizers and can significantly reduce episodes.

## Understanding Shoulder Instability

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body, but that mobility comes at the cost of stability. The ball (humeral head) is much larger than the socket (glenoid), making it vulnerable to displacement.

**Types of instability:**

**Traumatic:** Following a dislocation or injury
- Usually unidirectional (one direction)
- Often requires rehabilitation or surgery
- Common in contact sports

**Atraumatic:** Without significant injury
- Often multidirectional
- May be due to loose ligaments
- More common in hypermobile individuals

**Microinstability:** Subtle looseness without frank dislocation
- Common in overhead athletes
- May cause pain without obvious instability

## Symptoms of Instability

- Feeling that shoulder will "pop out"
- Apprehension with certain positions (arm overhead, behind back)
- Previous dislocations or subluxations
- Dead arm sensation
- Clicking or catching
- Pain with overhead activities

## Goals of Exercise

**Strengthen dynamic stabilizers:**
- Rotator cuff muscles
- Scapular stabilizers
- Deltoid and other shoulder muscles

**Improve neuromuscular control:**
- Proprioception (position sense)
- Reaction time
- Coordination

**Achieve functional stability:**
- Perform activities without apprehension
- Return to sport safely

## Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

### Rotator Cuff Activation

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow bent 90 degrees, arm at side
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. Control return
5. 3 sets of 15

**Prone Y-T-W Raises**
1. Lie face down on bench or bed
2. Y: Arms overhead at 45 degrees, thumbs up, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze shoulder blades, lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each position

### Scapular Stabilization

**Scapular Clocks**
1. Arm supported on wall or table
2. Move shoulder blade: up, down, forward, backward
3. Isolate scapular movement
4. 10 movements each direction

**Low Rows**
1. Band anchored at waist level
2. Pull elbows back, squeeze shoulder blades
3. 3 sets of 15

**Serratus Punches**
1. Lie on back, arm straight up
2. Push arm toward ceiling (shoulder blade lifts)
3. Return slowly
4. 3 sets of 15

### Posture and Positioning

**Chin Tucks**
1. Draw chin back (double chin)
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 15 repetitions

**Corner Stretch**
1. Stand in corner, hands on walls
2. Lean forward to stretch chest
3. Hold 30 seconds

## Phase 2: Strengthening (Weeks 4-8)

### External Rotation Progressions

**Standing External Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate forearm outward against band
3. Control return
4. 3 sets of 15

**External Rotation at 90° Abduction**
1. Arm out to side, elbow bent (90/90 position)
2. Rotate forearm up toward ceiling
3. This is the position of apprehension—train it
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Prone External Rotation**
1. Lie face down, arm hanging off edge
2. Elbow bent 90 degrees
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. 3 sets of 15

### Internal Rotation Strengthening

**Standing Internal Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate forearm inward against band
3. 3 sets of 15

**Subscapularis Lift-Off**
1. Hand behind back
2. Lift hand away from back
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. 2-3 sets of 10

### Scapular Strengthening

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. External rotation at end
4. 3 sets of 15

**Prone Rows**
1. Lie face down on bench
2. Row weights up, squeeze shoulder blades
3. 3 sets of 12-15

**Push-Up Plus**
1. Standard push-up position
2. At top, push further (protract shoulder blades)
3. Feel serratus anterior working
4. 2-3 sets of 10-15

## Phase 3: Dynamic Stability (Weeks 8-12)

### Rhythmic Stabilization

**Ball on Wall**
1. Hand on ball against wall
2. Partner provides gentle pushes
3. Resist without letting ball move
4. 30-60 seconds

**Body Blade or Oscillation**
1. Hold body blade or oscillating device
2. Create and control oscillations
3. 30-60 seconds various positions

### Closed Chain Exercises

**Quadruped Position**
1. Hands and knees
2. Maintain stable shoulder position
3. Progress: lift opposite arm/leg

**Wall Push-Ups**
1. Standard push-ups against wall
2. Progress angle as tolerated
3. 3 sets of 15

**Push-Up Holds**
1. Hold push-up position
2. Maintain scapular stability
3. 30-60 seconds

### Proprioception Training

**Ball Catches**
1. Partner tosses ball
2. Catch in various positions
3. Progress to single arm

**Perturbation Training**
1. Arm supported, partner provides unexpected pushes
2. React and stabilize
3. 30-60 second sets

## Phase 4: Sport-Specific (Weeks 12+)

### Throwing Progression

**Phase 1:** Wrist flicks, no arm motion
**Phase 2:** Short toss, easy effort
**Phase 3:** Increase distance gradually
**Phase 4:** Increase velocity gradually
**Phase 5:** Full throwing (when cleared)

### Overhead Athletes

**Posterior Capsule Stretch**
1. Cross-body stretch
2. Sleeper stretch (side-lying)
3. Hold 30 seconds

**Plyometric Training**
1. Medicine ball throws
2. Start light, progress weight
3. Various directions

### Contact Sports

**Reactive Exercises**
1. Partner provides resistance
2. Push and resist in multiple directions
3. Simulate sport demands

**Impact Preparation**
1. Pad work (if applicable)
2. Gradual return to contact
3. May need brace for competition

## Exercises to Approach with Caution

### High-Risk Positions

**Apprehension position:** Arm abducted and externally rotated (like throwing position)
- Train this position, but progress gradually
- Stop if feeling unstable

**Behind-the-back positions:**
- Limit range initially
- Progress as stability improves

### Exercises to Modify

**Bench Press:**
- Don't go too deep initially
- Use dumbbells for better control
- Progress depth gradually

**Dips:**
- High stress on shoulder
- Limit depth
- Progress slowly

**Pull-Ups Behind Neck:**
- Avoid completely
- Do pull-ups to front instead

## Sample Weekly Program

### Day 1: Rotator Cuff Focus
1. Warm-up: Band work, 2 min
2. Side-lying ER: 3×15
3. Standing ER at 90°: 2×15
4. Standing IR: 3×15
5. Prone Y-T-W: 2×10 each
6. Face pulls: 3×15

### Day 2: Scapular Focus
1. Warm-up: Arm circles, 2 min
2. Low rows: 3×15
3. Serratus punches: 3×15
4. Push-up plus: 2×12
5. Ball on wall stability: 3×30 sec
6. Corner stretch: 3×30 sec

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4: Dynamic Stability
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Rhythmic stabilization: 3×1 min
3. Quadruped holds: 3×30 sec
4. Push-up progression: 3×12
5. Ball catches: 2 min
6. Shoulder stretches: 5 min

### Day 5: Sport-Specific (If Applicable)
1. Full warm-up
2. Sport-specific movements
3. Gradual intensity progression
4. Cool-down and stretching

## Bracing and Taping

### When to Use
- Return to high-risk sports
- History of multiple dislocations
- Feeling of instability during activity

### Types
- **Shoulder brace:** Limits range of motion
- **Athletic tape:** Provides proprioceptive feedback
- **Neoprene sleeve:** Warmth and mild compression

### Long-Term
- May need for sports long-term
- Supplement with strengthening
- Consider surgical consultation for recurrent instability

## When Surgery May Be Needed

**Consider surgical consultation if:**
- Multiple dislocations despite rehabilitation
- Significant bone loss (bony Bankart)
- High-level athlete with instability
- Failed conservative treatment

**Surgical options:**
- Bankart repair (labral repair)
- Capsular shift
- Latarjet (bone block procedure)

**Post-surgical rehabilitation** follows similar principles but with specific protocols.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Strengthen rotator cuff** — primary dynamic stabilizers
2. **Don't neglect scapula** — base of shoulder stability
3. **Train the apprehension position** — carefully, progressively
4. **Proprioception matters** — include balance and reaction work
5. **Progress to sport-specific** — prepare for actual demands
6. **Consider bracing** — for return to high-risk activities
7. **Be patient** — stability takes 3-6 months to build
8. **Surgery may be needed** — for recurrent dislocations

Shoulder instability is manageable with dedicated rehabilitation. Build a strong, stable foundation, progress carefully, and you can return to your activities with confidence.
