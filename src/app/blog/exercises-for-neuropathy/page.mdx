# Exercises for Neuropathy: Improve Nerve Health and Function

Peripheral neuropathy—nerve damage causing numbness, tingling, and pain in hands and feet—doesn't have to mean stopping exercise. In fact, the right exercises can improve symptoms, maintain function, and reduce fall risk. Here's how to stay active safely with neuropathy.

## How Exercise Helps Neuropathy

### Benefits

**Circulation improvement:**
- Better blood flow to nerves
- Delivers nutrients for nerve health
- May slow progression

**Blood sugar control:**
- Critical for diabetic neuropathy
- Exercise improves insulin sensitivity
- Better glucose management

**Pain reduction:**
- Releases natural pain-relievers
- Improves mood
- Distraction from discomfort

**Functional benefits:**
- Maintains strength
- Improves balance (reduces falls)
- Preserves mobility
- Better quality of life

## Safety First

### Important Precautions

**Foot care:**
- Always wear proper footwear (no barefoot exercise)
- Check feet before and after exercise
- Well-fitting, supportive shoes
- No shoes that rub or cause blisters
- White socks help spot any bleeding

**Balance safety:**
- Have support nearby
- Exercise in safe environment
- Avoid exercises with high fall risk
- Good lighting

**Pain awareness:**
- Some discomfort is normal
- Sharp or worsening pain is not
- Modify if needed

**Blood sugar (diabetic neuropathy):**
- Check blood sugar before and after
- Have fast-acting carbs available
- Know hypoglycemia symptoms

### When Not to Exercise

- Open wounds or infections on feet
- Acute flare of symptoms
- Blood sugar too high or low
- After new medication changes (consult doctor)

## Balance Exercises

### Critical for Fall Prevention

**With neuropathy, balance is often compromised. These exercises help:**

**Chair-Supported Standing**
- Stand behind sturdy chair
- Hands on chair back
- Practice standing tall
- Progress to fingertip touch
- 30-60 seconds

**Weight Shifts**
- Hold chair
- Shift weight to one foot
- Hold 10 seconds
- Shift to other foot
- 10 shifts each side

**Single-Leg Stand (Supported)**
- Hold chair back
- Lift one foot slightly
- Hold 10-30 seconds
- Switch legs
- Progress to lighter touch

**Tandem Stand**
- One foot in front of other (heel to toe)
- Hold chair for support
- Hold 20-30 seconds
- Switch which foot is in front

**Heel-Toe Walking**
- Walk in a line, heel touching toe
- Chair or wall nearby for support
- 10-20 steps

### Eyes-Closed Progression

**Only when stable with eyes open:**
- Reduces reliance on vision
- Trains proprioception
- Start with chair support always
- Progress very gradually

## Foot and Ankle Exercises

### Maintaining Function

**Toe Exercises**
- Scrunch toes tight, then spread wide
- 15 reps
- Helps maintain foot muscle function

**Ankle Pumps**
- Point toes down, then pull up
- 20 reps
- Improves circulation

**Ankle Circles**
- Rotate ankle in circles
- 10 each direction each foot
- Maintains mobility

**Towel Scrunches**
- Place towel on floor
- Scrunch towel with toes
- 15 reps each foot
- Builds intrinsic foot muscles

**Marble Pickups** (if able)
- Pick up marbles with toes
- Place in bowl
- 10 per foot
- Fine motor control

### Calf Exercises

**Seated Calf Raises**
- Sitting in chair
- Raise heels off floor
- Lower slowly
- 15-20 reps
- Helps circulation

**Standing Calf Raises** (with support)
- Hold onto chair
- Rise up on toes
- Lower slowly
- 15 reps
- Important for walking

## Walking Program

### Best Exercise for Neuropathy

**Walking helps:**
- Improves blood sugar
- Enhances circulation
- Maintains cardiovascular health
- Keeps you functional

**Guidelines:**
- Start with what you can do (even 5 minutes)
- Proper footwear essential
- Safe, level surfaces
- Build up gradually
- Aim for 30 minutes most days eventually

**Walking safely with neuropathy:**
- Look ahead, not just at feet
- Use walking poles if helpful
- Avoid uneven terrain initially
- Well-lit paths
- Tell someone your route

## Strength Training

### Maintaining Muscle

**Upper Body (Seated):**

**Seated Arm Raises**
- Sit tall, weights optional
- Raise arms to sides, then overhead
- 10-12 reps each

**Seated Rows** (with band)
- Sit on floor or chair
- Pull band toward body
- 12-15 reps

**Bicep Curls**
- Seated or standing (with support)
- Light weights
- 12 reps each arm

**Lower Body:**

**Sit-to-Stand**
- Rise from chair without hands (if able)
- Lower slowly
- 8-10 reps
- Use hands on thighs if needed

**Mini Squats** (with support)
- Hold chair back
- Small squat, not deep
- 10-12 reps

**Leg Extensions**
- Seated, extend one leg
- Hold 3 seconds
- 10 each leg

**Lying Hip Exercises**
- Side-lying leg lifts: 10 each
- Bridges: 10-12 reps
- Clamshells: 12 each side

## Low-Impact Cardio Options

### Beyond Walking

**Swimming/Water Exercise**
- Excellent for neuropathy
- Water supports body weight
- Reduces fall risk
- Good for all fitness levels
- Temperature should be comfortable

**Cycling**
- Stationary bike safest
- No balance required
- Good cardio without foot impact
- Start with low resistance

**Chair Aerobics**
- Seated exercises to music
- Upper body movements
- Leg movements while seated
- No fall risk

**Recumbent Bike**
- Even more stable than upright
- Good for those with significant balance issues

## Managing Symptoms During Exercise

### If Pain Increases

- Reduce intensity
- Shorter sessions
- Different exercise type
- Check footwear
- Consult healthcare provider if persistent

### For Numbness

- Extra attention to foot position
- Check feet after exercise
- Don't rely on pain signals—watch feet
- Proper footwear crucial

### For Balance Issues

- Always have support available
- Don't rush
- Chair exercises are valid
- Water exercise reduces fall risk

## Progress Tracking

### What to Monitor

- Walking distance/time
- Balance hold times
- Strength (reps, weight)
- Pain levels
- Blood sugar patterns (diabetic)

### Realistic Expectations

**Week 1-4:**
- Building routine
- May notice improved energy
- Blood sugar may improve (diabetic)

**Month 2-3:**
- Better balance
- Increased endurance
- Possibly reduced symptoms

**Ongoing:**
- Maintaining function
- Preventing decline
- Quality of life improvement

## When to See a Doctor

**Report these:**
- Increased numbness or tingling
- New weakness
- Wounds that don't heal
- Significant pain change
- Balance getting worse despite exercise
- Falls

## Key Takeaways

**Most important exercises:**
- Walking (or other cardio you can do)
- Balance training (daily)
- Foot and ankle exercises
- Strength training (2-3x/week)

**Safety priorities:**
- Proper footwear always
- Check feet before and after
- Have support for balance exercises
- Safe environment
- Monitor blood sugar (diabetic)

**Key principles:**
- Something is always better than nothing
- Consistency over intensity
- Modify as needed
- Safety first, always

**Benefits to remember:**
- Better blood sugar control
- Improved circulation
- Reduced fall risk
- Maintained function
- Better quality of life

Neuropathy changes how you exercise, not whether you should. Work within your abilities, prioritize safety, and stay consistent. Your body—and your nerves—will benefit.
