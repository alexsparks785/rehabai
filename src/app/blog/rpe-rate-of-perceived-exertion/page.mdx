      <h2>RPE vs. RIR: What's the Difference?</h2>

      <p>
        You'll often see RPE and RIR (Reps in Reserve) used interchangeably. They're closely related:
      </p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>RPE</strong> = How hard was that set? (scale of effort)</li>
        <li><strong>RIR</strong> = How many more reps could you have done? (reps left in the tank)</li>
      </ul>

      <p>
        They're essentially the same concept from different angles. RPE 8 = 2 RIR. Some people find one more intuitive than the other.
      </p>

      <h2>Why Use RPE Instead of Percentages?</h2>

      <p>
        Traditional programming often prescribes weights as percentages of your one-rep max (1RM). For example: "Squat 4×6 at 75%." This works, but it has limitations:
      </p>

      <h3>Daily Fluctuations</h3>
      <p>
        Your true max varies day to day based on sleep, stress, nutrition, and accumulated fatigue. A weight that's 75% of your max on a good day might be 85% on a bad day.
      </p>

      <h3>1RM Testing Issues</h3>
      <p>
        To use percentages accurately, you need to know your true 1RM. But maxing out is fatiguing, carries injury risk, and your max changes as you progress. RPE doesn't require knowing your max.
      </p>

      <h3>Individual Variation</h3>
      <p>
        Some people can do many reps at 80% of their max; others can only do a few. RPE accounts for individual differences in rep capacity.
      </p>

      <h3>Autoregulation</h3>
      <p>
        RPE allows "autoregulation"—adjusting training based on how you actually feel rather than what a spreadsheet says. Good day? Push a bit harder. Bad day? Back off without feeling like you failed.
      </p>

      </p>
      <ul>
        <li>Occasionally take sets to actual failure (RPE 10) to learn what it feels like</li>
        <li>Record your RPE predictions before sets and compare to actual results</li>
        <li>Start with a conservative estimate; adjust as you learn</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Step 3: Select Weight Based on Feel</h3>

        Warm up, then choose a weight you estimate will hit your target RPE. After the set, rate how it actually felt. Adjust for subsequent sets if needed.

      <h2>Limitations of RPE</h2>

      <h3>Requires Self-Awareness</h3>

      </p>

      <h3>Easy to Sandbag</h3>
      <p>
        Without external accountability, it's tempting to rate sets as harder than they actually were. You need honesty with yourself.
      </p>

      <h3>Exercise-Dependent</h3>
      <p>
        RPE is easier to rate on compound lifts (squats, deadlifts, presses) than isolation exercises. It's hard to know how many more reps you have on lateral raises.
      </p>

      <h3>Doesn't Replace Programming Structure</h3>
      <p>
        RPE tells you how hard to work, not what exercises to do or how to progress long-term. You still need a well-designed program.

        <li><strong>Consider form:</strong> If form breaks down, you're at or near failure</li>
