# TFCC Injury Exercises: Rehabilitate Your Ulnar Wrist

The TFCC (triangular fibrocartilage complex) is a crucial structure on the pinky side of your wrist. Injuries here cause pain, clicking, and weakness—but targeted exercises can help restore function.

## Understanding the TFCC

The triangular fibrocartilage complex is a cartilage and ligament structure that:
- Cushions the ulnar (pinky) side of the wrist
- Stabilizes the distal radioulnar joint
- Allows smooth forearm rotation
- Absorbs force through the wrist

**Location:** Between the ulna (forearm bone) and the carpal bones on the pinky side of the wrist.

## Types of TFCC Injuries

### Traumatic Tears (Type 1)
- From falls, twisting injuries, or force
- Acute onset of pain
- May involve specific tear patterns

### Degenerative Tears (Type 2)
- From wear and tear over time
- More common with age
- May have gradual onset

**Common causes:**
- Falling on outstretched hand
- Twisting injury (opening jar, turning doorknob)
- Racquet sports (tennis, badminton)
- Repetitive loading
- Age-related degeneration

## Symptoms

**Classic TFCC symptoms:**
- Pain on the pinky side of wrist
- Pain worse with gripping, twisting, pushing
- Clicking or popping with forearm rotation
- Weakness with grip
- Pain with weight-bearing on hand
- Swelling on ulnar wrist

**Aggravating activities:**
- Opening doors/jars
- Pushing up from chair
- Twisting motions
- Racquet sports
- Weight-bearing (push-ups, yoga)

## Getting Proper Diagnosis

TFCC injuries can be tricky to diagnose. Evaluation may include:
- Physical examination (specific tests like the fovea sign)
- X-rays (rule out fractures, assess ulnar variance)
- MRI (may show tears)
- MRI arthrogram (more accurate for tears)
- Wrist arthroscopy (gold standard)

**See a hand specialist** if symptoms persist—not all TFCC injuries show on imaging.

## Phase 1: Protection and Pain Relief

### Activity Modification
- Avoid painful gripping and twisting
- Limit weight-bearing on hand
- Modify work/sports activities

### Splinting
A wrist splint or ulnar gutter splint may help:
- Limits painful motion
- Allows healing
- Wear during activities, remove for exercises

### Pain Management
- Ice: 15-20 minutes after activity
- NSAIDs as directed by physician
- Rest from aggravating activities

## Range of Motion Exercises

### Forearm Rotation (Pronation/Supination)

This is the key motion for the TFCC.

**How to do it:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Start in neutral (thumb up)
3. Slowly rotate palm up (supination)
4. Return to neutral
5. Slowly rotate palm down (pronation)
6. 10-15 repetitions

**Key:** Move slowly and stay within pain-free range.

### Wrist Flexion/Extension

**How to do it:**
1. Support forearm, hand off edge
2. Bend wrist down (flexion)
3. Bend wrist up (extension)
4. 10-15 repetitions

### Ulnar/Radial Deviation

**How to do it:**
1. Forearm supported, thumb up
2. Move wrist toward thumb (radial)
3. Move wrist toward pinky (ulnar)
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Caution:** Ulnar deviation may be painful—limit range if needed.

### Wrist Circles

**How to do it:**
1. Support forearm
2. Make small circles with wrist
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Keep movement pain-free

## Stretching Exercises

### Wrist Flexor Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Extend arm, palm up
2. Use other hand to gently pull fingers back
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3 times

### Wrist Extensor Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Extend arm, palm down
2. Gently push wrist down with other hand
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat 3 times

### Forearm Pronator Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Arm extended, palm down
2. Use other hand to rotate forearm toward supination
3. Hold 30 seconds

### Forearm Supinator Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Arm extended, palm up
2. Use other hand to rotate toward pronation
3. Hold 30 seconds

## Strengthening Exercises

### Isometric Strengthening

**Isometric Supination**
1. Elbow at side, palm up
2. Place other hand on palm
3. Try to rotate outward against resistance
4. No movement occurs
5. Hold 5-10 seconds
6. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Pronation**
1. Elbow at side, palm down
2. Place other hand on back of hand
3. Try to rotate inward against resistance
4. Hold 5-10 seconds
5. 10 repetitions

**Isometric Wrist Flexion/Extension**
1. Place palm against underside of table
2. Push up gently (flexion resistance)
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. Place back of hand against table
5. Push up (extension resistance)
6. 10 reps each

### Dynamic Strengthening

**Resistance Band Supination/Pronation**
1. Hold band, elbow at side
2. Anchor band under foot
3. Rotate forearm against resistance
4. 2-3 sets of 15 repetitions
5. Both directions

**Wrist Curls with Weight**
1. Light weight (1-2 lbs initially)
2. Forearm supported, wrist at edge
3. Curl wrist up and down
4. 2-3 sets of 15
5. Palm up (flexion) and palm down (extension)

**Deviation with Weight**
1. Hold weight (like hammer)
2. Lift by moving wrist toward thumb (radial)
3. Lower slowly
4. 2-3 sets of 15

**Caution with ulnar deviation** — this directly loads the TFCC.

### Forearm Rotation with Resistance

**Hammer Rotations**
1. Hold hammer or weighted bar
2. Rotate forearm fully both directions
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Resistance Band Rotation**
1. Band anchored at waist height
2. Rotate forearm against resistance
3. 2-3 sets of 15 each direction

## Grip Strengthening

### Neutral Grip Exercises

Start with neutral wrist position (thumb up).

**Tennis Ball Squeeze**
1. Hold ball with neutral wrist
2. Squeeze firmly
3. Hold 5 seconds
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Hand Gripper**
1. Use gripper with neutral wrist
2. 2-3 sets of 10-15

### Progressed Grip

**Putty Exercises**
1. Squeeze and manipulate putty
2. Various grips (full, pinch)
3. 2-3 minutes

## Ulnar Wrist Stability Exercises

### DRUJ Stability

**Press-Up (Modified)**
1. Hands on table, partial weight
2. Maintain neutral wrist
3. Hold 10-30 seconds
4. Progress to more weight as tolerated

**Quadruped Position**
1. On hands and knees
2. Shift weight gently
3. Progress: lift opposite arm/leg
4. Challenges wrist stability

### Dart Thrower's Motion

This diagonal movement is often well-tolerated with TFCC injuries.

**How to do it:**
1. Start with wrist extended and radially deviated (thumb up and back)
2. Move to flexed and ulnar deviated (pinky down and forward)
3. Like throwing a dart
4. 10-15 repetitions

## Functional Progression

### Weight-Bearing Progression

**Level 1:** Wall push-ups (minimal wrist load)
**Level 2:** Elevated push-ups (hands on counter)
**Level 3:** Push-ups on knees
**Level 4:** Full push-ups
**Level 5:** Unstable surfaces

**Progress only when current level is pain-free.**

### Twisting Activities

**Light twisting:**
- Turning pages
- Light doorknobs
- Opening loose lids

**Progress to:**
- Opening jars (with assistive devices first)
- Firmer knobs
- Tool use

## Sport-Specific Rehabilitation

### Racquet Sports
1. Grip strengthening first
2. Practice swings without ball
3. Light rallying
4. Gradual return to play
5. Consider technique analysis

### Weightlifting
1. Avoid Olympic lifts initially
2. Use wrist wraps when returning
3. Start with machines and cables
4. Progress to free weights
5. Watch for symptoms

### Yoga/Weight-Bearing
1. Use modifications (fists instead of flat hands)
2. Use wedges to reduce wrist extension
3. Limit time in weight-bearing poses
4. Progress gradually

## Sample Daily Program

### Week 1-3 (Acute/Protection)
- Rest from aggravating activities
- Ice as needed
- Gentle ROM: 2x daily, 10 reps each
- Splint during activities

### Week 4-6 (Mobility)
**Morning:**
1. ROM exercises: 15 reps each
2. Stretches: 30 sec each
3. Isometric strengthening: 10 reps

**Evening:**
1. ROM exercises: 15 reps
2. Stretches: 30 sec each
3. Isometrics: 10 reps

### Week 7-10 (Strengthening)
**3-4 times per week:**
1. Stretches: 5 min
2. Resistance band rotation: 2x15
3. Light wrist curls: 2x15
4. Grip exercises: 2-3 min
5. Dart thrower motion: 15 reps

### Week 10+ (Return to Function)
- Continue strengthening
- Add weight-bearing progressively
- Sport-specific training
- Gradual return to activities

## When Conservative Treatment Fails

**Consider surgical consultation if:**
- Symptoms persist beyond 3-6 months
- Instability doesn't improve
- Unable to perform daily activities
- Specific tear pattern needs repair

**Surgical options:**
- Arthroscopic debridement (clean up)
- TFCC repair
- Ulnar shortening osteotomy (if ulnar positive variance)
- DRUJ stabilization procedures

**Post-surgical rehabilitation** follows similar phases but with surgeon-specific protocols.

## Preventing Re-Injury

- Continue forearm strengthening
- Warm up before activity
- Use wrist support for high-demand activities
- Avoid extreme positions
- Listen to pain signals
- Maintain grip strength

## Key Takeaways

1. **Rotation is key** — pronation/supination exercises are essential
2. **Protect early** — splinting and activity modification
3. **Isometrics first** — safe strengthening progression
4. **Progress weight-bearing gradually** — build tolerance slowly
5. **Dart thrower motion** — often well-tolerated diagonal movement
6. **Grip strengthening important** — functional recovery
7. **Be patient** — TFCC injuries heal slowly (3-6 months)
8. **Seek help if not improving** — may need imaging or surgery

TFCC injuries can be frustrating due to slow healing, but consistent rehabilitation usually leads to significant improvement. Work with your healthcare team, follow the phases, and you can return to the activities you enjoy.
