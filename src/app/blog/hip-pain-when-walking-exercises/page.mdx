Hip pain that shows up when you walk is frustrating because walking is fundamental to daily life. The good news: most causes of walking-related hip pain respond well to the right exercises.

Let's identify what's causing your pain and give you the tools to fix it.

## Common Causes of Hip Pain When Walking

### Hip Osteoarthritis

**What it feels like:** Deep, aching pain in the groin or front of hip. Often worse in the morning and after sitting. May improve slightly with gentle movement, then worsen with prolonged walking.

**Key signs:**
- Stiffness, especially in the morning
- Reduced range of motion
- Grinding or clicking sensation
- Pain during weight-bearing

### Hip Bursitis (Trochanteric Bursitis)

**What it feels like:** Pain on the outside of the hip, often sharp. Worse when lying on that side at night. Aggravated by stairs and prolonged walking.

**Key signs:**
- Tenderness directly over the outside hip bone
- Pain worse when crossing legs
- Pain lying on affected side

### Hip Flexor Strain/Tendinitis

**What it feels like:** Pain in the front of the hip, often into the upper thigh. Worse when lifting the leg, climbing stairs, or walking uphill.

**Key signs:**
- Pain when bringing knee toward chest
- Discomfort getting up from sitting
- Possible snapping sensation

### Gluteal Tendinopathy

**What it feels like:** Pain on the side of the hip, similar to bursitis (often occurs together). May refer down the outer thigh.

**Key signs:**
- Pain with single-leg standing
- Difficulty climbing stairs
- Pain lying on affected side

### IT Band Syndrome

**What it feels like:** Pain on the outside of hip or knee. Often starts after certain distance of walking/running.

**Key signs:**
- Predictable onset (same distance each time)
- Tightness along outer thigh
- Relief when stopping

### Referred Pain from Lower Back

**What it feels like:** Hip pain that doesn't quite fit typical patterns. May be accompanied by back stiffness.

**Key signs:**
- Pain changes with back position
- Difficulty pinpointing exact location
- May have tingling or numbness

## Phase 1: Reduce Inflammation and Pain

Before strengthening, address acute pain with these gentle approaches.

### Rest from Aggravating Activities

You don't need complete rest, but modify what increases pain. If walking a mile causes pain, walk half a mile. Find your threshold.

### Ice or Heat

- **Acute pain (first 72 hours):** Ice for 15-20 minutes
- **Chronic pain:** Heat often feels better and reduces stiffness
- **Experiment:** See which works for you

### Gentle Range of Motion

Movement is medicine, even during pain. These shouldn't hurt.

**Supine hip circles:**
- Lie on back
- Bend one knee toward chest
- Make small circles with knee
- 10 each direction, each hip

**Pelvic tilts:**
- Lie on back, knees bent
- Gently flatten lower back into floor
- Then gently arch
- 15-20 repetitions

**Sidelying leg slides:**
- Lie on unaffected side
- Slide top leg forward and back
- Keep movement pain-free
- 15 repetitions

## Phase 2: Improve Hip Mobility

Once acute pain settles, restore range of motion.

### Hip Flexor Stretch

Tight hip flexors are almost universal in people with hip pain.

**Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch:**
- Kneel on affected side
- Other foot in front, knee at 90°
- Tuck your pelvis under (flatten lower back)
- Lean forward slightly
- Hold 30 seconds, repeat 2-3 times

**Key:** The pelvic tuck is crucial—without it, you're just stretching your back.

### Piriformis Stretch

Often tight in hip pain, especially with pain that refers down the leg.

**Figure-4 stretch:**
- Lie on back
- Cross affected ankle over opposite knee
- Pull bottom knee toward chest
- Hold 30 seconds, repeat 2-3 times

### 90-90 Hip Stretch

Addresses both internal and external rotation.

- Sit with front leg bent 90° in front of you
- Back leg bent 90° behind you
- Sit tall, lean forward over front leg
- Hold 30 seconds
- Switch sides

### Hip Flexor with Rotation

**World's greatest stretch:**
- Lunge position, back knee on floor
- Front foot outside front hand
- Reach same-side hand to ceiling, rotate
- Hold 20 seconds each side

### Standing Hip Circles

- Stand on one leg (hold wall for support)
- Make circles with other hip
- 10 each direction, each leg
- Focus on moving through full range

## Phase 3: Strengthen Key Muscles

This is where lasting improvement happens. Weak hip muscles are the root cause of most walking-related hip pain.

### Glute Bridges

Foundation exercise for hip strength.

- Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat
- Squeeze glutes and lift hips toward ceiling
- Hold 2 seconds at top
- Lower slowly
- 3 sets of 15

**Progression:** Single-leg bridges (bend one knee to chest, bridge with other leg)

### Clamshells

Crucial for lateral hip stability.

- Lie on side, knees bent 90°
- Keep feet together
- Lift top knee toward ceiling (like opening a clamshell)
- Don't let hips roll back
- 3 sets of 15 each side

**Progression:** Add resistance band around knees

### Side-Lying Hip Abduction

- Lie on side, bottom leg bent for stability
- Keep top leg straight
- Lift top leg toward ceiling (leading with heel, not toe)
- Lower slowly
- 3 sets of 15 each side

**Key:** Don't lift so high that your hip rolls forward

### Standing Hip Abduction

- Stand holding wall or chair
- Keep standing leg slightly bent
- Lift other leg out to side
- Control the lowering
- 3 sets of 12 each side

**Progression:** Add ankle weight or resistance band

### Step-Ups

Critical for walking function.

- Use a step or sturdy platform (4-8 inches to start)
- Step up with affected leg
- Focus on pushing through the heel
- Lower slowly with control
- 3 sets of 10 each leg

**Progression:** Increase step height, add hand weights

### Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

Best exercise for hip stability during walking.

- Stand on one leg
- Hinge forward at hip, reaching opposite hand toward floor
- Keep back flat
- Return to standing
- 3 sets of 8 each side

**Modification:** Hold wall or chair for balance initially

### Monster Walks

- Place resistance band around ankles (or above knees)
- Slight squat position
- Walk sideways, keeping tension on band
- 10 steps each direction, 3 sets

## Condition-Specific Exercises

### For Hip Osteoarthritis

**Priorities:**
1. Maintain range of motion (daily gentle movement)
2. Strengthen without aggravating (limit deep squats, high-impact)
3. Water exercise is excellent (reduces joint stress)

**Best exercises:**
- Stationary cycling (maintains motion with minimal stress)
- Pool walking
- Gentle bridges
- Seated leg extensions
- Standing hip abduction

**Avoid:** Deep lunges, running, high-impact activities during flares

### For Hip Bursitis

**Priorities:**
1. Avoid compression of bursa (don't lie on it, avoid deep adduction)
2. Strengthen glutes to reduce stress on bursa
3. Stretch ITB carefully

**Best exercises:**
- Clamshells (reduce TFL demand)
- Side-lying abduction (only to neutral, not past midline)
- Bridges
- Modified stretching (avoid crossing affected leg deeply)

**Avoid:** Lying on affected side, deep adductor stretches

### For Hip Flexor Issues

**Priorities:**
1. Stretch hip flexors (often shortened from sitting)
2. Strengthen core to reduce hip flexor demand
3. Eccentric strengthening for tendinitis

**Best exercises:**
- Hip flexor stretches (half-kneeling)
- Dead bugs (core without hip flexor overuse)
- Slow leg lowers (eccentric hip flexor work)
- Glute strengthening (improve hip extension)

**Avoid:** Sit-ups, excessive stair climbing during acute phase

### For Gluteal Tendinopathy

**Priorities:**
1. Reduce compression (avoid side-lying on affected side, crossing legs)
2. Isometric loading initially
3. Progress slowly to dynamic exercises

**Best exercises:**

**Early phase:**
- Isometric hip abduction against wall (hold 30-45 seconds)
- Bridges
- Standing hip hitch (stand on step, let free leg drop, hitch hip to lift)

**Later phase:**
- Side-lying abduction (not past neutral)
- Single-leg bridges
- Step-ups

## Complete Walking Rehab Program

### Week 1-2: Foundation

**Daily:**
- Gentle mobility (hip circles, pelvic tilts)
- Hip flexor stretch: 2×30 seconds each
- Figure-4 stretch: 2×30 seconds each

**Every other day:**
- Bridges: 2×12
- Clamshells: 2×12 each
- Standing hip abduction: 2×10 each

**Walking:** Pain-free distance only. If you can walk 10 minutes without increased pain, walk 10 minutes.

### Week 3-4: Build Strength

**Daily:**
- Mobility routine (5 minutes)

**3 times per week:**
- Bridges: 3×15
- Clamshells with band: 3×12 each
- Side-lying abduction: 3×12 each
- Monster walks: 2×10 each direction
- Step-ups: 2×10 each (low step)

**Walking:** Gradually increase by 10-15% per week if pain allows

### Week 5-8: Progress

**3 times per week:**
- Single-leg bridges: 3×10 each
- Banded clamshells: 3×15 each
- Single-leg Romanian deadlift: 3×8 each
- Step-ups (higher step): 3×10 each
- Monster walks: 3×15 each direction

**Walking:** Continue gradual progression toward goal distance

## Tips for Walking with Hip Pain

### Gait Modifications

- **Shorten your stride:** Overstriding increases hip stress
- **Walk slower initially:** Speed can come back as you strengthen
- **Avoid uneven surfaces:** Start on flat, even ground
- **Use a cane correctly:** If needed, in the opposite hand of painful hip

### Walking Surface Matters

- **Best:** Flat, even surfaces (track, sidewalk, treadmill)
- **Worse:** Hills, uneven trails, soft sand
- **Pool walking:** Excellent alternative during flares

### When to Push, When to Back Off

**Keep walking if:**
- Pain stays below 3/10 during activity
- Pain settles within 30 minutes of stopping
- No increased pain or stiffness next morning

**Back off if:**
- Pain above 5/10 during walking
- Pain lasts hours after activity
- Increased stiffness or pain next day

## When to See a Professional

Get evaluated if:

- Pain persists longer than 6 weeks despite exercises
- Significant limping that doesn't improve
- Night pain that wakes you
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness
- Hip "gives way" or feels unstable
- Sudden onset of severe pain

## The Path Forward

Hip pain when walking is common but treatable. The formula is straightforward:

1. **Reduce inflammation** (modify activity, ice/heat)
2. **Restore mobility** (stretching, gentle movement)
3. **Build strength** (the real solution for most hip pain)
4. **Progress walking gradually** (let strength catch up to demands)

Most people see significant improvement in 4-8 weeks of consistent exercise. Stay patient, stay consistent, and you'll walk comfortably again.
