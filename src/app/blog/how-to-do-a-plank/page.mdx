# How to Do a Plank: Build Core Strength with Perfect Form

The plank looks simple—just holding a position. But proper plank form requires full-body tension and builds serious core strength. Here's how to do it right and progress to harder variations.

## The Perfect Plank

### Forearm Plank (Standard)

**Setup:**
- Forearms on floor, elbows directly under shoulders
- Fists together or palms flat
- Toes tucked, balls of feet on floor
- Body in straight line from head to heels

**Key points:**
- Core braced (like preparing for a punch)
- Glutes squeezed
- Neutral spine (no sagging or piking)
- Shoulders away from ears
- Look at floor between hands

### High Plank (Push-Up Position)

**Setup:**
- Hands directly under shoulders
- Arms straight
- Same body position as forearm plank
- Balls of feet on floor

**When to use:**
- Easier to hold for many people
- Transition for push-ups
- Different shoulder demand

## Common Plank Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Hips Too High

**The problem:** Butt sticking up in the air

**Why it's bad:**
- Reduces core engagement
- Makes it easier (cheating)
- Not training the correct pattern

**The fix:**
- Lower hips to body line
- Engage glutes
- Check from the side

### Mistake 2: Hips Sagging

**The problem:** Hips drop toward floor

**Why it's bad:**
- Strains lower back
- Means core isn't engaged
- Common sign of fatigue

**The fix:**
- Squeeze core harder
- Tuck pelvis slightly
- End set if you can't maintain
- Use easier variation

### Mistake 3: Holding Breath

**The problem:** Not breathing during hold

**Why it's bad:**
- Reduces endurance
- Increases blood pressure
- Unsustainable

**The fix:**
- Breathe normally
- Shallow breaths are okay
- Don't hold breath

### Mistake 4: Head Position

**The problem:** Looking up or letting head hang

**Why it's bad:**
- Neck strain
- Breaks spinal alignment

**The fix:**
- Neutral neck
- Look at floor between hands
- Chin slightly tucked

### Mistake 5: Shoulders Up by Ears

**The problem:** Shrugging shoulders

**Why it's bad:**
- Neck and shoulder tension
- Not using back muscles properly

**The fix:**
- Press shoulders down
- Think "proud chest"
- Active shoulders

### Mistake 6: Only Focusing on Time

**The problem:** Holding bad form just to hit time goal

**Why it's bad:**
- Defeats the purpose
- Reinforces bad patterns
- Risk of injury

**The fix:**
- Form first, time second
- End set when form breaks
- Quality over quantity

## Plank Progressions

### Beginner Progressions

**If standard plank is too hard:**

**Incline Plank:**
- Forearms on bench or step
- Reduces load significantly
- Great starting point
- Lower incline as you progress

**Knee Plank:**
- Standard position but on knees
- Straight line from knees to head
- Master before full plank
- Goal: 30-45 seconds with good form

**Wall Plank:**
- Hands on wall
- Body at angle
- Very easy starting point

### Intermediate Progressions

**Once you can hold 60+ seconds:**

**Plank with Arm Reach:**
- Extend one arm forward
- Hold 3-5 seconds
- Return, switch arms
- Anti-rotation challenge

**Plank with Leg Lift:**
- Lift one leg slightly
- Hold 3-5 seconds
- Switch legs
- Increases core demand

**Plank Shoulder Taps:**
- High plank position
- Tap opposite shoulder
- Alternate hands
- Resist rotation

**Side Plank:**
- On one forearm, feet stacked
- Body forms straight line
- Targets obliques
- Hold each side

### Advanced Progressions

**For those who've mastered the basics:**

**Long-Lever Plank:**
- Elbows further forward than shoulders
- Creates longer lever
- Dramatically harder
- Build time gradually

**Weighted Plank:**
- Partner places weight on back
- Or wear weight vest
- Progressive overload
- Don't compromise form

**RKC Plank:**
- Maximum full-body tension
- Squeeze everything as hard as possible
- Shorter holds (10-20 seconds)
- Incredibly intense

**Plank on Stability Ball:**
- Forearms on ball
- Requires constant stabilization
- Core works overtime

**Body Saw:**
- Forearm plank position
- Rock body forward and back
- Increases range of motion

## Side Plank Form

### Basic Side Plank

**Setup:**
- On one forearm, elbow under shoulder
- Feet stacked (or staggered for easier)
- Hips lifted, body straight
- Top arm on hip or extended up

**Key points:**
- Don't let hips sag
- Stack shoulders
- Straight line from head to feet
- Breathe normally

### Side Plank Progressions

**Easier:**
- Bottom knee bent and on floor
- Top leg extended

**Harder:**
- Top leg raised
- Hip dips (lower and lift hips)
- Rotation (thread the needle)
- Copenhagen plank (top leg on bench)

### Plank Challenge

**Plank ladder:**
- 10 sec hold, 10 sec rest
- 20 sec hold, 10 sec rest
- 30 sec hold, 10 sec rest
- 40 sec hold, 10 sec rest
- 50 sec hold, 10 sec rest
- 60 sec hold, done

## How Long Should You Hold a Plank?

### Time Guidelines

**Quality vs. Quantity:**
- A 30-second perfect plank > 2-minute sloppy plank
- Form is the priority
- End when form breaks

**General targets:**
- Beginner: 20-30 seconds
- Intermediate: 45-60 seconds
- Advanced: 60-90 seconds

**Beyond 90 seconds?**
- Progress to harder variations instead
- Long holds have diminishing returns
- Challenge yourself differently

### When to Progress

**Move to harder variation when:**
- Current hold is easy for 60 seconds
- Form stays perfect throughout
- You're not challenged anymore

## Benefits of Planks

### What Planks Train

**Core stabilization:**
- Resisting spinal extension
- Anti-movement strength
- Functional core training

**Full body engagement:**
- Shoulders, chest, back
- Glutes, quads
- Not just "abs"

### Why Planks Matter

- Protects lower back
- Improves posture
- Better performance in other exercises
- Functional strength
- No equipment needed

## The Bottom Line

Perfect plank form:
- Body in straight line (no sagging or piking)
- Core braced, glutes squeezed
- Shoulders down, neutral neck
- Breathe normally
- Quality over time

Progression path:
1. Incline or knee plank
2. Standard forearm plank
3. Add arm reaches or leg lifts
4. Side planks
5. Advanced variations (long-lever, RKC)

The plank is a foundational core exercise. Master it with perfect form, then progress to challenging variations. A few minutes of plank work several times per week will build core strength that transfers to everything else you do.

Don't chase time—chase tension and perfect form.
