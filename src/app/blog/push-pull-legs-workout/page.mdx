# Push Pull Legs Workout: The Complete PPL Training Guide

Looking for a workout split that's logical, effective, and endlessly customizable? Push Pull Legs (PPL) might be the perfect fit. This training split organizes exercises by movement pattern, allowing optimal recovery while hitting each muscle group with ideal frequency.

## What is Push Pull Legs?

Push Pull Legs divides your training into three workout types based on movement patterns:

**Push Day:** Chest, shoulders, triceps (all pushing movements)
**Pull Day:** Back, biceps, rear delts (all pulling movements)
**Legs Day:** Quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves

### Why PPL Works

- **Logical grouping:** Muscles that work together train together
- **Built-in recovery:** Push muscles rest on pull days and vice versa
- **Flexible frequency:** Run it 3, 4, 5, or 6 days per week
- **Balanced development:** Equal attention to all muscle groups
- **Scalable:** Works for beginners through advanced lifters

### The Movement Logic

When you bench press, your chest, front delts, and triceps all work together. By training them on the same day, you:
- Get synergy from compound movements
- Allow complete recovery before training them again
- Avoid overlap that impairs recovery

## PPL Training Frequencies

### 3-Day PPL (Beginner/Busy Schedule)

**Schedule:**
- Monday: Push
- Wednesday: Pull
- Friday: Legs

**Pros:** Manageable time commitment, good recovery
**Cons:** Each muscle only trained once per week (suboptimal for growth)

### 4-Day PPL (Balanced)

**Schedule:**
- Monday: Push
- Tuesday: Pull
- Thursday: Legs
- Friday: Push
- (Next week starts with Pull)

**Pros:** Better frequency than 3-day, still reasonable recovery
**Cons:** Uneven weekly structure

### 6-Day PPL (Classic)

**Schedule:**
- Monday: Push
- Tuesday: Pull
- Wednesday: Legs
- Thursday: Push
- Friday: Pull
- Saturday: Legs
- Sunday: Rest

**Pros:** Optimal 2x per week frequency, maximum growth stimulus
**Cons:** Significant time commitment, requires good recovery

### 5-Day PPL (Hybrid)

**Schedule:**
- Week 1: Push, Pull, Legs, Push, Pull
- Week 2: Legs, Push, Pull, Legs, Push
- Continue rotating...

**Pros:** More sustainable than 6-day, still good frequency
**Cons:** Slightly uneven muscle group frequency

## Progression Strategies

### For Compound Lifts

**Double Progression:**
1. Set rep ranges (e.g., 6-8 reps)
2. Use same weight until hitting top of range on all sets
3. Add weight, drop to bottom of range
4. Repeat

**Example:**
- Week 1: 185 lbs × 6, 6, 6, 5
- Week 2: 185 lbs × 7, 7, 6, 6
- Week 3: 185 lbs × 8, 8, 7, 7
- Week 4: 185 lbs × 8, 8, 8, 8 ✓
- Week 5: 190 lbs × 6, 6, 6, 5

### For Isolation Lifts

Focus on:
- Hitting rep targets before adding weight
- Quality contractions over heavy weight
- Slight weekly increases (2.5-5 lbs)

## Common PPL Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Neglecting Pull Days

**The Problem:** More effort on chest than back
**The Fix:** Equal or greater volume on pull days; back thickness matters

### Mistake 2: Too Much Arm Work

**The Problem:** 6-8 sets of curls per session
**The Fix:** Arms get hit during compounds; 4-6 direct sets per muscle is plenty

### Mistake 3: Skipping Leg Day

**The Problem:** "I'll do legs next week"
**The Fix:** Legs first if motivation is an issue; no skipping

### Mistake 4: Same Workout Both Days

**The Problem:** Identical push days, identical pull days
**The Fix:** Vary exercises, rep ranges, or emphasis between sessions

### Mistake 5: Not Enough Recovery

**The Problem:** Running 6-day PPL without adequate sleep/nutrition
**The Fix:** If recovery suffers, drop to 4-5 days or improve recovery factors

## PPL for Different Goals

### PPL for Strength

**Modifications:**
- Start each day with heavy compound (3-5 reps)
- Reduce isolation volume
- Longer rest periods (3-5 minutes on compounds)
- Focus on progressive overload

**Example Push Day:**
- Bench Press: 5×3 at 85%
- Overhead Press: 4×5 at 80%
- Incline DB Press: 3×6-8
- Lateral Raise: 3×12-15
- Tricep Work: 3×8-10

### PPL for Hypertrophy

**Modifications:**
- Moderate rep ranges (8-12 primary)
- Higher volume (more sets per muscle)
- Shorter rest (60-90 seconds for isolation)
- Include intensity techniques (drop sets, rest-pause)

### PPL for Fat Loss

**Modifications:**
- Maintain training intensity (don't reduce weights)
- Slightly reduce volume (recovery is impaired in deficit)
- Add conditioning on off days or after lifting
- Keep protein high

## Customizing Your PPL

### Adding Weak Point Training

Have a lagging body part? Add volume strategically:

**Weak Chest:** Add flye variation to pull day (won't interfere)
**Weak Back:** Add pull-aparts or band work to push day
**Weak Arms:** Add one extra set per session or arm-focused day

### Adjusting for Recovery

**Signs you need less volume:**
- Strength declining
- Persistent soreness
- Poor sleep despite fatigue
- Loss of motivation

**Adjustments:**
- Remove 1-2 sets per muscle group
- Take an extra rest day
- Reduce training days (6 → 5 or 4)

### Periodization

**4-Week Block Example:**
- Week 1: Moderate volume, moderate intensity
- Week 2: High volume, moderate intensity
- Week 3: Moderate volume, high intensity
- Week 4: Deload (50% volume)

## The Bottom Line

Push Pull Legs is one of the most versatile and effective training splits available. Its logical organization allows for optimal recovery, the flexible frequency accommodates any schedule, and the structure works for beginners through advanced lifters.

Start with 3-4 days per week if you're new to PPL. Master the movement patterns, establish recovery habits, and build consistency. As you adapt, consider moving to a 6-day program for maximum growth potential.

The best program is one you'll follow consistently. PPL's clear structure and logical design make it easy to stick with long-term—and that consistency is what builds impressive results.
