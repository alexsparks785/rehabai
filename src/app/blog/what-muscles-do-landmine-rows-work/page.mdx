## The Landmine Row: Angled Back Building

Landmine rows—performed with one end of a barbell anchored in a corner or landmine attachment—offer a unique pulling angle that builds back thickness in ways traditional rows can't match. The arc of motion and neutral grip create a joint-friendly option that still delivers serious results.

## Primary Muscles Targeted

### Latissimus Dorsi (High Activation)

Your lats are the primary movers in landmine rows:

- **Upper lat fibers**: Engaged at peak contraction
- **Lower lat fibers**: Work through the full range
- **Lat width and thickness**: Both developed

The angled pull hits the lats differently than horizontal or vertical pulls.

### Rhomboids (Very High)

The rhomboids work intensely to retract your shoulder blades:

- **Rhomboid major**: Primary scapular retraction
- **Rhomboid minor**: Assists retraction

The arc of motion in landmine rows creates excellent rhomboid activation at the top of each rep.

### Middle and Lower Trapezius (High)

Your mid-back traps work throughout the movement:

- **Middle trapezius**: Scapular retraction
- **Lower trapezius**: Scapular depression and control

These muscles are often underdeveloped compared to the upper traps, and landmine rows address this.

### Rear Deltoids (Moderate-High)

Your rear delts assist the pulling motion:

- **Posterior deltoid**: Shoulder extension
- Important for complete shoulder development

### Biceps (Secondary but Significant)

Your biceps work as synergists:

- **Biceps brachii**: Elbow flexion
- **Brachialis**: Assists elbow flexion
- **Brachioradialis**: Forearm involvement

The neutral or semi-pronated grip shifts some work to the brachialis.

## Secondary Muscles Engaged

### Erector Spinae

Your spinal erectors maintain posture:

- **Iliocostalis**: Spine stability
- **Longissimus**: Holds position
- **Spinalis**: Maintains neutral spine

### Core Musculature

Your core stabilizes throughout:

- **Obliques**: Anti-rotation (especially single-arm versions)
- **Transverse abdominis**: Deep stability
- **Rectus abdominis**: Prevents flexion

### Forearms and Grip

Grip strength is challenged:

- **Finger flexors**: Hold the bar
- **Wrist stabilizers**: Control the implement

## Why Landmine Rows Are Unique

### The Arc of Motion

Unlike cable rows or barbell rows (straight line), landmine rows follow an arc. This:

- Changes the resistance curve
- Reduces stress at the bottom position
- Increases tension at the top
- Feels more natural for many lifters

### Joint-Friendly Mechanics

The angled setup is easier on:

- **Shoulders**: Neutral grip reduces impingement risk
- **Lower back**: More upright postures possible
- **Wrists**: Natural hand position

### Versatility

One landmine setup enables dozens of exercises—rows are just the beginning.

## Landmine Row Variations

### Two-Arm Landmine Row (T-Bar Style)

- Straddle the bar, grip with both hands
- Most similar to traditional T-bar rows
- Allows heaviest loads

### Single-Arm Landmine Row

- Stand to the side, row with one arm
- Greater range of motion
- Better lat stretch
- Anti-rotation core demand

### Meadows Row

Named after bodybuilder John Meadows:

- Perpendicular stance to the bar
- Overhand grip on the fat end
- Extreme lat stretch
- Pull toward hip, not chest

### Chest-Supported Landmine Row

- Use an incline bench for support
- Eliminates lower back involvement
- Pure back isolation

## Landmine Rows vs Other Rowing Exercises

| Aspect | Landmine Row | Barbell Row | Cable Row | Dumbbell Row |
|--------|--------------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| Path of motion | Arc | Straight | Straight | Free |
| Lower back demand | Low-moderate | High | Low | Moderate |
| Grip options | Neutral/varied | Pronated | Varied | Neutral |
| Setup complexity | Moderate | Low | Low | Low |
| Range of motion | Very high | Moderate | High | Very high |
| Anti-rotation | High (1-arm) | Low | Low | Moderate |

## How to Perform Landmine Rows Correctly

### Setup (Two-Arm Version)

1. Anchor barbell in corner or landmine attachment
2. Load the free end with weight
3. Straddle the bar, facing the anchor point
4. Use a V-handle or grip the bar directly below the plates
5. Bend at hips, maintain flat back
6. Start with arms extended

### The Movement

1. Initiate pull by retracting shoulder blades
2. Drive elbows back toward hips
3. Pull bar toward lower chest/upper abdomen
4. Squeeze back muscles at the top
5. Lower under control, feeling the stretch
6. Reset shoulder blades before next rep

### Setup (Single-Arm Version)

1. Stand perpendicular to the bar
2. Stagger stance for balance
3. Grip near the weighted end
4. Row toward your hip, not your chest
5. Rotate torso slightly as you pull

## Common Form Errors

- **Using momentum**: Jerking the weight defeats the purpose
- **Rounding the back**: Maintain neutral spine
- **Pulling too high**: Row to abdomen, not chest
- **Forgetting scapular retraction**: Lead with shoulder blades
- **Cutting range short**: Get full stretch at bottom

## Programming Landmine Rows

### For Back Thickness

- 8-12 reps per set
- 3-4 sets
- Focus on squeeze at top
- Control the eccentric

### For Strength

- 5-8 reps per set
- 4-5 sets
- Heavier loads
- Full rest between sets

### As a Finisher

- 15-20 reps
- 2-3 sets
- Lighter weight
- Pump and burn focus

### Workout Integration

**Back Day Option A:**
1. Pull-ups: 4 x 6-8
2. Landmine Rows: 4 x 10
3. Single-Arm Cable Rows: 3 x 12
4. Face Pulls: 3 x 15

**Back Day Option B:**
1. Barbell Rows: 4 x 6
2. Lat Pulldowns: 3 x 10
3. Meadows Rows: 3 x 12 each arm
4. Reverse Flyes: 3 x 15

## Setting Up a Landmine Without Equipment

No landmine attachment? No problem:

1. **Corner method**: Wedge barbell into a corner (place a towel to protect walls)
2. **Heavy dumbbell**: Place collar end inside the handle of a heavy dumbbell on the floor
3. **Weight plate**: Put collar through the hole of a heavy plate on the ground

## Who Should Do Landmine Rows?

### Ideal For

- Those with lower back sensitivity
- Lifters wanting variety in back training
- People who find barbell rows uncomfortable
- Athletes needing rotational strength (single-arm)
- Home gym owners with limited equipment

### Use Caution If

- You have shoulder impingement (test grip positions)
- You're unable to maintain hip hinge position
- Your gym doesn't allow corner setups (check first)

5. Face Pulls: 3 x 15

## The Bottom Line

Landmine rows target your lats, rhomboids, mid-traps, rear delts, and biceps with a unique arc of motion that's easier on your joints than traditional barbell rows. The setup is versatile, allowing dozens of variations from a single anchor point.

Add landmine rows to your program when you want to build back thickness with a joint-friendly movement, or when you need variety from standard rowing patterns. The single-arm versions are particularly valuable for addressing imbalances and building anti-rotation core strength.

*Note: This guide provides general information about anatomy and exercise. Individual anatomy varies. Start with lighter weights to master the movement pattern before adding load.*
