Good form isn't about looking perfect—it's about moving safely and effectively. Bad form can lead to injury and reduced results. Good form protects your joints and maximizes muscle activation.

Here are the most common mistakes in fundamental exercises and exactly how to fix them.

## Why Form Matters

### Safety

Poor form puts stress on joints, ligaments, and discs instead of muscles. This leads to:
- Acute injuries
- Overuse injuries
- Chronic pain

### Effectiveness

Bad form often means:
- Wrong muscles doing the work
- Reduced range of motion
- Less muscle activation
- Slower progress

### Long-Term Success

Good form is sustainable form. You can train for decades with proper technique.

## Squat Form

### Common Mistakes

**1. Knees Caving In**
- Knees collapse toward each other
- Stresses knee ligaments
- Usually means weak glutes

**Fix:**
- Consciously push knees out
- "Screw feet into floor" (external rotation)
- Strengthen glutes with clamshells, band walks
- Use a resistance band above knees for feedback

**2. Excessive Forward Lean**
- Torso falls forward
- Puts stress on lower back
- Often from tight ankles or weak quads

**Fix:**
- Keep chest up ("proud chest")
- Work on ankle mobility
- Try heel elevation (small plates under heels)
- Goblet squat helps learn upright torso

**3. Heels Rising**
- Weight shifts to toes
- Less glute activation
- Usually ankle mobility limitation

**Fix:**
- Drive through heels
- Work on ankle flexibility
- Elevate heels slightly if needed
- Can also try wider stance

**4. Butt Wink (Lower Back Rounding)**
- Lower back rounds at bottom of squat
- Puts discs at risk under load
- Often flexibility or motor control issue

**Fix:**
- Don't go as deep
- Work on hip flexor and hamstring flexibility
- Brace core throughout
- Find your comfortable depth

**5. Not Reaching Depth**
- Stopping well above parallel
- Reduces muscle activation
- Sometimes fear or mobility limitation

**Fix:**
- Work on mobility gradually
- Use box squat to target depth
- Practice bodyweight squats to depth
- Some depth limitation is individual

### Perfect Squat Checklist

- Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider
- Toes slightly out (15-30°)
- Brace core before descending
- Initiate by pushing hips back AND bending knees
- Keep knees tracking over toes
- Chest up, back neutral
- Descend to comfortable depth
- Drive through whole foot to stand

## Push-Up Form

### Common Mistakes

**1. Sagging Hips**
- Hips drop toward floor
- Strains lower back
- Usually weak core

**Fix:**
- Squeeze glutes
- Engage core (belly button toward spine)
- Imagine a straight line from head to heels
- Modify to knees if needed

**2. Flaring Elbows**
- Elbows point straight out (90° from body)
- Stresses shoulder joint
- Common but not ideal

**Fix:**
- Keep elbows at 45° angle from body
- Think "arrow" not "T"
- Hands slightly wider than shoulders

**3. Head Dropping or Lifting**
- Looking down or straining neck up
- Creates neck strain
- Breaks body alignment

**Fix:**
- Keep head neutral (in line with spine)
- Look at floor about 6 inches ahead of hands
- Don't crane neck to look forward

**4. Partial Range of Motion**
- Not going low enough
- Reduces muscle activation
- Sometimes from weakness

**Fix:**
- Chest should nearly touch floor
- Full extension at top
- Use easier variation if you can't do full range
- Quality over quantity

**5. Hands Too Far Forward**
- Hands positioned ahead of shoulders
- Inefficient leverage
- Harder on shoulders

**Fix:**
- Hands should be directly under shoulders (at bottom)
- Wrists, elbows, shoulders aligned when viewed from side

### Perfect Push-Up Checklist

- Hands shoulder-width, fingers forward
- Arms fully extended at start
- Core tight, body straight
- Lower until chest nearly touches
- Elbows at 45° angle
- Press back up to full extension
- Keep head neutral throughout

## Deadlift Form (and Hip Hinge)

### Common Mistakes

**1. Rounding the Back**
- Lower back rounds during lift
- Major injury risk
- The most important thing to avoid

**Fix:**
- Keep chest up
- "Proud chest" cue
- Brace core hard
- If you can't maintain flat back, reduce weight
- Practice hip hinge pattern with dowel on back

**2. Bar Drifting Away**
- Bar moves away from body
- Increases load on back
- Inefficient

**Fix:**
- Keep bar in contact with legs throughout
- "Drag the bar up your legs"
- Arms hang straight down

**3. Hyperextending at Top**
- Leaning back excessively at top
- Stresses lower back
- Unnecessary

**Fix:**
- Stand tall, don't lean back
- Squeeze glutes at top
- Lock out by pushing hips forward, not by arching back

**4. Squatting the Deadlift**
- Dropping hips too low at start
- Turns it into a squat
- Bar moves forward around knees

**Fix:**
- Hips higher than knees at start
- Shoulders over or slightly in front of bar
- It's a hip hinge, not a squat

**5. Looking Up**
- Craning neck to look forward
- Strains cervical spine
- Creates spinal extension

**Fix:**
- Keep head neutral
- Eyes look at floor few feet ahead
- Neck in line with spine

### Perfect Deadlift Checklist

- Feet hip-width, bar over mid-foot
- Hinge at hips to grip bar
- Flat back, chest up
- Arms straight, shoulders over bar
- Brace core
- Drive through floor with legs
- Keep bar close to body
- Stand tall at top (don't lean back)
- Reverse the movement to lower

## Lunge Form

### Common Mistakes

**1. Knee Shooting Past Toe**
- Front knee goes way past toes
- Can stress knee joint
- Often from narrow stance

**Fix:**
- Take a longer stride
- Vertical shin angle is ideal
- Drop back knee toward floor, not forward

**2. Torso Falling Forward**
- Leaning forward during lunge
- Reduces glute activation
- Stresses lower back

**Fix:**
- Keep torso upright
- Engage core
- Look forward, not down

**3. Losing Balance**
- Wobbly, unstable
- Usually narrow base
- Weak hip stabilizers

**Fix:**
- Wider stance (hip-width, not inline)
- Use wall or chair for support initially
- Strengthen glutes and core

**4. Back Knee Not Dropping**
- Not going low enough
- Reduces range and effectiveness
- Sometimes fear of discomfort

**Fix:**
- Back knee should go toward floor
- Full range is more effective
- Use pad under knee if floor is hard

### Perfect Lunge Checklist

- Step into a stride length stance
- Feet hip-width apart (not inline)
- Lower by dropping back knee toward floor
- Front knee stays over ankle
- Torso stays upright
- Push through front foot to return
- Core engaged throughout

## Plank Form

### Common Mistakes

**1. Hips Too High (Piking)**
- Butt sticking up
- Reduces core work
- Easier but less effective

**Fix:**
- Lower hips to form straight line
- Think "flat back"
- Check in mirror or video

**2. Hips Sagging**
- Hips dropping toward floor
- Strains lower back
- Core isn't engaged

**Fix:**
- Squeeze glutes
- Draw belly button toward spine
- Modify to knees if you can't maintain
- Shorter holds with good form

**3. Head Position**
- Looking up or letting head hang
- Strains neck
- Breaks spinal alignment

**Fix:**
- Head neutral (in line with spine)
- Look at floor between hands
- Don't crane neck

**4. Holding Breath**
- Not breathing during plank
- Raises blood pressure
- Causes fatigue faster

**Fix:**
- Breathe normally throughout
- Practice breathing while braced

### Perfect Plank Checklist

- Forearms on floor, elbows under shoulders
- Body in straight line (head to heels)
- Core engaged (belly button to spine)
- Glutes squeezed
- Breathe normally
- Hold without losing form

## Row Form

### Common Mistakes

**1. Using Momentum**
- Swinging weight up
- Using body to "cheat"
- Reduces muscle work

**Fix:**
- Slow, controlled movement
- Pause at top of each rep
- Reduce weight if needed

**2. Not Squeezing at Top**
- Rushing through movement
- Missing full contraction
- Reduces effectiveness

**Fix:**
- Pull to ribs
- Squeeze shoulder blades together
- Hold 1 second at top

**3. Rounding Back**
- Spine rounds during movement
- Strains lower back
- Common with bent-over rows

**Fix:**
- Keep back flat
- Brace core
- Support yourself (one arm on bench)

**4. Only Using Arms**
- Not engaging back muscles
- Arms fatigue before back is worked

**Fix:**
- Initiate by pulling shoulder blade back
- Think "elbow to ceiling" or "elbow to hip"
- Feel the squeeze in your back

### Perfect Row Checklist

- Maintain flat back throughout
- Initiate with shoulder blade
- Pull weight to ribs/hip
- Squeeze shoulder blades at top
- Lower with control
- Keep core engaged

## General Form Principles

### The Universal Rules

1. **Control the weight:** Both directions matter
2. **Full range of motion:** When safely possible
3. **Neutral spine:** For most exercises
4. **Breathe:** Don't hold your breath
5. **Quality over quantity:** Perfect reps over many reps
6. **Appropriate load:** Can you maintain form? If not, reduce weight

### When Form Breaks Down

- Too much weight
- Too many reps
- Fatigue
- Weakness in stabilizers
- Mobility limitations

**Solution:** Reduce load, stop the set, or regress the exercise

### Learning Form

- Start with bodyweight
- Practice in front of mirror
- Video yourself
- Work with qualified trainer
- Progress slowly

## Quick Reference

| Exercise | Key Cues |
|----------|----------|
| Squat | Knees out, chest up, heels down |
| Push-up | Elbows 45°, core tight, full range |
| Deadlift | Flat back, bar close, hips drive |
| Lunge | Long stride, knee over ankle, torso up |
| Plank | Straight line, squeeze glutes, breathe |
| Row | Shoulder blade first, squeeze, control |

**When in doubt:**
- Reduce weight/difficulty
- Video yourself
- Seek qualified guidance
- Prioritize safety over performance

Good form takes practice, but it's worth every rep. Build the patterns correctly from the start, and you'll train safely and effectively for life.
