# AC Joint Separation Exercises: Recover from Shoulder Separation

An AC joint separation (acromioclavicular joint injury) is a common shoulder injury, especially in contact sports. The right rehabilitation helps restore strength and function, whether treating conservatively or recovering from surgery.

## Understanding AC Joint Separation

The AC joint connects the collarbone (clavicle) to the shoulder blade (acromion). Separation occurs when ligaments connecting these bones are damaged.

**Grades of injury:**

**Grade I:** Sprain, ligaments stretched but intact
- Minimal displacement
- Usually heals well with conservative care

**Grade II:** Partial tear of AC ligaments
- Some displacement visible
- Coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments intact
- Most heal conservatively

**Grade III:** Complete AC and partial CC ligament tear
- Obvious bump (clavicle sits higher)
- Controversial treatment (may be conservative or surgical)

**Grades IV-VI:** More severe displacements
- Often require surgery
- Clavicle displaced backward, downward, or under coracoid

**Common causes:**
- Fall directly onto shoulder
- Contact sports impact
- Falling on outstretched hand (less common)

## Treatment Overview

**Conservative (Grades I-II, some III):**
- Sling for comfort (1-2 weeks typically)
- Pain management
- Progressive rehabilitation
- Return to activity: 4-12 weeks depending on grade

**Surgical (Grades IV-VI, some III):**
- Ligament reconstruction or repair
- Longer immobilization
- Structured rehabilitation
- Return to activity: 4-6 months

## Phase 1: Acute Management (Weeks 1-2)

### Pain Control and Protection

**Sling use:**
- Wear for comfort (not 24/7)
- Remove for exercises
- Usually 1-2 weeks for mild injuries

**Ice:**
- 15-20 minutes, several times daily
- Helps reduce swelling and pain

**Activity modification:**
- Avoid lifting and reaching
- Protect from further impact
- Sleep on back or uninvolved side

### Early Motion

**Pendulum Exercises**
1. Lean forward, arm hanging
2. Make small circles (gravity assisted)
3. 10 circles each direction
4. Several times daily

**Elbow, Wrist, and Hand Motion**
- Full elbow flexion/extension
- Wrist circles
- Fist making and finger spreads
- Keep distal joints mobile

**Assisted Forward Flexion**
1. Use good arm to lift affected arm
2. Only to tolerance
3. 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 2: Progressive Mobility (Weeks 2-4)

### Active Range of Motion

**Active Forward Flexion**
1. Lift arm forward and up
2. Progress range as tolerated
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active Abduction**
1. Lift arm out to side
2. Progress range gradually
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active External Rotation**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
2. Rotate forearm outward
3. 15-20 repetitions

### Scapular Exercises

**Scapular Squeezes**
1. Squeeze shoulder blades together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Shoulder Shrugs**
1. Raise shoulders toward ears
2. Hold 3 seconds
3. Lower slowly
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Scapular Clock**
1. Move shoulder blade in all directions
2. Up, down, forward, backward
3. 10 movements each direction

### Stretching

**Pec Stretch**
1. Doorway stretch
2. Hold 30 seconds
3. Repeat 3 times

**Posterior Capsule Stretch (Gentle)**
1. Cross-body stretch
2. Very gentle initially
3. Hold 20-30 seconds

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 4-8)

### Rotator Cuff Strengthening

**Side-Lying External Rotation**
1. Lie on uninvolved side
2. Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. 3 sets of 15

**Standing External Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate outward against band
3. 3 sets of 15

**Standing Internal Rotation with Band**
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate inward against band
3. 3 sets of 15

### Scapular Strengthening

**Rows**
1. Band or cable at chest height
2. Pull elbows back, squeeze shoulder blades
3. 3 sets of 15

**Face Pulls**
1. Band at face height
2. Pull toward face, elbows high
3. 3 sets of 15

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Lie face down on bench
2. Y: Arms overhead, thumbs up, lift
3. T: Arms out to sides, lift
4. W: Elbows bent, squeeze and lift
5. 2-3 sets of 10 each

**Serratus Punches**
1. Arm straight up (lying on back)
2. Push toward ceiling
3. 3 sets of 15

### Deltoid Strengthening

**Lateral Raises**
1. Light weight
2. Lift arms out to sides
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Front Raises**
1. Light weight
2. Lift arm forward
3. 2-3 sets of 15

### Upper Trapezius

**Shrugs with Weight**
1. Hold light weights
2. Shrug shoulders up
3. Hold 3 seconds
4. 2-3 sets of 15

## Phase 4: Progressive Loading (Weeks 8-12)

### Push Exercises

**Wall Push-Ups**
1. Start vertical
2. Progress angle
3. 3 sets of 15

**Counter Push-Ups**
1. Hands on counter
2. Lower chest, push back
3. 3 sets of 15

**Knee Push-Ups**
1. On knees
2. Full range
3. 3 sets of 12

### Pressing Progression

**Chest Press (Machine)**
1. Light resistance
2. Full range if tolerated
3. 3 sets of 12

**Dumbbell Press**
1. Light weight
2. Neutral or palms-in grip may be easier
3. Progress weight gradually

### Pulling Exercises

**Lat Pulldown**
1. Light to moderate weight
2. 3 sets of 12-15

**Cable Rows**
1. Seated row
2. 3 sets of 15

### Overhead (When Ready)

**Overhead Press**
1. Start very light
2. Progress slowly
3. May be last to return to full

## Phase 5: Return to Function (Weeks 12+)

### Sport-Specific Training

**Contact sports:**
1. Non-contact drills first
2. Gradual return to contact
3. May need padding over AC joint

**Throwing sports:**
1. Progressive throwing program
2. Start with light tosses
3. Gradual distance and velocity

**Weightlifting:**
1. Progress all lifts gradually
2. Bench press often last to normalize
3. May never lift as heavy overhead

### Functional Criteria

**Before full return:**
- Full, pain-free range of motion
- 90%+ strength compared to other side
- Can perform sport-specific tasks
- No apprehension with contact

## Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

### Phase 1 (Weeks 0-6)

**Immobilization:**
- Sling for 4-6 weeks typically
- Remove only for exercises

**Exercises:**
- Elbow, wrist, hand motion
- Pendulums (may wait 2-3 weeks)
- Scapular squeezes
- Grip exercises

**Restrictions:**
- No active shoulder motion initially
- No lifting
- Strict protocol adherence

### Phase 2 (Weeks 6-12)

**Progressive ROM:**
- Active-assisted, then active motion
- Full ROM goal by week 12

**Begin strengthening:**
- Isometrics
- Light rotator cuff exercises
- Scapular stabilization

### Phase 3 (Weeks 12-24)

**Progressive strengthening:**
- All shoulder exercises
- Push and pull exercises
- Overhead gradually

**Functional progression:**
- Sport-specific preparation
- Gradual return to activities

## Managing the "Bump"

Many AC separations leave a visible bump (clavicle sitting higher). This is usually:
- Cosmetic only
- Not affecting function
- Permanent (bone position changed)
- Not usually painful once healed

**The bump does NOT indicate:**
- Treatment failure
- Need for surgery
- Long-term problems (usually)

## Sample Weekly Program (Week 6, Conservative)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, 2 min
2. Active ROM: All directions, 15 reps each
3. Side-lying ER: 3×15
4. Standing IR band: 3×15
5. Rows: 3×15
6. Scapular exercises: 10 min

### Day 2
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Face pulls: 3×15
3. Prone Y-T-W: 2×10 each
4. Lateral raises: 2×15
5. Wall push-ups: 3×15
6. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4
1. Full warm-up
2. All strengthening exercises
3. Progress intensity if tolerated
4. Cool-down stretches

## Key Takeaways

1. **Most Grade I-II heal well conservatively** — give it time
2. **Early motion is important** — prevents stiffness
3. **Strengthen rotator cuff and scapula** — supports the joint
4. **Progress pushing/pressing last** — most stressful for AC joint
5. **The bump may be permanent** — doesn't mean poor function
6. **Protect from re-injury** — padding for contact sports
7. **Surgical cases need strict protocol** — follow your surgeon's plan
8. **Full recovery takes 2-6 months** — depending on grade

AC joint separations can be frustrating but usually heal well with proper rehabilitation. Be patient, strengthen progressively, and protect the joint during return to activity.
