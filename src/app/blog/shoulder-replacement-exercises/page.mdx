# Shoulder Replacement Exercises: Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

Shoulder replacement surgery (arthroplasty) replaces damaged joint surfaces with artificial components. Proper rehabilitation is essential for restoring function and getting the most from your new joint.

## Types of Shoulder Replacement

### Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (TSA)
- Traditional replacement
- Ball on humeral side, socket on glenoid
- Requires intact rotator cuff
- Good for arthritis with healthy muscles

### Reverse Total Shoulder Arthroplasty (RTSA)
- Ball and socket switched
- Ball on glenoid side, socket on humerus
- Designed for massive rotator cuff tears
- Relies on deltoid for elevation

### Hemiarthroplasty
- Only humeral head replaced
- Socket left natural
- Less common now

**Your rehabilitation depends on which procedure you had.**

## Key Differences in Rehabilitation

### TSA Rehabilitation
- Rotator cuff must be protected (subscapularis repair)
- Limited internal rotation initially
- External rotation progressed carefully
- Rely on rotator cuff for function

### RTSA Rehabilitation
- Deltoid is primary mover
- Often can elevate arm earlier
- Different precautions than TSA
- External rotation may be limited permanently

## Phase 1: Protection (Weeks 0-6)

### Sling Use
- Typically 4-6 weeks
- Remove for exercises and hygiene
- Support arm when not in sling

### Precautions
**TSA:**
- Limit external rotation (often 30°)
- No internal rotation behind back (6 weeks)
- No active internal rotation

**RTSA:**
- Limit extension behind body
- Avoid extremes of range
- Follow specific surgeon limits

### Early Exercises

**Pendulum Exercises**
1. Lean forward, support yourself
2. Let arm hang and swing gently
3. Small circles and motions
4. 10-15 circles each direction
5. 3-4 times daily

**Passive Forward Elevation** (Supine)
1. Lie on back
2. Good arm lifts surgical arm
3. To allowed range (usually 90-120°)
4. 10-15 repetitions
5. 3 times daily

**Passive External Rotation** (TSA - Limited)
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°
2. Rotate outward to allowed limit
3. Use stick or good arm
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Elbow, Wrist, Hand Exercises**
- Full elbow motion
- Wrist circles and flexion/extension
- Finger movements
- Maintain mobility

### Scapular Exercises

**Scapular Squeezes**
1. Squeeze shoulder blades together
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. 15-20 repetitions

## Phase 2: Active Motion (Weeks 6-12)

### Transition
- Sling discontinued
- Begin active-assisted motion
- Progress to active motion

### Active-Assisted Exercises

**Pulley Exercises**
1. Over-door pulley
2. Good arm assists surgical arm
3. Progress range
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Wand Exercises**
1. Hold stick with both hands
2. Forward elevation
3. External rotation (as allowed)
4. 15-20 repetitions

### Active Range of Motion

**Active Forward Flexion**
1. Lift arm forward under own power
2. Progress range gradually
3. 15-20 repetitions

**Active Abduction** (RTSA Often Earlier)
1. Lift arm out to side
2. Use deltoid
3. Progress range
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Active External Rotation** (When Cleared)
1. Elbow at side
2. Rotate outward actively
3. Progress range
4. 15-20 repetitions

### RTSA-Specific

**Deltoid Activation**
1. TSA uses rotator cuff
2. RTSA relies on deltoid
3. Focus on deltoid-driven elevation
4. Shrug plus abduction

## Phase 3: Strengthening (Weeks 12-16)

**Begin when cleared by surgeon.**

### Isometric Exercises

**Isometric External Rotation**
1. Push against wall or doorframe
2. No movement, just tension
3. Hold 5-10 seconds
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Isometric Abduction**
1. Stand next to wall
2. Push arm outward against wall
3. Hold 5-10 seconds

### Light Resistance

**TSA: Rotator Cuff Focus**
- Side-lying external rotation
- Band internal/external rotation
- Light resistance only initially

**RTSA: Deltoid Focus**
- Lateral raises
- Front raises
- Abduction strengthening

### Scapular Strengthening

**Rows**
1. Light band or weight
2. Pull elbows back
3. 2-3 sets of 15

**Prone Y-T-W**
1. Face down on bench
2. Very light weight or none
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each

## Phase 4: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 16+)

### Gradual Progression

**Increase Resistance:**
- Progress slowly
- Monitor for pain
- Quality over quantity

### Functional Activities

**Light Activities:**
- Reaching for objects
- Light lifting
- Daily activity practice

**Progress to:**
- Moderate lifting (as allowed)
- Recreational activities
- Work-related tasks

### Exercise Progression

**Continue:**
- Rotator cuff strengthening (TSA)
- Deltoid strengthening (RTSA)
- Scapular exercises
- Flexibility maintenance

## Long-Term Expectations

### Range of Motion Goals

**TSA:**
- Forward flexion: 130-150°
- External rotation: 40-60°
- Internal rotation: Varies

**RTSA:**
- Forward flexion: 120-140°
- Abduction: Good (deltoid-driven)
- External rotation: Often limited (20-40°)

### Activities

**Usually OK:**
- Swimming
- Golf
- Doubles tennis
- Cycling
- Light weight training

**Usually Avoid:**
- Heavy lifting overhead
- Contact sports
- High-impact activities
- Repetitive overhead work

### Joint Protection

**Lifelong considerations:**
- No heavy lifting (usually <15-20 lbs)
- Avoid repetitive overhead stress
- Report new pain or weakness
- Regular follow-up with surgeon

## Sample Weekly Schedule (Week 10)

### Day 1
1. Warm-up: Pendulums, ROM
2. Pulley exercises: 15 reps
3. Active ROM: All directions
4. Isometrics: 10 reps each
5. Stretching: 5 min

### Day 2
1. ROM exercises: 10 min
2. Light scapular exercises
3. Stretching

### Day 3
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Wand exercises: 15 reps each
3. Active ROM: All directions
4. Begin light resistance (if cleared)

### Day 4: Rest or Light ROM

### Day 5
1. Full ROM program
2. All strengthening exercises
3. Progress as tolerated
4. Stretching

## Managing Common Issues

### Stiffness
- Very common after replacement
- Maintain ROM exercises
- May need physical therapy
- Patience required

### Weakness
- Expected initially
- Progressive strengthening helps
- RTSA may have persistent limitations

### Night Pain
- Common early on
- Sleep positioning important
- Usually improves over months

### Crepitus/Clicking
- Often normal
- Report to surgeon if painful
- Usually not concerning

## When to Contact Your Surgeon

- Sudden increase in pain
- New weakness or instability
- Signs of infection
- Dislocation concerns (RTSA)
- Any concerning symptoms

## Returning to Activities

### Work
- Desk work: 2-4 weeks
- Light manual: 3-4 months
- Heavy manual: May be restricted

### Recreation
- Golf: 3-4 months
- Swimming: 3-6 months
- Tennis: 4-6 months

### Driving
- Usually 4-6 weeks
- When off pain medication
- When can safely control vehicle

## Key Takeaways

1. **TSA vs. RTSA differ** — different muscles, different rehab
2. **Protect the repair** — follow precautions strictly
3. **Passive before active** — don't rush progression
4. **Strengthen when cleared** — usually week 12+
5. **Expect limitations** — not a normal shoulder
6. **Joint protection lifelong** — avoid heavy overhead lifting
7. **Be patient** — full recovery takes 6-12 months
8. **Follow up regularly** — monitor joint long-term

Shoulder replacement provides excellent pain relief and improved function for most patients. Follow your rehabilitation program, respect your joint's limitations, and you'll enjoy years of improved shoulder function.
