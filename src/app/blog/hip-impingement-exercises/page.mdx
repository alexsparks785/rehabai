# Hip Impingement Exercises: Manage FAI Pain and Improve Function

Hip impingement (femoroacetabular impingement or FAI) causes groin pain when bone abnormalities create friction in the hip joint. The right exercises can reduce symptoms and improve function—often avoiding the need for surgery.

## Understanding Hip Impingement

FAI occurs when extra bone on the femoral head (cam), acetabulum (pincer), or both creates abnormal contact during hip movement.

**Types:**
- **Cam:** Bump on femoral head (ball)
- **Pincer:** Extra bone on acetabulum (socket)
- **Mixed:** Both types (most common)

**Symptoms:**
- Groin pain with hip flexion
- Pain sitting, squatting, or crossing legs
- Stiffness after sitting
- Pain with athletic activities
- Clicking or catching sensation
- Limited hip range of motion

**Key concept:** The bone shape won't change with exercise, but you can reduce symptoms by:
- Avoiding positions that cause impingement
- Strengthening muscles that control hip position
- Improving movement patterns
- Maintaining available mobility

## Positions to Avoid or Modify

**Aggravating positions:**
- Deep hip flexion (past 90°)
- Hip flexion + internal rotation
- Deep squats
- Sitting with legs crossed
- Activities that compress the hip

**Modify by:**
- Limiting squat depth
- Avoiding end-range positions
- Using external rotation cues
- Taking breaks from sitting

## Phase 1: Pain Management and Mobility

### Hip Flexor Mobility (Without Deep Flexion)

**Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch**
1. Kneel on affected side (use padding)
2. Front foot forward, knee at 90°
3. Tuck pelvis under (posterior tilt)
4. Lean forward gently
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Avoid deep hip flexion on back leg

**Prone Hip Extension**
1. Lie face down
2. Lift leg straight back
3. Keep pelvis on floor
4. Hold 5 seconds
5. 10-15 repetitions

### Gentle Hip Rotation

**Supine External Rotation**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Let knee fall outward
3. Only to comfortable range
4. Hold 20-30 seconds
5. Repeat other side

**Seated Figure-4** (Modified)
1. Sit in chair
2. Cross ankle over opposite knee
3. Gentle forward lean (don't push knee down)
4. Stay within pain-free range
5. Hold 30 seconds

### Lumbar and Pelvic Mobility

**Pelvic Tilts**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Flatten low back to floor (posterior tilt)
3. Arch low back (anterior tilt)
4. 15-20 repetitions

**Cat-Cow** (Modified)
1. On hands and knees
2. Arch and round spine
3. Keep hips relatively still
4. Focus on lumbar motion
5. 10-15 repetitions

## Phase 2: Core and Hip Strengthening

### Core Stability (Critical for FAI)

**Dead Bug**
1. Lie on back, arms up, knees bent 90°
2. Lower opposite arm and leg
3. Keep low back pressed to floor
4. 10 reps each side

**Bird Dog**
1. On hands and knees
2. Extend opposite arm and leg
3. Keep spine neutral
4. 10 reps each side

**Pallof Press**
1. Band anchored at side
2. Press band straight out
3. Resist rotation
4. 10-15 reps each side

### Glute Strengthening

**Glute Bridge**
1. Lie on back, knees bent
2. Squeeze glutes, lift hips
3. Hold 3-5 seconds
4. 3 sets of 15

**Clamshells**
1. Side-lying, knees bent 45°
2. Keep feet together, lift top knee
3. Don't roll backward
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Side-Lying Hip Abduction**
1. Lie on side, bottom knee bent
2. Top leg straight
3. Lift toward ceiling
4. 3 sets of 15 each side

**Single-Leg Glute Bridge**
1. One leg extended
2. Bridge on single leg
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Hip Stabilization

**Standing Hip Circles** (Small)
1. Stand on one leg
2. Small circles with free leg
3. 10 each direction

**Lateral Band Walks**
1. Band around ankles or above knees
2. Side-step with tension
3. 15-20 steps each direction

**Monster Walks**
1. Band above knees
2. Walk forward with wide stance
3. 15-20 steps

## Phase 3: Movement Pattern Retraining

### Squat Modifications

**Box Squat** (Limited Depth)
1. Squat to box or bench
2. Stop at or above 90° hip flexion
3. Focus on pushing knees out
4. 3 sets of 12

**Goblet Squat** (Limited Depth)
1. Hold weight at chest
2. Squat to comfortable depth
3. External rotation cue (knees out)
4. 3 sets of 12

**Sumo Squat**
1. Wide stance, toes out
2. Often more comfortable for FAI
3. Limit depth as needed
4. 3 sets of 12

### Hip Hinge Training

**Romanian Deadlift**
1. Hinge at hips, soft knees
2. Keep spine neutral
3. Feel hamstrings stretch
4. 3 sets of 12

**Single-Leg RDL**
1. Balance on one leg
2. Hinge forward
3. 2-3 sets of 10 each side

### Step-Up Modifications

**Forward Step-Up** (Low Box)
1. Step onto low box/step
2. Drive through heel
3. Avoid excessive forward knee travel
4. 3 sets of 12 each side

## Phase 4: Functional Training

### Single-Leg Strength

**Bulgarian Split Squat** (Modified)
1. Rear foot elevated
2. Limited depth (don't go too low)
3. Keep torso upright
4. 3 sets of 10 each side

**Single-Leg Squat to Box**
1. Squat to high box on one leg
2. Control the descent
3. 2-3 sets of 8 each side

### Hip Stability Under Load

**Pallof Press with Hip Hinge**
1. Combine anti-rotation with hinge
2. 10 reps each side

**Single-Leg Deadlift with Rotation**
1. Hinge and reach across body
2. Anti-rotation challenge
3. 8-10 reps each side

### Return to Activity

**Sport-specific movements:**
- Start at low intensity
- Avoid deep hip flexion positions
- Progress gradually
- Modify technique as needed

## Activity-Specific Modifications

### Running
- Usually well-tolerated
- Focus on glute activation
- Avoid excessive hip flexion (overstriding)

### Cycling
- Raise seat height
- Avoid drops (deep flexion)
- Consider bike fit adjustment

### Weight Training
- Limit squat depth
- Avoid deep lunges
- Hip hinge preferred over squat
- Sumo stance often better

### Yoga
- Avoid deep hip flexion poses
- Modify pigeon pose
- Use props for support
- Skip extreme positions

### Soccer/Kicking Sports
- May need technique modification
- Strengthen hip stabilizers
- Consider activity reduction during flares

## Exercises to Avoid or Modify

**Problematic exercises:**
- Deep squats (ass to grass)
- Deep lunges
- Full-depth leg press
- Extreme hip flexion stretches
- Piriformis stretch (4-figure)

**Modify by:**
- Limiting depth
- Using external rotation
- Keeping within pain-free range
- Substituting safer alternatives

## Sample Weekly Program

### Day 1: Mobility and Core
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Hip flexor stretch: 3×30 sec each
3. Pelvic tilts: 20 reps
4. Dead bug: 3×10 each side
5. Bird dog: 3×10 each side
6. Glute bridge: 3×15

### Day 2: Hip Strengthening
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Clamshells: 3×15 each
3. Side-lying abduction: 3×15 each
4. Lateral band walks: 2×20 each direction
5. Single-leg glute bridge: 2×10 each
6. Box squat: 3×12

### Day 3: Rest or Light Activity

### Day 4: Movement Training
1. Warm-up: 5 min
2. Goblet squat (limited depth): 3×12
3. Romanian deadlift: 3×12
4. Step-ups: 3×12 each
5. Pallof press: 3×12 each side

### Day 5: Integration
1. Full warm-up
2. Single-leg RDL: 3×10 each
3. Bulgarian split squat: 3×10 each
4. Monster walks: 2×20
5. Sport-specific movements

## When to Consider Surgery

**Surgical consultation if:**
- Significant symptoms despite 3-6 months of quality rehab
- Large bone abnormality confirmed on imaging
- Labral damage requiring repair
- Unable to participate in desired activities

**Surgical options:**
- Hip arthroscopy (most common)
- Labral repair
- Bone reshaping (osteoplasty)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Avoid deep hip flexion** — stay above 90° when symptomatic
2. **Strengthen glutes and core** — control hip position
3. **External rotation is your friend** — opens up joint space
4. **Modify, don't eliminate** — adjust exercises rather than stopping
5. **Hip hinge over squat** — often better tolerated
6. **Progress gradually** — don't rush back to aggravating activities
7. **Conservative care works for many** — give it 3-6 months
8. **Surgery is an option** — if rehab fails

Hip impingement doesn't have to end your active lifestyle. With proper exercise modifications and strengthening, many people manage symptoms successfully without surgery.
