# Post-Concussion Exercises: Safe Return to Activity After Head Injury

Concussion recovery has evolved significantly—we now know that prolonged rest isn't best. Guided, progressive exercise actually helps most people recover faster. This guide covers evidence-based approaches to exercise after concussion.

## Understanding Concussion Recovery

A concussion is a functional brain injury, not structural damage visible on imaging. Recovery involves the brain restoring normal function, which exercise can actually support.

**Common symptoms:**
- Headache
- Dizziness and balance problems
- Fatigue
- Difficulty concentrating
- Light and noise sensitivity
- Sleep disturbances
- Mood changes
- Visual problems

**Recovery timeline:**
- Most adults: 10-14 days
- Adolescents: 2-4 weeks
- Children: May take longer
- Some develop prolonged symptoms (post-concussion syndrome)

## Important: Medical Clearance First

**Before starting exercise after concussion:**
- Be evaluated by a healthcare provider
- Rule out more serious injury
- Get clearance for activity
- Follow your provider's specific guidance

**Red flags requiring immediate care:**
- Severe or worsening headache
- Repeated vomiting
- Seizures
- Weakness or numbness in limbs
- Slurred speech
- One pupil larger than the other
- Loss of consciousness
- Increasing confusion

## The Evolution of Concussion Management

**Old approach:** Complete rest until symptom-free
**Current approach:** Brief rest (24-48 hours), then gradual return to activity

**Why exercise helps:**
- Improves cerebral blood flow regulation
- Reduces symptoms in many people
- Prevents physical deconditioning
- Supports psychological well-being
- May speed recovery

## Phase 1: Relative Rest (Days 1-2)

### What Relative Rest Means
- Limited physical and cognitive activity
- Not complete bed rest
- Gentle walking is usually OK
- Avoid symptom-provoking activities

### Light Activities Usually OK
- Walking at comfortable pace
- Light household activities
- Short periods of reading or screen use (if tolerated)

### Activities to Avoid
- Sports and exercise
- Driving (until cleared)
- Work/school (may need modified schedule initially)
- Activities with fall or collision risk

## Phase 2: Light Aerobic Exercise (Days 2-7)

### Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test Protocol

This approach uses sub-symptom threshold exercise—exercising at an intensity that doesn't worsen symptoms.

**Finding your threshold:**
1. Start walking or cycling at light intensity
2. Gradually increase effort
3. Stop when symptoms increase (headache, dizziness, etc.)
4. Note heart rate at that point

**Daily exercise:**
- Exercise at 80% of symptom threshold heart rate
- Duration: 20-30 minutes
- Continue until symptoms no longer provoked by exercise

### Exercise Options

**Walking**
- Treadmill or outdoor
- Easy to moderate pace
- Monitor for symptom increase

**Stationary Bike**
- Low resistance
- Comfortable pace
- Good option for balance issues

**Elliptical**
- Light resistance
- Controlled pace

### Monitoring Guidelines
- Stop if symptoms increase significantly
- Some symptom fluctuation is normal
- Symptoms should settle within 1 hour after exercise
- If symptoms don't settle, reduce intensity next session

## Phase 3: Sport-Specific Activity (When Appropriate)

### Criteria to Progress
- Tolerating light aerobic exercise without symptom exacerbation
- Symptoms at or near baseline
- Cleared by healthcare provider

### Activities in This Phase
- Running or higher-intensity aerobic activity
- Sport-specific movements (without contact)
- Resistance training (light to moderate)

### What to Avoid
- Contact or collision
- Activities with fall risk
- Heading in soccer
- High-intensity plyometrics initially

## Vestibular and Oculomotor Rehabilitation

Many concussion symptoms relate to vestibular and visual processing dysfunction.

### Gaze Stabilization

**VOR x1 Exercise**
1. Hold a target (finger or card) at arm's length
2. Focus on the target
3. Turn head side-to-side slowly
4. Keep eyes on target (it should stay clear)
5. Start: 30 seconds
6. Progress: faster movement, 1-2 minutes

**Vertical VOR x1**
Same as above but move head up and down.

### Smooth Pursuit

**How to do it:**
1. Hold finger at arm's length
2. Move finger slowly side-to-side
3. Follow with eyes only (head still)
4. Repeat up-down
5. Repeat diagonally
6. 30-60 seconds each direction

### Saccades (Quick Eye Movements)

**How to do it:**
1. Hold two targets at arm's length, shoulder-width apart
2. Quickly shift gaze between targets
3. 20-30 shifts
4. Repeat vertical and diagonal

### Convergence

**Near-Far Focus:**
1. Hold finger at arm's length
2. Focus on finger
3. Move finger toward nose (about 6 inches away)
4. Keep single, clear image
5. Move back out
6. 10-15 repetitions

### Vestibular Habituation

For those sensitive to movement or busy environments:

**Head Movements While Standing:**
1. Stand safely (near support)
2. Turn head side-to-side
3. Start slow, progress speed
4. Up-down movements
5. 30-60 seconds each

**Walking with Head Turns:**
1. Walk in straight line
2. Turn head side-to-side while walking
3. Maintain balance and direction
4. Progress: busier environments

## Balance Training

Balance is often affected after concussion.

### Static Balance Progressions

**Level 1:**
- Feet together, eyes open: 30 seconds
- Feet together, eyes closed: 30 seconds

**Level 2:**
- Tandem stance (heel-toe), eyes open: 30 seconds
- Tandem stance, eyes closed: 30 seconds

**Level 3:**
- Single-leg stance, eyes open: 30 seconds
- Single-leg stance, eyes closed: 30 seconds

**Level 4:**
- Single leg on foam pad
- Add cognitive tasks (counting backward)

### Dynamic Balance

**Tandem Walking**
Walk heel-to-toe in a straight line, 10-20 steps

**Walking with Head Movements**
Walk while turning head side-to-side or up-down

**Figure-8 Walking**
Walk in figure-8 pattern

**Balance Beam** (or line on floor)
Walk along narrow path

## Cervical Spine Exercises

Neck issues are common with concussion and can contribute to symptoms.

### Cervical Range of Motion

**Gentle movements:**
- Rotation (look over each shoulder)
- Side bending (ear to shoulder)
- Flexion/extension (chin to chest, look up)
- 5-10 reps each direction
- Stay within comfort

### Cervical Strengthening

**Chin Tucks**
1. Draw chin back (make double chin)
2. Hold 5-10 seconds
3. Release
4. 10-15 repetitions

**Isometric Strengthening**
1. Hand on forehead, push head into hand (no movement)
2. Hold 5-10 seconds
3. Repeat for back of head, each side
4. 10 reps each direction

### Cervical Stretching

**Upper Trapezius Stretch**
1. Sit on hand of side to stretch
2. Tilt head away
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. Repeat each side

## Return-to-Sport Protocol

The international consensus return-to-sport protocol has 6 stages.

### Stage 1: Symptom-Limited Activity
- Daily activities that don't provoke symptoms
- Gradual reintroduction of work/school

### Stage 2: Light Aerobic Exercise
- Walking, swimming, stationary cycling
- 10-15 minutes, moderate intensity
- No resistance training

### Stage 3: Sport-Specific Exercise
- Running, skating, throwing drills
- No head impact activities
- Progressive increase in intensity

### Stage 4: Non-Contact Training Drills
- More complex drills
- May begin resistance training
- Progress intensity and duration

### Stage 5: Full Contact Practice
- Following medical clearance
- Full participation in practice
- Restore confidence and assess functional skills

### Stage 6: Return to Competition
- Normal game play

**Key rules:**
- Minimum 24 hours at each stage (may be longer for younger athletes)
- If symptoms return, go back one stage and rest 24 hours
- Medical clearance required before contact (Stage 5)

## Managing Specific Symptoms

### Exercise-Induced Headache
- Reduce intensity
- Ensure hydration
- Consider neck involvement
- Gradual progression

### Dizziness
- Focus on vestibular exercises
- Progress balance training
- Start with stable surfaces

### Fatigue
- Shorter exercise sessions
- Multiple sessions per day (instead of one long one)
- Adequate sleep

### Light Sensitivity
- Exercise in controlled lighting
- Sunglasses outdoors
- Gradual exposure to normal light

### Cognitive Symptoms
- Limit exercise duration if concentration difficult
- Single-task exercises first
- Gradually add cognitive demands

## Exercise and Post-Concussion Syndrome

If symptoms persist beyond expected recovery (typically >4 weeks in adults):

### Sub-Symptom Threshold Training
Research shows controlled aerobic exercise helps many with prolonged symptoms.

**Protocol:**
1. Establish symptom threshold (Buffalo Treadmill Test)
2. Exercise at 80% of threshold
3. Daily for 20-30 minutes
4. Progress intensity as tolerance improves

### Multimodal Rehabilitation
May need to address:
- Vestibular dysfunction
- Cervical spine issues
- Visual processing problems
- Autonomic dysfunction
- Psychological factors

**Consider referral** to a concussion specialist or multidisciplinary program.

## Sample Recovery Program

### Week 1
**Days 1-2:** Relative rest, light walking only
**Days 3-7:**
- Light walking or stationary bike: 15-20 min below symptom threshold
- Gentle cervical ROM
- Basic balance: feet together stance

### Week 2
- Progress aerobic duration to 25-30 min
- Add gaze stabilization exercises: 5 min, 2x daily
- Balance progression: tandem stance
- Gentle neck stretches

### Week 3
- Increase aerobic intensity if tolerated
- Add smooth pursuit and saccade exercises
- Balance: single-leg work
- Begin isometric neck strengthening

### Week 4+
- Progress through return-to-sport stages
- Continue vestibular and balance training
- Sport-specific movements (non-contact)
- Medical clearance before contact

## When to Seek Additional Help

**Consult a healthcare provider if:**
- Symptoms not improving after 2 weeks (adults) or 4 weeks (youth)
- Symptoms worsening
- New or concerning symptoms
- Unable to return to normal activities

**Specialized care may include:**
- Concussion specialist evaluation
- Vestibular physical therapy
- Vision therapy
- Neuropsychological testing
- Mental health support

## Prevention

### For Athletes
- Proper technique (tackling, heading)
- Sport-specific rules
- Appropriate protective equipment
- Strength and conditioning

### General
- Seatbelts in vehicles
- Helmets for cycling, skiing, etc.
- Fall prevention for older adults
- Safe environments

## Key Takeaways

1. **Brief rest, then activity** — 24-48 hours rest, then gradual return
2. **Exercise helps recovery** — sub-symptom threshold aerobic exercise
3. **Listen to your body** — stop if symptoms significantly worsen
4. **Progress gradually** — follow the 6-stage return-to-sport protocol
5. **Address vestibular symptoms** — gaze stabilization and balance training
6. **Don't rush return to contact** — medical clearance required
7. **Seek help if prolonged** — symptoms beyond 2-4 weeks need evaluation
8. **Each concussion is unique** — individualized management is key

Concussion recovery benefits from a structured, progressive approach to exercise. Work with your healthcare provider, follow the protocols, and be patient with the process.
