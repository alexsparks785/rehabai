# What Muscles Do Leg Curls Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Leg curls are the primary isolation exercise for the hamstrings—directly targeting the back of the thigh in a way that compound movements can't fully replicate. Whether lying, seated, or standing, leg curls build the hamstring strength and size that supports knee health and athletic performance.

Let's break down exactly what muscles leg curls target and how to maximize hamstring development.

## Primary Muscles Worked

### Hamstrings - Maximum Isolation

All three hamstring muscles work:

**Biceps Femoris (Long and Short Head):** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Outer hamstring
- Primary knee flexor
- Creates hamstring width

**Semitendinosus:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Inner hamstring
- Knee flexor and hip extensor
- Visible on inner thigh

**Semimembranosus:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Deep inner hamstring
- Knee flexor
- Important for stability

**Activation level:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (Maximum isolation)

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Gastrocnemius (Calves) - Moderate Activation

The calf crosses the knee:
- Assists with knee flexion
- Works more when ankle is plantarflexed
- Minor contribution

### Sartorius - Low Activation

This thigh muscle:
- Minor assistance
- Crosses the knee joint

## Lying vs. Seated Leg Curls: Key Differences

### Lying Leg Curl

**Position:** Face down (prone), hips extended

**Hamstring state:**
- Shortened at the hip
- Emphasis may shift slightly

**Benefits:**
- Classic, comfortable position
- Good for heavy loading
- Less quad involvement

### Seated Leg Curl

**Position:** Seated, hips flexed

**Hamstring state:**
- Stretched at the hip (hip flexion stretches hamstrings)
- Greater stretch under load

**Benefits:**
- Stretched-position training (may enhance growth)
- Research suggests potentially superior for hypertrophy
- Different feel, many prefer it

### Which Is Better?

Recent research suggests **seated leg curls may have a slight hypertrophy advantage** due to the stretched position. However, both are highly effective—include both if possible.

| Factor | Lying | Seated |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Hamstring stretch | Moderate | Maximum |
| Comfort | Good | Good |
| Potential for growth | High | Potentially higher |
| Hip position | Extended | Flexed |

## How to Perform Lying Leg Curls

### Setup
1. Lie face down on machine
2. Ankle pad just above heels
3. Knees just off the edge of bench
4. Grip handles for stability

### Execution
1. **Curl:** Bend knees, bring heels toward glutes
2. **Squeeze:** Contract hamstrings at top
3. **Peak:** Hold briefly at full contraction
4. **Lower:** Controlled extension
5. **Stop:** Just before full lockout (maintain tension)
6. **Repeat**

### Key Technique Points

- **Control the negative:** Don't let weight drop
- **Full contraction:** Curl as far as possible
- **Don't hyperextend:** Stop just before lockout
- **Hips stay down:** Don't lift off the bench
- **Squeeze at top:** Feel the hamstrings contract

## How to Perform Seated Leg Curls

### Setup
1. Sit in machine, back against pad
2. Ankle pad above heels
3. Thigh pad securing legs
4. Legs extended to start

### Execution
1. **Curl:** Bend knees, bring heels under seat
2. **Squeeze:** Contract hamstrings fully
3. **Hold:** Brief pause at peak
4. **Extend:** Controlled return
5. **Maintain tension:** Don't fully lock out
6. **Repeat**

### Key Technique Points

- **Stay seated:** Don't lift off the seat
- **Control throughout:** No swinging
- **Full range:** Complete contraction to near-full extension
- **Squeeze:** Feel the hamstrings at the bottom of each curl

## Common Mistakes

### Lifting Hips (Lying)
- **Problem:** Momentum, less hamstring isolation
- **Fix:** Keep hips pressed into bench

### Partial Range of Motion
- **Problem:** Missing full contraction or stretch
- **Fix:** Complete ROM, full curl to near-full extension

### Hyperextending at Bottom
- **Problem:** Losing tension, stress on knee
- **Fix:** Stop just short of full lockout

### Going Too Fast
- **Problem:** Momentum does the work
- **Fix:** Controlled tempo, 2-3 seconds each direction

### Curling Toes
- **Problem:** Calf takes over
- **Fix:** Keep ankle neutral or slightly dorsiflexed

## Programming Recommendations

### For Hamstring Size
- 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps
- Include both lying and seated if possible
- 2x per week

### For Strength
- 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps
- Heavier weight with control
- Full recovery between sets

### As Part of Leg Training
- After compound movements (squats, RDLs)
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Finish hamstrings thoroughly

### For Injury Prevention
- Regular inclusion in programming
- Balanced with quad work
- Don't neglect hamstrings

## Leg Curls vs. Other Hamstring Exercises

| Exercise | Knee Flexion | Hip Extension | Hamstring Focus |
|----------|--------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Leg Curls | Maximum | None | Isolation |
| RDLs | None | Maximum | Hip-dominant |
| Good Mornings | None | Maximum | Hip-dominant |
| Nordic Curls | Maximum | None | Eccentric focus |
| Glute-Ham Raise | Maximum | Maximum | Complete |

Leg curls are the only common exercise that isolates knee flexion.

## Leg Curl Variations

### Lying Leg Curl (Standard)
- Face down, curl heels to glutes
- Most common machine

### Seated Leg Curl
- Seated, curl under seat
- Stretched-position emphasis

### Standing Leg Curl
- One leg at a time
- Cable or machine
- Addresses imbalances

### Nordic Curls (Bodyweight)
- Eccentric emphasis
- No machine needed
- Advanced, highly effective

### Stability Ball Leg Curl
- Feet on ball, curl toward glutes
- Also works glutes
- Home-friendly option

## Who Should Do Leg Curls?

### Everyone Training Legs
Leg curls are fundamental:
- Direct hamstring isolation
- Balances quad-dominant training
- Supports knee health

### Especially Important For:
- Athletes (hamstring injury prevention)
- Those doing lots of quad work
- Runners and sprinters
- Anyone wanting balanced legs

### May Be Lower Priority If:
- Already doing significant hip extension work
- Very limited time (compounds first)
- But still beneficial to include

## Complementary Exercises

For complete hamstring development:

**Knee Flexion (Leg Curls):**
- Lying leg curls
- Seated leg curls
- Nordic curls

**Hip Extension:**
- Romanian deadlifts
- Good mornings
- Stiff-leg deadlifts

**Combined:**
- Glute-ham raises
- Cable pull-throughs

**Include both patterns** for complete hamstrings.

## The Injury Prevention Connection

Strong hamstrings help prevent:
- Hamstring strains (common in sprinting sports)
- ACL injuries (hamstrings assist ACL function)
- Knee instability

Leg curls, especially eccentric-focused work, are a key component of hamstring injury prevention programs.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Leg curls isolate all three hamstring muscles** through knee flexion
2. **Seated may have a slight edge** for hypertrophy (stretched position)
3. **Include both lying and seated** if possible
4. **Control the negative**—don't drop the weight
5. **Maintain tension**—don't fully lock out
6. **Pair with hip extension work** (RDLs) for complete hamstrings

## The Bottom Line

Leg curls are essential for complete hamstring development. While Romanian deadlifts and other hip extension exercises are important, leg curls provide the knee flexion work that compounds can't fully deliver.

Include both lying and seated variations if your gym has both. Control every rep, squeeze at the contraction, and don't neglect this fundamental movement. Your hamstrings—and your injury resilience—will thank you.
