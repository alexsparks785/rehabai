# What Muscles Do Frog Pumps Work? Complete Anatomy Guide

Frog pumps are a glute isolation exercise performed with feet together and knees out—resembling a frog's legs. This unique position minimizes hamstring involvement, making frog pumps one of the purest glute exercises available. Here's exactly what's working and why the position matters.

## Primary Muscle Worked

### Gluteus Maximus

**Activation Level: Very High (Maximum Isolation)**

The glutes are the sole focus:
- Primary hip extensor in this position
- Works through hip extension against gravity
- Minimal assistance from other muscles
- Peak contraction at full hip extension

**Why frog pumps isolate glutes so well:**

The feet-together, knees-out position:
1. Shortens the hamstrings (bent knees)
2. Externally rotates the hips
3. Abducts the hips
4. Creates a position where glutes MUST do the work

This makes frog pumps possibly the best bodyweight glute isolation exercise.

## Secondary Muscles Worked

### Gluteus Medius and Minimus

**Activation Level: Moderate**

The lateral glute muscles contribute:
- Maintain the knees-out position
- Hip abduction component
- Stabilize throughout
- More active than in standard bridges

### Hip Adductors

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

The inner thigh muscles:
- Work to maintain foot contact
- Slight involvement due to position
- Not a primary target

### Core Stabilizers

**Activation Level: Low to Moderate**

Minimal core demand:
- Back stays on floor
- Some pelvic stability required
- Less demanding than standing exercises

### Hamstrings

**Activation Level: Very Low (By Design)**

This is the key:
- Bent knee position shortens hamstrings
- Hamstrings can't contribute effectively
- Forces glutes to work alone
- Why frog pumps exist

## Why Feet Together, Knees Out?

The frog position creates unique biomechanics:

| Position Element | Effect |
|------------------|--------|
| Feet together | Creates pivot point, reduces hamstring leverage |
| Knees bent | Shortens hamstrings, reduces their contribution |
| Knees out (abducted) | Adds external rotation, more glute max fiber recruitment |
| Soles touching | Stabilizes the position |

**The result:** Glutes work in isolation. If you feel your hamstrings in frog pumps, something is wrong.

## Frog Pumps vs Glute Bridges

| Aspect | Frog Pump | Glute Bridge |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Foot position | Soles together | Flat on floor |
| Knee position | Out (abducted) | Straight ahead |
| Hamstring involvement | Very Low | Moderate |
| Glute isolation | Maximum | High |
| Range of motion | Shorter | Moderate |
| Best for | Pure glute activation | General glute work |

Frog pumps sacrifice some range of motion for superior glute isolation.

## Muscle Activation by Phase

### Starting Position
- Lying on back
- Soles of feet together
- Knees falling out to sides
- Arms at sides or on belly
- Lower back neutral or slight posterior tilt

### Lift Phase (Concentric)
- Squeeze glutes to lift hips
- Push through edges of feet
- Hips rise toward ceiling
- Knees stay out
- Focus on glute contraction, not height

### Top Position
- Maximum glute squeeze
- Hips fully extended
- Brief hold (1-2 seconds)
- Feel the glute contraction

### Lower Phase (Eccentric)
- Controlled descent
- Maintain glute tension
- Light touch at bottom
- Don't rest between reps

## Common Mistakes

**Going too high**: Hyperextending the lower back. Stop when hips are extended—don't arch.

**Losing knee position**: Knees coming together. Keep them out throughout.

**Rushing**: Speed reduces glute activation. Slow, controlled, squeeze.

**Not squeezing at top**: Missing the peak contraction. Hold and squeeze for 1-2 seconds.

**Feet too far from body**: Increases hamstring involvement. Bring feet closer to hips.

**Relaxing between reps**: Losing tension. Keep glutes engaged throughout the set.

## How to Maximize Glute Activation

1. **Squeeze before you lift**: Engage glutes first
2. **Think "drive knees out"**: Maintain abduction
3. **Push through foot edges**: Not the toes
4. **Hold the squeeze**: 1-2 seconds at top
5. **Posterior pelvic tilt**: Slight tuck at top
6. **Keep tension**: Don't rest at bottom
7. **Feel it in glutes ONLY**: If you feel hamstrings, adjust position

## Variations

| Variation | Primary Change |
|-----------|----------------|
| Bodyweight frog pump | Baseline |
| Banded frog pump | Band above knees, more abduction resistance |
| Weighted frog pump | Dumbbell on hips |
| Elevated frog pump | Shoulders on bench, more ROM |
| Pulse frog pump | Pulses at top, extended tension |
| Single-leg frog pump | One leg only, advanced |

### Banded Frog Pumps

Adding a band above the knees:
- Increases glute medius activation
- Provides resistance to push against
- Enhances the mind-muscle connection
- Highly recommended progression

### Weighted Frog Pumps

Adding a dumbbell or plate on hips:
- Progressive overload
- Place weight on hip crease
- Moderate weight is sufficient
- Range of motion limits heavy loading

## Programming Recommendations

**For Glute Activation (Pre-Workout):**
- 2-3 sets of 20-30 reps
- Before squats, deadlifts, or leg day
- Focus on feeling the glutes
- Light or no weight

**For Glute Development:**
- 3-4 sets of 15-25 reps
- Add band or weight
- Slow tempo with squeeze
- 2-3 times per week

**For Burnout/Finisher:**
- 2-3 sets to failure
- After main glute work
- High reps, constant tension
- Maximum pump

**Superset Ideas:**
- Frog pumps + glute bridges (isolation + compound)
- Frog pumps + hip thrusts (activation + strength)
- Frog pumps + fire hydrants (different angles)

## When to Use Frog Pumps

**As activation drill:**
- Before any lower body workout
- Wake up the glutes
- Establish mind-muscle connection

**When glutes won't fire:**
- If you never feel glutes in other exercises
- Frog pumps help you find them
- Build the connection, then transfer

**For pure isolation:**
- When you want NO hamstring involvement
- Glute-focused without anything else
- Maximum isolation

**High-rep finisher:**
- At the end of leg/glute day
- Pump and burn
- Metabolic stimulus

## Who Benefits Most

**Those who can't feel their glutes:**
- Frog pumps make it nearly impossible NOT to feel them
- Best activation exercise for "glute amnesia"

**People with tight/overactive hamstrings:**
- Removes hamstring from the equation
- Glutes can work without hamstring compensation

**Anyone wanting pure glute work:**
- Maximum isolation
- No other muscle dominates

**Beginners:**
- Easy to learn
- Feel exactly what's working
- Build foundation for other exercises

## Limitations

**Limited range of motion:**
- Shorter ROM than bridges or thrusts
- May need other exercises for full development

**Can't load heavily:**
- Not a strength exercise
- Best for activation and high-rep work

**Position may be uncomfortable:**
- Some people can't get into the frog position
- Hip mobility may limit

## Key Takeaways

- **Primary muscle**: Gluteus maximus (very high, maximum isolation)
- **Secondary**: Glute medius/minimus (moderate), minimal everything else
- **Key feature**: Almost zero hamstring involvement
- **Position**: Feet together, knees out, push through foot edges
- **Best for**: Glute activation, isolation, mind-muscle connection
- **Limitations**: Short ROM, can't load heavy
- **Perfect for**: Pre-workout activation, burnouts, learning to feel glutes

Frog pumps are the ultimate glute isolation exercise. If you struggle to feel your glutes during other exercises, start with frog pumps. They teach you what glute activation actually feels like.
