# 5x5 Workout Program: The Simple System for Building Strength

Sometimes the best programs are the simplest. The 5x5 workout has been building strong bodies for over 60 years because it works. Five sets of five reps on compound lifts, adding weight each session. No complexity, no fluff—just progressive overload and results.

## What is a 5x5 Program?

A 5x5 program centers around performing 5 sets of 5 reps on major compound exercises, typically training 3 days per week, with systematic weight increases every session.

### Core Principles

**The Formula:**
- 5 sets × 5 reps per exercise
- 3 workouts per week (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri)
- Compound movements (squat, bench, deadlift, row, press)
- Add weight each successful session (typically 5 lbs/2.5 kg)
- Simple alternating workout structure

**Why 5x5 Works:**
- **Heavy enough** to build strength (not just endurance)
- **Enough volume** to stimulate muscle growth
- **Not too much** to prevent recovery
- **Simple to progress** with small, consistent increases

### A Brief History

The 5x5 concept dates back to the 1960s, with Reg Park (Arnold Schwarzenegger's idol) as a notable early advocate. Bill Starr popularized it in the 1970s with "The Strongest Shall Survive." Modern versions like StrongLifts 5x5 and variations of Starting Strength have introduced millions to the protocol.

## The Classic 5x5 Structure

### StrongLifts 5x5 (Most Popular)

**Workout A:**
- Squat: 5 × 5
- Bench Press: 5 × 5
- Barbell Row: 5 × 5

**Workout B:**
- Squat: 5 × 5
- Overhead Press: 5 × 5
- Deadlift: 1 × 5

**Weekly Schedule:**
| Week | Monday | Wednesday | Friday |
|------|--------|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Workout A | Workout B | Workout A |
| 2 | Workout B | Workout A | Workout B |
| 3 | Workout A | Workout B | Workout A |
| Continue alternating... |

### Key Points

- **Squat every session:** The foundation of the program
- **Deadlift only 1x5:** Already fatigued from squats, plus deadlifts are demanding
- **Alternate A and B:** Different upper body focus each day
- **Rest 1-2 days between:** Allow recovery

## How Progression Works

### The Linear Progression Model

**Starting Point:**
- Begin with an empty bar or light weight
- Focus on learning perfect form
- Even if it feels too easy, trust the process

**Every Successful Workout:**
- Add 5 lbs (2.5 kg) to squat, bench, row, and press
- Add 10 lbs (5 kg) to deadlift
- "Successful" = completed all 5 sets of 5 reps

**Example Squat Progression:**
| Session | Weight |
|---------|--------|
| 1 | 95 lbs |
| 2 | 100 lbs |
| 3 | 105 lbs |
| 4 | 110 lbs |
| ... | ... |
| 24 | 210 lbs |

That's 115 lbs added in 8 weeks (24 sessions, 3x/week)!

### When You Fail

You won't complete all reps forever. When you fail:

**First Failure:**
- Repeat the same weight next session
- You get 3 attempts at each weight

**Second Failure (same weight):**
- Try one more time next session

**Third Failure (same weight):**
- Deload: reduce weight by 10%
- Work back up with better form/recovery

### Micro-Progression

As weights get heavier, 5 lb jumps become harder. Options:

- **Fractional plates:** 0.5-1.25 lb plates exist
- **Smaller jumps:** 2.5 lb increases on upper body lifts
- **Weekly progression:** Add weight once per week instead of per session

## Exercise Technique Essentials

### Squat

**Setup:**
- Bar on upper back (high bar) or rear delts (low bar)
- Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out
- Core braced, chest up

**Execution:**
- Descend by pushing hips back and bending knees
- Reach parallel (hip crease below knee top)
- Drive up through whole foot
- Lockout hips and knees at top

**Common Errors:**
- Not hitting depth
- Knees caving inward
- Forward lean (bar drifting forward)
- Heels rising

### Bench Press

**Setup:**
- Lie on bench, eyes under bar
- Feet flat on floor
- Grip just outside shoulder width
- Retract shoulder blades, slight arch

**Execution:**
- Unrack with straight arms
- Lower bar to mid-chest (around nipple line)
- Touch chest, pause briefly
- Press up in slight arc back toward face
- Lockout over shoulders

**Common Errors:**
- Bouncing off chest
- Flaring elbows 90 degrees
- Butt lifting off bench
- Inconsistent bar path

### Barbell Row

**Setup:**
- Stand over bar, feet hip-width
- Hinge forward, flat back
- Grip just outside knees
- Torso roughly 45 degrees (or more horizontal)

**Execution:**
- Pull bar to lower chest/upper abdomen
- Squeeze shoulder blades at top
- Lower with control
- Reset position each rep

**Common Errors:**
- Using momentum/swinging
- Rounding lower back
- Standing too upright
- Not pulling to body

### Overhead Press

**Setup:**
- Bar in front rack position (on shoulders)
- Grip just outside shoulders
- Elbows slightly in front of bar
- Feet hip-width, core braced

**Execution:**
- Press bar straight up
- Move head back slightly to clear chin
- As bar passes face, push head through
- Lockout with bar over mid-foot

**Common Errors:**
- Pressing around face (bar goes forward)
- Excessive back lean
- Not locking out fully
- Grip too wide

### Deadlift

**Setup:**
- Bar over mid-foot
- Feet hip-width
- Bend and grip bar (just outside legs)
- Flat back, chest up, shoulders over or slightly ahead of bar

**Execution:**
- Push floor away while lifting chest
- Bar stays close to body
- Stand tall, lock hips and knees together
- Lower by hinging hips, then bending knees

**Common Errors:**
- Starting with bar too far away
- Rounding lower back
- Jerking the bar
- Hyperextending at top

## Rest Between Sets

### General Guidelines

| Lift | Weight Relative to Max | Rest Time |
|------|------------------------|-----------|
| Easy | Light warmup | 1 minute |
| Moderate | Working weight, manageable | 1.5-2 minutes |
| Challenging | Working weight, difficult | 3 minutes |
| Very Hard | Near failure | 3-5 minutes |

### The Recovery Reality

As weights increase, you'll need more rest. Don't rush. It's better to rest 5 minutes and complete your set than rest 2 minutes and fail.

**Minimum Effective Rest:**
- Squat: 2-3 minutes (up to 5 when heavy)
- Bench: 2-3 minutes
- Row: 2 minutes
- Press: 2-3 minutes
- Deadlift: 2-3 minutes

## Warming Up for 5x5

### Sample Warm-Up Protocol

**For a 225 lb squat working set:**

| Set | Reps | Weight | Purpose |
|-----|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | 5 | 45 lbs (bar) | Movement prep |
| 2 | 5 | 95 lbs | Grooving pattern |
| 3 | 3 | 135 lbs | Building toward work weight |
| 4 | 2 | 185 lbs | Priming nervous system |
| 5-9 | 5 | 225 lbs | Working sets |

### Warm-Up Principles

- **Start with the bar:** Every time, every lift
- **Decrease reps as weight increases:** Save energy for work sets
- **Don't rush:** 60-90 seconds between warm-up sets
- **Gauge readiness:** Warm-ups tell you how the day will go

## Programming Variations

### Starting Strength (Rippetoe)

Similar but with some differences:

**Workout A:**
- Squat: 3 × 5
- Bench Press OR Overhead Press: 3 × 5
- Deadlift: 1 × 5

**Workout B:**
- Squat: 3 × 5
- Overhead Press OR Bench Press: 3 × 5
- Power Clean: 5 × 3

**Key Differences:**
- Only 3 × 5 (not 5 × 5)
- Includes power cleans
- Different exercise rotation

### Madcow 5x5 (Intermediate)

For those who've milked linear gains:

**Structure:**
- Week-to-week progression (not session-to-session)
- Ramping sets (work up to one heavy set of 5)
- Adds back-off sets and accessories
- Better for intermediate lifters

### 5x5 with Accessories

Add after main lifts (optional):

**Workout A:**
- Squat 5x5, Bench 5x5, Row 5x5
- *Add:* Dips 3x8, Plank 3x30s

**Workout B:**
- Squat 5x5, Press 5x5, Deadlift 1x5
- *Add:* Chin-ups 3x8, Back Extension 3x10

## Common 5x5 Questions

### How Long Should 5x5 Take?

- **Beginner (lighter weights):** 45-60 minutes
- **Intermediate (heavier weights):** 60-90 minutes
- **Including warm-up and rest:** Up to 90 minutes

### Can I Add Cardio?

Yes, but don't overdo it:
- Light cardio (walking, easy cycling) is fine
- Intense cardio may impair recovery
- Do cardio after lifting or on off days
- Prioritize recovery if strength stalls

### What About Abs?

The compound lifts work your core significantly. But you can add:
- Planks: 3 × 30-60 seconds
- Hanging leg raises: 3 × 10
- Ab wheel rollouts: 3 × 8-10

**Add at the end** of 1-2 workouts per week.

### Can I Substitute Exercises?

Ideally, no. The program is built around these specific movements. However:

| If you can't... | Substitute with... |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Back squat | Front squat, safety bar squat |
| Barbell bench | Dumbbell bench (harder to progress) |
| Barbell row | Pendlay row, cable row |
| Deadlift | Trap bar deadlift, Romanian deadlift |
| Overhead press | Seated press, dumbbell press |

### When Should I Move to a Different Program?

When linear progression stalls repeatedly:
- You've deloaded 2-3 times on the same lift
- You can't add weight for 2-3 weeks
- You've been on the program for 4-6+ months

Time for an intermediate program (Texas Method, Madcow, 5/3/1).

## Tips for Success

### 1. Prioritize Recovery

- **Sleep 7-9 hours:** Non-negotiable
- **Eat enough:** You can't build strength in a deficit
- **Manage stress:** Life stress affects gym performance

### 2. Master Form First

- Record yourself lifting
- Study technique videos
- Consider a few coaching sessions
- Don't ego lift—form always matters

### 3. Be Patient

- The program works over months, not weeks
- Early sessions feel easy; that's by design
- Trust the process even when it seems slow

### 4. Don't Modify Too Much

- The program is tested and proven
- Adding exercises reduces recovery
- If you want a different program, do a different program

### 5. Track Everything

- Log weights, sets, reps
- Note how you felt
- Track sleep and nutrition
- Data helps you troubleshoot stalls

## The Bottom Line

The 5x5 workout program is a proven path to building strength. It's simple, effective, and has worked for decades. The only requirements are:

1. **Show up** three times per week
2. **Lift** with good form
3. **Add weight** when successful
4. **Recover** properly between sessions
5. **Be patient** and trust the process

You don't need fancy equipment, complicated periodization, or secret techniques. You need consistency, progressive overload, and time.

Start with the bar if needed. Add 5 pounds each session. In 3 months, you'll be lifting weights that seem impossible today. In 6 months, you'll be genuinely strong. In a year, you'll have transformed.

Five sets. Five reps. Unlimited potential.
