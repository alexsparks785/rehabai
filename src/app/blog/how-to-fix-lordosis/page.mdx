# How to Fix Lordosis: Exercises for Excessive Lower Back Curve

Lordosis refers to the natural inward curve of your lower back. Some curve is normal and healthy—it's part of your spine's shock-absorbing design. But when that curve becomes excessive (hyperlordosis), it can cause lower back pain, hip discomfort, and postural problems.

## What Is Hyperlordosis?

Hyperlordosis is an exaggerated inward curve of the lumbar spine. You might recognize it as:

- A pronounced arch in the lower back
- The belly pushing forward
- The butt sticking out prominently
- A "Donald Duck" posture

This posture compresses the facet joints in your lower back, tightens the hip flexors, and weakens the core and glutes over time.

## What Causes Excessive Lordosis?

### Muscular Imbalances
- **Tight hip flexors** pull the pelvis forward into anterior tilt
- **Tight lower back muscles** pull the spine into more arch
- **Weak abdominals** can't counteract the forward pull
- **Weak glutes** fail to posteriorly tilt the pelvis

### Lifestyle Factors
- Prolonged sitting (shortens hip flexors)
- High heels (shifts weight forward, increasing curve)
- Pregnancy (belly weight pulls pelvis forward)
- Belly fat (same mechanism as pregnancy)
- Weak core from sedentary lifestyle

### Other Causes
- Spondylolisthesis (vertebral slippage)
- Disc degeneration
- Poor postural habits
- Previous injury

## Self-Assessment

### The Wall Test
1. Stand with your back against a wall
2. Your head, upper back, and butt should touch the wall
3. Slide your hand behind your lower back

**Results:**
- If your hand slides through easily with lots of space, you likely have hyperlordosis
- Normal: you should be able to slide a flat hand through, but not much more
- If you can't fit your hand at all, you may have reduced lordosis (flat back)

### The Lying Test
1. Lie on your back on a hard surface
2. Check the space under your lower back

**Results:**
- Large gap you can fit your fist through = likely hyperlordosis
- Small gap for a flat hand = normal
- No gap = possible flat back

## Exercises to Fix Hyperlordosis

### Phase 1: Release Tight Muscles

#### 1. Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling Lunge)
**Why:** Tight hip flexors are the #1 driver of anterior pelvic tilt and lordosis.

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on your right knee with left foot forward
2. Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt)
3. Squeeze your right glute
4. Shift your weight forward slightly
5. Keep your torso upright—don't lean forward
6. Hold for 60-90 seconds per side

**Key:** The glute squeeze and pelvic tuck are essential. Without them, you won't effectively stretch the hip flexor.

#### 2. Quad/Rectus Femoris Stretch
**Why:** The rectus femoris crosses both the hip and knee, contributing to anterior pelvic tilt.

**How to do it:**
1. From the kneeling hip flexor position
2. Grab your back foot and pull it toward your butt
3. Maintain the posterior pelvic tilt
4. Hold for 60 seconds per side

#### 3. Lower Back Release (Child's Pose)
**Why:** Stretches the tight erector spinae that pull your spine into extension.

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on the floor
2. Sit back toward your heels
3. Reach your arms forward on the floor
4. Let your forehead rest on the ground
5. Focus on rounding your lower back
6. Hold for 60-90 seconds

#### 4. Cat-Cow (Emphasize Cat)
**Why:** Mobilizes the spine and teaches posterior pelvic tilt.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Cow: Drop your belly, lift your head and tailbone
3. Cat: Round your back toward the ceiling, tuck your chin and tailbone
4. Hold the "cat" position for 3-5 seconds
5. Do 10-15 reps, emphasizing the cat (rounded) position

### Phase 2: Strengthen Weak Muscles

#### 5. Dead Bug
**Why:** Strengthens the deep core while teaching pelvic control.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with arms reaching toward the ceiling
2. Lift your legs to 90 degrees at hips and knees
3. Press your lower back firmly into the floor
4. Slowly lower your right arm overhead and left leg toward the floor
5. Don't let your lower back arch off the floor
6. Return to start and switch sides
7. Do 10-12 reps per side

**Key:** If your back arches, you've gone too far. Reduce range of motion.

#### 6. Posterior Pelvic Tilt (Lying)
**Why:** Teaches the movement pattern you need to correct lordosis.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat
2. Flatten your lower back into the floor by tilting your pelvis backward
3. Imagine pulling your belly button down and your pubic bone up
4. Hold for 5-10 seconds
5. Do 15-20 reps

**Progression:** Do this standing against a wall.

#### 7. Glute Bridges with Posterior Tilt
**Why:** Strengthens glutes while reinforcing proper pelvic position.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent
2. Flatten your lower back first (posterior tilt)
3. Drive through your heels to lift your hips
4. At the top, squeeze your glutes—don't overarch your back
5. Your body should be a straight line from knees to shoulders
6. Hold for 2-3 seconds
7. Lower slowly
8. Do 15-20 reps

#### 8. Plank with Posterior Tilt
**Why:** Strengthens the entire anterior core in a functional position.

**How to do it:**
1. Get into a forearm plank position
2. Tuck your tailbone under (round your lower back slightly)
3. Squeeze your glutes
4. Keep your abs braced
5. Hold for 30-60 seconds

**Common mistake:** Most people plank with too much arch. Aim for a slight posterior tilt.

#### 9. Reverse Crunch
**Why:** Targets the lower abs that posteriorly tilt the pelvis.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back with hands at your sides
2. Lift your legs to 90 degrees at hips and knees
3. Contract your abs to lift your hips off the floor
4. Roll your pelvis toward your ribcage
5. Lower slowly with control
6. Do 12-15 reps

#### 10. Bird Dog
**Why:** Builds core stability while training neutral spine position.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Find a neutral spine (slight natural curve, not excessive)
3. Extend your right arm forward and left leg back
4. Keep your hips level—don't rotate
5. Hold for 2-3 seconds
6. Return and switch sides
7. Do 10 reps per side

## Your Daily Routine

### Morning (5 minutes)
- Cat-cow: 10 reps (emphasize cat)
- Hip flexor stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Posterior pelvic tilt: 10 reps

### Midday (3 minutes)
- Standing hip flexor stretch: 30 seconds per side
- Standing posterior pelvic tilt: 10 reps
- Wall test check-in

### Evening (10 minutes)
- Child's pose: 90 seconds
- Hip flexor + quad stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Dead bugs: 10 per side
- Glute bridges with posterior tilt: 15 reps
- Plank with posterior tilt: 30-45 seconds

## Postural Corrections

### Standing
- Think "stacked": ears over shoulders, shoulders over hips, hips over ankles
- Gently engage your lower abs
- Untuck your tailbone slightly (but don't clench your glutes constantly)
- Distribute weight evenly on both feet

### Sitting
- Sit on your sit bones, not your tailbone
- Keep your pelvis neutral (not tilted forward)
- Use lumbar support if needed
- Feet flat on the floor

### Sleeping
- If you sleep on your back, place a pillow under your knees to reduce lumbar curve
- Side sleeping with a pillow between your knees helps maintain pelvic alignment
- Avoid sleeping on your stomach, which increases lordosis

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Overcorrecting:** Some people tuck so hard they flatten their spine completely. A small natural curve is normal and healthy.

**Only stretching, not strengthening:** You need both. Stretching releases tight muscles; strengthening builds the muscles that maintain proper position.

**Ignoring the glutes:** Weak glutes are part of the problem. Strengthen them, not just your abs.

**Constantly clenching:** You don't need to walk around squeezing your abs and glutes all day. Build the strength, then let good posture become automatic.

**Expecting fast results:** Postural changes take 6-12 weeks of consistent work.

## When to Seek Help

See a professional if:
- You have significant pain, especially radiating pain
- Your lordosis is severe or rapidly worsening
- You have numbness or weakness in your legs
- The condition doesn't improve after 8-12 weeks
- You've had spinal surgery or have a known spinal condition

## The Bottom Line

Hyperlordosis is usually a muscle imbalance problem: tight hip flexors and lower back muscles pulling against weak abs and glutes. The fix is straightforward—stretch what's tight, strengthen what's weak, and practice good posture throughout your day.

Start with hip flexor stretches and dead bugs—they address the most common culprits. Add glute work to build the posterior chain that counteracts the forward pull. Be patient; postural changes happen gradually.

Most people see meaningful improvement within 6-8 weeks of consistent daily work. Your spine didn't get this way overnight, and it won't change overnight. But it will change.
