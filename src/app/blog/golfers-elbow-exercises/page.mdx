Golfer's elbow causes pain on the inside of the elbow, affecting the tendons of the forearm flexor muscles. Like its counterpart tennis elbow, it affects far more than just athletes—anyone who performs repetitive gripping, throwing, or wrist movements can develop this condition.

This guide covers the exercises proven to help, along with stretches and strategies for lasting relief.

## What Is Golfer's Elbow?

Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) affects the tendons that attach to the medial (inside) part of the elbow. These tendons connect the muscles that flex your wrist and fingers and rotate your forearm inward.

### Symptoms
- Pain on the inside of the elbow
- Pain radiating into the forearm
- Weakness in grip and wrist
- Pain when making a fist or gripping
- Pain when flexing the wrist against resistance
- Stiffness in the elbow
- Numbness or tingling in ring and pinky fingers (occasionally)

### Common Causes
- Repetitive gripping or throwing
- Golf (especially poor swing mechanics)
- Throwing sports (baseball, javelin)
- Racquet sports
- Weightlifting (especially curls and pull-ups)
- Occupational: carpentry, plumbing, typing
- Climbing

### Golfer's Elbow vs. Tennis Elbow

| Feature | Golfer's Elbow | Tennis Elbow |
|---------|---------------|--------------|
| Location | Inside of elbow | Outside of elbow |
| Muscles affected | Wrist flexors | Wrist extensors |
| Pain with | Gripping, wrist flexion | Gripping, wrist extension |
| Less common | Yes (about 10-20% as common) | More common |

## Treatment Principles

Golfer's elbow treatment follows similar principles to tennis elbow:

1. **Reduce aggravating activities** initially
2. **Progressive loading** through eccentric exercises
3. **Address contributing factors** (technique, workload, strength imbalances)
4. **Gradual return** to full activity
5. **Ongoing prevention** exercises

## Stretching Exercises

### Wrist Flexor Stretch
The primary stretch for golfer's elbow.

1. Extend arm in front, palm facing up
2. Use other hand to pull fingers down and back
3. Keep elbow straight
4. Feel stretch on inner forearm
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Repeat 3 times, several times daily

### Wrist Extensor Stretch
For overall forearm flexibility:

1. Extend arm in front, palm facing down
2. Use other hand to push fingers down
3. Keep elbow straight
4. Feel stretch on outer forearm
5. Hold 30 seconds
6. Repeat 3 times

### Pronator Stretch
1. Arm at side, elbow bent 90 degrees
2. Palm facing up
3. Use other hand to rotate forearm so palm faces down
4. Feel stretch in inner forearm
5. Hold 20-30 seconds each side

### Prayer Stretch (Reverse)
1. Place backs of hands together, fingers pointing down
2. Raise hands toward chest level
3. Feel stretch in wrists and inner forearms
4. Hold 20-30 seconds

## Strengthening Exercises

### Eccentric Wrist Flexion

The primary exercise for golfer's elbow.

1. Sit with forearm on thigh or table, wrist hanging off, palm up
2. Hold light dumbbell (1-3 lbs)
3. Use other hand to assist lifting wrist into flexion (curled up)
4. Remove helper hand
5. Slowly lower the weight (3-5 seconds) using only the affected arm
6. Use helper hand to lift again
7. 3 sets of 15 reps, once or twice daily

**Key points:**
- Focus on the slow lowering phase
- Lifting is assisted; lowering is the exercise
- Start very light

### Reverse Tyler Twist (with FlexBar)

1. Hold FlexBar vertically, affected hand at bottom
2. Grip bar with wrist flexed (curled)
3. Grasp top of bar with other hand
4. Twist the bar with the top hand while holding bottom firm
5. Extend both arms in front
6. Slowly release the twist with the affected (bottom) hand
7. 3 sets of 15 reps

### Wrist Curls (Eccentric Emphasis)

1. Same setup as eccentric flexion
2. Can also perform with both the lifting and lowering
3. Lower for 3 seconds, lift for 1 second
4. 3 sets of 15 reps

### Pronation/Supination

1. Hold dumbbell or hammer by the end of handle
2. Forearm supported, wrist hanging off edge
3. Slowly rotate palm down (pronation) then palm up (supination)
4. 3 sets of 15 reps

### Grip Strengthening

Once acute pain has decreased:

1. Squeeze stress ball or therapy putty
2. Hold 5 seconds
3. Release fully
4. 2-3 sets of 15 reps
5. Progress resistance gradually

### Finger Extension

For muscle balance:

1. Place rubber band around all fingers
2. Spread fingers apart against resistance
3. Hold 3 seconds
4. 2-3 sets of 15 reps

## Progressive Program

### Weeks 1-2
**Daily:**
- Wrist flexor stretch: 3 × 30 seconds
- Pronator stretch: 3 × 20 seconds
- Eccentric wrist flexion: 3 × 10 (very light)
- Ice after exercises if needed

### Weeks 3-4
**Daily:**
- All stretches
- Eccentric wrist flexion: 3 × 15
- Begin grip exercises: 2 × 10

### Weeks 5-8
**Daily or every other day:**
- Stretching routine
- Eccentric wrist flexion: 3 × 15 (progress weight as tolerated)
- Wrist curls (full range): 2 × 15
- Pronation/supination: 2 × 15
- Grip strengthening: 3 × 15

### Weeks 9-12
**3-4 times per week:**
- Full exercise routine
- Gradual return to activities
- Continue daily stretching

## Activity Modifications

### Golf
- Check grip pressure (lighter grip reduces strain)
- Assess swing mechanics with a pro
- Strengthen grip and forearm before increasing play
- Warm up properly before playing

### Throwing Sports
- Ensure proper throwing mechanics
- Adequate warm-up before throwing
- Gradual progression of throwing volume
- Strengthen shoulder and core (they control arm position)

### Weightlifting
- Avoid exercises that cause pain (modify or substitute)
- Watch grip on pulling exercises
- May need to reduce weight on curls, rows, pull-ups
- Ensure proper wrist position (neutral when possible)

### Computer Work/Typing
- Ergonomic keyboard and mouse position
- Wrist in neutral position
- Take frequent breaks
- Avoid resting wrists on hard surfaces

## Complementary Treatments

### Ice
- 15-20 minutes after exercises or aggravating activities
- Ice massage with frozen cup can be helpful

### Counterforce Brace
- Worn on forearm, 1-2 inches below elbow
- Reduces strain on the tendon attachment
- Use during activities, not 24/7

### Self-Massage
1. Find tender spots on inner forearm
2. Apply pressure with thumb or massage tool
3. Hold 30-60 seconds on each spot
4. Can also use cross-friction massage perpendicular to muscle fibers

### Heat Before Activity
- Warm the forearm muscles before exercises or activity
- Heating pad or warm towel for 5-10 minutes
- Followed by gentle stretching

## Timeline Expectations

- **Weeks 1-2:** Pain management phase, begin gentle exercises
- **Weeks 3-6:** Progressive loading, gradual improvement
- **Weeks 6-12:** Significant improvement, return to most activities
- **3-6 months:** Full recovery in most cases

Golfer's elbow often takes longer to resolve than expected. Consistency with exercises is more important than intensity.

## Prevention

### Continue Exercises
Even after pain resolves:
- Maintain eccentric exercises 2-3 times weekly
- Daily stretching before activities
- This prevents recurrence

### Strengthen the Entire Chain
- Shoulder stability affects elbow loading
- Core strength influences arm mechanics
- Don't just focus on the forearm

### Warm Up Properly
Before golf, throwing, or lifting:
- Light cardio to increase blood flow
- Forearm stretches
- Progressive loading (start light)

### Monitor Training Load
- Avoid sudden spikes in activity
- The 10% rule: increase volume no more than 10% per week
- Allow adequate recovery between sessions

### Technique Assessment
Poor mechanics increase tendon strain:
- Get coaching for sports techniques
- Review lifting form
- Assess workplace ergonomics

## When to Seek Help

**See a healthcare provider if:**
- No improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent exercise
- Significant weakness persists
- Numbness or tingling in fingers
- Symptoms are getting progressively worse
- Pain is severe or affecting sleep

**Additional treatment options:**
- Physical therapy for hands-on treatment
- Corticosteroid injection (short-term relief)
- PRP injection
- Shockwave therapy
- Surgery (rarely needed)

## The Bottom Line

Golfer's elbow responds well to targeted exercises, particularly eccentric loading of the wrist flexors. While healing takes time, most cases resolve with consistent conservative treatment.

**Key takeaways:**
- Eccentric wrist flexion is the primary exercise
- Start with very light resistance
- Progress gradually based on symptoms
- Continue exercises after pain resolves to prevent recurrence
- Address contributing factors (technique, workload)
- Expect 2-3 months for significant improvement

The tendon needs progressive loading to heal and strengthen. Stay consistent with your exercises, modify aggravating activities, and the majority of golfer's elbow cases resolve without surgery.
