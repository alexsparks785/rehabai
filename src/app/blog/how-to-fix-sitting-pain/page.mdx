# How to Fix Sitting Pain: Exercises for Desk Workers

If you're reading this, you're probably sitting—and you probably don't feel great about it. Sitting for hours creates a predictable pattern of tightness, weakness, and pain. The good news: this pattern is reversible. Here's how to undo the damage of desk life.

## The Sitting Pain Pattern

Prolonged sitting creates a consistent set of problems:

**What gets tight:**
- Hip flexors (shortened position for hours)
- Chest and front shoulders
- Hamstrings (yes, even though they're "lengthened")
- Upper traps and neck muscles
- Lower back (sometimes)

**What gets weak:**
- Glutes (inactive and stretched)
- Deep core stabilizers
- Mid-back muscles (rhomboids, lower traps)
- Neck flexors

**What hurts:**
- Lower back (most common)
- Neck and upper shoulders
- Between shoulder blades
- Hips
- Tailbone/sit bones

## Why Sitting Causes Pain

### Spinal Compression
Sitting loads your spine more than standing. Without movement, discs lose hydration and vertebrae compress.

### Muscle Deactivation
Your glutes essentially turn off when you sit. Your core doesn't need to stabilize. These muscles weaken from disuse.

### Postural Drift
You start sitting well, then slowly slouch. Shoulders round forward. Head drifts forward. Lower back rounds. This position strains muscles and joints.

### Reduced Blood Flow
Sitting compresses blood vessels and reduces circulation, particularly to your legs and spine.

### Static Loading
Even "good" posture becomes painful if held too long. Your body needs movement variety, not a perfect position.

## Quick Fixes for Immediate Relief

### The 5-Second Reset
Do this every 30-60 minutes:
1. Squeeze your glutes hard for 5 seconds
2. Pull your shoulders back and down
3. Tuck your chin (make a double chin)
4. Take one deep breath
5. Release

### Standing Decompression
Stand up and do this sequence:
1. Reach arms overhead and lean back slightly (gentle extension)
2. Side bend left, hold 5 seconds
3. Side bend right, hold 5 seconds
4. Let arms hang, roll shoulders backward 5 times
5. Total time: 30 seconds

### Hip Flexor Quick Release
While standing:
1. Step one foot back
2. Tuck your tailbone under
3. Squeeze the glute of your back leg
4. Hold 30 seconds per side

## Exercises to Reverse Sitting Damage

### Address the Hips

#### 1. Hip Flexor Stretch (Kneeling)
**Why:** Sitting shortens your hip flexors for hours. This is the antidote.

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on your right knee, left foot forward
2. Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt)
3. Squeeze your right glute
4. Lean forward slightly until you feel the stretch
5. Reach your right arm overhead for a deeper stretch
6. Hold for 90-120 seconds per side

**Key:** The tailbone tuck and glute squeeze are essential. Without them, you're just hanging on your hip capsule.

#### 2. Glute Bridge
**Why:** Reactivates glutes that have been asleep all day.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Push through your heels to lift your hips
3. Squeeze your glutes hard at the top
4. Hold for 3 seconds
5. Lower slowly
6. Do 15-20 reps

#### 3. 90-90 Hip Stretch
**Why:** Opens the hips in rotation, countering the fixed sitting position.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with both knees bent at 90 degrees
2. Front shin perpendicular to your body
3. Back shin behind you, also at 90 degrees
4. Sit up tall, feel the stretch in your front hip
5. Hold 60-90 seconds, then switch sides

### Address the Back

#### 4. Cat-Cow
**Why:** Moves your spine through flexion and extension, reversing static sitting posture.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Cow: Drop belly, lift head and tailbone, arch back
3. Cat: Round back, tuck chin and tailbone
4. Move slowly, coordinating with your breath
5. Do 15-20 reps

#### 5. Thoracic Extension (Foam Roller)
**Why:** Specifically targets the rounded upper back from desk posture.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie with foam roller across your mid-back
2. Support your head with your hands
3. Gently extend backward over the roller
4. Hold for a breath, return to start
5. Move the roller up and down your upper back
6. Spend 60-90 seconds total

#### 6. Child's Pose with Reach
**Why:** Stretches the entire back while decompressing the spine.

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel and sit back toward your heels
2. Reach arms forward on the floor
3. Let your forehead rest down
4. Walk hands to the right, stretch left side
5. Hold 30 seconds, then walk hands left
6. Return to center, hold 30 seconds

### Address the Shoulders and Neck

#### 7. Doorway Chest Stretch
**Why:** Opens the chest that rounds forward while typing.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand in a doorway
2. Place forearms on the door frame, elbows at shoulder height
3. Step one foot forward
4. Lean through the doorway until you feel a stretch across your chest
5. Hold for 45-60 seconds

#### 8. Chin Tucks
**Why:** Strengthens neck flexors and reverses forward head posture.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit or stand with good posture
2. Pull your chin straight back (make a double chin)
3. Hold for 5-10 seconds
4. Release and repeat
5. Do 15-20 reps

#### 9. Wall Angels
**Why:** Strengthens mid-back muscles while stretching chest.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against a wall
2. Feet a few inches from the wall
3. Press your lower back, upper back, and head into the wall
4. Raise arms to goal-post position (elbows bent 90 degrees)
5. Slide arms up and down the wall, keeping contact
6. Do 10-15 reps

### Build Core Stability

#### 10. Dead Bug
**Why:** Builds the deep core strength that sitting erodes.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back, arms reaching toward ceiling
2. Lift legs to 90 degrees at hips and knees
3. Press your lower back flat into the floor
4. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg
5. Don't let your back arch
6. Return and switch sides
7. Do 10-12 per side

#### 11. Bird Dog
**Why:** Teaches spinal stability with movement.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Extend right arm forward and left leg back
3. Keep your back flat and hips level
4. Hold for 3 seconds
5. Return and switch sides
6. Do 10 per side

## Your Daily Anti-Sitting Routine

### Morning (5 minutes)
Before you sit down for the day:
- Cat-cow: 10 reps
- Hip flexor stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Glute bridges: 15 reps
- Wall angels: 10 reps

### Hourly (30 seconds)
Every hour, stand and do:
- The 5-second reset
- 5 air squats
- 5 shoulder rolls

### Midday (5 minutes)
Break up your sitting with:
- Standing hip flexor stretch: 45 seconds per side
- Doorway chest stretch: 45 seconds
- Cat-cow: 10 reps
- Chin tucks: 15 reps

### Evening (10 minutes)
Reverse the day's damage:
- Thoracic foam rolling: 90 seconds
- Hip flexor stretch: 90 seconds per side
- 90-90 stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Child's pose: 60 seconds
- Dead bugs: 10 per side
- Glute bridges: 20 reps
- Chest stretch: 60 seconds

## Workstation Setup Matters

### Chair Height
- Feet flat on the floor
- Knees at or slightly below hip level
- Thighs parallel to floor

### Monitor Position
- Top of screen at eye level
- About arm's length away
- Directly in front of you (not off to one side)

### Keyboard and Mouse
- Elbows at roughly 90 degrees
- Wrists neutral (not bent up or down)
- Shoulders relaxed, not shrugged

### The Best Position Is Your Next Position
Even perfect ergonomics won't save you from pain if you don't move. Fidget. Shift. Stand up. Sit back down differently.

## Movement Strategies for Desk Work

### The 30-30-30 Rule
Every 30 minutes, move for 30 seconds through 30 degrees of range. Even small movements help.

### Phone Calls Standing
Whenever you're on a call, stand or walk.

### Walking Meetings
If you don't need to share screens, walk and talk.

### Pomodoro with Movement
Work for 25 minutes, then take a 5-minute movement break.

### Alternate Sitting and Standing
If you have a standing desk, don't just trade all-day sitting for all-day standing. Alternate every 30-60 minutes.

## When to See a Professional

Get evaluated if:
- Pain is severe or getting worse despite these strategies
- You have numbness, tingling, or weakness in your arms or legs
- Pain radiates down your leg (sciatica symptoms)
- You have difficulty sitting for even short periods
- Pain significantly impacts your work or daily activities

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Only stretching, not strengthening:** You need both. Stretching tight muscles helps; strengthening weak muscles prevents the tightness from returning.

**Doing a big routine once a day:** Frequency matters more than duration. Many small movement breaks beat one long session.

**Perfecting your sitting posture:** There is no perfect posture. The goal is movement variety, not a single ideal position.

**Ignoring the hips:** Lower back pain often originates from tight hip flexors and weak glutes. Address the whole system.

**Pushing through pain:** Discomfort during stretching is fine. Sharp pain is a signal to stop.

## The Bottom Line

Sitting pain is predictable—and reversible. The human body isn't designed to hold any position for hours. It's designed to move.

Start with hip flexor stretches and glute bridges—they address the most common issues. Add hourly movement breaks. Build a short morning and evening routine.

Most people see significant improvement within 1-2 weeks of consistent practice. The key is frequency: move often, in many directions, throughout the day.

Your body will adapt to whatever you ask of it. Ask it to move, and it will feel better. Ask it to sit frozen for 8 hours, and it will complain. The choice is yours.
