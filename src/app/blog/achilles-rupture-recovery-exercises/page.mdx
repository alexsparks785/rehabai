# Achilles Tendon Rupture Recovery: Exercises After Surgery or Conservative Treatment

An Achilles tendon rupture is a significant injury requiring careful, progressive rehabilitation—whether you had surgery or are managing conservatively. This guide provides phase-by-phase exercises to help you regain strength, mobility, and function safely.

## Understanding Achilles Rupture Recovery

The Achilles tendon is the strongest tendon in your body, connecting your calf muscles to your heel bone. When it ruptures, the healing process is slow—tendons have poor blood supply compared to muscle.

**Treatment options:**
- **Surgical repair:** Tendon is sutured back together
- **Conservative management:** Functional bracing without surgery
- **Both approaches work:** Similar long-term outcomes with proper rehab

**Recovery timeline:**
- Full healing: 6-12 months
- Return to walking: 3-4 months
- Return to sport: 6-12 months depending on demands

## Important: Follow Your Surgeon's Protocol

This guide provides general rehabilitation principles. Your surgeon or physical therapist may have specific protocols based on your surgical technique or healing progress. **Always defer to their instructions**, especially regarding:
- Weight-bearing progression
- Boot/brace weaning
- Stretching restrictions
- Return to activity timing

## Phase 1: Protection Phase (Weeks 0-6)

During this phase, the tendon is healing and very vulnerable. You'll likely be in a boot or cast.

### Goals:
- Protect the repair/healing tendon
- Maintain circulation
- Prevent complications (blood clots, stiffness elsewhere)
- Begin gentle, protected motion

### Exercises (Begin After Clearance—Usually Week 2)

#### Ankle Pumps (Controlled Range)
**How to do it:**
1. Sitting or lying with leg elevated
2. Gently point toes down (plantarflexion) and pull up (within boot limits)
3. Stay within pain-free range
4. 20-30 reps, several times daily

**Note:** Initially keep within boot/cast limits. Do not pull into dorsiflexion (toes toward shin) aggressively.

#### Toe Wiggling and Spreading
Keep toes mobile to prevent stiffness.
- Curl and spread toes
- 20 reps, multiple times daily

#### Seated Knee Flexion/Extension
Maintain knee mobility without stressing Achilles.
- Sitting: bend and straighten knee gently
- 15-20 reps

#### Hip Exercises (Lying)
Maintain hip strength—you'll need it for crutch walking.
- Hip abduction (leg out to side)
- Hip flexion (knee to chest)
- Glute squeezes
- 15-20 reps each, 1-2 sets

#### Upper Body Strength
Stay active above the injury.
- Seated exercises for arms and core
- Maintain fitness where possible

### Blood Clot Prevention
**Critical:** Achilles injuries have elevated DVT risk.
- Ankle pumps frequently
- Stay hydrated
- Follow anticoagulation protocol if prescribed
- Report calf pain, warmth, or swelling immediately

## Phase 2: Early Motion Phase (Weeks 6-12)

Transition out of the boot begins. Movement increases but loading is still protected.

### Goals:
- Restore ankle range of motion
- Begin weight-bearing progression
- Initiate gentle strengthening
- Normalize gait pattern

### Boot Weaning (Typically Begins Week 6-8)
Follow your protocol exactly. Common approach:
- Remove one heel wedge per week
- Then transition from boot to supportive shoe
- Progress from two crutches to one to none

### Range of Motion Exercises

#### Active Ankle Range of Motion
**How to do it:**
1. Sitting with leg supported
2. Move ankle: point down (plantarflexion), then up toward neutral
3. **Caution:** Don't force dorsiflexion beyond neutral until cleared
4. Circles in both directions

**Reps:** 20-30 in each direction, several times daily

#### Alphabet Exercise
Trace the alphabet with your big toe.
- Engages all ankle motions
- Start with lowercase, progress to uppercase
- 1-2 times through, 2-3 times daily

#### Towel Stretch (Gentle)
**Begin only when cleared for stretching (often week 8-10):**
1. Sit with leg extended
2. Loop towel around foot
3. Gently pull foot toward you (dorsiflexion)
4. Hold 20-30 seconds, very mild stretch only
5. 3-5 repetitions

### Early Strengthening

#### Isometric Calf Contractions
**How to do it:**
1. Sitting, push ball of foot gently against floor or wall
2. Hold 5-10 seconds
3. No movement, just tension
4. 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

#### Theraband Plantarflexion (Low Resistance)
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with leg extended, band around forefoot
2. Gently point foot against resistance
3. Slow and controlled
4. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

**Start with:** Lightest resistance band

#### Seated Heel Raises
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with feet flat
2. Lift heels off ground
3. Lower slowly
4. Both legs together initially
5. 15-20 reps, 2-3 sets

### Gait Training
- Practice heel-toe walking pattern
- Use mirrors for feedback
- Focus on equal weight and timing between legs
- May need gait training with physical therapist

## Phase 3: Progressive Strengthening (Weeks 12-24)

Loading increases significantly. The tendon is stronger but not yet ready for high-speed or explosive activities.

### Goals:
- Build calf strength and endurance
- Progress to single-leg activities
- Normalize walking and stair climbing
- Begin proprioception training

### Progressive Calf Strengthening

#### Standing Bilateral Heel Raises
**How to do it:**
1. Stand holding support for balance
2. Rise onto toes with both feet
3. Pause at top
4. Lower slowly (3 seconds down)
5. Progress by adding weight or reducing support

**Reps:** 3 sets of 15-20

#### Single-Leg Heel Raises (Begin ~Week 16-20)
This is a milestone exercise.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand on injured leg only
2. Rise onto toes
3. Lower slowly (3-4 seconds)
4. Use finger support initially
5. Progress to no support

**Target:** 3 sets of 15-20 with good form before returning to sport

**Note:** It's normal for the injured side to be weaker. Don't rush.

#### Seated Calf Raises (Soleus Focus)
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with weight on knees
2. Raise heels as high as possible
3. Lower slowly
4. Progress weight gradually

**Why soleus matters:** The soleus is a key stabilizer and shock absorber.

#### Eccentric Heel Drops
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on step on both feet
2. Rise onto toes
3. Shift weight to injured leg
4. Lower heel below step level slowly (3-5 seconds)
5. Push back up with both legs

**Reps:** 3 sets of 15

### Mobility and Flexibility

#### Standing Calf Stretch (Gastrocnemius)
**When cleared:**
1. Step forward into lunge position
2. Keep back heel on ground
3. Lean forward until stretch in calf
4. Hold 30 seconds
5. Keep back knee straight

#### Standing Soleus Stretch
1. Same position as above
2. Bend back knee while keeping heel down
3. Shifts stretch to soleus
4. Hold 30 seconds

#### Foam Rolling (Gentle)
Roll calf muscles (not directly on tendon).
- 1-2 minutes per side
- Moderate pressure only

### Balance and Proprioception

#### Single-Leg Balance
**Progression:**
1. Two legs → one leg (injured)
2. Eyes open → eyes closed
3. Stable surface → foam pad
4. Arms out → arms crossed → arms reaching

**Goal:** 30 seconds single-leg balance with eyes closed

#### Weight Shifts
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on injured leg
2. Reach opposite leg forward, back, and to sides
3. Maintain balance
4. 10 reaches each direction

#### Bosu Ball Balance
Once basic balance is solid:
- Stand on Bosu dome side up
- Progress to single leg
- Add catches or reaches

## Phase 4: Return to Running and Sport (Weeks 24+)

High-impact activities begin. The tendon should handle walking and stairs without issue.

### Criteria Before Starting Running
- Pain-free daily activities
- Single-leg heel raise: 25+ reps with good form
- 80% strength compared to uninjured side (if measurable)
- Clearance from medical team

### Walking to Running Progression

**Week 1:** Walk 20-30 minutes, 3-4 times
**Week 2:** Walk 10 min → Jog 2 min → Walk 3 min, repeat 3 times
**Week 3:** Walk 5 min → Jog 5 min → Walk 2 min, repeat 3 times
**Week 4:** Walk 3 min → Jog 8 min → Walk 2 min, repeat 2-3 times
**Progress gradually** to continuous jogging over 6-8 weeks

**Pain rule:** Stop if pain exceeds 3/10 or persists next day.

### Plyometric Progression

**Level 1: Low-Intensity**
- Two-leg hops in place
- Pogo jumps
- Rope skipping (low height)

**Level 2: Moderate**
- Box jumps (step down initially)
- Lateral hops
- Forward/backward line hops

**Level 3: High-Intensity**
- Single-leg hops
- Depth jumps
- Sport-specific jumping drills

**Progression rate:** Stay at each level 2-4 weeks before advancing.

### Sport-Specific Training
Gradual return to sport demands:
1. Sport-specific movements at slow speed
2. Increase speed gradually
3. Add competitive elements
4. Modified practice
5. Full practice
6. Return to competition

## Comparing Surgical vs. Conservative Recovery

### Surgical Repair
**Pros:**
- Lower re-rupture rate (historically)
- May allow earlier mobilization

**Cons:**
- Surgical risks (infection, wound issues)
- Longer initial restriction

### Conservative (Non-Surgical)
**Pros:**
- No surgical risks
- Modern protocols show similar outcomes

**Cons:**
- Historically higher re-rupture rate (but gap narrowing with functional bracing)
- May require longer immobilization

**Key for both:** Quality rehabilitation is the most important factor for outcomes.

## Common Problems and Solutions

### Stiffness (Limited Dorsiflexion)
**Solution:** Consistent stretching, heel raises through full range, manual therapy if needed.

### Weakness
**Solution:** Progressive calf strengthening. Single-leg heel raises are your benchmark.

### Tendon Thickening
**Normal finding.** The healed tendon is often thicker than the original. This doesn't predict problems.

### Re-Rupture
**Highest risk:** Weeks 6-12 during transition out of protection.
**Prevention:** Follow protocol strictly, don't progress too fast, report pain immediately.

### Chronic Achilles Tendinopathy
Some people develop ongoing tendon issues after rupture.
**Management:** Eccentric loading programs, load management, possibly medical consultation.

## Long-Term Maintenance

Even after full recovery:
- Continue calf strengthening 2-3 times per week
- Maintain flexibility
- Warm up thoroughly before activity
- Monitor for early warning signs
- Gradually progress training loads

## When to Contact Your Doctor

- Sudden increase in pain or swelling
- New popping sensation
- Inability to push off foot
- Signs of infection (redness, warmth, drainage at surgical site)
- Calf pain or swelling concerning for blood clot

## Sample Week: Phase 3 (Month 4)

**Monday:**
- Standing heel raises: 3×20
- Single-leg balance: 3×30 sec
- Theraband exercises: 2×20
- Stretching: 5 minutes

**Tuesday:**
- Walking 30 minutes
- Seated heel raises: 3×20
- Bosu balance: 3×45 sec

**Wednesday:**
- Eccentric heel drops: 3×15
- Single-leg heel raises: 3×10 (building)
- Weight shifts: 10 each direction
- Foam rolling: 5 minutes

**Thursday:**
- Rest or gentle walking

**Friday:**
- Single-leg heel raises: 3×12
- Step-ups: 3×15
- Balance board: 3×1 min
- Stretching: 5 minutes

**Weekend:**
- Walking/light activity
- Stretching

## Key Takeaways

1. **Follow your protocol** — surgeon/PT instructions override general guidelines
2. **Don't rush boot weaning** — the tendon is vulnerable during this transition
3. **Single-leg heel raise is your benchmark** — 25+ reps before running
4. **Eccentric exercises are key** — build tendon resilience
5. **Progress plyometrics gradually** — walk → jog → run → jump
6. **Expect a long recovery** — 6-12 months to full activity is normal
7. **Maintenance is forever** — continue calf work to prevent issues
8. **Early pain or setbacks** — report immediately, don't push through

Achilles rupture recovery requires patience, consistency, and trust in the process. With proper rehabilitation, most people return to full activity—including competitive sports. Take it one phase at a time, hit your milestones, and don't skip steps.
