      <h1>How to Fix Frozen Shoulder: Exercises for Adhesive Capsulitis</h1>

      <p>When your shoulder gradually becomes so stiff you can't reach behind your back or lift your arm—that's frozen shoulder. It's one of the most frustrating conditions because it develops slowly, lasts a long time, and severely limits daily activities. Here's how to work through it.</p>

      <h2>What Is Frozen Shoulder?</h2>

      <p>Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) occurs when the capsule surrounding the shoulder joint becomes inflamed and then thickened and tight, forming adhesions. This progressively restricts movement until the shoulder becomes "frozen."</p>

      <p><strong>Classic symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Gradual onset of pain and stiffness</li>
        <li>Difficulty reaching behind back (putting on bra, tucking in shirt)</li>
        <li>Difficulty reaching overhead</li>
        <li>Pain at night, often disrupting sleep</li>
        <li>Limited motion in ALL directions (not just one movement)</li>
        <li>Pain with end-range movements</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>The Three Stages</h2>

      <h3>Stage 1: Freezing (2-9 months)</h3>
      <p>Pain is the dominant symptom. Movement becomes increasingly limited. Pain often worst at night.</p>

      <h3>Stage 2: Frozen (4-12 months)</h3>
      <p>Pain may decrease, but stiffness is severe. Significant loss of motion makes daily tasks difficult.</p>

      <h3>Stage 3: Thawing (5-24 months)</h3>
      <p>Gradual improvement in motion. Stiffness slowly resolves.</p>

      <p><strong>Total duration:</strong> 1-3 years is typical, though some cases resolve faster with treatment.</p>

      <h2>Who Gets Frozen Shoulder?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Most common in ages 40-60</li>
        <li>More common in women</li>
        <li>Diabetes significantly increases risk</li>
        <li>Thyroid disorders</li>
        <li>After periods of immobilization</li>
        <li>After surgery or injury</li>
        <li>Sometimes occurs without clear cause</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Exercises by Stage</h2>

      <h3>Freezing Stage: Gentle Movement, Pain Management</h3>

      <p>During the painful freezing stage, aggressive stretching can worsen inflammation. Focus on gentle movement within tolerance.</p>

      <h4>1. Pendulum Exercises</h4>
      <p>Lean forward, supporting yourself on a table with your good arm. Let the affected arm hang down relaxed. Gently swing the arm in small circles, forward/back, and side to side. Let gravity and momentum do the work—don't actively muscle through it.</p>
      <p><strong>1-2 minutes, several times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Passive External Rotation</h4>
      <p>Hold a stick or cane with both hands. Use the good arm to gently push the affected arm into external rotation (rotating forearm outward). Go only to the point of mild discomfort, not pain.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>3. Supine Flexion</h4>
      <p>Lie on your back. Use your good arm to lift the affected arm overhead as far as comfortable. The lying position relaxes the shoulder muscles, allowing more movement.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps</strong></p>

      <h3>Frozen Stage: Progressive Stretching</h3>

      <p>As pain decreases, more aggressive stretching becomes appropriate.</p>

      <h4>4. Towel Stretch (Internal Rotation)</h4>
      <p>Hold a towel behind your back—good arm on top, affected arm on bottom. Use the good arm to gently pull the affected arm up your back. This targets the motion needed for reaching behind you.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Cross-Body Stretch</h4>
      <p>Bring your affected arm across your body at chest height. Use your good hand to gently pull the elbow toward the opposite shoulder.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps</strong></p>

      <h4>6. Wall Climb (Flexion)</h4>
      <p>Face a wall. Walk your fingers up the wall as high as you can, keeping elbow relatively straight. Mark your progress and try to improve gradually over days/weeks.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold at top for 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Wall Climb (Abduction)</h4>
      <p>Stand sideways to a wall. Walk fingers up the wall to the side, raising your arm out away from your body.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold at top for 15-30 seconds, 5-10 reps</strong></p>

      <h4>8. Doorway Stretch</h4>
      <p>Stand in a doorway with elbow bent 90 degrees, forearm on the door frame. Gently step through to stretch the front of the shoulder. Adjust elbow height to target different parts of the capsule.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 15-30 seconds each position</strong></p>

      <h3>Thawing Stage: Strengthening and Full Recovery</h3>

      <p>As motion returns, add strengthening to rebuild function.</p>

      <h4>9. External Rotation with Band</h4>
      <p>Elbow at side, bent 90 degrees. Hold resistance band and rotate forearm outward against resistance. This strengthens the rotator cuff.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15</strong></p>

      <h4>10. Internal Rotation with Band</h4>
      <p>Same position, rotate forearm inward against band resistance.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15</strong></p>

      <h4>11. Scapular Exercises</h4>
      <p>Prone Y, T, and W raises to rebuild shoulder blade control and strength that's been lost during immobility.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12 each</strong></p>

      <h4>12. Progressive Overhead Work</h4>
      <p>As motion improves, gradually return to overhead activities. Start with light movements and progress to reaching, lifting, and eventually exercise.</p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Freezing Stage (High Pain)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Pendulums: 1-2 minutes, 3-4 times daily</li>
        <li>Passive stretches: Gentle, within pain tolerance</li>
        <li>Heat before, ice after if needed</li>
        <li>Anti-inflammatory medication as recommended by doctor</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Frozen Stage (Stiff, Less Pain)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>All stretches: 5-10 reps each, 2-3 times daily</li>
        <li>Hold stretches longer (30 seconds)</li>
        <li>Push further into the stretch (mild discomfort okay)</li>
        <li>Heat before stretching</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Thawing Stage (Improving)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Continue stretching for maintenance</li>
        <li>Add strengthening: 3x/week</li>
        <li>Progress to functional activities</li>
        <li>Gradually return to normal use</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Tips for Faster Recovery</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Heat before stretching:</strong> Warm shower or heating pad makes tissue more pliable</li>
        <li><strong>Consistency matters most:</strong> Multiple short sessions beat one long session</li>
        <li><strong>Don't force through severe pain:</strong> Aggressive stretching in the freezing stage can worsen inflammation</li>
        <li><strong>Sleep position:</strong> Avoid lying on affected shoulder; hug a pillow</li>
        <li><strong>Use the arm:</strong> Don't completely stop using it—gentle activity is good</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Other Treatment Options</h2>

      <p>Exercise is foundational, but other treatments may help:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Physical therapy:</strong> Professional guidance through the stages</li>
        <li><strong>Corticosteroid injection:</strong> Can help during the painful freezing stage</li>
        <li><strong>Hydrodilatation:</strong> Injecting fluid to stretch the capsule</li>
        <li><strong>Manipulation under anesthesia:</strong> For stubborn cases</li>
        <li><strong>Arthroscopic release:</strong> Surgical cutting of the capsule (rare)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long Until Recovery?</h2>

      <p>Frozen shoulder is notoriously slow:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Average total duration:</strong> 1-3 years</li>
        <li><strong>With consistent treatment:</strong> Many improve faster (6-12 months)</li>
        <li><strong>Most people:</strong> Eventually recover full or near-full motion</li>
        <li><strong>Some:</strong> May have mild permanent limitation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>When to See a Doctor</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Initial diagnosis confirmation</li>
        <li>Pain not controlled with OTC medications</li>
        <li>No improvement after 4-6 weeks of exercises</li>
        <li>Considering injection therapy</li>
        <li>Symptoms after trauma (may be different injury)</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Prevention (If You've Had It Before)</h2>

      <p>Frozen shoulder can recur, and having it once increases risk in the other shoulder:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>Maintain shoulder mobility with regular stretching</li>
        <li>If immobilized for any reason, start gentle motion early</li>
        <li>Manage diabetes and thyroid conditions</li>
        <li>Keep shoulders moving through full range regularly</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Frozen shoulder tests your patience, but it does get better. Consistent gentle stretching through the freezing stage, more aggressive stretching as pain allows, and strengthening during recovery will help you regain your shoulder function. Most people eventually return to normal—it just takes time.</p>
