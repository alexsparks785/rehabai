      <h1>How to Fix Jumper's Knee: Exercises for Patellar Tendinitis</h1>

      <p>That sharp pain right below your kneecap that flares up every time you jump, squat, or go up stairs? That's jumper's knee—and it's one of the most stubborn overuse injuries in sports. The good news: with the right exercises, especially eccentric loading, most cases heal without surgery.</p>

      <h2>What Is Jumper's Knee?</h2>

      <p>Jumper's knee (patellar tendinopathy) is an overuse injury of the patellar tendon—the strong band that connects your kneecap to your shinbone. Repeated stress causes micro-damage and degeneration in the tendon.</p>

      <p><strong>Symptoms:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Pain at the bottom tip of the kneecap</li>
        <li>Pain with jumping, running, and squatting</li>
        <li>Pain going up and down stairs</li>
        <li>Stiffness after sitting</li>
        <li>Pain may ease during activity but worsen after</li>
        <li>Tenderness when pressing the base of the kneecap</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>What Causes Jumper's Knee?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Repetitive jumping:</strong> Basketball, volleyball, high jump</li>
        <li><strong>Rapid increase in training:</strong> Too much too soon</li>
        <li><strong>Hard surfaces:</strong> Training on concrete or hard courts</li>
        <li><strong>Tight quads and hamstrings:</strong> Increase tendon load</li>
        <li><strong>Weak quad strength:</strong> Tendon compensates for muscle weakness</li>
        <li><strong>Poor landing mechanics:</strong> Stiff landings stress the tendon</li>
        <li><strong>Training errors:</strong> Inadequate recovery between sessions</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Why Rest Alone Doesn't Work</h2>

      <p>Like other tendinopathies, jumper's knee is a degenerative condition, not primarily inflammatory. The tendon has become weakened and needs progressive loading to rebuild—not complete rest. Rest may reduce pain temporarily, but symptoms return when you resume activity because the tendon never got stronger.</p>

      <h2>The Solution: Eccentric and Heavy Slow Resistance Training</h2>

      <p>Research strongly supports two approaches for patellar tendinopathy:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Eccentric exercise:</strong> Muscle lengthening under load</li>
        <li><strong>Heavy slow resistance (HSR):</strong> Heavy weights with slow tempo</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Both stimulate tendon remodeling and healing.</p>

      <h2>Key Exercises</h2>

      <h3>Eccentric Decline Squat (Gold Standard)</h3>

      <h4>1. Single-Leg Decline Squat</h4>
      <p>The most researched exercise for patellar tendinopathy:</p>
      <ol>
        <li>Stand on a 25-degree decline board (or improvise with a wedge/board)</li>
        <li>Shift weight to the affected leg</li>
        <li>Slowly lower down over 3-5 seconds (the eccentric part)</li>
        <li>Use your other leg to help stand back up</li>
        <li>Repeat—only the lowering phase is on the affected leg</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, twice daily. Expect some pain (up to 5/10) during exercise.</strong></p>

      <h4>2. Double-Leg Decline Squat (Easier Version)</h4>
      <p>If single-leg is too painful, start with both legs. Same slow descent, 3-5 seconds down.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 15, twice daily</strong></p>

      <h3>Heavy Slow Resistance</h3>

      <h4>3. Leg Press (Heavy, Slow)</h4>
      <p>Use a leg press machine with moderate-heavy weight. Lower for 3 seconds, pause 2 seconds at bottom, push up for 3 seconds. Full range of motion. This builds tendon strength without the impact of jumping.</p>
      <p><strong>4 sets of 6-8 reps, 3x/week</strong></p>

      <h4>4. Leg Extension (Heavy, Slow)</h4>
      <p>Same tempo: 3 seconds up, 2 second pause at top, 3 seconds down. Focus on the last 30 degrees of extension where the patellar tendon works hardest.</p>
      <p><strong>4 sets of 6-8 reps, 3x/week</strong></p>

      <h4>5. Hack Squat or Smith Machine Squat</h4>
      <p>Heavy, controlled squats with the same 3-2-3 tempo. These allow you to load the tendon without balance being a limiting factor.</p>
      <p><strong>4 sets of 6-8 reps, 3x/week</strong></p>

      <h3>Supporting Exercises</h3>

      <h4>6. Spanish Squat</h4>
      <p>Loop a heavy band around a post at knee height and around the back of your knees. Step back to create tension. Squat while the band keeps your shins vertical. This loads the tendon differently and is often less painful.</p>
      <p><strong>3 sets of 12-15</strong></p>

      <h4>7. Isometric Wall Sit</h4>
      <p>Hold a wall sit position with knees at about 60-70 degrees. Isometrics can reduce pain and begin loading the tendon early in rehab.</p>
      <p><strong>5 sets of 45 seconds, 3x/week</strong></p>

      <h3>Stretching</h3>

      <h4>8. Quad Stretch</h4>
      <p>Standing or lying, pull heel toward glute. Tight quads increase tendon load.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each side, 2-3 times daily</strong></p>

      <h4>9. Hamstring Stretch</h4>
      <p>Straight leg stretch—tight hamstrings also affect knee mechanics.</p>
      <p><strong>Hold 30-45 seconds each side</strong></p>

      <h2>The Program</h2>

      <h3>Phase 1: Pain Reduction (Weeks 1-2)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Isometric wall sits: 5 x 45 seconds, daily</li>
        <li>Quad and hamstring stretching: 2-3 times daily</li>
        <li>Reduce aggravating activities (jumping, deep squats)</li>
        <li>Ice after activity if needed</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 2: Eccentric Loading (Weeks 2-8)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Decline squats: 3 x 15, twice daily</li>
        <li>Spanish squats: 3 x 12</li>
        <li>Continue stretching</li>
        <li>Some pain during exercise is expected and okay (up to 5/10)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 3: Heavy Slow Resistance (Weeks 4-12)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Leg press (3-2-3 tempo): 4 x 6-8</li>
        <li>Leg extension (3-2-3 tempo): 4 x 6-8</li>
        <li>Continue decline squats: 3 x 15, once daily</li>
        <li>3 sessions per week</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Phase 4: Return to Sport (Weeks 12+)</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Gradual reintroduction of jumping</li>
        <li>Continue maintenance strength work</li>
        <li>Progress landing difficulty slowly</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Managing Pain During Rehab</h2>

      <p>Some discomfort during exercises is acceptable and even necessary for tendon healing. Use this guide:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>0-3/10 pain:</strong> Acceptable, can progress</li>
        <li><strong>4-5/10 pain:</strong> Acceptable during exercise if it settles after</li>
        <li><strong>6+/10 pain:</strong> Too much—reduce load or volume</li>
        <li><strong>Pain worse 24 hours later:</strong> Did too much—back off</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Activity Modification</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Reduce jumping volume:</strong> Especially during acute phase</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid deep squatting:</strong> If painful, limit depth</li>
        <li><strong>Soften landings:</strong> Land with more knee bend</li>
        <li><strong>Cross-train:</strong> Cycling and swimming maintain fitness without tendon load</li>
        <li><strong>Patellar tendon strap:</strong> May reduce pain during activity</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>How Long to Heal?</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>4-6 weeks:</strong> Noticeable pain reduction with consistent loading</li>
        <li><strong>3-4 months:</strong> Significant improvement for most</li>
        <li><strong>6-12 months:</strong> Full return to sport for stubborn cases</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Tendon healing is slow because of limited blood supply. Patience and consistency are essential.</p>

      <h2>When to See a Professional</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>No improvement after 8 weeks of consistent exercise</li>
        <li>Severe pain limiting daily activities</li>
        <li>Pain at rest</li>
        <li>Significant swelling</li>
        <li>Considering injection therapy</li>
      </ul>

      <h2>Preventing Recurrence</h2>

      <ul>
        <li>Continue maintenance strength training</li>
        <li>Progress training loads gradually</li>
        <li>Address landing mechanics</li>
        <li>Don't neglect quad and hamstring flexibility</li>
        <li>Adequate recovery between jump-heavy sessions</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Jumper's knee is stubborn but beatable. The key is progressive loading through eccentric and heavy slow resistance exercises—giving the tendon the stimulus it needs to heal and strengthen. Stay consistent, accept that some discomfort during exercise is normal, and you'll get back to jumping pain-free.</p>
