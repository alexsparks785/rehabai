# How to Fix Hip Mobility: Exercises for Tight Hips

Tight hips affect everything—your squat depth, your walking gait, your lower back, and even how you sit. The hip is designed to be one of the most mobile joints in the body, but modern life conspires to lock it down. Here's how to restore what sitting has stolen.

## Understanding Hip Mobility

The hip is a ball-and-socket joint capable of movement in all planes:
- **Flexion:** Bringing knee toward chest
- **Extension:** Leg moving backward
- **Abduction:** Leg moving out to side
- **Adduction:** Leg moving across body
- **External rotation:** Knee turning outward
- **Internal rotation:** Knee turning inward

When any of these movements is restricted, your body compensates elsewhere—usually in your lower back or knees.

## Signs of Poor Hip Mobility

- Can't squat deep without heels lifting or back rounding
- Lower back pain, especially after sitting
- Tight feeling in the front of hips when standing
- Difficulty crossing legs or sitting cross-legged
- Knees cave in during squats
- Can't keep feet straight when squatting
- Walking feels restricted
- Hip pain after long periods of sitting

## What Causes Tight Hips?

### Prolonged Sitting
The #1 culprit. Sitting keeps your hips flexed for hours, shortening the hip flexors and deactivating the glutes.

### Lack of Movement Variety
If you only move in one plane (forward and back, like walking), the hips stiffen in other directions.

### Muscle Weakness
Weak glutes and hip stabilizers force other muscles to tighten up for stability.

### Past Injury
Hip, back, or knee injuries can lead to protective muscle tightness.

### Training Imbalances
Heavy quad work without adequate hip and glute work can create restrictions.

## Self-Assessment

### Deep Squat Test
1. Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
2. Squat as deep as possible
3. Note: Can you get thighs below parallel? Do heels stay down? Does back round?

### Thomas Test (Hip Flexor Tightness)
1. Sit on edge of a table or bed
2. Lie back and pull one knee to chest
3. Let the other leg hang off the edge
4. If the hanging thigh rises above horizontal, hip flexors are tight
5. If knee straightens excessively, rectus femoris is tight

### 90-90 Position
1. Sit on floor with both knees bent at 90 degrees (one in front, one behind)
2. Can you sit upright without leaning? Is one side significantly harder?

### Internal/External Rotation
1. Sit in a chair, feet flat
2. Rotate each knee inward and outward
3. Compare sides; note any significant restrictions

## Hip Mobility Exercises

### Hip Flexor Stretches

#### 1. Half-Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on one knee (put a cushion under it)
2. Other foot forward, knee at 90 degrees
3. Tuck your tailbone under (posterior pelvic tilt)
4. Squeeze the glute of the back leg
5. Lean forward slightly until you feel the stretch
6. Hold 90-120 seconds per side

**Key:** The tailbone tuck is essential. Without it, you're just hanging on the joint.

#### 2. Couch Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Kneel with one knee in the corner where wall meets floor
2. Back shin goes up the wall
3. Other foot forward in a lunge
4. Stay upright (don't lean forward)
5. Tuck tailbone and squeeze glute
6. Hold 90-120 seconds per side

**This is intense—ease into it.**

#### 3. Standing Hip Flexor Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Stand in a staggered stance
2. Tuck tailbone under
3. Squeeze back glute
4. Lean torso back slightly
5. Hold 45-60 seconds per side

### Hip Rotation Stretches

#### 4. 90-90 Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Sit with front leg at 90 degrees, shin perpendicular to body
2. Back leg at 90 degrees, shin behind you
3. Sit as tall as possible
4. Lean forward over front leg for deeper stretch
5. Hold 60-90 seconds per side

**Progression:** Rotate between sides smoothly (90-90 switches).

#### 5. Pigeon Pose
**How to do it:**
1. From all fours, bring one knee forward behind your wrist
2. Slide the other leg back
3. Square your hips
4. Fold forward over front leg
5. Hold 90-120 seconds per side

**Modification:** If too intense, do figure-4 stretch lying down instead.

#### 6. Figure-4 Stretch (Supine)
**How to do it:**
1. Lie on your back
2. Cross one ankle over opposite knee
3. Pull the uncrossed leg toward chest
4. Feel stretch in the crossed leg's hip
5. Hold 60-90 seconds per side

### Hip Adductor Stretches

#### 7. Frog Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Spread knees wide, feet wider than knees
3. Keep ankles in line with knees
4. Sink hips back and down
5. Hold 60-90 seconds

**Can rock forward and back to work different angles.**

#### 8. Wide-Legged Forward Fold
**How to do it:**
1. Stand with feet wide apart
2. Fold forward from hips
3. Let head hang
4. Hold 60-90 seconds

#### 9. Side Lunge Stretch
**How to do it:**
1. Stand wide, feet parallel
2. Shift weight to one side, bending that knee
3. Keep other leg straight
4. Feel stretch in straight leg's inner thigh
5. Hold 30-45 seconds per side

### Hip Extension

#### 10. Prone Hip Extension
**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down
2. Squeeze one glute and lift that leg
3. Keep leg straight
4. Don't arch your lower back
5. Hold 5-10 seconds, 10 reps per side

#### 11. Kneeling Hip Extension
**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on all fours
2. Extend one leg straight back
3. Squeeze glute at the top
4. Don't rotate hips
5. 15 reps per side

### Dynamic Mobility Drills

#### 12. Hip Circles
**How to do it:**
1. Stand on one leg (hold something for balance)
2. Lift other knee to hip height
3. Make large circles with your knee
4. 10 forward, 10 backward per leg

#### 13. Leg Swings
**How to do it:**
1. Hold onto something for balance
2. Swing one leg forward and back (front-to-back swings)
3. Then swing side to side (lateral swings)
4. 15-20 swings each direction per leg

#### 14. Deep Squat Hold
**How to do it:**
1. Squat as deep as possible
2. Keep heels down (elevate them if needed initially)
3. Use arms inside knees to push them out
4. Hold 1-2 minutes total (accumulate time)

**Goal:** Work toward 5 minutes of total deep squat time daily.

#### 15. Cossack Squat
**How to do it:**
1. Stand wide, feet slightly turned out
2. Shift weight to one side, squatting deep on that leg
3. Keep other leg straight, foot flexed
4. Alternate sides
5. 8-10 per side

## Strengthening in New Ranges

Mobility without strength doesn't stick. Build strength through your full range.

### Glute Bridges
- 3 sets of 15
- Focus on full hip extension at the top

### Goblet Squats (Full Depth)
- Work to your deepest comfortable position
- 3 sets of 10-12

### Romanian Deadlifts
- 3 sets of 10-12
- Strengthens hip hinge pattern

### Clamshells
- 2-3 sets of 15-20 per side
- Strengthens hip external rotators

### Copenhagen Plank (Adductors)
- 2-3 sets of 20-30 seconds per side
- Strengthens inner thigh

## Your Daily Hip Mobility Routine

### Morning (5 minutes)
- Hip circles: 10 each direction per leg
- Leg swings: 15 each direction per leg
- Deep squat hold: 1 minute

### After Sitting (3 minutes)
- Standing hip flexor stretch: 30 seconds per side
- Figure-4 stretch (can do in chair): 30 seconds per side
- Hip circles: 10 per leg

### Evening (15 minutes)
- Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch: 90 seconds per side
- 90-90 stretch: 60 seconds per side
- Pigeon or figure-4: 60 seconds per side
- Frog stretch: 60 seconds
- Deep squat hold: 2 minutes
- Cossack squats: 8 per side

## Timeline for Improvement

- **Week 1-2:** Stretches feel intense, some early changes
- **Week 3-4:** Noticeable improvement in range
- **Week 6-8:** Significant functional gains (deeper squats, less stiffness)
- **3+ months:** Major transformation possible with consistency

**Key principle:** Daily short sessions beat occasional long ones. Frequency matters most.

## Common Mistakes

**Holding breath:** Breathe deeply during stretches. Tension inhibits mobility.

**Forcing through pain:** Discomfort is fine; sharp pain means back off.

**Only stretching hip flexors:** Address ALL directions of hip movement.

**Neglecting thoracic spine and ankles:** These affect hip mechanics.

**Skipping strengthening:** New range needs strength to maintain.

**Being impatient:** Tissue changes take weeks to months.

## When to Seek Help

**See a professional if:**
- Sharp pain in the hip joint
- Clicking or catching that's painful
- Significant asymmetry between sides
- Pain that doesn't improve with consistent work
- History of hip injury or surgery
- Numbness or tingling in the leg

## The Bottom Line

Hip mobility is foundational—it affects how you sit, stand, walk, squat, and run. Modern life restricts it, but consistent work can restore it.

Start with the hip flexors—they're tight on almost everyone who sits. Add rotation work (90-90, pigeon). Don't forget the adductors (frog stretch). Finish with dynamic drills and strengthening.

The deep squat hold is your best single test and exercise. If you can sit comfortably in a deep squat for 5 minutes, your hips are doing well.

Spend 15-20 minutes daily on hip mobility for 6-8 weeks, and you'll move like a different person. Your hips are meant to be free—help them remember.
