# Swimming Exercises: Dryland Training for Better Performance

What you do out of the pool matters just as much as your lap count. Dryland training builds the strength, stability, and power that make you faster and more efficient in the water—while keeping shoulders healthy.

**Key insight:** Swimmer's shoulder is one of the most common overuse injuries in sports. Proper dryland work focuses on shoulder stability and scapular control, not just strength.

---

## Physical Demands of Swimming

### What Swimming Requires

- **Shoulder mobility and stability:** 6,000-10,000 shoulder revolutions per session
- **Core rotational control:** Transfers power from hips to arms
- **Hip flexibility:** Kick power comes from hip mobility
- **Scapular stability:** Anchors the arm stroke
- **Lat and posterior chain strength:** Main propulsion muscles
- **Ankle flexibility:** Efficient flutter and dolphin kicks

### Common Swimming Injuries

| Injury | Cause | Prevention Focus |
|--------|-------|------------------|
| Swimmer's shoulder | Impingement, rotator cuff overload | Scapular stability, external rotation strength |
| Neck pain | Breathing mechanics, head position | Thoracic mobility, cervical stability |
| Lower back pain | Poor body position, excessive extension | Core stability, hip flexor flexibility |
| Knee pain (breaststroke) | MCL stress during kick | Hip mobility, knee control |
| Ankle issues | Overuse from kicking | Ankle mobility, calf strength |

## Pre-Swim Warm-Up (10 Minutes)

Do this before every pool session.

### Arm Circles

**How to do it:**
1. Start with small circles, gradually increasing size
2. 10 forward, 10 backward
3. Progress to large circles across full range

### Band Pull-Aparts

**How to do it:**
1. Hold resistance band at shoulder width, arms extended
2. Pull hands apart, squeezing shoulder blades
3. Return with control
4. 15-20 reps

**Why it matters:** Activates posterior shoulder and scapular muscles before loading them.

### Shoulder External Rotation with Band

**How to do it:**
1. Elbow at side, bent 90°, hold band
2. Rotate forearm outward against resistance
3. Keep elbow pinned to side
4. 15 reps each arm

### Leg Swings

**How to do it:**
1. Hold wall for balance
2. Swing leg forward and back (10 each)
3. Swing leg side to side (10 each)
4. Switch legs

### Thoracic Rotations

**How to do it:**
1. Hands on hips or behind head
2. Rotate upper body left, then right
3. Keep hips facing forward
4. 10 each direction

### Streamline Position Holds

**How to do it:**
1. Stand against wall, arms overhead in streamline
2. Press lower back into wall
3. Hold 30 seconds
4. 3 reps

## Shoulder Stability Exercises

Healthy shoulders = sustainable swimming career.

### Y-T-W Raises

**Why it matters:** Strengthens lower trap and posterior shoulder—the muscles most likely to be underdeveloped in swimmers.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down on bench or floor
2. Y: Raise arms at 45° angle (thumbs up), lower
3. T: Raise arms straight out to sides (thumbs up), lower
4. W: Elbows bent, externally rotate and lift (like goal posts)
5. 10 reps each position

### Face Pulls

**How to do it:**
1. Attach band at face height
2. Pull band toward face, separating hands
3. End with hands by ears, elbows high
4. Squeeze shoulder blades at end range
5. 12-15 reps

### External Rotation at 90°

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on side, upper arm along body
2. Elbow bent 90°, forearm across belly
3. Rotate forearm toward ceiling
4. Lower with control
5. 12-15 reps each side

### Serratus Punch

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arm pointing toward ceiling with weight
2. Push weight further toward ceiling by protracting scapula
3. Lower scapula with control
4. 12-15 reps each arm

**Why it matters:** Serratus anterior stabilizes the scapula during overhead movement.

### Wall Slides

**How to do it:**
1. Stand with back against wall
2. Arms against wall, elbows bent 90° (like goal posts)
3. Slide arms up wall, keeping contact
4. Slide back down
5. 10-15 reps

## Core Stability Exercises

Swimming requires core stability during rotation, not just strength.

### Dead Bug

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back, arms toward ceiling, knees at 90°
2. Press lower back into floor (no arch)
3. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg
4. Return, switch sides
5. 8-10 reps each side

### Bird Dog

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously
3. Keep back flat, hips level
4. Hold 2-3 seconds
5. 10 reps each side

### Pallof Press

**Why it matters:** Trains anti-rotation—exactly what your core does during the catch and pull phase.

**How to do it:**
1. Stand perpendicular to band anchor
2. Hold band at chest
3. Press arms straight out
4. Resist rotation for 3-5 seconds
5. Return with control
6. 10 reps each side

### Side Plank with Rotation

**How to do it:**
1. Side plank on forearm
2. Reach top arm under body (rotate)
3. Open back up, reaching arm to ceiling
4. 8-10 reps each side

### Hollow Body Hold

**Why it matters:** Mimics the streamline position under tension.

**How to do it:**
1. Lie on back
2. Press lower back into floor
3. Lift legs and shoulders slightly
4. Arms by ears or sides
5. Hold 20-30 seconds
6. 3-5 sets

### Flutter Kicks (Dryland)

**How to do it:**
1. Lie face down, legs extended
2. Lift legs slightly off ground
3. Flutter kick with straight legs
4. Keep hips stable
5. 30-60 seconds

## Power and Propulsion Exercises

Building power translates to faster times.

### Lat Pull-Downs

**How to do it:**
1. Cable or band attached overhead
2. Pull down to upper chest
3. Squeeze lats at bottom
4. Control return
5. 10-12 reps

**Swimming connection:** Lats are the primary propulsion muscle.

### Pull-Ups or Assisted Pull-Ups

**How to do it:**
1. Grip bar slightly wider than shoulders
2. Pull until chin clears bar
3. Lower with control
4. Use band for assistance if needed
5. Work toward unassisted reps

### Bent-Over Rows

**How to do it:**
1. Hinge at hips, flat back
2. Pull weights toward hips
3. Squeeze shoulder blades at top
4. Lower with control
5. 10-12 reps

### Medicine Ball Slams

**How to do it:**
1. Hold med ball overhead
2. Slam into ground with force
3. Catch and repeat
4. 8-10 reps

**Why it matters:** Develops explosive lat engagement.

### Push-Ups with Plus

**How to do it:**
1. Standard push-up
2. At top, push further (round upper back)
3. Protract scapulae, then return to neutral
4. 10-12 reps

**Why it matters:** Adds serratus work to the basic push-up.

## Hip and Ankle Mobility

Efficient kicking requires mobile hips and ankles.

### 90/90 Hip Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with front leg at 90°, back leg at 90°
2. Lean forward over front shin
3. Hold 30-60 seconds
4. Switch sides

### Frog Stretch

**Why it matters:** Opens hip adductors—important for breaststroke kick.

**How to do it:**
1. Start on all fours
2. Widen knees as far as comfortable
3. Rock hips back toward heels
4. Hold 30-60 seconds

### Ankle Dorsiflexion Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on one knee, other foot forward
2. Push knee forward over toes
3. Keep heel on ground
4. Hold 30 seconds each side

### Ankle Plantarflexion (Point)

**Why it matters:** Pointed ankles create efficient kick.

**How to do it:**
1. Sit with legs extended
2. Point toes as far as possible
3. Add resistance with band around foot
4. 15-20 reps each foot

### Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch

**How to do it:**
1. Kneel on one knee
2. Tuck pelvis (flatten lower back)
3. Shift hips forward
4. Hold 30-45 seconds each side

## Sample Dryland Programs

### Pre-Season (3x per week, 45-60 min)

Build base strength and mobility.

**Warm-up (5 min):**
- Arm circles, leg swings, band pull-aparts

**Shoulder stability (10 min):**
- Y-T-W raises: 2x10 each
- External rotation at 90°: 2x12 each
- Face pulls: 2x15

**Core (10 min):**
- Dead bug: 3x10 each
- Side plank with rotation: 2x8 each
- Hollow body hold: 3x30 seconds

**Strength (15-20 min):**
- Pull-ups or lat pull-downs: 3x8-10
- Push-ups with plus: 3x12
- Bent-over rows: 3x10

**Mobility (10 min):**
- 90/90 stretch: 60s each side
- Kneeling hip flexor: 45s each side
- Ankle work: 15 reps each

### In-Season (2x per week, 30-40 min)

Maintain strength without fatiguing for pool work.

**Warm-up (5 min):**
- Dynamic movements

**Prehab circuit (10 min):**
- Band pull-aparts: 2x15
- Y-T-W: 1x10 each
- External rotation: 2x12

**Maintenance strength (15 min):**
- Lat pull-down: 2x10
- Bird dog: 2x10 each
- Flutter kicks: 2x30 seconds
- Push-ups: 2x10

**Mobility (5-10 min):**
- Key areas for you

### Taper Period (1-2x per week, 20 min)

Maintain activation without fatigue.

**Light activation only:**
- Band pull-aparts: 1x15
- Face pulls: 1x12
- Dead bug: 1x8 each
- Hollow body: 2x20 seconds
- Mobility as needed

## Stroke-Specific Considerations

### Freestyle and Backstroke

**Focus areas:**
- Shoulder internal rotation flexibility (recovery)
- Lat strength (pull)
- Core rotation control
- Hip flexibility (kick from hips)

**Key exercises:**
- Lat pull-downs, face pulls, thoracic rotation

### Breaststroke

**Focus areas:**
- Hip adductor flexibility (whip kick)
- Knee stability (MCL protection)
- Pec strength (pull)
- Lower back stability

**Key exercises:**
- Frog stretch, clamshells, dead bug, push-ups

### Butterfly

**Focus areas:**
- Shoulder mobility (extreme overhead position)
- Thoracic extension
- Core undulation control
- Hip flexor flexibility

**Key exercises:**
- Cat-cow, serratus punch, hollow body, hip flexor stretch

## Common Mistakes

### Training Errors

- **Too much pressing, not enough pulling:** Swimmers already press a lot—balance with rows and pulls
- **Ignoring shoulder stability:** Jumping to heavy weights without prehab work
- **Training to fatigue before swim practice:** Dryland should enhance, not drain
- **Skipping mobility:** Tight ankles and hips limit technique

### Red Flags to Address

- **Shoulder pain during or after swimming:** Stop and assess—likely impingement
- **Neck tightness:** Check breathing pattern and thoracic mobility
- **Lower back pain:** Core stability and hip flexor flexibility
- **Knee pain during breaststroke:** Hip mobility and knee control work

---

## The Bottom Line

Dryland training makes you a better swimmer and keeps you in the pool longer. Prioritize shoulder health over raw strength—mobility and stability before power.

**Start here:**
1. Do the warm-up before every pool session (non-negotiable)
2. Add 2 full dryland sessions per week in off-season
3. Focus on shoulder stability and core, then add strength
4. Maintain with 1-2 sessions during competition season
5. Address mobility limitations—they limit technique

What you do on land shows up in the water.
